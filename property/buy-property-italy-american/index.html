How to Buy Property in Italy as an American: Complete Guide (2025)

Step-by-step guide for Americans buying property in Italy — the legal process, notary fees, purchase taxes, mortgages, due diligence checklist, and the key mistakes to avoid. Updated 2025.

Americans can buy property in Italy freely — the US-Italy reciprocity agreement puts American buyers on exactly the same legal footing as Italian citizens. No restrictions, no special permits, no minimum investment requirements.

What IS complicated is the process. Italian real estate transactions involve a notary (who is a public official, not your lawyer), mandatory due diligence that many buyers skip, taxes that can add 10–15% to your purchase price, and a bureaucratic timeline that typically runs 2–6 months.

Can Americans Buy Property in Italy?

Yes — completely freely. The US and Italy have a reciprocity agreement that grants American citizens the same property rights as Italian nationals:

  • No foreign buyer restrictions
  • No special permits required
  • Eligible for "prima casa" (first home) tax benefits if you establish Italian residency
  • Can apply for Italian mortgages
  • Can inherit Italian property

The Five People Involved in an Italian Property Purchase

1. The Notaio (Notary) — NOT Your Lawyer

The Italian notary is a public official appointed by the state. They verify identities, conduct legal title searches, draft and execute the final deed of sale (rogito), collect and pay transfer taxes, and register the new ownership.

Critical warning: The notary represents the state and the transaction — not you personally.

2. Your Lawyer (Avvocato) — Essential

Hire a real estate lawyer who specializes in Italian property and speaks English. They investigate building permits, planning compliance, condominium disputes, hidden debts. For any transaction over €100,000, this is not optional.

3. Geometra (Surveyor)

Technical expert who verifies that the property matches its cadastral description and checks for unauthorized building works. Costs €500–2,000.

4. Real Estate Agent (Agente Immobiliare)

Standard commission: 3–5% of purchase price, split between buyer and seller.

Step-by-Step: The Italian Property Purchase Process

Step 1: Property Search and Offer

Find your property through agents (Immobiliare.it and Idealista.it are the main portals). When you find something you want, submit a written offer (proposta di acquisto) — this is legally binding once accepted.

Step 2: Preliminary Contract (Compromesso)

Once your offer is accepted, sign the compromesso — the preliminary purchase contract. At this point you pay a deposit, typically 10–30% of the purchase price.

Two types of deposit:

  • Caparra confirmatoria: If you back out, you lose the deposit. If the seller backs out, they return double your deposit.
  • Acconto: Simple advance payment. Less protection for the buyer.
⚠ Important

Important: Have your lawyer review the compromesso before signing.

Step 3: Due Diligence (Critical)

Your lawyer and geometra conduct:

Legal checks:

  • Title search — verifying ownership and any mortgages or liens
  • Urban planning compliance — verifying building permits are in order
  • Condominium checks — any unpaid fees transfer to the new owner

Technical checks:

  • Energy Performance Certificate (APE) — mandatory
  • Structural survey
  • Verification that the property matches its cadastral plans
💡 Tip

Fabrizio's note: Urban planning compliance is the single most common issue in Italian property purchases. Unauthorized additions are extremely common in Italy — if not resolved before closing, they become your problem.

Step 4: Final Deed (Rogito) and Closing

The rogito is the final notarial deed of sale. At this meeting you pay the remaining purchase balance, all taxes and fees, and ownership transfers to you.

Italian Mortgages for Americans

For non-residents: Maximum LTV: 50–60%. You need at least 40–50% as a down payment.

For Italian residents (Americans who have moved to Italy): Maximum LTV: up to 80%.

Practical alternative: Many Americans find it easier to use equity from US property (HELOC, cash-out refinance) rather than navigating Italian mortgage approval.

Property Taxes After Purchase

IMU (Imposta Municipale Unica):

  • Prima casa (main residence): Exempt from IMU
  • Seconda casa: IMU applies — typically €500–3,000/year

Capital gains tax on sale: 26% on profit if sold within 5 years. Exempt if held more than 5 years.

Common Mistakes Americans Make

1. Skipping the independent lawyer Relying only on the notary leaves you without anyone protecting your interests.

2. Signing the compromesso before due diligence Your lawyer should review everything before you sign.

3. Ignoring urban planning compliance Unauthorized building works must be regularized before sale, or they transfer to you.

4. Underestimating total costs Budget 10–15% in taxes and fees on top of the asking price.

5. Using the wrong money transfer method Use Wise for large transfers — saves thousands vs. bank wire.

6. Not checking condominium arrears Unpaid condominium fees from the previous owner transfer to the new owner.

FAQ

Do Americans need Italian residency to buy property in Italy? No. Non-residents can buy freely. However, Italian residency gives you access to "prima casa" tax benefits (2% vs 9% registration tax).

How long does the buying process take? Typically 2–6 months from accepted offer to closing.

Is buying property in Italy a path to residency? Not directly. Owning property doesn't grant residency rights. However, it satisfies the "proof of accommodation" requirement for your visa application.
