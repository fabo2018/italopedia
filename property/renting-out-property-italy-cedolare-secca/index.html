Renting Out Property in Italy: Cedolare Secca vs Ordinary Regime

If you own Italian property and rent it out, you have a choice in how that income is taxed. The cedolare secca is a flat 21% (or 10% for some contracts) substitute tax — simple, predictable, and usually the better choice. Here is how both regimes work and which one you should use.

Italy taxes rental income from Italian property — regardless of whether the owner is an Italian resident, an American resident in Italy, or a non-resident American who owns Italian property. If money comes in as rent for an Italian property, Italy wants a share.

The choice of tax regime determines how much Italy takes and how you calculate and pay it. Most individual property owners find the cedolare secca simpler and financially superior to the ordinary regime — but there are exceptions.

The Two Tax Regimes

Italian property rental income can be taxed under one of two systems:

  1. Cedolare secca: A flat substitute tax of 21% (or 10% for certain contract types) applied to gross rent. Replaces IRPEF, registration tax, and stamp duty on the contract. Simple, flat, final.
  2. Ordinario (ordinary IRPEF regime): Rental income is added to your total Italian income and taxed at your marginal IRPEF bracket (23–43%), applied to 95% of gross rent (a 5% deemed deduction). You also pay registration tax on the lease.

Cedolare Secca: How It Works

The cedolare secca was introduced in 2011 and has become the default choice for most individual landlords. Key features:

  • Rate: 21% of gross rent for standard lease agreements (contratto a libero mercato)
  • Rate: 10% of gross rent for "assisted" leases (contratti a canone concordato) — these are below-market leases agreed under collective agreements in designated high-demand cities
  • Replaces: IRPEF on rental income, registration tax (normally 2% of annual rent), bollo on the contract
  • Requires: An election in the lease agreement and registration of the lease at the Agenzia delle Entrate
  • Available for: Residential properties rented to individuals for residential use. Not available for commercial leases.
  • Cannot be combined with: Deductions for property expenses (repairs, depreciation). The cedolare is a substitute tax on gross rent — no deductions.

Making the election: the landlord elects cedolare secca either in the lease contract itself or by filing a specific form (RLI — Richiesta di Registrazione e adempimenti Locazione) at the Agenzia delle Entrate.

Ordinary IRPEF Regime

Under the ordinary regime, rental income is added to your other Italian income and taxed at your IRPEF bracket rates. Italy allows a 5% deemed deduction, so only 95% of gross rent is added to your taxable income.

You also pay registration tax (imposta di registro) of 2% of the annual rent on the lease, due within 30 days of signing and annually on renewal.

When the ordinary regime might be preferable:

  • Your total Italian income is very low — if your marginal IRPEF rate is 23% (first bracket), and you have significant deductible property expenses, the ordinary regime with deductions might produce a lower effective tax
  • Your property has significant annual maintenance costs that could be deducted
  • The rental income is from a commercial property (cedolare is not available)

Which Regime to Choose

For most Americans renting out Italian property, the cedolare secca at 21% is the better choice because:

  • Simplicity: one flat rate, no complex deduction tracking
  • If your IRPEF bracket is above 21% (which it will be for most earners at the 35% or 43% bracket), the cedolare's 21% rate saves money
  • No registration tax on the lease saves 2% of annual rent
Example: €12,000/year rental income, owner in 35% IRPEF bracket

Cedolare secca (21%): €12,000 × 21% = €2,520 tax + €0 registration = €2,520 total

Ordinary regime (35%): €12,000 × 95% × 35% = €3,990 tax + €240 registration = €4,230 total

Cedolare saves: €1,710/year

Lease Registration Requirements

Italian law requires registration of rental leases at the Agenzia delle Entrate within 30 days of signing (or before the lease start date if earlier). This applies to leases of any duration.

Short leases (under 30 days): do not require registration. This is the basis for most Airbnb-type arrangements.

Registration under cedolare secca: when you elect cedolare, the registration tax (2%) is waived. You still register the lease but pay no registration fee.

Registration under ordinary regime: you pay 2% of the annual rent, plus stamp duty (marche da bollo on the contract pages), annually.

Registration process: through the Agenzia delle Entrate online portal (with SPID) using the RLI form, or at an Agenzia delle Entrate office. Your commercialista can handle this for you.

Airbnb and Short-Term Rentals

Short-term rentals (affitti brevi) — defined as residential leases of 30 days or less — are a separate regime under Italian law (L. 96/2017):

  • Not subject to lease registration requirement
  • Taxed under cedolare secca at 21% — the same rate as long-term cedolare
  • Platforms like Airbnb operating in Italy are required to withhold 21% from landlord payments and remit to the Agenzia delle Entrate on your behalf
  • Require registration in the national CIN (Codice Identificativo Nazionale) registry — a new national registration system introduced in 2024

The CIN requirement: As of September 2024, all short-term rental properties in Italy must have a CIN (national identification code) visible on the listing. Failure to register and display the CIN: fines up to €8,000. Register through the Ministero del Turismo portal.

Municipal regulations: individual cities have added their own short-term rental restrictions (notably Florence and Venice with significant limits). Check your specific commune's regulations before listing.

Non-Resident Property Owners

Americans who own Italian property but are not Italian residents (living in the US) owe Italian tax on Italian rental income. The same cedolare secca vs ordinary regime choice applies.

Practical consideration: as a non-resident, you still file an Italian tax return (Modello Redditi PF) to report rental income and pay the applicable tax. This requires an Italian codice fiscale and some form of representation in Italy — either a commercialista who handles your return or, for simple situations, direct filing through the Agenzia delle Entrate portal.

US Reporting of Italian Rental Income

As a US citizen, Italian rental income must be reported on your US Form 1040, Schedule E. Italy taxes the income first (cedolare secca or IRPEF). The Foreign Tax Credit on Form 1116 allows you to credit Italian tax paid against your US tax liability on the same income.

The cedolare secca is generally treated as a creditable foreign income tax for US FTC purposes, though some practitioners have raised questions about its substitute-tax nature. Most practitioners treat it as creditable, making double taxation on Italian rental income uncommon when FTC is applied correctly.

FAQ

Can I deduct Italian mortgage interest under cedolare secca?
No. The cedolare secca replaces the ordinary income tax calculation and prohibits expense deductions. If you have a mortgage on the rental property and want to deduct interest, you must use the ordinary regime instead. For most high-bracket earners, even with mortgage interest, the cedolare secca is still preferable — calculate both scenarios for your specific situation.
What if my tenant stops paying rent? Do I still owe cedolare on unpaid rent?
Generally no. Italian tax law allows you to reduce the taxable rental income for arrears that are not collected if certain conditions are met — specifically, if you have obtained a court order confirming non-payment (sfratto per morosità). Simply having an unpaid tenant is not sufficient to exclude the income; you need documentation of the legal default.
Does the cedolare secca apply to renting to a business?
No. Cedolare secca is available only for residential leases to individuals for residential use. If you rent to a company, an office, or any non-residential tenant, you must use the ordinary regime (IRPEF + registration tax).
