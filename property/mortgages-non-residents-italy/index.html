Italian Mortgages for Non-Residents: Can Americans Get a Mortgage in Italy? (2025)

Complete guide to getting a mortgage (mutuo) in Italy as an American non-resident — which banks lend to foreigners, LTV limits, required documents, interest rates, and the full application process.

FB
By Fabrizio Boggio
Updated May 2026 9 min read VERIFIED
✉ 𝕏 ⌘

Yes, Americans can get a mortgage in Italy. Italian banks lend to non-EU citizens, including Americans, for Italian property purchases. But the conditions are significantly stricter than for Italian residents, and the process is more complex than getting a mortgage in the U.S.

This guide covers what Italian banks require, which institutions are most accessible for Americans, what the process looks like, and the alternatives if a traditional Italian mortgage doesn't work for your situation.

Key Differences: Italian Mortgage vs. U.S. Mortgage

Before diving into the process, understand what's different:

Feature Italian Mortgage U.S. Mortgage
Max LTV (non-resident) 50–70% Up to 97%
Max LTV (resident) 80% Up to 97%
Rates (2025) 3.5–5.5% (fixed); 3–4.5% (variable) Varies
Processing time 60–90 days 30–45 days
Insurance requirement Life insurance required Varies
Maximum term 30 years 30 years
Pre-approval Rare / informal Standard
Language Italian English

The LTV gap is the most important difference. In Italy, as a non-resident American, you'll typically finance 50–70% of the property value — meaning you need 30–50% as a down payment. On a €200,000 property, that's €60,000–100,000 cash upfront.

Which Italian Banks Lend to Americans?

Not all Italian banks handle non-resident foreign mortgages. The ones with established programs:

Intesa Sanpaolo

Italy's largest bank. Has a dedicated non-resident mortgage product (Mutuo per non residenti). Offices throughout Italy and some branches in the U.S.
- LTV for non-residents: Up to 60%
- Minimum loan: €80,000
- Documentation: Extensive — see below

Unicredit

Second largest Italian bank. Has handled non-resident mortgages and has some international branches.
- LTV for non-residents: Up to 60%
- English-language support: Better than most Italian banks

Mediobanca (via CheBanca!)

The retail arm of Mediobanca. More competitive rates, digital-friendly application.
- LTV: Up to 70% for qualified non-resident applicants
- Minimum loan: €50,000

BNL (BNP Paribas Group)

French-Italian bank — their French parent means more international banking sophistication.
- LTV: Up to 60%
- International income: Better experience handling foreign income documentation

Credem (Credito Emiliano)

Regional bank strong in Emilia-Romagna. Less experience with non-residents but willing in some cases.

Cassa Rurale / Banche di Credito Cooperativo

Local cooperative banks. Can be more flexible in rural areas for properties they know well. Less equipped for foreign income verification.

Eligibility Requirements

Income Documentation

Italian banks need to understand your income. For Americans, this means:

Employed by U.S. company:
- Last 2 years of U.S. tax returns (1040)
- Last 3 pay stubs or payroll statements
- Employment contract or letter confirming employment
- Last 3 months of bank statements showing salary deposits
- All documents must be translated into Italian (traduzione giurata) and may need apostille

Self-employed / Partita IVA:
- Last 2 years of tax returns (1040 + Schedule C)
- 6 months bank statements
- Description of business activity
- If you have Italian Partita IVA: Italian tax returns (Modello Redditi) + last F24 payments

Retiree:
- Social Security Award Letter (and/or pension statement)
- Last 2 years of 1040
- Investment/brokerage statements if relevant
- Bank statements confirming regular deposits

Rental income:
- Lease agreements
- Bank statements showing rental deposits
- 1040 Schedule E

Credit History

Italian banks cannot access U.S. credit bureaus (Experian, Equifax, TransUnion). This is a double-edged situation:
- They can't see your positive credit history
- They also can't see any negatives

What they can check:
- Italian credit registry (CRIF) — if you have any Italian financial history
- EU-wide credit data (limited)
- Bank references

Bring a U.S. credit report even though the bank can't formally use it — it demonstrates financial responsibility.

Down Payment / Equity

You need 30–50% of the property value in cash, demonstrable through bank statements. The source of funds matters:
- Savings: demonstrated by 6–12 months of bank statements
- Sale of U.S. property: sale documentation
- Inheritance: estate documentation
- Investment liquidation: brokerage statements

Italian banks increasingly request anti-money laundering (AML) documentation for large fund transfers. Be prepared to explain the origin of your down payment clearly.

Age Limits

Most Italian banks require the mortgage to be fully repaid by your 75th or 80th birthday. A 65-year-old applicant is therefore limited to a 10–15 year mortgage term (not 25–30 years). This shortens the term and increases monthly payments.

Interest Rates in Italy (2025)

Italian mortgage rates as of early 2025, following ECB rate movements:

Rate Type Range (2025)
Fixed rate (tasso fisso) 3.5–5.5%
Variable rate (tasso variabile) linked to Euribor 3.0–4.5%
Mixed rate (tasso misto) 3.5–5.0%

Fixed vs. variable: Post-2022 rate hike environment has made fixed rates more popular among foreign buyers who want payment certainty. Variable rates (linked to 3-month or 12-month Euribor) can decrease if the ECB cuts rates further — but create payment uncertainty.

The Italian Mortgage Application: Step by Step

Step 1: Property Selection

You need a specific property to apply for a mortgage. Italian banks don't issue pre-approval letters like U.S. banks — they assess the specific property as collateral.

Step 2: Bank Inquiry (Richiesta Preventiva)

Contact the bank and request a preliminary assessment. Bring:
- Your income documentation
- Description of the property you want to buy
- Your Codice Fiscale
- Passport

The bank will indicate whether they can proceed and at what indicative rate and LTV.

Step 3: Formal Application (Domanda di Mutuo)

Submit the formal application with complete documentation. The bank submits your file to their internal credit committee.

Step 4: Property Appraisal (Perizia)

The bank sends an appraiser (perito) to assess the property's market value. The mortgage is calculated on the appraised value (not the sale price, if the sale price is higher). The appraisal fee: €300–600, typically paid by the applicant.

Step 5: Credit Committee Decision

The bank's credit committee reviews income, property, LTV, and risk. Typical timeline: 15–30 days from complete file submission.

Step 6: Offer Letter (Foglio Informativo Europeo Standardizzato / ESIS)

If approved, the bank issues a standardized European mortgage offer (ESIS document) detailing all terms. You have a 14-day reflection period to accept or decline — mandatory under EU consumer credit law.

Step 7: Signing at Notaio

The mortgage deed is signed simultaneously with (or just before) the property purchase deed at the notaio. The bank's representative or their notaio is present.

The bank transfers funds directly to the seller (you never receive the mortgage money directly).

Required Insurance

Italian mortgages require two mandatory insurance policies:

1. Assicurazione Incendio e Scoppio (Fire and Explosion Insurance)
- Required by Italian law for mortgaged property
- Annual cost: €100–300 depending on property value and coverage
- Can be purchased from any insurer (not required to use the bank's insurer — shop around)

2. Assicurazione Vita (Life Insurance)
- Required by most banks as a condition of mortgage
- Policy must cover the mortgage balance
- Annual cost: €300–1,500+ depending on age and coverage amount
- Banks pressure you to use their insurance product (often overpriced) — you have the right to use any insurer

Alternatives to an Italian Bank Mortgage

If the Italian mortgage process is too complex or you don't qualify, consider:

Cash Purchase

The simplest path. Roughly 50–60% of Italian property purchases by foreigners are cash transactions. With the LTV limits making mortgage financing less attractive anyway, cash avoids 60–90 days of bank processing, reduces notaio costs (no mortgage deed), and makes you a more attractive buyer to sellers.

If you're drawing down retirement accounts (IRA, 401k) to buy Italian property, model the tax implications carefully.

U.S. Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC)

If you own U.S. property with significant equity, a U.S. HELOC at U.S. rates may be cheaper and simpler than an Italian mortgage. You borrow in USD against your U.S. home and use the funds in Italy. Currency risk exists (if the dollar strengthens vs. euro, your Italian assets decline in dollar terms).

International Property Finance Specialists

Some international mortgage brokers specialize in cross-border property finance. They work with multiple Italian banks and understand documentation requirements for foreign income. A broker's fee (0.5–1% of loan amount) may be worth the time saved.

Reputable in this space: Idealista Mortgages, Expat Mortgages Italy, some international branches of Banca Sella.

Tax Implications of Italian Mortgage Interest

Italian mortgage interest is deductible on Italian taxes (19% deduction on up to €4,000/year of interest, for primary residence). If you're an Italian tax resident and the property is your primary residence, this provides meaningful tax benefit.

For U.S. tax purposes: mortgage interest on a foreign primary or secondary residence is generally deductible on Schedule A, subject to the same limits as U.S. mortgage interest deductions.

Practical Tips

Build a relationship with the bank first. If you're opening an Italian bank account (which you need anyway), use the same bank you plan to approach for a mortgage. Having a 6–12 month banking relationship makes the mortgage application substantially easier.

Use a bilingual mortgage broker. The documentation requirements and bank communication in Italian are significant obstacles. A broker who navigates both English and Italian bank requirements saves weeks of confusion.

Get your U.S. documents apostilled and translated in advance. Don't wait until the bank requests them — it takes 2–4 weeks. Have apostilled, translated copies of your last 2 tax returns and key financial documents ready before you start the process.

Apply for a longer term than you need. Italian mortgage payments on a 25-year term are significantly lower than on a 15-year term. You can always make extra payments to pay off early (check for prepayment penalties — some Italian mortgages have them in the first 5 years).
