Property Purchase Taxes in Italy: Exact Calculation

When you buy Italian property, you pay 3–10% in taxes on top of the purchase price — plus notary fees. The exact amount depends on whether it is your primary residence, whether you are buying from a private seller or a developer, and whether you qualify for the agevolazioni prima casa reduction. Here is the full calculation.

Every Italian property purchase incurs taxes that are collected by the notary at the closing (rogito) and remitted to the Italian tax authorities. Unlike the US, where closing costs are largely fees rather than taxes, Italy's system imposes substantial transfer taxes on every property transaction. The good news: the tax base is the cadastral value — not the purchase price — for transactions between private sellers, which is often significantly lower than market value.

Overview of Italian Property Purchase Taxes

Three taxes apply to every Italian residential property purchase:

  1. Imposta di registro (registration tax) — the main transfer tax. Rate: 2% for prima casa (primary residence) or 9% for non-primary residence. Applied to the cadastral value for private-to-private sales.
  2. Imposta ipotecaria (mortgage/land registry tax) — €50 (fixed) for prima casa; 2% of cadastral value for non-prima casa
  3. Imposta catastale (cadastral tax) — €50 (fixed) for prima casa; 1% of cadastral value for non-prima casa

When buying from a developer (nuova costruzione or recent renovation), IVA (VAT) replaces the imposta di registro: 4% IVA for prima casa, 10% IVA for non-prima casa.

Prima Casa — The Big Discount

The agevolazioni prima casa (primary residence tax benefits) dramatically reduce purchase taxes for buyers who will make the property their primary residence. To qualify:

  • The property must be a non-luxury residential unit (not classified as A/1, A/8, or A/9 in the catasto)
  • You must not own any other property in the same comune
  • You must establish your primary residence (residenza anagrafica) in the property's municipality within 18 months of purchase
  • You must not own other prima casa-purchased property in Italy

For Americans buying their Italian home as their primary residence: if you plan to register your residency at the address, you can claim prima casa benefits. This is a real advantage that reduces your tax from 9% to 2% on the cadastral value — a significant saving on any transaction.

Important: if you claim prima casa but fail to establish residency within 18 months, the Agenzia delle Entrate will retroactively levy the full 9% plus penalties and interest. Do not claim prima casa unless you genuinely intend to register as a resident at that address.

Cadastral value vs purchase price: The imposta di registro is applied to the cadastral value, which for residential property is calculated as: rendita catastale (a registered annual value assigned by the catasto) × a coefficient of 120 (for prima casa) or 160 (for non-prima casa).

In practice, the cadastral value is typically 30–60% of the actual purchase price for established properties in desirable areas. This means the effective tax rate as a percentage of what you actually pay is much lower than the statutory rates suggest.

Buying from a Developer (New Construction)

When buying a new apartment directly from a developer (costruttore) — defined as a property sold within 5 years of completion — IVA (VAT) applies instead of the imposta di registro:

  • Prima casa: 4% IVA on the purchase price (not cadastral value)
  • Non-prima casa: 10% IVA on the purchase price
  • Luxury properties: 22% IVA
  • Plus: fixed imposta di registro €200, plus fixed ipotecaria and catastale fees

Important difference: IVA is applied to the actual purchase price, not the cadastral value. A new apartment sold for €350,000 with a 4% IVA (prima casa) incurs €14,000 in IVA. The same apartment bought from a private seller might have a cadastral value of €120,000, with 2% registration tax equaling only €2,400. New construction is often tax-disadvantaged compared to resale for this reason.

Worked Example: €280,000 Apartment

Scenario A: Buying from private seller, claiming prima casa, cadastral value €95,000
  • Imposta di registro: 2% × €95,000 = €1,900
  • Imposta ipotecaria: €50
  • Imposta catastale: €50
  • Total taxes: €2,000 (0.71% of purchase price)
Scenario B: Buying from private seller, NOT prima casa, same cadastral value
  • Imposta di registro: 9% × €95,000 = €8,550
  • Imposta ipotecaria: 2% × €95,000 = €1,900
  • Imposta catastale: 1% × €95,000 = €950
  • Total taxes: €11,400 (4.07% of purchase price)
Scenario C: Buying from developer, prima casa, purchase price €280,000
  • IVA: 4% × €280,000 = €11,200
  • Imposta di registro: €200 fixed
  • Other fixed fees: ~€250
  • Total taxes: ~€11,650 (4.16% of purchase price)

Notary Fees

The notary fee (parcella notarile) is separate from the taxes and covers the notary's professional services for drafting and executing the rogito. Fees are regulated but not fixed:

  • Typical notary fee for a €280,000 residential transaction: €1,500–€3,000
  • Fees vary based on transaction value, complexity, and the specific notary
  • You choose the notary — shop around; fees can vary 30–40% between notaries for the same transaction
  • Notary fee is deductible in your Italian tax return (19% deduction)

Real Estate Agent Fees (Mediazione)

Italian real estate agents charge both buyer and seller. The Italian system differs from the US:

  • Standard buyer's commission: 2–4% of purchase price
  • Standard seller's commission: 2–4% of purchase price
  • Both are negotiable and vary by region and agency
  • Agent fees are fully deductible in your Italian return (19% deduction on the full amount)

Total Acquisition Cost: What to Budget

For a €280,000 resale apartment claimed as prima casa:

  • Taxes: ~€2,000
  • Notary fee: ~€2,000
  • Agent fee (buyer's side, 3%): ~€8,400
  • Total acquisition costs on top of purchase price: ~€12,400 (4.4%)

For a non-prima casa purchase of the same property, add ~€9,400 in additional taxes. Budget approximately 5–8% of the purchase price for total acquisition costs beyond the price itself, depending on prima casa status and property type.

FAQ

Can a non-resident American claim prima casa benefits?
Yes — the prima casa benefit requires you to establish residency in the municipality within 18 months of purchase, but you do not need to already be a resident at the time of purchase. Americans who plan to move to Italy and establish their Italian primary residence at the purchased property can claim prima casa benefits at the rogito.
What is the cadastral value and how is it calculated?
The cadastral value (valore catastale) is a government-assigned taxable value based on the property's category, size, and location as recorded in the catasto (land registry). It is calculated as: rendita catastale × coefficient. For residential properties, the coefficient is 120 for prima casa and 160 for non-prima casa. The rendita catastale is visible on the property's visura catastale — a document your notary will pull.
Do I pay purchase taxes again when I inherit Italian property?
Inheritance of Italian property triggers inheritance tax (not purchase tax), but also a separate imposta ipotecaria and catastale for the title transfer — typically 2% and 1% of the cadastral value respectively. The full purchase tax structure only applies to voluntary purchases, not inheritances.
