Short-Term Rentals in Italy: Rules, Taxes, and Airbnb Reality in 2025

Italy's short-term rental landscape shifted in 2024 with new tax rates, mandatory CIR registration codes, and city-level restrictions. If you own or plan to own Italian property for Airbnb-style rental, here is what you need to know.

What counts as a short-term rental in Italy

Italian law defines locazione turistica breve (short-term tourist rental) as a residential rental contract of maximum 30 days, made outside the context of an entrepreneurial activity, with no services provided beyond essential ones (linens, cleaning between stays).

The 30-day threshold is the critical dividing line:

  • 30 days or fewer: short-term rental rules apply — simplified tax treatment, no registration as a formal business in most cases
  • More than 30 days: the rental falls under ordinary residential tenancy law (Law 431/1998) and becomes a standard rental contract with all the protections and obligations that entails

This distinction matters in practice. A guest who books 25 days is a short-term tourist. A guest you allow to stay 35 days without a formal contract has accidentally become a tenant with legal rights that are much harder to unwind.

The no services element is also important. The moment you provide hotel-style services — meals, daily maid service, concierge, reception, organized activities — your activity moves from locazione turistica into affittacamere (B&B-style accommodation) or full hotel territory, which requires a different set of permits and is classified as an entrepreneurial activity requiring a partita IVA from the start.

Short-term rental vs. affittacamere
An affittacamere is a licensed B&B-style operation renting individual rooms (not the entire property) and typically including breakfast. It requires a specific SCIA, a partita IVA, and classification under regional tourism law. If you are renting entire apartments on Airbnb without meals or daily services, you are in the locazione turistica breve category — simpler, but still regulated.

Tax rates in 2024: the 26% change on second properties

The Legge di Bilancio 2024 (Law 213/2023, approved December 2023, effective January 2024) made a significant change to the cedolare secca regime for short-term rentals that caught many owners off-guard.

The rates

Property numberCedolare secca rate from 2024Previous rate
First property rented short-term21%21%
Second and subsequent properties26%21%

The intent of this change was explicit in the parliamentary debate: to discourage multi-property Airbnb investment that was removing apartments from the long-term rental market in tourist cities. The 5-percentage-point increase on second properties translates directly into reduced yield on STR portfolios.

What "first property" means

The 21% rate applies to income from the first property you rent short-term in a given tax year. If you have two apartments, the income from the second is taxed at 26%, regardless of which one you declare first. The owner designates which property is the "first" in their tax return — but you only get one at the lower rate.

Note: this applies to the short-term rental categorization specifically. A long-term rental (4+4 or 3+2 concordato) of a separate property uses different cedolare secca rules and is not affected by this change.

The alternative: ordinary IRPEF

Landlords are not required to use cedolare secca. They can declare short-term rental income through ordinary IRPEF. This makes sense if your total income is low enough that your marginal rate is below 21% (which is unusual), or in specific situations where you have offsettable deductions. For most owners, cedolare secca remains advantageous even at 26% compared to the 35–43% marginal IRPEF rates at higher incomes.

CIR registration code: mandatory for all listings

The Codice Identificativo Regionale (CIR) is a mandatory registration code for short-term rental properties. Every region of Italy has implemented (or is implementing) a CIR system, requiring owners to register their property before accepting bookings and to display the CIR code on all listings — Airbnb, Booking.com, their own websites, and any advertising.

How to obtain a CIR

The process varies slightly by region but typically involves:

  1. Registering with the regional tourism portal (each region has its own platform)
  2. Providing property details: address, number of rooms, maximum occupancy, ownership documents
  3. Receiving the CIR code (usually immediately or within a few days)

In Tuscany, the portal is called SIRED. In Lazio, it is the regional tourism system. In Lombardy, owners register through the SIPRA platform. If you are uncertain where to register for your specific property's region, your local comune's tourism office (Ufficio Turismo) can direct you to the correct system.

Displaying the CIR

Platforms like Airbnb and Booking.com now require hosts to enter their CIR when setting up or updating a listing in Italy. Failure to display the CIR can result in the listing being removed from the platform. Municipalities are increasingly running enforcement operations to identify properties advertising without a CIR, and fines can be substantial — up to €8,000 in some regions.

CIR vs. CIN: national vs. regional codes
In 2024, Italy also introduced the Codice Identificativo Nazionale (CIN) through the Tourism Ministry — a national-level registration distinct from the regional CIR. Both may be required depending on your region. The CIN system is managed through the national BDSR database (Banca Dati delle Strutture Ricettive). Check whether your region requires both codes or whether the CIN has replaced the regional CIR — the situation was still evolving in early 2025.

SCIA and municipal permits

The Segnalazione Certificata di Inizio Attività (SCIA) is a formal notification to the municipality that you are beginning a short-term rental activity. It is required in many (not all) Italian municipalities, particularly larger cities, before you accept your first guest.

The SCIA is submitted to the municipality's SUAP (Sportello Unico per le Attività Produttive) — the single business permit office. You submit it, the municipality reviews it, and in most cases you can begin activity immediately upon filing (hence "certificata di inizio" — it is a notification, not a request for permission).

What the SCIA typically requires you to confirm:

  • Property meets fire safety requirements (fire extinguisher, emergency exit plan if applicable)
  • Carbon monoxide and smoke detectors installed
  • Property complies with habitability standards
  • You have notified local law enforcement of guest arrivals (see below)

Guest registration with police (notifica alla questura)

Italian law requires all accommodation providers — including private short-term rental hosts — to report the identity of guests to local law enforcement (questura or commissariato) within 24 hours of arrival. This is done through an online portal called Alloggiati Web, run by the Interior Ministry.

This requirement is real and enforced. Airbnb provides some support documentation for Italian hosts, but the obligation to register guests falls on the host, not the platform. Set up your Alloggiati Web account before your first guest arrives. The process is straightforward once registered but requires a initial setup period.

Tourist tax (tassa di soggiorno)

Most Italian cities and tourist municipalities now levy a tassa di soggiorno (tourist tax) on overnight stays. This is charged per person per night and varies significantly by municipality and accommodation type.

As a host, you are responsible for collecting the tourist tax from guests and remitting it to the municipality on the schedule they specify (usually quarterly or monthly). Airbnb handles tourist tax collection and remittance automatically in most major Italian cities — check your Airbnb host dashboard to confirm whether your municipality is covered. If it is not, you collect it manually.

Children under 10–14 (age threshold varies by municipality) and long-term residents are typically exempt. Check your specific municipality's rules.

Platform withholding: how Airbnb handles Italian taxes

Since 2017, online rental platforms operating in Italy have been required to act as withholding agents for cedolare secca. Airbnb applies the applicable rate (21% or 26% depending on whether you have designated the property as your first or second STR property) to your rental income and remits it directly to the Italian tax authority on your behalf.

What this means in practice

  • Airbnb will withhold tax from your payouts at 21% by default unless you have configured your account to indicate this is a second or subsequent property, in which case it withholds 26%
  • At year end, Airbnb provides a certificazione delle ritenute (withholding certificate) documenting the total income and tax withheld
  • You still must declare this income in your Italian tax return (Modello 730 or Modello Redditi) — the withholding is not a substitute for filing
  • If total tax withheld exceeds your actual tax liability, you receive a refund through your tax return
Non-resident owners and Italian tax
If you are an American who owns Italian property but does not live in Italy, you still have Italian tax obligations on Italian-source rental income. Italy and the United States have a bilateral tax treaty (Convention of 1984) that prevents double taxation. Income taxed in Italy (via cedolare secca or IRPEF) generates a foreign tax credit on your US federal return. You will need both an Italian commercialista and a US accountant who understands foreign income reporting for the cleanest compliance picture.

City-level restrictions: Venice, Florence, Milan

Beyond the national regulatory framework, individual cities have begun imposing their own restrictions on short-term rentals — driven by housing pressure, overtourism concerns, and neighborhood community impact.

Venice

Venice is the most restrictive. The city has imposed a moratorium on new short-term rental authorizations in the historic center (sestieri of Cannaregio, Castello, Dorsoduro, San Marco, San Polo, Santa Croce) since 2023. Existing authorized STRs can continue, but new ones are effectively banned in the centro storico. Operators on the Giudecca and in the lagoon islands face similar restrictions. Properties on the mainland (Mestre, Marghera) are not subject to the same ban.

Florence

Florence adopted a formal STR restriction in 2024 applying to the UNESCO-designated historic center (Zone A). New short-term rental registrations in the historic center are prohibited. This affects the most desirable tourist areas — the neighborhoods around the Duomo, Piazza della Repubblica, Santa Croce, and Oltrarno. Properties already registered and operating before the cutoff date were grandfathered. Outside the historic center, registration remains possible.

Milan

Milan has debated STR restrictions for several years but as of early 2025 has not implemented a ban comparable to Venice or Florence. The city has increased enforcement of CIR/CIN registration requirements and tourist tax compliance. Discussions about limiting STRs in certain central neighborhoods continued through 2024–2025. Given Milan's housing market pressures, further regulation is likely — monitor developments if you own in central Milan.

Other cities

Rome, Bologna, and Naples have all implemented mandatory CIR registration and tightened enforcement but have not imposed moratoriums on new registrations as of early 2025. Bologna in particular has been actively discussing limiting STRs in the historic center.

When you need a partita IVA

Most individual short-term rental owners in Italy do not need a partita IVA (VAT number / business registration). The cedolare secca regime is specifically designed for individuals renting outside an entrepreneurial activity framework.

However, you may cross into requiring a partita IVA if:

  • You offer additional services beyond basic property rental (daily cleaning, guided tours, breakfast, airport transfers packaged with the stay)
  • You manage multiple properties that are not your own — acting as a property manager for third-party owners typically constitutes an entrepreneurial activity
  • You are operating as a company rather than as an individual — any corporate structure automatically requires a partita IVA
  • Your income volume or activity pattern leads the tax authority to determine you are operating a business rather than casually renting personal property

If you need a partita IVA, you will typically open it under ATECO code 55.20.51 (affittacamere per brevi soggiorni) or 68.20.01 (locazione immobiliare di beni propri — for simpler property rental). Your commercialista will guide you to the correct code. With a partita IVA and VAT-registered status, your revenue is subject to VAT (typically 10% for hospitality services), and the flat cedolare secca regime no longer applies.

Practical operations: management, costs, and logistics

Property management companies (gestori)

If you are a non-resident owner — particularly an American who bought Italian property as an investment — self-managing an Italian short-term rental from abroad is logistically demanding. Italy has a growing market of full-service property management companies (gestori di affitti brevi) who handle:

  • Guest communication and booking management
  • Check-in and check-out (including smart lock installation and management)
  • Cleaning and linen service
  • Alloggiati Web police registration
  • Tourist tax collection and remittance (for municipalities where Airbnb does not do it automatically)
  • Minor maintenance coordination

Management fees typically run 15–25% of gross rental income. This cuts into yields but is often the only viable option for remote owners. Major operators include Italystay, Wonderful Italy, and numerous regional specialists. Evaluate contracts carefully — some require exclusivity and have onerous cancellation terms.

Smart locks

Given that Italian short-term rental law requires identity document verification of all guests, keyless entry via smart lock paired with identity verification apps (some gestori use automated systems integrated with their booking management platforms) has become standard in urban markets. Budget €200–€500 for a quality smart lock installation.

Does the 26% rate apply if I only rent one of my two properties short-term?
The 26% rate applies to the second and subsequent properties you rent short-term in a tax year. If you own two properties but only actively rent one of them short-term, only the one you rent counts. The second property that sits unused (or that you use personally) does not trigger the higher rate on the one you do rent. You pay 21% on the rental income from your single actively-rented STR property.
Can I use the property myself some of the year and rent it short-term the rest?
Yes. Mixed personal-use and STR activity is extremely common in Italy. You pay cedolare secca only on the rental income you receive during the periods the property was rented. Periods of personal use generate no rental income and no rental tax obligation — though the property may still be subject to IMU if it is not your primary residence.
What happens if I rent for more than 30 days without a proper tenancy contract?
This is a common risk. A stay that begins as a 25-day booking and extends informally to 40 days has legally converted from a tourist rental into a residential tenancy — whether you have a written contract or not. At that point, the guest has tenant protections and cannot be evicted the way a tourist can. Always use a proper written contract if you intend any stay to exceed 30 days.
Does Airbnb withholding satisfy my Italian tax obligation?
No. Airbnb's withholding and remittance of cedolare secca reduces your tax liability for the year (it is a down payment on your tax obligation), but it does not eliminate your obligation to file a tax return. You must still declare the rental income and the withholding amounts in your annual Modello 730 or Redditi return. The commercialista will reconcile the withholdings against your total liability.
I bought property in a historic center of Florence before the restriction. Can I still operate a short-term rental?
Properties that were already registered and operating as short-term rentals before the Florence restriction took effect are generally grandfathered — meaning they can continue to operate under their existing registration. If you purchased an already-registered STR property, verify carefully that the registration transfers to you with the purchase. Buying property in the Florence historic center and starting a new STR registration after the cutoff date is not permitted.
