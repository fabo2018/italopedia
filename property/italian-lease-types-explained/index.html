Italian Lease Types Explained: 4+4, 3+2, Transitorio, and Cedolare Secca

Italy's rental market is one of the most regulated in Europe. Understanding the difference between a canone libero and a canone concordato contract can save you hundreds of euros a month — and protect you legally.

Italy's rental law at a glance

Italian residential tenancy law is primarily governed by two sources: the Civil Code (Articles 1571–1614) and Law 431/1998, which substantially reformed the rental market by creating the concordato contract system. Unlike the United States, where rental terms are largely left to market negotiation, Italy imposes firm minimum durations, renewal rights, and notice requirements that apply regardless of what a contract says.

This is not bureaucracy for its own sake. It reflects a deliberate policy choice to provide housing security for tenants. For Americans accustomed to 12-month leases at market rent with little security, the Italian system feels very different — and mostly in your favor as a renter.

The four main residential contract types you will encounter are:

  • Contratto a canone libero — the 4+4 standard lease
  • Contratto a canone concordato — the 3+2 locally-negotiated rate lease
  • Contratto transitorio — short-term lease for documented temporary needs
  • Contratto per studenti universitari — student lease near universities

Separately, cedolare secca is a tax regime — not a contract type — that significantly affects both landlord and tenant and is important to understand before you sign anything.

Key principle: unregistered contracts have no legal standing
All Italian residential rental contracts must be registered with the Agenzia delle Entrate (Italy's tax authority) within 30 days of signing. An unregistered contract is legally null. As a tenant in an unregistered contract, you have no enforceable eviction protection, cannot register your residency (residenza) at the address, and have no legal recourse if the landlord suddenly demands you leave. Never accept a verbal arrangement or an "informal" rental without a registered contract.

Contratto a canone libero (4+4)

The contratto a canone libero is the most common rental contract in Italy for unfurnished apartments. "Libero" means the rent is freely negotiated between landlord and tenant — there is no cap or formula, it is whatever the market bears.

The structure is a mandatory 4-year initial term, automatically renewable for another 4 years, giving the system its informal name "4+4." Here is how it works in practice:

  • Initial 4-year term: Once signed and registered, neither party can unilaterally terminate the contract before the end of four years, except in specific circumstances (landlord's own use of property, major renovation, etc. — all requiring formal legal procedures).
  • Renewal: At the end of year four, the contract automatically renews for another four years unless either party gives written notice (via registered mail, raccomandata) at least six months before the expiry date. This means you need to send notice by month 42 if you intend to leave at month 48.
  • Landlord's ability to terminate: Landlords can refuse renewal at the first expiry (end of year 4) only for specific legally recognized reasons: they need the property for their own use, for a first-degree relative, for sale, or for substantial renovation. They cannot simply refuse renewal because they want to raise the rent or found a different tenant.
  • Rent adjustment: The contract can include an annual ISTAT adjustment clause (tied to the consumer price index), typically at 75% of the official ISTAT rate. This is optional but common — always check whether your contract includes it.
Furnished vs. unfurnished (arredato vs. non arredato)
Most 4+4 contracts in Italy are for unfurnished apartments. Furnished rentals exist but are less common in the long-term market and often command a premium. If you are renting furnished, make absolutely sure an inventory (inventario) is attached to the contract and signed by both parties — it is your protection against deposit disputes at the end of the tenancy.

Contratto a canone concordato (3+2)

The contratto a canone concordato is Italy's regulated-rent lease, where the monthly rent is not freely negotiated but is instead set according to tables agreed between local landlord associations and tenant unions (sindacati degli inquilini). This is Law 431/1998 in action: the state created a parallel track where both landlord and tenant accept a lower-than-market rent in exchange for tax benefits and legal certainty.

The rent under a concordato contract is typically 15–30% below free-market levels, depending on the municipality. In some cities with high housing costs — Milan, Rome, Bologna, Florence — the gap can be even larger.

Why would a landlord accept lower rent?

The incentive structure is real:

  • Tax advantage: Landlords who choose the cedolare secca flat tax on a concordato contract pay only 10% instead of the standard 21% on free-market leases (see the cedolare secca section below).
  • IMU/TASI reduction: In many municipalities, landlords with registered concordato contracts receive a 25% reduction on the property tax (IMU).
  • Legal protection: The contract has full legal standing and the eviction process, should it ever be needed, is more predictable.

The 3+2 structure

The initial term is three years, renewable for two more years. At the end of the three-year term, renewal is automatic unless the landlord provides notice for a legally recognized reason. The two-year renewal period functions similarly — automatic unless formally terminated.

At the end of the 3+2 cycle (five years total), the landlord has more flexibility to renegotiate the rent or decline renewal than under a 4+4 contract. This is the trade-off for the lower initial rent.

How to find out the concordato rate in your city
Contact the local comune's housing office or reach out directly to a tenant union (SUNIA, SICET, or UNIAT are the major ones) in the city where you are renting. They can tell you the exact rate applicable to your address based on zone and property type. This is a free service for tenants.

Contratto transitorio (1–18 months)

The contratto transitorio allows for short-term rentals between one and eighteen months. It exists to serve genuine temporary housing needs: a worker on a fixed-term assignment, someone waiting for their owned property to become available, a person temporarily relocated for medical care.

Critically, this contract type cannot be used simply to avoid the protections of the 4+4 system. Law 431/1998 is explicit: the contract must document the genuine temporary need of either the landlord or the tenant. Without documented cause, a transitorio contract can be challenged in court and re-qualified as a 4+4 lease, with all the rights that entails retroactively.

What counts as a valid transitorio reason?

  • Tenant on a documented fixed-term work contract or transfer
  • Tenant awaiting completion of a property purchase
  • Landlord temporarily vacating their primary residence (documented)
  • Tenant undergoing medical treatment requiring temporary presence in another city

The temporary need must be stated explicitly in the contract. A landlord offering a transitorio contract without documenting any specific need is taking a legal risk — and so, frankly, is the tenant, who may find themselves without enforceable protections.

The rent on a transitorio contract is regulated: it cannot exceed the concordato rate in that municipality. This prevents the transitorio from being used as a pretext for charging above-market rents on short contracts.

Contratto per studenti universitari (6–36 months)

A specialized variant of the transitorio designed for university students, available in municipalities with recognized universities. The term runs between 6 and 36 months — useful because academic years and degree programs have different durations.

To qualify:

  • The tenant must be an enrolled university student
  • The property must be located in a municipality with a university campus (the student does not have to study at the university in that exact city, but the city must have one)
  • Proof of enrollment is typically required

Rent is again regulated at concordato levels. For American students attending Italian universities or exchange programs, this is often the correct contract type — not a transitorio, not a tourist rental.

Cedolare secca: the flat tax regime

Cedolare secca (literally "dry withholding") is not a type of rental contract. It is a tax regime that landlords can opt into instead of declaring rental income through normal IRPEF (income tax) channels. Understanding it matters enormously as a tenant because it directly affects what a landlord can and cannot do during your tenancy.

How cedolare secca works

Under ordinary IRPEF taxation, rental income is added to the landlord's other income and taxed at their marginal rate, which in Italy ranges from 23% to 43%. For landlords with moderate incomes, this is often 27–38%.

Cedolare secca replaces this with a flat tax:

Contract typeCedolare secca rate
Canone libero (4+4 and transitorio)21%
Canone concordato (3+2 and student)10%

For a landlord paying 35% marginal IRPEF, opting for cedolare secca at 21% is a significant saving. The 10% rate on concordato contracts is an even more powerful incentive — it is why landlords in cities like Bologna and Turin are increasingly willing to offer concordato contracts.

The critical implication for tenants

When a landlord opts into cedolare secca, they must notify the tenant formally (via the registration paperwork at the Agenzia delle Entrate). The key legal consequence: a landlord on cedolare secca cannot increase the rent for any reason during the period they remain in that regime. This includes:

  • No ISTAT cost-of-living adjustments
  • No renegotiation requests
  • No supplemental charges

From a tenant's perspective, a cedolare secca contract is a rent-freeze guarantee. If your landlord is on cedolare secca and your contract includes an ISTAT clause, that clause is legally unenforceable for the duration of the cedolare secca election. Make sure you know whether your landlord has chosen this regime — it should be noted in the registered contract documents.

Cedolare secca and the tenant's notification right
Landlords who want to switch to cedolare secca on an existing contract must notify the tenant using a specific form (available from the Agenzia delle Entrate). The switch is always in the tenant's interest for the reasons above, so if your landlord offers to register this option, there is generally no downside to accepting. The landlord's tax saving does not come at your expense.

Registration with the Agenzia delle Entrate

Every residential rental contract must be registered with the Agenzia delle Entrate within 30 days of signing. This applies without exception to all contract types, all durations above 30 days, and both Italian and foreign landlords.

What registration means in practice

  • The contract is filed in the tax authority's system and assigned a registration number
  • Registration tax is due: typically 2% of annual rent for canone libero contracts (this cost is legally split 50/50 between landlord and tenant, though practices vary)
  • If the landlord opts for cedolare secca, there is no registration tax — this is an additional incentive for both parties
  • Only a registered contract allows you to register your residenza (official residence) at the address — which is essential for accessing Italy's public services

What to do if your landlord refuses to register

A landlord refusing to register the contract is committing tax evasion and creating a legally precarious situation for both parties. If your landlord proposes an unregistered rental:

  • You can register the contract yourself at the Agenzia delle Entrate. The tenant has the legal right to do this unilaterally, and any costs paid by the tenant can be recovered from the landlord.
  • Document everything in writing before signing anything informal
  • Consult a local patronato or tenant union (SUNIA is the largest)

What expats need to know: deposits, utilities, pets, and more

Security deposit (caparra)

Italian law caps the security deposit at a maximum of three months' rent. In practice, most landlords ask for two or three months. The deposit must be returned within a reasonable time after the end of the tenancy (typically 30 days), minus any legitimate deductions for damages beyond normal wear and tear.

Normal wear and tear — normale usura — is a legal concept in Italy. Scuffs on walls, minor carpet wear, and faded paint are not grounds for deduction. Actual damage is. Always do a written walkthrough at the start and end of your tenancy, with photos, and insist both parties sign the inventory.

Utilities

In Italian rentals, the tenant almost always takes responsibility for:

  • Electricity (luce) — you take over the existing contract with Enel or another provider, or open a new one
  • Gas (gas) — same process as electricity
  • Internet — your own contract with TIM, Fastweb, Vodafone, etc.

The landlord typically covers the spese condominiali ordinarie (ordinary building maintenance fees) though this can vary — check your contract. Water (acqua) is sometimes included in condominium fees, sometimes billed separately. Always clarify before signing.

Condominium fees (spese condominiali)

Italian apartment buildings are managed as condomini, with shared maintenance costs managed by an amministratore di condominio (building manager). There are two categories:

  • Spese ordinarie: Regular maintenance (elevator, hallway cleaning, garden upkeep). Generally the landlord's responsibility.
  • Spese straordinarie: Major works (new roof, façade repairs, elevator replacement). Always the landlord's responsibility.

However, some landlords try to pass ordinary condominium fees to tenants. This is legally permissible if explicitly agreed in the contract, so read carefully. The standard position is that ordinary fees above a certain threshold remain with the landlord.

Pets

Italian law (Law 220/2012 on condominium rules, and general tenancy principles) does not prohibit tenants from keeping pets, even if a contract includes a no-pets clause. A court cannot enforce a contractual prohibition on pets, though individual condominium rules can restrict certain animals in common areas. Do not assume a no-pets clause in your lease is enforceable — but do check the condominium rules (regolamento condominiale) as a separate matter.

Can a landlord ask me to leave in the middle of a 4+4 contract?
Generally no. During an active 4+4 term, the landlord can only terminate early for very specific legally defined reasons: they need the property for personal use, for a first-degree relative, to sell the property, or to undertake major structural renovation. Each of these requires formal written notice and adherence to specific legal procedures. A landlord who simply wants you out does not have a legitimate basis for early termination.
Can I leave a 4+4 contract early as a tenant?
Yes, but you must give notice. For contracts of 4+ years, you are generally required to give six months' written notice (raccomandata). Some contracts specify different terms — check yours. You cannot simply walk out, and in theory you remain liable for rent during the notice period even if you have vacated.
What is the difference between a caparra confirmatoria and a caparra penitenziale?
A caparra confirmatoria is a standard deposit confirming both parties' intent to proceed. If the deal falls through due to the other party's fault, the injured party can keep the deposit or demand double (if they gave it). A caparra penitenziale allows either party to walk away by forfeiting their deposit — it is essentially a pre-agreed exit option. For rental deposits, most are confirmatoria by default. Know which type yours is before signing.
Do I need a codice fiscale to sign a lease in Italy?
Yes. The codice fiscale (Italian tax ID number) is required for the registration of any rental contract. You must have one before signing. Americans can obtain a codice fiscale at any Italian consulate in the US, or at the Agenzia delle Entrate or local comune once in Italy. It takes minutes to issue.
I found a 3+2 concordato apartment but the landlord wants market rent. Is that legal?
No. A contract labeled as canone concordato must use the locally agreed rent tables. If a landlord uses the concordato contract form but charges free-market rent, the contract is potentially null with regard to the rent clause. The tenant can challenge the rent and have it reduced to the legal concordato level. This situation does arise in practice — some landlords try to use the concordato form to get the tax benefits while charging free-market rent. Know the applicable rate in your area before signing.
