Italy's €1 House Programs: What's Real, What's Marketing, and How to Actually Do It (2025)

The honest, complete guide to Italy's €1 house programs — which municipalities are active, real renovation costs, the legal process, and whether it's actually worth it for Americans.

FB
By Fabrizio Boggio
Updated May 2026 9 min read VERIFIED
✉ 𝕏 ⌘

The headlines write themselves: "Buy a house in Italy for €1." Since 2019, the Italian €1 house story has been covered by CNN, the BBC, the New York Times, and essentially every travel and lifestyle publication on earth. Tens of thousands of Americans have clicked, dreamed, and wondered: is this real?

The short answer: yes, it's real — and the full story is far more interesting, more complicated, and more expensive than the headlines suggest.

I live in Italy. I know people who've done this. Here's everything the clickbait articles don't tell you.

Why Do These Programs Exist?

Italy has a severe depopulation crisis in its rural interior. Thousands of small towns (comuni) in regions like Sicily, Abruzzo, Molise, Calabria, Sardinia, and the Piedmont mountains have lost 30–60% of their populations since the 1950s as residents moved to cities or emigrated.

The result: entire neighborhoods of abandoned stone houses. No owners want them (no one to inherit them or heirs who've moved away). No one wants to buy them (no economic activity, poor infrastructure). The houses deteriorate, become eyesores, and create liability for municipalities.

The €1 house programs are a municipal economic development tool — a way to attract outside buyers who will renovate these properties, establish residency, inject money into local economies, and potentially revitalize dying communities.

For the municipality: A renovated home, a new taxpayer, and maybe a small business or tourism draw.
For the buyer: A chance to own property in Italy for a nominal price — with the understanding that they'll spend real money on renovation.

Active Programs in 2025

Dozens of Italian municipalities have run or are running €1 house programs. The most established and well-documented:

Sicily

Mussomeli (Caltanissetta province)
- Pioneered the modern version of the program
- Still active; contact the Comune di Mussomeli directly
- Well-organized program with English-language materials
- Renovation commitment: 3 years, minimum €15,000 investment
- Website: comune.mussomeli.cl.it

Sambuca di Sicilia (Agrigento province)
- The most viral auction: 2019, 100,000+ global applications for 16 houses
- Less active now but municipality open to inquiries
- One of Italy's most beautiful historic centers — worth visiting regardless

Salemi (Trapani province)
- Active program with dedicated support for foreign buyers
- English-language assistance available
- Contact: comune.salemi.tp.it

Gangi (Palermo province)
- One of Italy's "Most Beautiful Villages" (Borghi più belli d'Italia)
- Program has been intermittently active; verify current status

Southern Mainland

Biccari (Foggia, Puglia)
- Active program; €2,000 deposit required (refunded upon renovation completion)
- Municipality has been proactive in supporting foreign buyers

Ollolai (Nuoro, Sardinia)
- Program launched in 2018; properties from €1
- Remote mountain location — stunning landscape, challenging logistics

Fabbriche di Vergemoli (Lucca, Tuscany)
- Rare Tuscan program; not the iconic landscape but genuine Tuscany
- Higher renovation requirements than southern programs

Troina (Enna, Sicily)
- Active; renovation commitment required

Cinquefrondi (Reggio Calabria)
- Called itself "the anti-COVID city" when launching in 2020
- Properties from €1; renovation required within 3 years

How to Find New Programs

New programs launch regularly. The best sources:
- Borghi più belli d'Italia (borghibellissimi.it) — the official "most beautiful villages" network
- ItalyBestPlaces — aggregates active programs
- Direct Google search: "case a 1 euro [region name] 2025"
- Facebook groups: "1 Euro Houses Italy" has 50,000+ members with real-time updates

The Real Costs: What You're Actually Buying

This is the section the headline articles skip. The house is €1. Everything else is not.

Mandatory Costs Before You Own the House

Notaio (notary) fees: Italian law requires all property transfers to go through a licensed notary. The notary verifies title, prepares the deed (atto di compravendita), and registers the transfer.

  • Notary fee: €1,500–3,000 for a low-value property
  • Registration taxes: For a primary residence purchase — 2% of cadastral value (very low for these properties, typically €100–300); for secondary residence — 9% (still low given minimal cadastral values)
  • Total mandatory transaction cost: €2,000–4,000

Geometra (surveyor/technical inspector) fee: Before signing anything, you need a geometra to inspect the property and confirm:
- Structural soundness (or document what isn't sound)
- Planning/zoning compliance (abusivismo — illegal modifications — is common in older properties)
- Any outstanding municipal obligations or building violations
- Accurate floor plans for renovation permits

Cost: €500–1,500 for inspection and report

Bank guarantee/security deposit: Many municipalities require a deposit (typically €3,000–5,000) guaranteeing you'll complete the renovation within the required timeframe (usually 3 years). Returned upon renovation completion; forfeited if you abandon the project.

Renovation Costs: The Big Number

This is the real investment. €1 houses range from "needs new kitchen and bathroom" to "complete shell requiring everything." Honest ranges:

Property Condition Renovation Cost
Good bones, cosmetic work €20,000–40,000
Moderate work (plumbing, electrical, kitchen, baths) €40,000–80,000
Substantial structural work + full interior €80,000–150,000
Near-total reconstruction €150,000–300,000+

Note: Renovation in rural Italy costs less than urban areas but labor and materials still add up. A skilled muratore (mason) earns €25–40/hour; elettricista (electrician) €30–50/hour; idraulico (plumber) €30–50/hour.

Italian renovation work includes a 10% VAT on materials and labor (for primary residence construction; 22% for secondary). Budget this in.

Ongoing Costs After Renovation

  • IMU (municipal property tax): For primary residence, zero; for secondary/vacation home, 0.76–1.06% of cadastral value per year. On a €1 house with a cadastral value of €3,000–8,000, IMU might be €30–80/year.
  • Utilities connection: If the property has been disconnected from electricity, gas, and water, reconnection fees: €500–2,000
  • TARI (waste collection tax): €100–300/year

The Legal Process: Step by Step

Step 1: Research and Select

Contact the municipality directly. Ask for:
- Current list of available properties
- Full program requirements and timeline
- Renovation commitment details
- Municipality-recommended local geometra

Visit in person if at all possible. Photos online are unreliable indicators of actual condition.

Step 2: Property Inspection

Hire a local geometra before committing. The inspection should cover:
- Structural assessment
- Title history and any encumbrances
- Planning compliance check
- Realistic renovation cost estimate

Red flags to watch for:
- Properties listed as "catastalmente non in regola" (not in compliance with cadastral records) — require regularization before or during renovation
- Properties with multiple heirs who haven't all signed off
- Structures with outstanding demolition orders
- Asbestos (amianto) — present in many properties built before the 1994 Italian asbestos ban

Step 3: Preliminary Agreement

If you want to proceed, sign a compromesso (preliminary sale agreement) — optional but recommended. Typically includes:
- Purchase price (€1)
- Renovation commitment
- Deadline for final deed
- Refundable deposit (separate from municipal security deposit)

Step 4: Final Deed at the Notaio

The rogito notarile (final deed of sale) is signed at the notary's office. Both buyer and seller must be present (or represented by someone with notarized power of attorney). The notary:
- Reads the full deed aloud
- Verifies identities
- Registers the deed with the Agenzia delle Entrate and Land Registry

You need a Codice Fiscale to complete the purchase.

Step 5: Renovation

You typically have 3 years to complete renovation to livability standard. Some municipalities require:
- Approved renovation project before starting (Permesso di Costruire or SCIA/CILA depending on scope)
- Use of local contractors (some programs require or incentivize local labor)
- Inspections at milestones

Get the renovation permits right. Work without permits is "abusivismo" — illegal construction. This creates title problems you can never fully resolve and can result in demolition orders.

Step 6: Security Deposit Return

After renovation completion, request inspection from the municipality. If the work meets the standard required, your security deposit is returned.

Is It Worth It? The Honest Assessment

Financially

The €1 house is an opportunity when:
- You want rural Italian life and would buy a house in this area anyway
- You have €50,000–150,000+ for renovation and can absorb the project risk
- You plan to live there (primary residence tax benefits; no IMU)
- You want a creative project and aren't on a tight timeline

It's a trap when:
- You think you're getting something for nothing
- Your renovation budget is under €30,000
- You haven't visited and don't understand what rural Italian life requires
- You're planning to use it as a vacation rental (many programs prohibit short-term rentals for the first 5 years)

Emotionally and Practically

The Americans who thrive with these projects are people who:
- Speak Italian (B1 minimum) or are learning fast
- Have renovation/project management experience
- Have a support network in the area (or build one quickly — local geometra, muratore, etc.)
- Are genuinely excited about the specific village, not just "Italy"

Those who struggle:
- Underestimated the renovation complexity
- Tried to manage remotely from the U.S. (possible but very hard)
- Didn't vet contractors carefully (contractor fraud and project abandonment are real issues)
- Got attached to the Instagram version of the project and ignored the structural reality

Italian Government Incentives for Renovation

Two government incentives make renovation significantly cheaper:

Superbonus 110% (now reduced)
Originally a 110% tax credit for energy efficiency upgrades — effectively free renovation for eligible work. Significantly reduced since 2023; now at 70% (2024) and 65% (2025). Still meaningful for insulation, windows, and HVAC systems.

Bonus Ristrutturazione
50% tax deduction on renovation costs (materials + labor), claimed over 10 years on Italian income tax returns. Requires Italian tax residency and filing.

Saggio agevolato per borghi (PNRR funding)
Italy's National Recovery Plan includes €1 billion+ specifically for small village renovation and tourism development. Some programs offer additional grants to €1 house buyers who commit to specific uses (B&B, cultural tourism, etc.).

These incentives can reduce the effective renovation cost by 30–50% if you're an Italian tax resident. Another reason why primary residence + Italian residency makes the financial case stronger.
