Cost of Living in Lecce 2025: Southern Italy on a Budget

Lecce is called "the Florence of the South" — and the baroque stone architecture genuinely earns that comparison. But unlike Florence, the prices here haven't yet fully reflected how good this city actually is. American retirees on modest fixed incomes live very well here.

Why Lecce Deserves Serious Consideration

Lecce is the capital of the Salento peninsula — the heel of Italy's boot — in the region of Puglia. It has a population of roughly 90,000 in the city proper, making it a city that is genuinely walkable in a way that Rome or Naples can never be. The historic center is built from golden pietra leccese, a soft local limestone that Baroque architects carved into extraordinary architectural extravagance: churches, palaces, and piazzas layered with reliefs, cherubs, and ornamental flourishes that earned the city its Florentine comparison.

For Americans considering a move to Southern Europe, Lecce represents a specific value proposition: one of the cheapest costs of living of any Western European city with a genuinely high quality of life, in a climate that is reliably Mediterranean (long dry summers, mild winters), with an architectural heritage that most European cities would be proud to have as their main attraction.

The international discovery of Lecce has been happening gradually. It appeared in travel publications as a "hidden gem" for about fifteen years running, and at some point a place that appears on those lists long enough stops being quite so hidden. An international expat community exists here now — mostly British, German, American, and Australian — though it remains small enough that Lecce feels like an Italian city rather than an expat colony.

Rent: Centro Storico vs. Suburbs

Lecce's rental market is one of the most affordable in any Italian city with genuine urban infrastructure and cultural life. Data from Idealista.it (October 2024):

A two-bedroom apartment in the centro storico — genuinely within the historic walls, in a building with vaulted stone ceilings and original architectural details — for €700–€950/month is something that has no equivalent in Northern Italy or any major city in Spain, Portugal, or France at these price points. In practical terms, a couple arriving in Lecce with $3,000/month combined income (Social Security plus savings) can live very well here, including the centro apartment, good food, and regular travel.

Some important caveats on the Lecce rental market:

  • Centro storico apartments are often in buildings with no elevator and significant stone stairs — physically demanding if mobility is a concern
  • Older buildings may have substandard insulation; heating costs in January and February are real even in this southern climate
  • Air conditioning is essentially mandatory in summer — see the Cons section for why this matters
  • Short-term and tourist rentals have eaten into the long-term rental supply in parts of the centro — the available stock is smaller than it was five years ago, though still very affordable by any comparison

Groceries: The Southern Advantage

The cost advantage of Southern Italy in groceries is real and significant. Lecce is surrounded by agricultural land — the Salento produces enormous quantities of olive oil, wine, vegetables, figs, almonds, and seafood from the Adriatic and Ionian coasts. Produce travels shorter distances to market here than almost anywhere else in Italy.

Typical grocery costs in Lecce for one person:

  • Weekly groceries (cooking at home, eating well): €40–€60
  • Local olive oil (extra virgin, from a nearby frantoia): €6–€10/liter for excellent quality
  • Fresh vegetables at the outdoor market (Mercato Ettore Palumbo): very low — a full bag of seasonal produce for €5–€8
  • Fish at the market: €8–€18/kg depending on the type (orata, branzino, swordfish)
  • Mozzarella (local fior di latte): €3–€5/250g at a latteria
  • Wine (local Primitivo or Negroamaro, from a cantina): €3–€7/bottle for genuinely good local wine

Grocery costs in Lecce run roughly 20–30% cheaper than Bologna or Milan for comparable quality, and in some categories — olive oil, fresh produce, local wine — the quality is actually higher at the local price point because you're buying directly from regional production rather than a nationally distributed product.

Supermarkets in Lecce include Conad, Eurospin (budget), Famila, and MD Discount. For fresh products, the daily outdoor market is the preferred option for anyone who learns to use it.

Street Food and Eating Out

Lecce has a street food culture that is specific to Salento and genuinely excellent. The canonical Lecce street foods:

  • Pasticciotto leccese: A short-crust pastry filled with custard cream — the breakfast food of Lecce, eaten standing at a bar with a caffè. €1.20–€1.80 each. The best version is warm, with the pastry just slightly yielding and the cream still soft.
  • Frisa: Dried, twice-baked bread soaked in water, topped with tomatoes, olive oil, and oregano — a peasant dish that is genuinely delicious and genuinely cheap. €2–€4 at a trattoria.
  • Pitta di patate: A savory potato pie, typically filled with olives, capers, and onions — street food at its best.
  • Rustico leccese: A puff pastry filled with tomato, mozzarella, and béchamel. €1–€1.50, available at almost every bar.
  • Puccia: Round flatbread sandwich stuffed with local cured meats, vegetables, or mozzarella. €3–€5 for a substantial lunch.

Eating out in Lecce is cheap even by Southern Italian standards:

  • Lunch (two courses, wine, house): €14–€22 per person at a trattoria
  • Dinner (full meal, mid-range restaurant): €25–€40 per person
  • Pizza: €6–€10 at a local pizzeria
  • Espresso at a bar: €1.00–€1.20

Utilities

Utilities in Lecce are broadly in line with the national average, with some seasonal variations reflecting the Mediterranean climate:

  • Electricity: €35–€70/month in winter, €80–€150/month in summer (air conditioning drives significant consumption in July-August)
  • Gas (heating and cooking): €40–€80/month in winter (January–February), very low the rest of the year. Many apartments in the south use LPG (GPL) rather than methane gas — check which system your apartment uses, as LPG requires tank refills rather than a metered connection.
  • Water: €15–€30/month, though Puglia has historically had water supply issues — the region relies on the Acquedotto Pugliese, which can experience seasonal shortages in dry years.
  • Internet: FTTH fiber broadband is available in the centro storico and most residential areas. Providers include TIM, WINDTRE, Fastweb. Cost: €25–€35/month. Coverage is less ubiquitous than in northern cities — verify before committing to a specific address.
  • Total utilities (1BR, excluding internet): Approximately €100–€160/month in winter, €150–€240 in summer when AC is running heavily.

Transport and Getting Around

Lecce is a small city by Italian standards and the historic center is extremely walkable. The centro storico is pedestrianized in large part, and everything within the walls is within a 15-minute walk of everything else within the walls.

Within Lecce: Walking and cycling are the primary modes for centro residents. The city's bike infrastructure has improved but is still limited outside the centro. Local buses (STP Brindisi/Lecce) cover the wider city and province, but are less frequent and reliable than northern Italian transit systems. Cost: €1.10–€1.50 per ride.

Regional connections: Lecce is at the end of Italy — trains are the primary connection to the rest of the country. Lecce to Bari takes about 1.5 hours (€10–€15). Bari has the region's major international airport (Aeroporto di Bari Karol Wojtyla), with Ryanair, easyJet, and other carriers flying to major European hubs. Lecce to Rome is roughly 5–6 hours by train. Brindisi airport (closer, 40 minutes from Lecce) also has some international routes.

Car: More useful in Lecce than in Bologna or Milan. If you plan to explore the Salento coast — and the coastline here is genuinely spectacular, with the Adriatic on one side and the Ionian on the other — a car is the most practical option. Car ownership costs in Southern Italy include higher insurance than the national average (Puglia has elevated auto theft rates in some areas).

Healthcare

Lecce is served by the Ospedale Vito Fazzi, the main public hospital for the Lecce province, which is part of the ASL Lecce (Azienda Sanitaria Locale). As a registered resident, you enroll in SSN and are assigned a general practitioner free of charge.

Honest assessment: the public health system in Southern Italy is generally considered lower quality than the North, and ASL Lecce is no exception. Wait times for specialist appointments through the public system can be very long. Many residents — Italian and expat — supplement SSN coverage with private insurance or pay out of pocket for specialist visits (a private specialist consultation typically costs €60–€150). The quality of private clinics in Lecce is adequate for routine and moderate care. For serious conditions, many residents travel to Bari (where the Policlinico di Bari is a major teaching hospital) or, for the most serious situations, to Rome or Milan.

This is an honest limitation of Lecce as a relocation destination, particularly for retirees with significant healthcare needs. Factor private insurance (€60–€120/month) into your budget if healthcare access matters to you.

For Americans with Social Security income of $1,500–$2,000/month, Lecce can make the numbers work when much of Italy cannot.

The Expat Community

Lecce's international expat community is small but real and growing. The dominant nationalities are British (who discovered the Salento early), German, Dutch, and a growing American contingent, largely retirees and remote workers. French visitors are common but rarely settle permanently.

The expat presence has created some infrastructure:

  • English-speaking real estate agents who specialize in expat relocations
  • A handful of English-friendly doctors and lawyers
  • Facebook groups and WhatsApp communities (search "Expats in Lecce") that provide practical advice and connections
  • Some English-language yoga studios, cultural events, and social meetups

What Lecce does not have is the kind of full-service expat ecosystem you find in Florence or Rome — English-language bookstores, international schools for children, dense networks of English-speaking professionals. If you have school-age children or work in a field requiring close professional networks, this gap matters. For childless adults and retirees, it is largely irrelevant.

The Honest Cons

Intellectual honesty requires stating clearly what Lecce is not:

  • August is brutally hot. Temperatures routinely hit 35–38°C with high humidity from the surrounding sea. This is not a climate to underestimate. Many Italians from Northern Italy who move to Lecce find the first full summer genuinely shocking. Air conditioning is not a luxury here; it's a health necessity. Budget accordingly for electricity in summer.
  • Limited job market. Lecce's local economy is based on agriculture, tourism, and public sector employment. There are essentially no tech jobs, finance jobs, or high-salary private sector positions. This is a city for remote workers and retirees, not career builders. If your income stops being remote, you need a plan to leave.
  • Fewer English speakers than northern cities. The tourist industry has pushed some English capability into bars, hotels, and restaurants in the centro, but outside the tourist zone, expect Italian only. This is a feature for people who want to learn Italian; a friction point for people who don't.
  • Infrastructure gaps. The South has real infrastructure gaps compared to the North — train connectivity is less frequent, internet speeds in some buildings are lower, road infrastructure outside major routes is variable. These are real, if manageable, differences.
  • Limited direct flights from the US. There are no direct transatlantic flights to Lecce or Brindisi. You will always fly through Rome, Milan, Frankfurt, Amsterdam, or London. This adds friction for frequent US-Italy travel.

For most Americans weighing Puglia, Lecce offers the best combination of urban infrastructure, cultural life, and low cost. It's large enough to have everything you need day-to-day, small enough to feel human, and cheap enough to genuinely stretch a modest retirement income into a comfortable life.
