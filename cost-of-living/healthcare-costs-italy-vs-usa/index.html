# Healthcare Costs in Italy vs USA: What Americans Actually Pay in 2025

Healthcare is the single most financially transformative difference between living in Italy and living in the United States. For many Americans — especially those in their 50s and 60s who aren't yet Medicare-eligible — the healthcare cost comparison alone makes moving to Italy a financially compelling decision.

This guide gives you real numbers: what Americans pay for healthcare in Italy vs. the U.S., what's covered, what isn't, and what the long-term financial picture looks like.

The Fundamental Difference

The United States has a private, insurance-based healthcare system where costs are among the world's highest. Italy has a universal public healthcare system (Servizio Sanitario Nazionale / SSN) funded through taxes and small co-pays, with private care available alongside it.

For American expats with Italian residency:

  • You have the right to enroll in the SSN (for a small annual fee if you're not employed)
  • SSN covers most healthcare needs at dramatically lower cost than U.S. insurance
  • Private Italian healthcare is available for faster access and optional comfort

What You Pay in Italy: The Full Breakdown

SSN Enrollment Fee (Iscrizione Volontaria)

For Americans on an Elective Residency Visa or other non-employment visa, SSN enrollment is voluntary and requires an annual contribution:

Annual Income (worldwide)Annual SSN Fee
Under €10,386€387.34 (flat)
€10,386–€30,987€387 + 7.5% of excess
Over €30,987Capped ~€1,500

Example: Retiree with $35,000/year income (≈€32,000):

  • SSN enrollment: approximately €1,500/year
  • Full coverage for the year: all GP visits free, specialists €15–50

GP Visits (Medico di Medicina Generale)

Cost: €0 — completely free, unlimited visits

Emergency Room (Pronto Soccorso)

Italian emergency rooms are free for genuine emergencies (Codice Rosso — red, and Codice Giallo — yellow triage codes). These are life-threatening or serious conditions.

For non-urgent visits (Codice Verde — green, or Codice Bianco — white):

  • Green code: €25–50 access fee
  • White code: €50–100 (used for conditions that should have been handled by GP)

Reference: D.Lgs. 502/1992; Decree on emergency co-payments by region

Hospital Stays (Ricovero Ospedaliero)

For conditions requiring hospitalization:

  • Emergency admission: Free (no daily charge)
  • Elective/planned surgery (SSN): Free after referral process
  • Co-pay: €10–15/day for the first 3 days in some regions; many regions waive this

Comparison: A 3-day U.S. hospital stay without insurance: $10,000–50,000. With average employer insurance: $1,000–5,000 out-of-pocket. In Italy (SSN): €0–45.

Intramoenia: The Middle Path

Intramoenia (literally "within the walls") is a uniquely Italian system: SSN doctors seeing private patients in public hospital facilities after their public hours. This gives you:

  • Faster appointments than SSN waiting lists (typically 1–3 weeks)
  • Same specialist who works in the public system
  • Cost: €80–200 per visit (less than fully private clinics)

It's the best value option for non-urgent specialist care when the SSN wait is too long.

Private Health Insurance

Many American expats in Italy maintain some private health coverage — either international insurance for travel back to the U.S., or supplemental Italian private insurance for faster access:

Compare to U.S. ACA insurance: $8,000–20,000/year for equivalent coverage. The Italian SSN + private supplement approach saves $5,000–16,000/year per person.

Dental and Vision: Not Covered by SSN

The main gap in Italian public healthcare for adult residents:

Dental (Odontoiatria)

Italian SSN covers dental care for children under 16 and some emergency adult dental care. Adult routine dental is not covered and must be paid privately.

Tip: Many Italians cross into nearby countries for dental tourism (Croatia, Hungary, Albania) for major work. With Italian geography, this is very practical for residents of border regions.

Vision (Oftalmologia)

Eye exams: €50–100 private (free for children, elderly, chronic conditions via SSN)

Glasses: €80–300 for frames + lenses at Italian optical shops; prescription sunglasses €100–400

Contact lenses: similar to U.S. prices; available without prescription at pharmacies

What Americans Miss From U.S. Healthcare

Medicare doesn't transfer. U.S. Medicare provides zero coverage outside the United States. If you're 65+ and living in Italy, you need either the SSN (available to you as a resident) or private international insurance. The SSN is dramatically cheaper.

Concierge medicine equivalent: Not standard in Italy. Italians who want truly premium, always-available private care use specific private clinics, not a concierge GP system. Some English-speaking private GPs in major cities offer something similar at €100–200/month retainer.

Mental health access: Italy's mental health system (Salute Mentale, part of SSN) offers free or low-cost psychiatric and psychological care, but with long waiting times. Private therapy runs €60–120/session — similar to U.S. with insurance, much cheaper than U.S. without.

The Long-Term Financial Picture

For an American couple moving to Italy at 58, planning to live there until 75 (17 years):

U.S. healthcare costs (pre-Medicare to Medicare + supplement):

  • Ages 58–65 (pre-Medicare): $20,000–36,000/year × 7 years = $140,000–252,000
  • Ages 65–75 (Medicare + supplement): $8,000–15,000/year × 10 years = $80,000–150,000
  • Total: $220,000–402,000

Italy healthcare costs (SSN + private supplement):

  • SSN enrollment + supplement: €2,000–4,500/year × 17 years = €34,000–76,500
  • Total: approximately $37,000–84,000

Lifetime savings on healthcare alone: $136,000–318,000 for one couple.

This is perhaps the single strongest financial argument for the Italy move for Americans in their late 50s and early 60s.
