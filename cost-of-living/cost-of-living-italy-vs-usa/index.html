Cost of Living: Italy vs USA — What Americans Actually Save (and Spend More On) in 2025

Honest, data-driven comparison of what life costs in Italy vs. the United States — housing, food, healthcare, transport, and the real financial case for moving to Italy.

The promise of moving to Italy often comes with a vague claim: "it's cheaper than the U.S." Sometimes this is true. Sometimes it's not. It depends entirely on where in the U.S. you're comparing from, where in Italy you're going, and how you live.

I live in Italy and talk to Americans who've made this move every month. Here's an honest, category-by-category comparison with real numbers — not the cherry-picked comparisons you find in expat fantasy articles.

The Honest Framework

Italy is significantly cheaper than high-cost U.S. cities: If you're moving from San Francisco, New York, Los Angeles, Boston, Seattle, or Washington D.C., Italy almost universally offers a better cost-per-quality-of-life ratio.

Italy is roughly comparable to mid-cost U.S. cities: Austin, Denver, Chicago, Atlanta, Portland — the comparison depends heavily on which Italian city and lifestyle.

Italy can be more expensive than low-cost U.S. areas: Rural Tennessee, Mississippi, Kansas, or mid-sized Midwestern cities often have cheaper housing than Italian cities. The tradeoff is not purely financial for this comparison group.

Housing: The Biggest Variable

Housing is where Italy's advantage is clearest for anyone coming from an expensive U.S. metro — and least clear for those from affordable U.S. areas.

Key insight: The Italy advantage is massive vs. coastal U.S. cities, moderate vs. mid-tier U.S. cities, and reverses for cheap U.S. cities.

Buying a property compounds the advantage. A 3-bedroom house in Turin costs €200,000–350,000. The same quality in Austin: $550,000–800,000. In San Francisco: $1.2–2M+.

Food: Italy Wins Almost Everywhere

Food is one of Italy's clearest financial advantages — and the quality is higher.

Milan €70–100 (~$77–110)
Florence €65–95 (~$72–105)
Turin €60–90 (~$66–99)
Naples €50–75 (~$55–83)

Why Italy is cheaper for groceries:
- Shorter supply chains (much food produced locally)
- Markets (mercati rionali) sell fresh produce at below-supermarket prices
- Less processed food in the Italian diet → lower cost per meal
- Wine is table wine, not a luxury purchase (€2–5 for a bottle that would cost $15+ in the U.S.)

The espresso alone is revelatory. A double espresso in New York: $5–7. In Italy: €1.30. Americans who drink 2 coffees a day save $80–120/month on coffee alone.

Healthcare: Italy's Biggest Advantage

This is where Italy's advantage over the U.S. is most dramatic and most meaningful for retirement planning.

Annual Healthcare Costs: Uninsured/Self-Pay Scenario

Item U.S. Italy (SSN)
Basic health coverage $6,000–18,000/year (ACA market) €387–1,500/year (SSN)
GP visit $150–400 (without insurance) €0 (with SSN)
Specialist visit $250–800 €15–50 co-pay
Emergency room $1,500–5,000+ €0–50
MRI $1,500–3,500 €15–80 (SSN) / €200–400 (private)
Prescription (30-day supply) $30–300+ €1–15

For a retired American couple:
- U.S. healthcare costs (pre-Medicare, ages 60–65): $24,000–36,000/year
- Italy SSN (same couple): €800–3,000/year

This single line item can make Italy financially transformative for Americans in their 50s and early 60s who aren't yet Medicare-eligible. Even accounting for all other Italy costs, healthcare savings alone often exceed $20,000/year.

After 65, Medicare doesn't cover you in Italy — you'd need the SSN or private insurance. But the SSN still costs dramatically less than U.S. Medicare + supplement premiums.

Transportation: No Contest

Monthly Transportation Costs

Mode/Location Monthly Cost
Car ownership (U.S. average) $800–1,200 (car payment + insurance + gas + maintenance)
Car ownership (Italy, similar quality) €400–700
Public transit, NYC $132/month (MetroCard)
Public transit, Chicago $105/month (Ventra)
Public transit, Milan €39/month (ATM pass)
Public transit, Turin €35/month
Public transit, Rome €35/month

Italy's transit advantage is exceptional. For Americans in major Italian cities, a €35–39 monthly pass covers all urban transport. No car payment, no insurance, no parking ($300–600/month in many U.S. cities), no gas.

For Italy's excellent intercity train network:
- Milan to Rome: €35–80 (Frecciarossa, 3 hours)
- Turin to Milan: €15–25 (45 minutes)
- Florence to Venice: €25–50 (2 hours)

U.S. equivalent flight: $150–400, plus airport time.

What Costs More in Italy vs. the U.S.

To be honest, some things genuinely cost more in Italy:

Electronics and Technology

Item U.S. Price Italy Price
iPhone 15 Pro (256GB) $1,099 €1,299 (~$1,430)
MacBook Air M3 $1,099 €1,329 (~$1,460)
Mid-range laptop $600–800 €700–1,000
Large TV (65") $600–900 €700–1,100

Electronics in Italy include 22% IVA (VAT) with no way to reclaim it as a resident. Buy your electronics in the U.S. before you move.

Clothing (Brand Name)

Major American/international brands often cost 10–20% more in Italy due to import distribution markups. Italian brands at Italian prices can be reasonable, but you're not getting deals on Nike, The North Face, or similar.

Exception: Italian fashion brands (Moncler, Prada, Gucci, etc.) cost the same or slightly less than U.S. prices due to no import markup. For luxury shoppers, this is an advantage.

Amazon Shipping and Selection

Amazon.it has a smaller selection than Amazon.com, and Prime delivery is often 2–3 days (vs. same-day or next-day). Some U.S. products aren't available in Italy. Ordering from Amazon.com with shipping to Italy adds €15–40 per order.

Heating and Cooling

Italian utility bills spike in winter (gas heating) and summer (air conditioning, which is less common in Italian apartments than U.S. homes). If you're used to central air and central heat in a well-insulated American house, Italian heating and cooling can be more expensive per square foot.

Quality Appliances

Italian apartments often come without appliances (dishwasher, washer/dryer). Equipping an apartment: €1,500–4,000 initial investment.

The Full Budget Comparison

Scenario: American couple, both 62, retiring in Italy

U.S. version (Boston suburb):
- Housing (rent or ownership equivalent): $3,200/month
- Food + dining: $1,800/month
- Healthcare: $2,800/month (ACA pre-Medicare)
- Transport (2 cars): $1,600/month
- Utilities + phone + internet: $500/month
- Entertainment, travel, misc: $1,500/month
- Total: ~$11,400/month ($136,800/year)

Italy version (Bologna or Turin):
- Housing (2-bed apartment, good neighborhood): €1,200/month
- Food + dining: €800/month
- Healthcare (SSN x2): €130/month
- Transport (public + 1 small car): €400/month
- Utilities + phone + internet: €300/month
- Entertainment, travel, misc: €700/month
- Total: ~€3,530/month (~$3,880/month, $46,600/year)

Annual saving: ~$90,000

Over 10 years: ~$900,000 in spending power difference — and that's before investment returns on the capital they're not spending.

This is why Italy retirement is not just a lifestyle choice but a compelling financial decision for the right American.

What You Need to Make It Work Financially

The savings above don't materialize automatically. They require:

  1. Actually living Italian. Shopping at the supermarket, cooking at home, taking the bus, drinking wine that costs €3 a bottle. Americans who try to replicate their U.S. lifestyle in Italy spend far more.

  2. Italian tax residency management. Italy taxes residents on worldwide income. The savings projections above assume proper tax planning (the U.S.-Italy treaty, FEIE, Flat Tax for New Residents if eligible).

  3. Currency exposure. Living on USD income in EUR means exchange rate fluctuations affect your real purchasing power. A weak dollar can erode Italy's cost advantage. Budget in euros and hedge exposure if income is dollar-denominated.

  4. The initial move costs. Visa fees, moving costs, Italian setup (furniture, deposits), travel: budget €5,000–15,000 one-time costs to establish yourself.
