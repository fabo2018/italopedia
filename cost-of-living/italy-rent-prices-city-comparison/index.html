# Italy Rent Prices by City: Complete 2025 Comparison for Americans

Rent is the single largest expense for most Americans moving to Italy — and the variance between cities is extraordinary. A 2-bedroom apartment in central Milan costs €2,500–4,000/month. The same apartment in Palermo costs €700–1,200. That difference — €1,300–2,800/month — is €15,600–33,600 per year. Over a decade, it's the difference between building wealth in Italy and struggling.

This guide gives you current, verified rental prices across Italy's major cities, organized by apartment type and neighborhood quality. Data sourced from Immobiliare.it and Idealista.it market reports, updated January 2025.

How to Read This Guide

Prices are presented as ranges reflecting:

  • Lower end: Functional neighborhood, older building, basic finishes
  • Upper end: Desirable neighborhood, renovated apartment, good finishes

"Center" = historic city center or most desirable residential areas

"Inner suburbs" = 10–20 minutes from center, good transit, real neighborhood feel

"Outer/peripheral" = 20–45 minutes from center, car often useful

All prices are monthly furnished rental unless noted. Unfurnished apartments cost 10–20% less.

What Affects Rent: Key Variables

Furnished vs. unfurnished: Furnished commands 10–20% premium but saves €3,000–8,000 in furniture costs. For stays under 3 years, furnished usually makes sense.

Contract type:

  • 4+4 contract (standard): 4-year term, renewable 4 years. Most regulated, best tenant protections, often slightly lower rent.
  • 3+2 transitorio: For temporary stays (3-year max). Slightly higher rent.
  • Short-term (affitto breve): 1–11 months. 20–40% premium. Less protection.

Building age: Pre-1970 buildings (cheaper) often have poor insulation and older heating systems. Budget 20–30% more for utilities in winter. Post-2000 buildings are more efficient.

Floor: In Italy, higher floors typically command higher rents (views, light, less street noise). Ground floor is cheapest but least desirable.

Elevator: No elevator in a 4th-floor apartment = meaningfully cheaper. For longer stays or anyone with mobility concerns, verify.

Finding Rentals: Best Italian Platforms

  • Immobiliare.it — Italy's largest real estate portal, most comprehensive listings
  • Idealista.it — strong in major cities; good filter tools
  • Casa.it — good secondary option
  • Subito.it — classifieds; some direct landlord listings
  • Facebook groups — "[City] Apartments for Rent" groups have many direct landlord listings bypassing agency fees

Agency fees (provvigione): Italian law allows agents to charge the tenant 1 month's rent as commission. This is negotiable and sometimes avoidable by finding direct landlord listings. Always ask if the listing is through an agency.

Practical Tips on Renting in Italy

Always visit before signing. Photos online are frequently misleading. Italian apartments range from beautifully renovated to poorly maintained, and the difference isn't always visible in listings.

Check the heating system. Ask: "riscaldamento autonomo o centralizzato?" Autonomous heating (your own boiler) gives you control; central heating (building-wide system) is convenient but you pay regardless of usage.

Verify the energy class (Classe Energetica). Required by law in rental listings. Class A or B = efficient, lower utilities. Class F or G = poorly insulated, high winter bills.

Get a written contract, registered with the Agenzia delle Entrate. Unregistered contracts exist but leave you legally unprotected. Registered contracts also allow you to deduct rent from Italian income taxes if applicable.
