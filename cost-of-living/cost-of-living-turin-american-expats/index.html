Cost of Living in Turin for American Expats (2025): Complete Budget Guide

Real monthly costs for American expats living in Turin, Italy — rent by neighborhood, groceries, transport, healthcare, dining out, and three complete budget scenarios from frugal to comfortable. Written from Piedmont.

Turin is the most underrated major city in Italy for American expats — and probably the best value in Northern Italy.

It has everything: a world-class food scene (this is the birthplace of Slow Food), stunning Baroque architecture, immediate Alpine access for skiing and hiking, a major university, and excellent public transport. And it costs significantly less than Milan, Florence, or Rome.

Rent in Turin is approximately 35–40% lower than in Milan for equivalent apartments. A comfortable expat lifestyle that would cost €3,500/month in Milan runs €2,200–2,500/month in Turin.

I live in Piedmont and know these numbers from the inside.

Housing: The Biggest Expense

Rental Prices by Neighborhood (2025)

Centro Storico / Quadrilatero Romano The historic heart of Turin — grid of grand streets, arcades, and the best restaurant concentration in the city.

  • 1BR: €900–1,300/month
  • 2BR: €1,300–1,800/month

Crocetta Prestigious, tranquil residential neighborhood with Liberty-style architecture and a famous daily market.

  • 1BR: €850–1,200/month
  • 2BR: €1,200–1,700/month

Prati / San Salvario Lively, multicultural neighborhood popular with young professionals and students.

  • 1BR: €700–950/month
  • 2BR: €950–1,300/month

Vanchiglia / Aurora Formerly working-class, now the most gentrifying neighborhood in Turin.

  • 1BR: €600–850/month
  • 2BR: €850–1,200/month

Utilities

  • Basic utilities for 80–85m²: €130–280/month (higher in winter — gas heating spikes in December–February)
  • Internet: €25–35/month for 1Gbps fiber

Food and Groceries

Monthly grocery budget (single person):

  • Frugal (Lidl/Eurospin + local markets): €180–220
  • Moderate: €250–320
  • Comfortable (Esselunga + quality products + wine): €350–450

Dining Out

  • Espresso at a bar: €1.30–1.80
  • Lunch menu at a trattoria (primo, secondo, water, bread): €12–18
  • Aperitivo (drink + generous food buffet): €8–12
  • Dinner for two at a mid-range restaurant with wine: €60–90
💡 Tip

Fabrizio's note: The Turin aperitivo culture is extraordinary value. From 6–9pm, most bars serve substantial food alongside drinks. A €9–10 Negroni or spritz typically comes with pasta, risotto, bruschette, and more — this is dinner for many locals.

Transportation

Public Transport

  • Monthly pass: €38 (unlimited, all lines)
  • Annual pass: €360

The metro line runs through the center. For most expats living centrally, public transport + walking covers everything.

Intercity

  • Turin–Milan: 45 minutes by high-speed train (~€15–30)
  • Turin–Rome: ~4.5 hours (~€40–80)
  • Turin–Paris: ~5.5 hours by TGV (~€50–120)

Healthcare

SSN (National Health Service) — Voluntary Enrollment

For Americans on the Elective Residency Visa: €2,000–2,789/year (updated 2025 rates)

Once enrolled:

  • GP visits: free
  • Specialist visits (with referral): €15–36 co-pay
  • Hospital: essentially free

FAQ

Is Turin safe for expats? Turin is generally a safe city. The central neighborhoods (Crocetta, Centro, San Salvario) are very safe. Crime rate is significantly lower than comparable US cities.

How does Turin compare to Milan for expats? Turin offers: lower costs (35–40% cheaper housing), less traffic, better food scene per capita, immediate Alpine access, more authentic Italian culture. Milan offers: larger international community, more English speakers, better job market, more flights.

Is Turin a good base for exploring Italy? Excellent. High-speed trains connect Turin to Milan (45 min), Genoa (2 hrs), Florence (3 hrs), and Rome (4.5 hrs). Plus immediate access to French Alps and Côte d'Azur.
