Italy vs Spain vs Portugal: Cost of Living Comparison for American Expats

These are the three most popular European destinations for Americans moving abroad. The honest answer is that all three are excellent choices — but they are not interchangeable. The right country depends on what you're actually optimizing for, and this guide makes that decision transparent.

The Honest Framing

The Italy-vs-Spain-vs-Portugal debate is one of the most common questions in American expat forums, and it's usually framed as a cost optimization problem: which country is cheapest? That framing misses what actually matters. These three countries have genuinely different cultures, climates, bureaucratic systems, social environments, and lifestyle profiles. Cost is a real factor, but it's rarely the one that should drive the decision.

With that said, cost differences are real and significant enough to matter at the margin, particularly for retirees on fixed incomes. Portugal is cheaper than Italy in most respects. Spain straddles the middle in most cost categories. Italy is the most expensive of the three, particularly in the north, though Southern Italy largely erases that gap.

Here's the complete picture, without the promotional framing that most expat content applies to whichever country the author happens to live in.

Food and Grocery Costs

All three countries benefit from Mediterranean agricultural traditions and produce good food cheaply by Northern European or American standards. There are real differences though:

Portugal is the cheapest of the three for food and dining, particularly in smaller cities. Spain is comparable to Italy in northern cities but cheaper in the south (Seville, Malaga, Murcia). Italy's food costs are highest but this is partly because the average quality available at a given price point is also highest — Italian food culture demands and delivers better ingredients at the supermarket and better execution at the trattoria.

One honest observation: Portugal's restaurant scene in Lisbon and Porto has gentrified significantly since 2015. The €10 bacalhau dinner that made Portugal famous in expat circles has been replaced, in tourist areas, by €25–30 tourist menu restaurants. You can still eat cheaply in Portugal but you need to know where to go. The same is true of Italy and Spain.

Internet quality and coverage are broadly comparable across the three countries in urban areas, with Portugal having invested significantly in fiber infrastructure in recent years.

Healthcare Access and Cost

All three countries have public national health systems that residents can access. Access is contingent on establishing legal residency and enrolling in the system.

Italy — SSN (Servizio Sanitario Nazionale)

Free GP care once enrolled. Specialist visits involve co-pays (ticket) of €15–€50. Hospital care is free or very cheap. Quality varies dramatically by region — Emilia-Romagna and Lombardy have excellent public systems; Calabria and parts of the deep south do not. Private supplemental insurance: €50–€150/month. Enrollment requires legal residency (elective residence visa or citizenship).

Spain — SNS (Sistema Nacional de Salud)

Broadly similar to Italy's system. GP free once enrolled; specialists involve small co-pays. Quality is generally high, with the Basque Country, Catalonia, and Madrid having particularly strong systems. Spain's public healthcare is widely considered the best of the three countries, ranking in the top 10 globally by most measures. Enrollment requires legal residency. Private supplemental insurance: €40–€120/month.

Portugal — SNS (Serviço Nacional de Saúde)

Portugal's public health system has historically been underfunded relative to Spain and Italy. Wait times for GP registration and specialist appointments are a real issue, particularly since the pandemic. Many expats in Portugal use private health insurance as their primary coverage rather than relying heavily on public SNS. Fortunately, private healthcare in Portugal is genuinely cheap — a private GP consultation runs €50–€80, and private health insurance costs €40–€100/month. This keeps the total cost manageable.

Healthcare Verdict for Expats

Spain has the strongest public healthcare system. Italy's system is excellent in the north and center but uneven in the south. Portugal's public system is the weakest of the three but private care is cheap enough to compensate. For retirees with significant health needs, Spain's public system is a genuine advantage. For younger, healthy remote workers, the differences matter less.

Portugal's D7 visa has the lowest income requirement of any of these options — at roughly €820/month, it is accessible to a much broader range of Americans than Italy's elective residence visa (which requires approximately €31,000 in verifiable annual passive income). This has made Portugal, and specifically Lisbon, the most popular choice for Americans who want to move to Southern Europe but don't have a large passive income stream.

Spain's NLV sits in the middle at around €27,800/year, which is achievable for many Americans with retirement savings. Italy's elective residence requirement is the highest but is structured specifically for financially independent retirees — if you have a pension, Social Security, and investment income that clearly exceeds the threshold, Italy is straightforward. If you're scraping to meet the minimum, Portugal is more forgiving.

Tax Regimes: The Big Picture

All three countries have created preferential tax regimes to attract wealthy foreign retirees and remote workers. The landscape has shifted in recent years as some regimes have been modified or ended.

Italy — 7% Flat Tax for Pensioners

Italy offers a 7% flat tax rate on all foreign-sourced income for pensioners who establish residency in qualifying Southern Italian municipalities (comuni with fewer than 20,000 inhabitants in Sicilia, Calabria, Sardinia, Campania, Basilicata, Abruzzo, Molise, and Puglia, as well as certain northern comuni). This regime lasts for 10 years. If your pension plus other foreign-sourced income is substantial, this regime is extraordinarily favorable. A pensioner with $100,000/year in US income pays approximately €7,000 in Italian tax, which is dramatically less than what the same income would attract in progressive tax brackets.

Portugal — NHR Regime (Now Ended/Modified)

Portugal's famous NHR (Non-Habitual Resident) regime — which offered 10 years of tax exemption on most foreign income — was significantly modified in 2024. The original NHR is no longer available for new applicants. It has been replaced by a more limited regime (IFICI, or "Tax Incentive for Scientific Research and Innovation") that is far more restrictive and focused on specific professions. For general retirees, the NHR advantage is largely gone. This materially reduces Portugal's tax appeal relative to Italy's southern flat-tax regime.

Spain — Beckham Law and General Tax System

Spain's "Beckham Law" (Régimen Especial de Trabajadores Desplazados) applies a flat 24% tax rate on Spanish-source income for remote workers who are new tax residents in Spain. It is available for 6 years. However, it applies only to people who move to Spain for work reasons, not retirees. For retirees, Spain's general progressive tax system applies, with rates that can reach 47% on high incomes. Spain is not tax-advantaged for retirees relative to the other two options.

Tax Regime Summary

For retirees with significant foreign income, Italy's 7% flat tax in qualifying southern municipalities is the most favorable regime currently available in Southern Europe — better than Portugal's modified NHR and far better than Spain's general progressive system. For younger remote workers, Spain's Beckham Law and Portugal's D8 digital nomad visa each have merit depending on income levels and employment situations.

Lifestyle Trade-offs: Being Honest

The lifestyle differences between these three countries are real and worth naming explicitly rather than leaving them in the soft language of "it depends on your preferences."

Italy

Best food culture of any country in the world. This is not Italian chauvinism — it is the objective consensus of anyone who has lived in all three countries. The quality, variety, and regional specificity of Italian food is unmatched. Italian cities have extraordinary architectural beauty. The arts, opera, design, and cultural heritage are unique. The language learning curve is real but manageable for English speakers. The bureaucracy is legendarily frustrating. Northern Italy (Piedmont, Lombardy, Veneto) has Central European infrastructure quality; Southern Italy is more chaotic and less efficient. Italy is the most historically complex of the three choices.

Spain

Best overall climate — particularly if you choose the Costa del Sol, the Canary Islands, or Valencia's Mediterranean coast. Spanish cities are highly livable, well-maintained, and have excellent public infrastructure. The language (Spanish) has a gentler learning curve than Italian for Americans, and Spanish opens doors across Latin America in a way that Italian and Portuguese don't. The food is excellent without being quite as culture-central as in Italy. The social culture — late dinners, long afternoons, siesta mentality — aligns well with a relaxed expat lifestyle. Spain's cities are excellent for families (good schools, outdoor lifestyle).

Portugal

Cheapest and most English-friendly. Portugal has the most established English-language expat ecosystem of the three, particularly in Lisbon and Porto. English proficiency among Portuguese under 40 is very high. The NHR regime (now ended) created an enormous English-speaking expat community in Lisbon that has developed infrastructure — English-language services, coworking spaces, international schools — that Italy and Spain don't have to the same degree. Portuguese is the hardest of the three languages for English speakers to learn. The Atlantic climate (cool, rainy winters; hot summers) is milder than the Mediterranean but not the sunny Southern Europe that most Americans imagine. The bureaucracy (SEF, now AIMA) has been notoriously dysfunctional for visa processing.

Who Should Choose Each Country

Choose Italy if:

  • Food and culinary culture are genuinely important to you — not just as a talking point but as something you'll engage with daily
  • You have sufficient passive income to qualify for the elective residence visa (approx. €31,000/year) or qualify for citizenship through descent
  • You're a pensioner who can access Italy's 7% flat tax regime in a qualifying southern municipality — this is a significant financial advantage
  • You want to live in or near one of the world's great cities (Rome, Florence, Milan, Turin, Venice, Naples) and experience their history and culture as a resident rather than a tourist
  • You have Italian heritage and feel a genuine connection to the country beyond its surface appeal

Choose Spain if:

  • Climate is your top priority — Spain's Mediterranean south and the Canary Islands offer the best weather in Europe for most Americans
  • You have children and need a good international school environment and active outdoor lifestyle
  • You already speak Spanish or find the language acquisition more appealing than Italian
  • You want access to Latin America as a platform for future travel and work
  • Healthcare quality is a primary concern — Spain's public health system is the best of the three
  • You're a remote worker who qualifies for the Beckham Law (24% flat tax on Spanish-source income for 6 years)

Choose Portugal if:

  • Cost minimization is your primary driver and you cannot meet Italy's or Spain's income thresholds comfortably
  • You want the easiest expat infrastructure — English speakers, established expat communities, English-language services
  • You value relative ease of integration into an expat social environment over integration into local culture
  • You're attracted by Lisbon and Porto as cities — they are genuinely beautiful, well-run, and cosmopolitan
  • The Atlantic climate (cooler, wetter than full Mediterranean) works for you — if you like San Francisco's climate, you'll like Lisbon's
Is Portugal still cheaper than Italy for expats after the NHR ended?
Yes, in terms of pure cost of living, Portugal remains cheaper than Italy overall — particularly for rent. The NHR's end reduces Portugal's tax advantage for retirees specifically, but doesn't change rental costs, grocery costs, or day-to-day living expenses. For someone making a cost-driven decision, Portugal is still meaningfully cheaper, particularly in smaller cities.
Can I switch countries after I arrive? Is it easy to move from one to another?
Administratively, you can leave one country and apply for residency in another, but you'll need to go through the visa application process again from scratch in the new country. There's no free movement for non-EU nationals between EU countries for residency purposes. Moving from Italy to Spain or Portugal as a non-citizen means reapplying. That said, people do it — it's just a significant administrative undertaking.
Where is the best place for a retired couple on $3,500/month total?
At $3,500/month combined, all three countries are viable, but you have the most comfort in Portugal (Coimbra, Braga, or smaller cities outside Lisbon) or Southern Italy (Lecce, Palermo, Catania). Spain at that budget works well in Valencia, Seville, or Malaga. You'd find it tight in Barcelona, Madrid, Rome, or Milan. The sweet spot is the second-tier cities in each country.
