# Italian Citizenship by Descent (Jure Sanguinis): Complete Guide for Americans (2025)

Millions of Americans have Italian ancestry. For decades, Italy offered one of the most generous citizenship-by-descent programs in the world — tracing back generations with no generational limit.

**That changed in 2025.** On May 24, 2025, Italy enacted **Law No. 74/2025** (the "Tajani Decree"), the most significant restriction to Italian citizenship by descent in over a century.

If you've been researching Italian citizenship through your great-grandparents or earlier ancestors, this guide is essential reading. The new rules affect millions of Italian-Americans, and getting the details right matters enormously.

---

## The 2025 Law Change: What Happened and Why

### Before May 24, 2025 (Old Rules)

Under Italy's 1912 Citizenship Law, there was **no generational limit** for jure sanguinis claims. If you could prove an unbroken chain of Italian citizenship back to an Italian-born ancestor alive after Italy's unification in 1861, you could apply — regardless of how many generations back that ancestor was. Great-grandparents, great-great-grandparents — all eligible.

This made Italy's jure sanguinis one of the most permissive in the world. Estimates suggest 60–80 million people globally could potentially claim Italian ancestry under the old rules.

### What Changed: Law No. 74/2025 (Effective May 24, 2025)

The Tajani Decree introduced a strict **generational limit**: For applications submitted after March 27, 2025, citizenship by descent is only available through a **parent or grandparent** born in Italy.

Great-grandparents, great-great-grandparents, and earlier ancestors no longer qualify for new applications.

**Why the change?** Italian consulates and municipalities were overwhelmed — applications surged 40% between 2014 and 2024, with massive backlogs in US consulates (often 3+ years for appointments). The government cited the need to ensure applicants have a "real and direct connection" to Italy.

---

## The Critical Cutoff: March 27, 2025

The most important date in the new law:

- **Applications submitted OR consulate appointments officially booked/confirmed before March 27, 2025, 11:59 PM (Rome time):** Processed under the **old rules** — no generational limit applies
- **Applications filed or appointments booked on or after March 28, 2025:** Subject to the **new rules** — parent or grandparent only

If you had a confirmed consulate appointment or submitted your application before this date, your case is evaluated under the previous, more permissive law.

---

## Who Can Still Claim Italian Citizenship by Descent (New Rules)

Under Law 74/2025, you qualify for jure sanguinis if:

**Condition 1:** You have an Italian-born **parent or grandparent** (not great-grandparent or earlier)

**Condition 2:** That parent or grandparent held **only Italian citizenship** (not dual citizenship, including Italian-American dual citizenship) at the time of your birth — or at the time of their death if they're deceased

**Additional requirement:** Your Italian ancestor must not have lost Italian citizenship through naturalization before your parent was born (the "transmission chain" must be unbroken)

---

## The Transmission Chain: How Citizenship Passes

Italian citizenship passes from parent to child automatically at birth — but the chain can be broken by naturalization.

**Example of an unbroken chain:**
- Your grandfather was born in Italy → Italian citizen
- Your grandfather naturalized as a US citizen **after** your parent was born → citizenship already transmitted to your parent
- Your parent was an Italian citizen when you were born → you're an Italian citizen

**Example of a broken chain:**
- Your grandfather was born in Italy → Italian citizen
- Your grandfather naturalized as a US citizen **before** your parent was born → citizenship NOT transmitted to your parent
- Your parent cannot transmit what they never had → you have no claim

### The "Minor Rule" (Critical Nuance)

Italy also recognizes that if a parent naturalized as a US citizen while their child was **still a minor** (under 21 before 1975, under 18 after 1975), the minor child "followed" the parent's naturalization — which could break the chain.

The 2025 law temporarily removed some minor rule restrictions. Specifically, people who:
- Were born in Italy OR lived in Italy for at least 2 consecutive years, AND
- Lost Italian citizenship due to the minor rule

...can **re-claim citizenship** by filing a declaration between **July 1, 2025 and December 31, 2027**.

---

## The 1948 Rule: Women's Citizenship (Still Applies)

Before 1948, Italian law did not allow mothers to transmit Italian citizenship to their children. The 1948 Italian Constitution changed this, but the pre-1948 limitation affected many family lines.

**If your Italian ancestor in the transmission line is female, and she was Italian before 1948:** Standard jure sanguinis through her may not work administratively — but Italian courts have consistently ruled in favor of applicants in these cases (the so-called "1948 cases" or "*casi 1948*").

The judicial route (applying in Italian courts rather than through the consulate) is often necessary for 1948 cases and typically takes 12–24 months.

## Full Document Checklist

The document requirements are largely unchanged under the new law. You need to trace and document the entire transmission chain from your Italian ancestor to yourself.

### For Each Person in Your Chain (Italian Ancestor → You)
- [ ] **Italian birth certificate** (atto di nascita) — for the Italian-born ancestor
- [ ] **Marriage certificates** — for every marriage in the chain
- [ ] **Naturalization record** — proving when (and importantly, if and when) your ancestor naturalized as a US citizen (critical for the minor rule analysis)
- [ ] **Death certificates** — for deceased ancestors

### For the Applicant (You)
- [ ] Valid US passport
- [ ] Your birth certificate
- [ ] Your marriage certificate (if applicable)
- [ ] Proof of no criminal record

### All Documents Must Be:
- Apostilled (for US documents submitted to Italian authorities)
- Translated into Italian by a certified translator
- Italian records obtained from the relevant Italian comune or church records (ANAGRAPHE)

> **Fabrizio's note:** Gathering these documents is often the hardest part. US vital records offices, Italian comuni, and church archives can all be involved. Allow **6–18 months** for document gathering alone, especially for older records. Genealogical research services that specialize in Italian records are often worth the investment.

## Two Application Routes

### Route 1: Italian Consulate in the US (Administrative Route)

Apply at the Italian consulate with jurisdiction over your US state of residence.

**Pros:** Lower cost, can be done while living in the US
**Cons:** Extremely long waits — US consulates are backlogged **3–5 years** for appointments in many jurisdictions. Some consulates in 2025 were not booking new appointments at all.

**Process:**
1. Gather all documents
2. Book an appointment (often multi-year wait)
3. Submit complete application at the appointment
4. Wait for review and decision (additional months after appointment)
5. If approved, receive Italian citizenship certificate

### Route 2: Italian Municipality (If You Move to Italy)

If you move to Italy and establish residency, you can apply directly at the *Comune* where your Italian ancestor was born or where you establish residency.

**Pros:** Potentially faster than US consulate; allows in-person follow-up
**Cons:** Requires living in Italy; municipalities also have backlogs (though generally shorter than US consulates)

### Route 3: Italian Court (Judicial Route)

For complex cases — including 1948 cases, cases denied by the consulate, or cases where an applicant wants to bypass the administrative queue — Italian courts offer another path.

**Pros:** Courts have upheld jure sanguinis as a birthright; 12–24 month resolution
**Cons:** Requires an Italian lawyer; higher costs (€3,000–8,000+ in legal fees)

Italian courts have consistently ruled that jure sanguinis is a right existing from birth — meaning the 2025 law's restrictions may be challenged for those who qualified before the change. Several cases challenging the new law were pending in Italian courts as of mid-2025.

## Italian Citizenship by Naturalization: The Alternative Path

If jure sanguinis doesn't apply to you (wrong generation, broken chain), Italian citizenship through naturalization is still available — it just takes longer.

As a US citizen residing in Italy:
- **After 10 years** of legal continuous residency → eligible to apply for Italian citizenship by naturalization
- Requirements: legal residency throughout, clean criminal record, demonstrated Italian language knowledge (B1 level), no serious tax violations

Italian citizenship by naturalization is the path for Americans who move to Italy on the Elective Residency Visa or Digital Nomad Visa and stay long-term.

## What Italian Citizenship Gives You

Italian citizenship is also **EU citizenship** — one of the most valuable passports in the world.

**Benefits:**
- **EU passport** — live, work, and travel freely in all 27 EU member states without visa requirements
- **Italian passport** — visa-free access to 190+ countries including the US, UK, Japan, and all Schengen countries
- **Access to Italian public services** — healthcare, education, pensions
- **Property rights** — no non-EU restrictions on property purchase in Italy or other EU countries
- **Your children inherit** — Italian citizenship passes to your children automatically

## The Legal Challenge: Is the 2025 Law Constitutional?

The 2025 reform is legally controversial. Italian and international lawyers argue that jure sanguinis under the 1912 law was a **birthright** — meaning those who qualified at birth cannot be retroactively excluded.

As of mid-2025, several cases challenging the Tajani Decree were before Italian courts. The Constitutional Court was expected to rule on related issues by late 2025 or 2026.

**Practical implication:** If you had a valid claim under the old rules and have not yet applied, consulting an Italian citizenship lawyer immediately is essential. A pending court challenge could restore eligibility — but you need legal representation to benefit from it.

## FAQ

**I have Italian great-grandparents — can I still claim citizenship?**
Under the new Law 74/2025 (effective May 24, 2025), new applications based on great-grandparents no longer qualify administratively. If your application was submitted or your consulate appointment was confirmed before March 27, 2025, the old rules apply. If not, consult an Italian lawyer — court challenges to the new law may restore eligibility.

**My Italian grandmother was born in Italy but naturalized as a US citizen before my mother was born. Does the chain still work?**
No — naturalization before the birth of the next generation breaks the chain. The citizenship cannot pass if your grandmother naturalized before your mother was born.

**I was born in Italy but my parents were American. Am I an Italian citizen?**
Not automatically — Italy uses jure sanguinis (by blood), not jus soli (by place of birth). Being born in Italy does not grant citizenship unless you had an Italian parent.

**Does Italian citizenship require me to give up US citizenship?**
No. The US generally allows dual citizenship, and Italy allows it. Becoming an Italian citizen does not require you to renounce US citizenship. You would hold both passports.

**How long does the jure sanguinis process take?**
Consulate route in the US: **3–5+ years** currently due to backlogs. Judicial route in Italy: **12–24 months** with a lawyer. Moving to Italy and applying at a comune: variable, often **1–2 years**.

**Is there a language requirement for jure sanguinis?**
No. Italian language proficiency is NOT required for citizenship by descent (jure sanguinis). It IS required for citizenship by naturalization (B1 level).
