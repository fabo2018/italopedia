# How to Open a Bank Account in Italy as an American (2025)

An Italian bank account isn't optional — it's essential. You need it to pay rent, receive your Permesso di Soggiorno notifications, pay taxes via F24 form, set up utilities, and function in daily Italian life.

The process is more bureaucratic than Americans expect, but there's a smart strategy that makes it much easier. This guide explains your options, the documents you need, the best banks for expats, and the combination most experienced expats use.

---

## Resident vs. Non-Resident Account: The Critical Distinction

Before choosing a bank, you need to understand the difference between these two account types — it affects everything.

### Resident Account (Conto Corrente Residente)
Available once you're registered at the Anagrafe and have Italian tax residency (spending 183+ days/year in Italy).

- Lower fees
- Full banking services (IBAN, debit card, credit card, direct debits, F24 payments)
- Access to mortgages and loans
- Better interest rates on savings

### Non-Resident Account (Conto Corrente Non-Residente / Conto Estero)
Available if you don't yet have Italian residency. More limited and more expensive.

- Higher fees (additional compliance costs for banks)
- Limited services at some banks
- Fewer banks offer this option
- Still functional for essential transactions (transfers, ATM withdrawals)

> **Practical advice:** If you arrive in Italy with your Permesso di Soggiorno ricevuta and Anagrafe registration, you can often open a resident account from the start. Don't wait — get your documents in order and open a resident account as soon as possible.

## What You Need to Open an Italian Bank Account

### For a Resident Account (standard):
- [ ] Valid US passport (original + photocopy)
- [ ] **Codice Fiscale** — absolutely required (see our guide)
- [ ] **Proof of Italian address** — Anagrafe registration certificate (certificato di residenza) or registered lease agreement
- [ ] Permesso di Soggiorno or ricevuta
- [ ] Proof of income (bank statements, pension letter, employment contract) — some banks require this, others don't

### For a Non-Resident Account:
Same documents as above, plus:
- US address documentation (utility bill or bank statement from home)
- Sometimes additional financial documentation (banks do extra AML due diligence)

## Your Banking Options: A Realistic Assessment

### Traditional Italian Banks

**Intesa Sanpaolo**
Italy's largest banking group. Most branches, deepest integration with Italian bureaucracy — you can set up SPID digital identity through them, pay F24 tax forms, access all government payment systems.

- **Best for:** Long-term residents, property buyers, anyone who needs Italian mortgage
- **Downsides:** Branch bureaucracy, pushy cross-selling of insurance products, limited English outside major cities
- **Monthly fees:** €2–8/month depending on account type
- **English support:** Available in major city branches

**UniCredit**
Italy's second-largest bank. Similar to Intesa Sanpaolo in scale and services.

- **Best for:** Same profile as Intesa — large, reliable, established
- **Downsides:** Same issues — bureaucratic, variable English support
- **Monthly fees:** €2–10/month

**FinecoBank**
The best of both worlds — technically a traditional Italian bank but with a completely digital-first approach. Low fees, excellent app, investment platform, Italian IBAN.

- **Best for:** Digital nomads, remote workers, anyone who wants Italian banking with modern UX
- **Downsides:** Requires Italian residency; account opening needs a financial advisor appointment (not fully self-service)
- **Monthly fees:** €0–3.95/month (conditions apply)
- **Italian IBAN:** Yes ✅

**Banca Monte dei Paschi di Siena (MPS)**
One of the oldest banks in the world (founded 1472). Large branch network, traditional service.

- **Monthly fees:** €2–6/month
- Less technologically forward but reliable

### Digital Banks (Neobanks)

**N26 (German)**
Hugely popular with expats across Europe. Excellent English-language app, instant notifications, free ATM withdrawals.

- **Best for:** Everyday spending, easy setup
- **Critical limitation:** Provides a **German IBAN** (DE...), not Italian. Some Italian landlords, utilities, or employers may not accept non-Italian IBANs.
- **Monthly fees:** €0 (basic) to €16.90/month (premium)
- **Italian IBAN:** No ❌

**Revolut (Lithuanian)**
Multi-currency accounts, excellent exchange rates, good budgeting tools. Extremely popular with expats globally.

- **Best for:** Managing USD/EUR exchange, international transfers, travel
- **Critical limitation:** Provides a **Lithuanian IBAN** (LT...), not Italian
- **Monthly fees:** €0 (basic) to €13.99/month (premium)
- **Italian IBAN:** No ❌

**Wise (formerly TransferWise)**
Not a bank but a licensed electronic money institution. Provides IBANs in multiple currencies including EUR (Belgian IBAN).

- **Best for:** Sending money US → Italy or Italy → US, holding multiple currencies, lowest exchange rate fees available
- **Monthly fees:** One-time card fee €6–9
- **Italian IBAN:** No — but widely accepted Belgian EUR IBAN ❌ (mostly)

## The Smart Strategy: Two Accounts

Most experienced expats in Italy use a **two-account system**:

### Account 1: Italian Bank (Intesa Sanpaolo, UniCredit, or Fineco)
Purpose: Italian IBAN for rent, utilities, F24 tax payments, salary, SSN contributions, SEPA direct debits, anything requiring an Italian bank

### Account 2: Revolut or Wise
Purpose: Receiving money from the US, converting USD to EUR at the best rates, online purchases, travel

> **Why this works:** Italian landlords and government systems often insist on Italian IBAN (IT...) for payments. But traditional Italian banks have terrible exchange rates and fees for international transfers. Keep your Italian account for Italy-specific payments, and use Wise/Revolut for all USD→EUR conversions and international transfers. You'll save hundreds of euros per year on exchange fees.

## Step-by-Step: Opening an Account at a Traditional Italian Bank

### Step 1: Gather Documents
See the checklist above. Have originals and photocopies ready.

### Step 2: Choose Your Bank and Book an Appointment
For Intesa Sanpaolo or UniCredit: book an appointment online or call your local branch. Ask specifically about opening a *conto corrente* for a foreign resident (or non-resident if applicable).

In major cities (Turin, Milan, Rome, Florence), look for branches that have English-speaking staff — this information is sometimes on the bank's website or you can call ahead.

### Step 3: Attend the Appointment
The appointment typically takes **45–90 minutes** — Italian banking bureaucracy is thorough. You'll fill out various forms, agree to terms and conditions (often in Italian — ask for English versions where available), and the staff will verify your documents.

Be prepared for **cross-selling**: Italian banks will try to sell you insurance products, investment accounts, and credit cards. You're not obligated. "No grazie" is sufficient.

### Step 4: Account Activation
The account may be activated immediately or take a few days. Your debit card typically arrives by mail within 5–10 business days.

### Italian Banking Fees to Know
- **Imposta di bollo** (stamp duty): legally required €34.20/year per account (charged proportionally quarterly) — this is mandatory, not negotiable
- **Account management fees:** €0–10/month depending on bank and account type
- **Card fees:** €0–30/year depending on card type
- **ATM withdrawal fees** (other banks): €1–3 per withdrawal

## Opening an Account Before You Arrive in Italy

This is possible but limited. Your options:

**Option A: Open a Wise or Revolut account now (US)**
Both can be opened from the US with a US address. Once you move to Italy, update your address. Use these as bridge accounts to receive money from the US and start paying Italian expenses until you open an Italian bank account.

**Option B: Non-resident account at Fineco**
Fineco can be opened as a non-resident with an Italian codice fiscale (which you can sometimes get at the Italian consulate in the US). Requires working with a Fineco financial advisor.

**Option C: Wait until you arrive**
The simplest option for most people. Within 2–4 weeks of arrival (after getting your Anagrafe registration), you can open a full resident account at any Italian bank.

## A Note on American Banking in Italy: FATCA Complications

American citizens face an extra layer of difficulty that EU citizens don't: **FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act)**.

Under FATCA, Italian banks must report information about accounts held by US persons to the Italian tax authority, which shares it with the IRS. Some Italian banks, particularly smaller regional banks, avoid opening accounts for American citizens to minimize compliance burden.

**Practical implication:** Stick to large banks (Intesa Sanpaolo, UniCredit, Fineco) — they have established FATCA compliance procedures and are accustomed to American clients. Avoid smaller local banks or cooperative banks (banche di credito cooperativo) — they may simply refuse.

At the account opening appointment, you'll be asked to complete **IRS Form W-8BEN or W-9** (depending on your status) to certify your US tax status. This is standard.

## Transferring Money from US to Italy: Best Options

**Wise** — consistently the best rates and lowest fees for large transfers (e.g., moving savings to Italy)
- Typically 0.4–0.7% above mid-market rate
- Fully online, fast (1–2 business days)

**Revolut** — good for regular smaller transfers
- Excellent rates up to monthly limits on free plans
- Good for recurring transfers up to €1,000–3,000/month

**Your US bank wire transfer** — avoid for anything large
- Typically 2–4% above mid-market rate + $25–45 wire fee
- Use only for emergencies or when no alternative is available

> **Example savings:** Transferring $10,000 USD to Italy via Wise costs approximately $45–70. Via a typical US bank wire transfer: $200–400. For Americans moving significant assets to Italy, Wise pays for itself immediately.

## FAQ

**Do I need an Italian bank account to rent an apartment?**
In practice, yes — most landlords require rent payment via Italian bank transfer (SEPA/IT IBAN). Some may accept Wise/Revolut with a EUR IBAN, but many specifically request an Italian IBAN.

**Can I open a bank account in Italy without an Italian address?**
As a non-resident: some banks offer limited non-resident accounts, but options are restricted. The easiest path is to get your Anagrafe registration first and then open a resident account.

**How long does it take to open an Italian bank account?**
Account opening appointment: 45–90 minutes. Account activation: same day to 3 business days. Debit card arrival: 5–10 business days by mail.

**Can I keep my US bank accounts when I move to Italy?**
Yes — and you should. Keep at least one US bank account (ideally with a bank that has low or no international transaction fees, such as Schwab or Charles Fidelity). You'll need it for US-based income, US tax payments, and as a financial backup. Just remember to report all foreign bank accounts on your annual FBAR if aggregate balances exceed $10,000.

**Which Italian bank is best for English-speaking service?**
In major cities: Intesa Sanpaolo and UniCredit branches in central areas often have English-speaking staff. FinecoBank's digital platform has English-language support. For fully English digital banking: N26 or Revolut — but remember the non-Italian IBAN limitation.

**What is SPID and do I need it?**
SPID (Sistema Pubblico di Identità Digitale) is Italy's national digital identity system — your login for all Italian government services online (tax filings, healthcare bookings, pension checks, etc.). You can create your SPID through major banks including Intesa Sanpaolo. Get it as soon as you have an Italian bank account — it unlocks a huge amount of online government functionality.
