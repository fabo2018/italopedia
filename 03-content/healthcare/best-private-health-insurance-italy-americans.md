# Best Private Health Insurance for Americans in Italy (2025): Cigna vs AXA vs Allianz

Every American moving to Italy needs international health insurance — first as a requirement for your visa application, then as practical protection before you enroll in Italy's national health service (SSN), and potentially as a supplement to the SSN indefinitely.

This guide covers the best providers, what you actually get at different price points, and the strategy most experienced expats use.

---

## Why Private Health Insurance in Italy

### You Need It for Your Visa
The Elective Residency Visa and Digital Nomad Visa both require proof of health insurance valid in Italy with minimum coverage of **€30,000**. Without it, your visa application is rejected.

### You Need It While Waiting for SSN Enrollment
Getting enrolled in Italy's national health service (SSN) takes weeks to months after arrival — you need to have your Permesso di Soggiorno, register at the Anagrafe, and visit your local ASL. Private insurance covers the gap.

### Many Expats Keep It Even After SSN Enrollment
Italy's SSN is comprehensive but has limitations:
- Specialist appointment waits of 4–8 weeks for routine care
- Limited English-speaking doctors (especially outside major cities)
- No coverage when you travel outside Italy
- Quality varies by region

Many American expats keep a private plan (often a basic or mid-tier one) alongside SSN enrollment for:
- **Travel coverage** — SSN only works in Italy
- **Faster specialist access** — private patients skip SSN queues
- **English-speaking doctors** — international plans typically have English-speaking provider networks
- **Direct billing** — pay nothing upfront, insurer handles the bill

## What to Look For: Key Features Checklist

When comparing plans, prioritize these:

**Coverage must-haves:**
- [ ] Minimum €30,000 coverage (visa requirement)
- [ ] Hospital inpatient coverage
- [ ] Emergency evacuation
- [ ] Coverage valid throughout Italy (not just one region)
- [ ] Pre-existing conditions policy (check carefully)

**Nice to have:**
- [ ] Outpatient/specialist visit coverage
- [ ] Mental health coverage
- [ ] Dental and vision (usually add-on)
- [ ] US coverage option (for visits home)
- [ ] Travel coverage (for European travel)
- [ ] Direct billing (vs. reimburse yourself)

**Watch out for:**
- High deductibles that make small claims worthless
- Geographic exclusions
- Exclusions for pre-existing conditions (can be significant for older Americans)
- Plans that don't include Italy in their core coverage area
- Claims process complexity

## The Smart Strategy: Layering SSN + Private

The approach most long-term American expats in Italy use:

**Year 1 (pre-SSN enrollment):**
Full private international plan — this is your primary coverage until SSN is established.

**After SSN enrollment:**
- Keep a **basic/mid-tier private plan** for travel coverage, faster specialist access, and English-speaking doctors
- Rely on **SSN** for primary care, hospitalizations, and regular GP visits (all low/no cost)
- Total cost: SSN (~€167/month) + private (~$100–200/month) = ~€300–400/month total healthcare

This layered approach costs less than a comprehensive private plan alone while giving you the best of both systems.

## FAQ

**Does my US health insurance work in Italy?**
Almost certainly not for long-term expatriate coverage. Most US plans cover emergency care abroad to some degree, but they don't cover routine care or extended stays outside the US. Don't rely on US insurance for your Italian life.

**Does Medicare cover me in Italy?**
No. Medicare does not cover healthcare outside the United States (with very limited exceptions). This is one of the most important facts for American retirees to understand — you need separate international coverage.

**Can I use private insurance at any Italian hospital?**
At public hospitals (SSN), yes — most international plans cover public hospital care. At private clinics, direct billing depends on whether the clinic is in your insurer's network. Verify in-network providers in your area before choosing a plan.

**What's the minimum coverage needed for the Elective Residency Visa?**
The Italian government requires minimum coverage of **€30,000** valid throughout Italy for the duration of your stay. Most international plans easily exceed this, but verify the exact coverage amount in the policy documents you submit with your visa application.

**Should I use a broker or buy direct?**
Using an independent broker (Pacific Prime, International Citizens Insurance, etc.) gives you access to multiple insurers and unbiased comparison. They're paid by the insurer, not by you, so there's no direct cost to using them. For most expats, a broker makes the comparison process easier.
