# Tessera Sanitaria: How to Get Italy's Health Card as an American (2025)

The **Tessera Sanitaria** is Italy's national health insurance card — the plastic card that gives you access to Italy's public healthcare system (SSN — *Servizio Sanitario Nazionale*). Without it, you pay full private rates for every doctor visit, prescription, and hospital stay.

For Americans used to the US healthcare system, Italy's SSN is a revelation: GP visits are free, specialist visits cost €15–36 in co-pays, prescriptions are heavily subsidized, and hospital care is essentially free. The system isn't perfect — waiting times for specialists can be long — but the value is extraordinary compared to US healthcare costs.

This guide explains exactly how Americans get the Tessera Sanitaria, including the **2025 updated costs** that most online guides have not yet reflected.

---

## What Is the Tessera Sanitaria?

The Tessera Sanitaria is a plastic card — similar in size to a credit card — issued by Italy's national health authority through the *Sistema Tessera Sanitaria*. It contains your personal data and codice fiscale and serves as proof of your enrollment in Italy's national health service.

**What it gives you access to:**
- Free GP (general practitioner / medico di base) visits
- Subsidized specialist visits (co-pay of €15–36)
- Subsidized prescription medications (co-pay of €0–8 typically)
- Public hospital care
- Emergency treatment
- Maternity and preventive care
- Subsidized diagnostic tests (blood work, X-rays, etc.)

Think of it as the Italian equivalent of having health insurance — except instead of premiums, you pay annual enrollment fees and small co-pays per service.

## Before You Can Get the Tessera Sanitaria: Prerequisites

You need three things in place before you can enroll in the SSN and get your Tessera Sanitaria:

1. **Codice Fiscale** — your Italian tax ID number (see our guide)
2. **Permesso di Soggiorno** (or at minimum, your postal ricevuta proving you've applied)
3. **Registered address** — either your Anagrafe registration confirmation or a signed registered lease

Without these three, the ASL cannot process your enrollment.

## Mandatory vs. Voluntary Enrollment: Which Are You?

This is the critical distinction that determines how much (if anything) you pay for SSN access.

### Mandatory Enrollment (Free)
Some non-EU residents are entitled to **free** SSN registration. This applies to:
- Work visa holders (employed or self-employed)
- Family reunification permit holders
- Asylum seekers and international protection holders
- Certain special categories

### Voluntary Enrollment (Annual Fee)
Americans on **Elective Residency Visas** and most other non-work visa categories fall under **voluntary enrollment**. You have the right to join the SSN, but you pay an annual contribution.

**2025 costs — important update:**
Italy's 2024 Budget Law (Law 213/2023) dramatically increased voluntary SSN fees effective January 2024. Many online guides still cite old figures. Current 2025 rates:

| Annual Income | Annual SSN Contribution |
|---------------|------------------------|
| Up to €31,925 | **€2,000 flat** |
| €31,926–€70,000 | €2,000 + 4% on income above €31,925 |
| Maximum contribution | **€2,788.87/year** |

> **Historical context:** Before 2024, the voluntary fee was approximately €387/year for general residents and €149.77/year for students. The 2024 increase was substantial. If you read a guide citing these old figures, the information is outdated.

**Students** (study visa holders): **€700/year** (separate reduced rate)

**Dependents:** Fiscally dependent family members can be included in your SSN enrollment at no additional cost — simply declare them at the ASL with a family self-declaration.

### Should You Enroll in the SSN or Keep Private Insurance?

This is a genuine decision with real trade-offs:

**Enroll in SSN if:**
- You plan to stay long-term (1+ years)
- You have chronic conditions requiring ongoing specialist care
- You want comprehensive coverage including hospital, surgery, maternity
- The €2,000/year cost is less than equivalent private coverage

**Keep private insurance if:**
- You're staying less than 1 year
- You prioritize fast specialist access (public waits can be 4–8 weeks)
- You travel frequently within Europe (SSN doesn't cover you abroad)
- You want English-speaking doctors (more common in private sector)

**Many expats do both** — enroll in SSN for comprehensive base coverage AND keep a smaller international private policy for travel coverage and faster specialist access.

## Step-by-Step: How to Get Your Tessera Sanitaria

### Step 1: Get Your Codice Fiscale
Required first. See our guide on How to Get a Codice Fiscale in Italy.

### Step 2: Apply for Your Permesso di Soggiorno
The ASL can accept your **postal ricevuta** (the receipt from the Kit Giallo post office submission). You don't need the physical permit card.

### Step 3: Confirm Your Registered Address
Either:
- Your Anagrafe registration confirmation (certificato di residenza)
- Your signed, registered lease agreement

Your Tessera Sanitaria will be mailed to this address — make sure it's accurate.

### Step 4: Pay the Annual SSN Contribution (Voluntary Enrollment Only)

For voluntary enrollment (Elective Residency Visa holders):
1. Go to any tabaccheria or bank and get the **F24 form** (or download from [agenziaentrate.gov.it](https://www.agenziaentrate.gov.it))
2. Fill in the form using **tax code 8846** for the SSN contribution
3. Pay at any bank, post office, or online via F24 portal
4. Keep the receipt — you'll need it at the ASL

For work visa holders (mandatory, free): no payment required.

### Step 5: Visit Your Local ASL Office

The **ASL** (Azienda Sanitaria Locale) is the local health authority for your municipality. In some regions it's called USL, ULSS, or ASP — different names, same function.

**Find your ASL:** Search "[your comune] ASL" or ask at your comune's Anagrafe office for the correct address.

**What to bring to the ASL:**
- [ ] Valid US passport
- [ ] Codice Fiscale
- [ ] Permesso di Soggiorno card or ricevuta
- [ ] Proof of address (Anagrafe certificate or registered lease)
- [ ] F24 payment receipt (for voluntary enrollment)
- [ ] Completed registration form (available at the ASL office)

The registration appointment is typically quick — 15–30 minutes. Some ASLs require advance appointments; others accept walk-ins. Call ahead or check their website.

### Step 6: Receive Your Temporary Certificate + Choose Your GP

At the ASL, you'll:
1. Submit your enrollment
2. Receive a **temporary paper certificate** that works immediately as proof of SSN enrollment
3. Choose your **Medico di Base** (GP / family doctor) from the ASL's list of available doctors

> **Choosing your GP is important.** This doctor becomes your primary healthcare contact for everything — referrals to specialists, prescriptions, certificates. Look for one who speaks English if possible, especially in smaller towns. Ask expat communities online for recommendations in your area.

### Step 7: Receive Your Physical Tessera Sanitaria by Mail

The physical plastic card is mailed to your registered address within **2–8 weeks** of enrollment. Some ASLs issue it immediately; others take longer. The temporary paper certificate is fully valid while you wait.

## Annual Renewal

Your SSN enrollment typically runs **January 1 to December 31** of each calendar year — regardless of when during the year you enrolled. This means if you enroll in October, you still need to renew in January.

**To renew:**
- Pay the F24 contribution again for the new year
- Bring the receipt + updated documents to the ASL
- Update your documents if your Permesso di Soggiorno has been renewed

## How Italy's Public Healthcare Actually Works

Once enrolled, here's what daily life looks like:

### Your GP (Medico di Base)
Your assigned GP is the center of your healthcare in Italy. Everything flows through them:
- **Free visits** — no co-pay for GP consultations
- **Referrals (impegnative)** — to see a specialist, you generally need a referral from your GP
- **Prescriptions** — your GP prescribes medications at subsidized rates
- **Sick notes, certificates** — for employers, insurance, etc.

GP office hours: typically mornings (8:00–12:00), some afternoons. Mix of walk-in and appointment — depends on the doctor.

### Specialist Visits
With a GP referral, you book specialist appointments through the **CUP** (Centro Unico di Prenotazione) system — by phone, online, or at the ASL.

- Co-pay: typically **€15–36** per specialist visit (some exemptions based on income or condition)
- Wait times: 2–8 weeks for routine visits; urgent cases are fast-tracked
- For faster access: some public doctors see patients privately (*intramoenia*) — public doctor, private rate, usually €80–150, faster appointments

### Emergency Care
**Emergency room (Pronto Soccorso):** Free for emergencies. Co-pay of approximately **€25** for non-urgent visits (classified as "white code" / codice bianco).

Dial **118** for ambulance in Italy.

### Prescriptions
With a GP prescription, medications are subsidized:
- **Class A medications** (essential/chronic): typically €0–8 co-pay
- **Class C medications** (non-essential): paid at full price
- Show your Tessera Sanitaria at the pharmacy

## FAQ

**Do I need the Tessera Sanitaria to see a doctor in Italy as a tourist?**
No. Tourists use their travel insurance or pay out of pocket. The Tessera Sanitaria is for residents with a valid Permesso di Soggiorno.

**Can I enroll in the SSN before my Permesso di Soggiorno arrives?**
Yes — the postal ricevuta (receipt from your Kit Giallo application) is sufficient for ASL enrollment. You don't need the physical card.

**Is €2,000/year worth it compared to keeping private insurance?**
For long-term residents, usually yes — the SSN provides comprehensive coverage including hospital care, which private insurance caps or excludes. The €2,000 is much less than equivalent private coverage. But for stays under 1 year, private insurance may be more economical.

**Does my spouse need their own SSN enrollment?**
If your spouse is fiscally dependent on you, they can be added to your enrollment at no extra cost. If they have independent income, they pay their own contribution.

**My GP doesn't speak English — what can I do?**
In smaller towns, English-speaking GPs are rare. Strategies: use Google Translate in appointments; bring a bilingual friend or Italian-speaking neighbor; look for private English-speaking GPs in your area (not SSN, but often €50–80/visit); ask in local expat Facebook groups for recommendations.

**What happens to my SSN coverage when my Permesso di Soggiorno expires during renewal processing?**
Your coverage continues as long as you renew your SSN contribution and have your Permesso renewal receipt. The ricevuta from your renewal application is sufficient documentation for the ASL.
