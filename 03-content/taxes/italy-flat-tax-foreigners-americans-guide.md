# Italy Flat Tax for Foreigners: The Complete Guide for Americans (2025–2026)

Italy has built one of the most aggressive tax incentive stacks in Europe for foreign residents — and Americans who understand the options can dramatically reduce their tax burden on foreign-sourced income.

But the landscape changed significantly in 2024, 2025, and again in April 2026. If you've read an article about "Italy's €100,000 flat tax" that was written before 2025, you're working with outdated numbers.

This guide covers all three Italian flat tax regimes that apply to Americans, with the latest numbers, the updated 2026 rules, and the critical US tax considerations that most expat guides ignore.

---

## The Three Flat Tax Regimes: Quick Overview

Italy doesn't have one flat tax — it has three distinct regimes, each targeting a different profile:

| Regime | Who It's For | Annual Tax | Duration |
|--------|-------------|-----------|----------|
| **HNWI Lump Sum** (neo-domiciliati) | High net worth individuals | €300,000 flat | Up to 15 years |
| **7% Retiree Regime** (Art. 24-ter TUIR) | Foreign retirees in Southern Italy | 7% on all foreign income | Up to 10 years |
| **Regime Forfettario** | Freelancers/self-employed | 5–15% on Italian income | Ongoing |

Most Americans will be interested in the **HNWI regime** or the **7% retiree regime**. This guide covers both in depth, plus the US tax implications that apply to all of them.

---

## Regime 1: The HNWI Lump Sum Tax (Neo-Domiciliati)

### What It Is

Formally known as the *regime forfettario per neo-residenti* (Art. 24-bis TUIR), this regime allows high net worth individuals who move their tax residence to Italy to pay a **single annual lump-sum tax** on all foreign-sourced income — regardless of how much that income actually is.

Whether you earn €500,000 or €50 million outside Italy, you pay one flat annual amount. No forms. No calculations. Just one payment.

### The Numbers — Critical 2024–2026 Updates

This regime has been raised twice in two years:

| When You Opted In | Annual Flat Tax |
|-------------------|----------------|
| Before August 2024 (original regime) | **€100,000/year** |
| From August 10, 2024 (Law Decree No. 113/2024) | **€200,000/year** |
| From January 1, 2026 (2026 Budget Law) | **€300,000/year** |

**Grandfathering:** If you opted in under the old rules, you keep your original rate. Those who opted in before August 2024 continue paying €100,000/year for the rest of their 15-year window. Those who opted in from August 2024 through December 2025 pay €200,000/year.

**Family members:** Can be added to the regime at an additional **€25,000 per person per year** (as of 2025 — this may change).

### Who Qualifies

To be eligible you must:
- Transfer your **tax residence to Italy** (spend 183+ days/year in Italy)
- Have **not been an Italian tax resident** for at least **9 of the previous 10 tax years**
- Have sufficient assets to make the regime economically worthwhile (it's primarily designed for HNWIs with €1M+ in liquid assets)

### What It Covers

The lump sum tax **replaces** Italian income tax, wealth taxes (IVIE and IVAFE), and inheritance/gift taxes on **foreign-sourced income and assets** for the duration of the regime.

Italian-sourced income (rent from Italian properties, Italian business income, etc.) is still taxed under ordinary Italian rates.

### Is It Worth It for Americans?

Here's the honest answer: **it depends on your income level, and the US complicates everything.**

The US taxes its citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live. Italy's flat tax regime does not reduce your US tax obligations. You still file a US return every year, still report FBAR and FATCA, still owe the IRS.

What the Italian flat tax does is cap your Italian tax bill. The Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) mechanism may allow you to offset Italian taxes paid against US taxes owed — but the interaction between the flat tax regime and US FTC rules is genuinely complex. A cross-border tax advisor (both a US CPA and an Italian *commercialista*) is not optional here.

## Regime 2: The 7% Flat Tax for Foreign Retirees — The Hidden Gem

This is the regime that most Americans don't know about — and it's arguably more valuable for retirees than the €300,000 HNWI regime.

### What It Is

Under **Article 24-ter of Italy's TUIR** (income tax code), foreign retirees who move to specific small municipalities in Southern Italy (and some Central Italy earthquake zones) can pay a **flat 7% tax on all foreign-sourced income** for up to **10 years**.

7% on your Social Security, pension, IRA withdrawals, dividends, rental income from US properties. Everything foreign-sourced, taxed at a flat 7% instead of Italy's standard progressive rates (23%–43%).

### The 2026 Expansion — Major Update

As of **April 7, 2026**, Law No. 34/2026 (Art. 26) expanded the eligible municipalities by raising the population threshold from **20,000 to 30,000 inhabitants**. This unlocked **74 new municipalities** across Southern Italy — including mid-sized towns with better infrastructure, hospitals, and international communities.

Newly eligible destinations include:
- **Puglia:** Ostuni, Conversano, Manduria, San Giovanni Rotondo
- **Sicily:** Noto, Erice, Scicli, Milazzo
- **Campania:** Pompei, Gragnano, Vico Equense; several towns in the Naples metro area
- **Sardinia:** Selargius, Assemini, Sestu (Cagliari area)
- **Abruzzo:** Several coastal towns

### Eligible Regions

The 7% regime applies to municipalities under 30,000 inhabitants in:
- Abruzzo
- Basilicata
- Calabria
- Campania
- Molise
- Puglia
- Sardinia
- Sicily

Plus certain municipalities in Central Italy affected by the 2009 and 2016–2017 earthquakes (Abruzzo, Lazio, Marche, Umbria earthquake zones), also under 30,000 inhabitants.

### Who Qualifies

- You must be **receiving pension income** (Social Security, private pension, IRA/401k distributions — confirmed to qualify)
- You must **not have been an Italian tax resident** for at least **5 of the previous tax years** (lower bar than the HNWI regime)
- You must establish residence in an **eligible municipality** (under 30,000 inhabitants in the regions above)
- Any nationality can apply — this is not limited to EU citizens

### What Income It Covers

The 7% applies to all **foreign-sourced income**, including:
- US Social Security
- Private pensions and annuities
- IRA and 401(k) distributions
- Dividends and investment returns
- Rental income from US properties
- Capital gains (foreign-sourced)
- Self-employment income (foreign-sourced)

Italian-sourced income is taxed under ordinary Italian rates.

### The Math: Why This Matters

Standard Italian income tax rates: 23%–43% (plus regional and municipal surcharges of 1%–4%).

Under the 7% regime, a retiree with $60,000/year in Social Security + pension income pays approximately **€4,200/year** in Italian income tax instead of €15,000–25,000+ under standard rates.

Over 10 years, that's a potential saving of €100,000–200,000 in Italian taxes — plus significant simplification of your annual tax filing.

### How to Apply

1. Move to an eligible municipality in one of the qualifying regions
2. Register at the local Anagrafe and establish Italian tax residency
3. File your first Italian tax return (*Modello Redditi*) and check the box to opt into the 7% regime (the election is made in your first year of Italian tax residency)
4. Pay the annual substitute tax — calculated at 7% of your foreign income

**Important:** The election must be made in your first Italian tax year. You cannot retroactively apply it. Get advice from an Italian *commercialista* before your first Italian tax year ends.

## The Critical US Tax Layer: What Most Guides Miss

This section is essential reading for Americans. Italy's flat tax regimes are excellent — but they don't replace your US tax obligations.

### You Still File a US Return Every Year

The US taxes citizens on worldwide income, full stop. Living in Italy on a flat tax regime does not exempt you from filing your annual IRS return or paying US taxes.

### The Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) — Your Main Tool

The Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116) allows you to offset taxes paid to Italy against your US tax liability. For most Americans, Italian taxes are **higher** than equivalent US taxes on the same income — meaning the FTC generates excess credits that can carry forward.

Under the 7% regime: you pay 7% to Italy on foreign income. Your US marginal rate on that same income might be 12%–22%. The FTC offsets the Italian tax paid, leaving you owing the difference (if any) to the US. For many retirees, the result is owing very little to the US after the FTC.

### The US–Italy Tax Treaty

The 1984 tax treaty (updated 1999) between the US and Italy provides framework for preventing double taxation. **Critical caveat:** the treaty contains a "saving clause" that allows the US to tax its citizens as if the treaty didn't exist. This limits the treaty's practical benefit for Americans and makes professional advice essential.

### FBAR and FATCA — Non-Negotiable

Regardless of which Italian tax regime you use, you must:

- **FBAR (FinCEN Form 114):** Report all Italian bank accounts if aggregate foreign account balances exceed **$10,000** at any time during the year. Filed by October 15.
- **FATCA (Form 8938):** Report foreign financial assets if they exceed **$200,000** for single filers living abroad (higher thresholds apply). Filed with your tax return.
- **Quadro RW:** Italy's own foreign asset declaration, required for Italian tax residents with foreign assets. This is the Italian equivalent of FBAR — you file both independently.

Italy signed a FATCA Model 1 IGA with the US in 2015. Italian banks report US person data to the Agenzia delle Entrate, which shares with the IRS. The IRS knows about your Italian accounts.

### The Bottom Line for Americans

Italy's flat tax regimes are genuinely attractive — but they require dual-system planning. Before moving:

1. Hire a **US CPA with international/expat specialization** (not just any CPA — they must know FBAR, FATCA, and the US–Italy treaty)
2. Hire an **Italian commercialista** familiar with foreign residents and the flat tax regimes
3. Ideally, have them talk to each other

The one-time cost of proper setup ($2,000–5,000) is trivial compared to the annual tax savings and the cost of getting it wrong.

## Regime 3: Regime Forfettario (for Freelancers)

A brief overview for completeness. The *Regime Forfettario* is a simplified flat-rate tax system for freelancers and self-employed workers earning Italian income under **€85,000/year**.

Tax rates: **5%** for the first 5 years of a new business, **15%** thereafter (on a deemed income basis — not gross revenue).

This is separate from the HNWI and retiree regimes and applies to Italian-sourced income from freelance or self-employment activity. Not relevant for retirees or passive income earners. Useful for digital nomads and independent contractors working Italian clients — though requires a *Partita IVA* (Italian VAT number).

## FAQ

**Is Italy's flat tax available to all Americans or only wealthy ones?**
The €300,000 HNWI regime is designed for ultra-high-net-worth individuals — paying €300k/year only makes sense if your foreign income is several million euros. The **7% retiree regime** is accessible to any American retiree receiving a foreign pension, with no minimum income requirement. This is the genuinely democratic option.

**Does Italy's flat tax eliminate my US tax obligations?**
No. The US taxes citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live. Italy's flat tax regimes reduce or simplify your Italian tax bill — they do not affect your US filing obligations. FBAR, FATCA, and your annual IRS return are still required.

**Can I use the 7% regime if I live in Tuscany or Piedmont?**
No. The 7% regime is geographically restricted to eligible municipalities in Southern Italy and certain Central Italy earthquake zones. Tuscany, Piedmont, Lombardy, and most of Northern and Central Italy do not qualify.

**What happens after the 7% regime ends (after 10 years)?**
You become subject to standard Italian progressive income tax rates (23%–43%). Many expats plan to either return to the US, apply for Italian citizenship (potentially changing their tax status), or restructure their income sources before the 10-year window closes.

**Does the 7% flat tax apply to US Social Security income?**
Yes. US Social Security income qualifies as foreign pension income under Italian rules and is covered by the 7% regime. This is confirmed by Italian tax authority guidance.

**I heard the flat tax was raised — can I still get the old €100,000 rate?**
No, if you're a new applicant. The €100,000 rate ended for new applications in August 2024. Current new applicants pay €300,000/year (from January 2026). Existing participants are grandfathered at their entry-year rate.
