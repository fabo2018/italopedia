# How to Buy Property in Italy as an American: Complete Guide (2025)

Americans can buy property in Italy freely — the US-Italy reciprocity agreement puts American buyers on exactly the same legal footing as Italian citizens. No restrictions, no special permits, no minimum investment requirements.

What IS complicated is the process. Italian real estate transactions involve a notary (who is a public official, not your lawyer), mandatory due diligence that many buyers skip, taxes that can add 10–15% to your purchase price, and a bureaucratic timeline that typically runs 2–6 months.

This guide covers everything: who's involved, what the process looks like step by step, what you'll actually pay, and how to protect yourself.

---

## Can Americans Buy Property in Italy?

Yes — completely freely. The US and Italy have a reciprocity agreement that grants American citizens the same property rights as Italian nationals. This means:

- No foreign buyer restrictions
- No special permits required
- Eligible for "prima casa" (first home) tax benefits if you establish Italian residency
- Can apply for Italian mortgages (with some conditions)
- Can inherit Italian property

The notary is legally required to verify reciprocity before finalizing any purchase by a foreign national. For Americans, this is a formality — reciprocity is confirmed immediately.

## The Five People Involved in an Italian Property Purchase

Understanding who does what prevents costly misunderstandings.

### 1. The Notaio (Notary) — NOT Your Lawyer
The Italian notary is a public official appointed by the state. They:
- Verify the identities of buyer and seller
- Conduct legal title searches (checking for mortgages, liens, and encumbrances)
- Draft and execute the final deed of sale (rogito)
- Collect and pay transfer taxes to the government
- Register the new ownership in the Land Registry

**Critical warning:** The notary represents the state and the transaction — not you personally. They verify legality, not your interests. If there are urban planning violations or undisclosed issues with the property, you may not find out until after closing.

### 2. Your Lawyer (Avvocato) — Essential
Hire a real estate lawyer who specializes in Italian property and speaks English. They do what the notary doesn't: investigate building permits, planning compliance, condominium disputes, hidden debts, unauthorized renovations, and protect your specific interests. For any transaction over €100,000, this is not optional.

### 3. Geometra (Surveyor)
Technical expert who verifies that the property matches its cadastral description, checks for unauthorized building works, and assesses structural condition. Costs €500–2,000. Essential for older properties.

### 4. Real Estate Agent (Agente Immobiliare)
Italian agents typically represent both buyer and seller and collect commission from both. Standard commission: 3–5% of purchase price, split between buyer and seller (so you pay 1.5–3%). Always confirm the commission structure upfront.

### 5. Your Bank (if using a mortgage)
Italian banks lend up to 50–60% LTV for non-residents, 80% for residents. More on mortgages below.

## Step-by-Step: The Italian Property Purchase Process

### Step 1: Property Search and Offer
Find your property through agents (Immobiliare.it and Idealista.it are the main portals). When you find something you want, you submit a written offer (*proposta di acquisto*) — this is legally binding once accepted.

**Before making an offer:**
- Have your codice fiscale (required for all property transactions)
- Have proof of funds or mortgage pre-approval
- Confirm your budget includes 10–15% on top of purchase price for taxes and fees

### Step 2: Preliminary Contract (Compromesso/Contratto Preliminare)
Once your offer is accepted, you sign the *compromesso* — the preliminary purchase contract. At this point you pay a deposit, typically **10–30% of the purchase price**.

**Two types of deposit:**
- **Caparra confirmatoria:** If you back out, you lose the deposit. If the seller backs out, they return double your deposit. Most common.
- **Acconto:** Simple advance payment. Less protection for the buyer.

The *compromesso* also sets the closing date (typically 1–3 months after signing) and lists all conditions of sale.

> **Important:** Have your lawyer review the *compromesso* before signing. This is where most problems are caught or missed.

### Step 3: Due Diligence (Critical)
Between the *compromesso* and the final *rogito*, your lawyer and geometra conduct due diligence:

**Legal checks:**
- Title search (visura catastale + ispezione ipotecaria) — verifying ownership and any mortgages or liens
- Urban planning compliance — verifying building permits are in order
- Condominium checks — any unpaid condominium fees transfer to the new owner
- Utility regularity

**Technical checks:**
- Energy Performance Certificate (APE) — mandatory for all sales
- Structural survey
- Verification that the property matches its cadastral plans

**2025 regulatory note:** Electronic deeds (rogito telematico) are now mandatory for all transactions following notarial digitalization requirements.

> **Fabrizio's note:** Urban planning compliance is the single most common issue in Italian property purchases, particularly for older properties and rural homes. Unauthorized additions or modifications are extremely common in Italy. If not resolved before closing, these become your problem as the new owner.

### Step 4: Final Deed (Rogito) and Closing
The *rogito* is the final notarial deed of sale, signed in front of the notary. At this meeting:
- You pay the remaining purchase balance (via bank transfer or certified check)
- You pay all taxes and fees
- The notary reads the entire deed aloud (this can take 2–3 hours)
- Ownership transfers to you
- The notary registers the transfer in the Land Registry

## Complete Cost Breakdown: What You Actually Pay

This is where Americans are most surprised. Budget 10–15% on top of the purchase price for taxes and fees.

### Purchase Taxes

**Buying from a private seller:**
| Scenario | Registration Tax | Fixed Taxes | Approx. Total |
|----------|-----------------|-------------|---------------|
| Prima casa (first home, Italian resident) | 2% of cadastral value | €50 + €50 | ~2% |
| Seconda casa (second home / non-resident) | 9% of cadastral value | €50 + €50 | ~9–10% |

**Buying from a developer/company (VAT applies instead):**
| Scenario | VAT | Fixed Registration | Approx. Total |
|----------|-----|-------------------|---------------|
| Prima casa | 4% | €200 | ~4–5% |
| Seconda casa | 10% | €200 | ~10–11% |

> **Key point:** Taxes are calculated on the *cadastral value* (valore catastale), not the actual purchase price. Cadastral values in Italy are often significantly lower than market values — which can significantly reduce your tax bill.

### Professional Fees

| Service | Typical Cost |
|---------|-------------|

| Lawyer (avvocato) | €1,500–5,000 depending on complexity |
| Geometra (surveyor) | €500–2,000 |
| **Total additional costs** | **~4–8% of purchase price** |

## Italian Mortgages for Americans

Can Americans get an Italian mortgage? Yes, but with significant limitations compared to Italian residents.

**For non-residents:**
- Maximum LTV: 50–60% (you need at least 40–50% as a down payment)
- Income verification: Italian banks want to see stable, documentable income
- Process: Slower and more documentation-heavy than for residents

**For Italian residents (Americans who have moved to Italy):**
- Maximum LTV: up to 80%
- Access to "prima casa" benefits
- Better rates

**Best Italian banks for American mortgage applicants:** Intesa Sanpaolo and UniCredit have the most experience with international buyers. Avoid smaller regional banks.

**Practical alternative:** Many Americans buying Italian property as second homes find it easier to use equity from US property (HELOC, cash-out refinance) rather than navigating Italian mortgage approval.

## Property Taxes After Purchase

Once you own Italian property, these are the ongoing taxes:

**IMU (Imposta Municipale Unica):**
Property tax paid to the local municipality. Rates: 0.86%–1.06% of cadastral value annually.
- **Prima casa (main residence):** Exempt from IMU
- **Seconda casa:** IMU applies — typically €500–3,000/year depending on property value and location

**TARI:**
Waste collection tax. Typically €200–600/year. Applied to actual residents.

**Rental income tax (if you rent the property):**
Option A: Standard IRPEF (Italian income tax, 23%–43%)
Option B: *Cedolare secca* flat tax — 21% for long-term rentals, 26% for short-term (tourist rentals via Airbnb etc.)

**Capital gains tax on sale:**
26% on profit if sold within 5 years of purchase. Exempt if held more than 5 years, OR if it was your primary residence for most of the ownership period.

## Common Mistakes Americans Make

**1. Skipping the independent lawyer**
Relying only on the notary leaves you without anyone actually protecting your interests. Not optional.

**2. Signing the compromesso before due diligence**
Many buyers rush to sign the preliminary contract to "secure" a property. Your lawyer should review everything before you sign and pay the deposit.

**3. Ignoring urban planning compliance**
"The previous owners did that extension 20 years ago, so it's fine." It's not — unauthorized building works must be regularized (sanatoria) before or as a condition of sale, or they transfer to you.

**4. Underestimating total costs**
Budgeting only the asking price without factoring in 10–15% in taxes and fees leads to nasty surprises at closing.

**5. Using the wrong money transfer method**
Transferring €200,000+ through your US bank's wire service can cost €4,000–8,000 in exchange rate losses. Use Wise for large transfers.

**6. Not checking condominium arrears**
Unpaid condominium fees from the previous owner transfer to the new owner in Italy. Your lawyer must verify there are no outstanding debts.

## FAQ

**Do Americans need Italian residency to buy property in Italy?**
No. Non-residents can buy freely. However, Italian residency gives you access to "prima casa" tax benefits (2% vs 9% registration tax) if the property becomes your primary residence, and better mortgage terms.

**How long does the buying process take?**
Typically 2–6 months from accepted offer to closing. Longer if the property needs urban planning regularization, if financing is involved, or if the title search reveals complications.

**Can I buy Italian property remotely without visiting?**
Technically yes — you can grant a power of attorney (procura speciale) to your lawyer to sign on your behalf. But visiting the property in person before committing to purchase is strongly advisable.

**Is buying property in Italy a path to residency?**
Not directly. Owning property in Italy doesn't grant residency rights. You still need the appropriate visa (Elective Residency, Digital Nomad, etc.) to live in Italy. However, owning property satisfies the "proof of accommodation" requirement for your visa application.

**What is the "1 euro house" program?**
Several Italian municipalities sell abandoned properties for €1 to attract buyers who commit to renovating them. See our dedicated guide on Italy's 1 euro house program.
