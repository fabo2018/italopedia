# Living in Rome as an American Expat: The Complete Guide (2025)

Rome is the city that rewires your relationship with time. Nowhere else do you casually eat lunch next to a 2,000-year-old aqueduct, or watch the sun set over a skyline that has barely changed in centuries. For many Americans, Rome is the Italian dream made concrete.

It's also the most complex Italian city to live in. The bureaucracy is slower here than anywhere else in Italy. The traffic is genuinely chaotic. And the cost of living is among the highest in the country. But the Americans who make it work in Rome — and there are tens of thousands of them — almost universally say the trade-offs are worth it.

This guide tells you exactly what to expect.

---

## Why Americans Choose Rome

Rome draws more American expats than any other Italian city, and for good reasons:

**History and culture at impossible density.** Living here means having the Pantheon (free to visit, open daily), the Colosseum, Vatican Museums, Borghese Gallery, Trastevere, and Campo de' Fiori as your daily backdrop. You don't visit these things — they're just part of your commute.

**The largest American expat community in Italy.** Over 500,000 foreign residents in Rome, with a particularly strong American presence — drawn by the American Academy, international schools, the FAO, embassies, and the Vatican. This means English-speaking doctors, American community events, and people who understand your experience.

**Mediterranean climate.** Long hot summers, mild winters (though it can get cold in January), and 300+ days of sunshine. This matters more than you think for quality of life.

**International connectivity.** Fiumicino (FCO) is one of Europe's better-connected airports with direct routes to major US cities.

**Food.** Rome's food culture is one of the world's great cuisines — carbonara, cacio e pepe, carciofi alla romana, supplì, gelato. And the markets, particularly Campo de' Fiori and Testaccio, are extraordinary.

---

## The Honest Downsides

**Bureaucracy at its worst.** Rome's Questura (immigration office) is notoriously backlogged. Permesso di Soggiorno appointments in Rome regularly take 4–6+ months. Municipal services move slower than in Northern Italian cities. Budget extra time for everything.

**Traffic and chaos.** Rome has 2.7 million people and infrastructure that was not designed for modern traffic. Scooters everywhere, chaotic intersections, and a bus system that runs on optimism. The metro covers only 2 lines.

**Cost.** Central Rome is significantly more expensive than most Italian cities. A central 1BR apartment runs €1,200–1,800/month. The overall monthly budget for a comfortable single person is €2,500–3,200.

**Tourist pressure.** If you live in popular neighborhoods (Trastevere, Centro Storico, near the Vatican), you're sharing your street with millions of tourists. The coffee bar queue in June is not the same as in November.

**Garbage and maintenance.** Rome has struggled with waste management and public space maintenance in recent years. It's improving but remains a genuine quality-of-life issue compared to Northern Italian cities.

## Best Neighborhoods for American Expats

### Trastevere
**Vibe:** Charming, cobblestone, lively, touristy
**Rent:** €1,300–1,800/month for 1BR
**Best for:** Young expats, digital nomads, people who love nightlife and don't mind tourists

Rome's most picturesque neighborhood — winding medieval streets, ivy-covered facades, and the best aperitivo scene in the city. The downside: extremely popular with tourists, especially in summer. Most car traffic is restricted, making it wonderfully walkable. Strong English-speaking community.

### Prati
**Vibe:** Elegant, residential, quiet, very central
**Rent:** €1,400–2,000/month for 1BR
**Best for:** Professionals, retirees, families who want central Rome without the tourist chaos

Just across the Tiber from the Vatican, Prati is one of Rome's most livable neighborhoods — elegant Liberty-style architecture, good restaurants, excellent supermarkets (including a good Conad), and a genuinely local feel. Walking distance to Vatican Museums. Preferred by American families and professionals.

### Testaccio
**Vibe:** Authentic, working-class turned gentrifying, food-obsessed
**Rent:** €1,100–1,600/month for 1BR
**Best for:** Food lovers, young professionals, people wanting authentic Roman neighborhood life

Testaccio is Rome's food neighborhood — home to the Mercato Testaccio (one of Rome's best covered markets), legendary trattorias, and the city's best supplì. Less touristy than Trastevere, more affordable, and with a strong local character. Near a metro stop (Piramide).

### Monteverde
**Vibe:** Residential, family-friendly, green, peaceful
**Rent:** €1,100–1,600/month for 1BR
**Best for:** Families, retirees, people who want space and quiet within the city

One of Rome's best-kept secrets for expat families. Monteverde Vecchio has beautiful parks (Villa Pamphilj — Rome's largest park is a 10-minute walk), good international schools nearby, local markets, and a genuinely Italian neighborhood feel with relatively few tourists. Well-connected by bus.

### Pigneto / Ostiense
**Vibe:** Alternative, artsy, younger, more affordable
**Rent:** €900–1,300/month for 1BR
**Best for:** Digital nomads, young creatives, budget-conscious expats who want an authentic Roman experience

East of the center, Pigneto has Rome's most interesting street art scene, independent restaurants, and a creative community. More affordable than western neighborhoods, increasingly popular with young expats and Italians who've been priced out of Trastevere.

### Parioli
**Vibe:** Upscale, quiet, diplomatic quarter, very residential
**Rent:** €1,500–2,500/month for 1BR
**Best for:** Families, high-budget expats, people wanting Rome's quietest central living

Rome's most expensive residential neighborhood — home to embassies, the American Academy, and Rome's wealthiest families. Less interesting culturally than other neighborhoods, but safe, quiet, and close to Villa Borghese.

## Transport in Rome

Rome's transport system is functional but requires patience.

**Metro:** Only 2 lines (A and B), intersecting at Termini station. Covers key areas but leaves large parts of the city unserved. Frequent delays.

**Buses and trams:** Extensive network but inconsistent timing. The ATAC app shows real-time arrivals. Line 8 tram (Largo Argentina to Trastevere) is reliable and useful.

**Monthly pass:** €35/month — extraordinary value covering all buses, trams, and metro.

**Cycling:** Rome is improving cycling infrastructure, but the city's hills, cobblestones, and chaotic traffic make cycling intimidating compared to Northern Italian cities. Electric bikes help.

**Walking:** The historic center is genuinely walkable if you live centrally. Many expats manage without any transport for daily life.

**Scooter:** The Roman solution to everything. A used scooter (€1,000–3,000) transforms your relationship with the city. Parking anywhere, weaving through traffic. Learning curve required — Roman traffic is aggressive by Italian standards.

## Healthcare in Rome

Rome has some of Italy's best hospitals — Policlinico Gemelli (one of Europe's top hospitals), Bambino Gesù (pediatrics), and several other major public hospitals.

**SSN enrollment:** At your local ASL (for Trastevere: ASL Roma 1). Voluntary enrollment for elective residency holders: €2,000/year. Wait times at public specialists can be 4–8 weeks for routine visits.

**Private options:** Rome has a strong private healthcare sector. Salvator Mundi International Hospital and Clinica Valle Giulia are popular with expats and have English-speaking staff. Private GP: €80–150/visit.

**English-speaking doctors:** Much more available in Rome than in smaller Italian cities — the international community has created genuine demand. The American expat community Facebook groups are your best source for specific doctor recommendations.

## Practical Realities: What Surprises Americans

**Rome runs late.** Dinner before 8pm marks you as a tourist. Aperitivo starts at 7pm. Things close for riposo in the early afternoon (less so now in tourist areas, but still common in residential neighborhoods).

**Cash is still king in some places.** More card payment now than ever, but some trattorias, markets, and small shops remain cash-only. Keep €50–100 cash on you.

**August is dead.** Many Romans flee the city in August. Restaurants close, services reduce. For expats, this is either wonderful (empty piazzas) or frustrating (everything shut).

**Pickpocketing is real.** Near Termini, on buses, in crowded tourist areas. Keep valuables in front pockets or use a money belt in tourist areas. This is tourist Rome, not neighborhood Rome — once you know your neighborhood, it's perfectly safe.

**The water is excellent.** Rome's tap water (acqua del Acea) comes from ancient springs and is among the best tap water in Europe. The city's nasoni (little noses) — public water fountains — are everywhere and drinkable.

## FAQ

**Is Rome safe for American expats?**
Generally yes. Rome has low violent crime rates. The main concerns are pickpocketing in tourist areas and around Termini station. Residential neighborhoods are very safe. Take normal city precautions and you'll be fine.

**How long does Permesso di Soggiorno take in Rome?**
Longer than most Italian cities. Expect 6–12 months from application to physical card. The ricevuta is legally valid in the meantime. Rome's Questura is among the most backlogged in Italy.

**Is English widely spoken in Rome?**
More than anywhere else in Italy outside of tourist areas. The international community, embassies, and Vatican presence have created a large English-speaking population. Daily life outside purely Italian contexts is manageable in English, though Italian remains essential for bureaucracy and local life.

**Is Rome good for families?**
Yes, particularly in neighborhoods like Monteverde, Prati, and Parioli. Good international schools, strong family culture, parks, and relative safety. The main challenge is space — apartments are smaller and more expensive than US equivalents.

**How does Rome compare to Milan for expats?**
Rome: better weather, more history, larger American community, more relaxed pace, lower quality of public services, higher tourist pressure. Milan: more efficient bureaucracy, stronger job market for European employment, more modern infrastructure, worse weather, less culture-per-square-meter. For retirees and lifestyle expats, Rome usually wins. For professionals seeking Italian employment, Milan is often better.
