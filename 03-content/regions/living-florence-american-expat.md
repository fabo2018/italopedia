# Living in Florence as an American Expat: Complete Guide (2025)

Florence is Italy's most concentrated city — cramming more Renaissance art, architecture, and food culture into 10 square kilometers than anywhere else on earth. The Uffizi, Duomo, Ponte Vecchio, Boboli Gardens, and the birthplace of the Italian language are all within walking distance. For Americans, it's also the most familiar Italian city — partly because so many have studied abroad here, partly because so many Americans have written about, painted, or fallen in love with it.

Living here is different from visiting. Magnificent but also challenging, crowded, expensive, and increasingly complex as the city navigates its relationship with mass tourism. This guide gives you the real picture.

---

## Why Florence Draws American Expats

**The art and architecture are incomparable.** Living surrounded by the greatest concentration of Renaissance art in the world — where you eat lunch near Michelangelo's David, walk daily across a bridge that Dante knew, and pop into churches containing masterpieces that would be the highlight of any US museum — changes how you see the world.

**Strong American expat infrastructure.** Florence has one of Italy's most established American communities, built around the NYU Florence campus, Syracuse in Florence, API, CAPA, and dozens of other American university programs. This means English-language services, American social networks, and expat-oriented businesses are unusually accessible.

**Scale.** Florence is Italy's 10th largest city — just 360,000 people in the historic center. It's genuinely walkable, human-scale, and manageable in a way that Rome and Milan are not. You can know your neighborhood, your butcher, your bar.

**Tuscany as a backyard.** Chianti wine country, Siena, San Gimignano, Lucca, the Cinque Terre, and the Tuscan countryside are all within 90 minutes. Florence is a base for one of the world's great road-trip regions.

---

## The Honest Challenges

**Tourism is extreme.** Florence receives 13–15 million tourists per year in a city of 360,000. In summer (June–August), the historic center is genuinely overwhelmed. Restaurants near the Duomo serve mediocre food at tourist prices. Queues form for everything. This is manageable if you live off the tourist circuit — but you need to be intentional about it.

**Cost of housing is high and rising.** Short-term rental platforms (Airbnb) have reduced available long-term housing significantly, particularly in the historic center. Central 1BR apartments rent for €1,400–1,800/month. Prices rose ~8% in 2025.

**Size means limited options.** Florence is smaller than Rome or Milan, which means fewer job opportunities for those seeking Italian employment, fewer specialized services, and a smaller dating pool for singles.

**The summer heat.** Florence in July and August is genuinely brutal — narrow medieval streets trap heat and the city can hit 38–40°C. Air conditioning in older apartments is often inadequate. Many residents leave in August.

## Best Neighborhoods for American Expats

### Oltrarno (including Santo Spirito and San Frediano)
**The neighborhood locals and expats both love most.**

**Vibe:** Artisan workshops, bohemian, authentic, lively piazza life
**Rent:** €1,300–1,800/month for 1BR
**Best for:** Anyone who wants authentic Florentine neighborhood life

Oltrarno ("beyond the Arno") is south of the river — historically the craftsmen's district, now Florence's most interesting neighborhood. Santo Spirito has the best piazza in Florence — a daily market in the morning, aperitivo on the church steps in the evening. San Frediano has artisan leather workshops alongside excellent osterie. San Niccolò offers stunning views back toward the center.

Less touristic than the north bank, more walkable, better value for the quality. The resident car permit situation is complicated (limited traffic zones) — most Oltrarno expats don't have cars.

### Centro Storico (Duomo, Santa Croce)
**Right in the middle of everything — literally.**

**Rent:** €1,500–2,200/month for 1BR
**Best for:** People who want to be at the center of it all and can afford it

Living in the historic center means waking up to the Duomo and walking to work past the Uffizi. It's extraordinary. It's also the most tourist-heavy area, the most expensive, and the most restricted (ZTL traffic zones everywhere). Best for people who prioritize location above all.

### San Marco / San Lorenzo
**Academic and student-adjacent Florence**

**Rent:** €1,200–1,600/month for 1BR
**Best for:** Students, academics, people who want central living at slightly lower cost

Home to several American university programs, the San Lorenzo market, and the Accademia (David). Less expensive than Oltrarno or Centro, with a more youthful energy. Good transport connections.

### Pitti / Boboli Area
**Quiet, prestigious, near everything**

**Rent:** €1,400–1,900/month for 1BR
**Best for:** Professionals and retirees wanting quality residential life with Oltrarno access

Just south of Palazzo Pitti and the Boboli Gardens. Quieter than Santo Spirito, more residential, good restaurants and bars. Strong expat presence. The Boboli gardens (annual pass ~€35) become your extended backyard.

### Campo di Marte / Coverciano
**Residential Florence at better prices**

**Rent:** €900–1,300/month for 1BR
**Best for:** Families, budget-conscious expats, people with cars

East of the center, more residential, less touristic. Good supermarkets, local bars, parks. Connected by bus to the historic center (20–30 minutes). Where many long-term expats end up when they prioritize space and budget over location.

Florence is more expensive than Bologna, Turin, or Naples, but cheaper than Milan or Rome. The quality-to-cost ratio is extraordinary — for €2,500/month you live very well in a city that would be a luxury travel destination for most Americans.

## The American Community in Florence

Florence may have the densest American presence per capita of any Italian city:

- **American university programs:** NYU Florence, Syracuse in Florence, SACI, API, CAPA, and many others bring thousands of American students annually — many stay
- **The Florentine:** English-language newspaper for expats — essential resource
- **British Institute of Florence:** Cultural events, language courses, community hub
- **Facebook groups:** "Foreigners in Florence," "Americans in Florence" — active communities
- **American Church in Florence:** Community anchor for many American expats

This means English-language doctors, American-familiar services, and social networks are unusually accessible for a city of this size.

## Transport in Florence

Florence's historic center is compact and mostly walkable. Beyond the center:

- **Buses:** ATAF/Busitalia network. Monthly pass €35. Reliable within the city.
- **Trams:** Line 1 connects the center to the train station (Santa Maria Novella) and beyond; Line 2 extends west. Expanding network.
- **Bikes:** Florence is flatter than Rome and increasingly cycle-friendly. Bike sharing available.
- **Cars:** Almost useless in the historic center (ZTL zones everywhere). If you live outside the center, a car is useful for exploring Tuscany. Expect to navigate the permit system.
- **Trains:** Santa Maria Novella station is central. High-speed trains to Bologna (35 min), Rome (1.5 hrs), Milan (1.75 hrs).

## Practical Florence Life

**The ZTL problem.** Florence's *Zona Traffico Limitato* (restricted traffic zone) covers most of the historic center. Driving in without a permit results in fines of €100+ per infraction. If you have a car and live centrally, getting a residential permit is essential but bureaucratic. Many central residents don't bother with a car at all.

**The grocery situation.** Florence has good supermarkets (Conad, Coop, Esselunga outside center) and extraordinary markets. The **Mercato Centrale** (San Lorenzo) is a covered market with excellent produce, meat, and cheese. **Mercato di Sant'Ambrogio** (near Santa Croce) is less touristy and beloved by locals.

**The restaurant trap.** Avoid any restaurant that has pictures of food on menus displayed outside near major tourist sites — they're tourist traps serving mediocre food at double the price. The best food in Florence is in the Oltrarno and in neighborhoods away from the main tourist circuit.

**Summer escape.** Most long-term Florence residents leave in August. The city empties of locals and fills with tourists. Plan summer travel — the Tuscan coast (Maremma, Elba), the mountains (Mugello, Garfagnana), or elsewhere in Europe.

## FAQ

**Is Florence affordable on a US retirement income?**
Comfortably, yes — if you're outside the absolute center. Social Security + a modest pension is sufficient for a good quality of life. The Elective Residency Visa requires ~€31k/year passive income, which is achievable for many American retirees.

**Is Florence good for families?**
Yes — particularly in Oltrarno, Campo di Marte, and the neighborhoods near American university campuses. International schools exist but are limited and expensive (€15,000–25,000/year). Many expat families use Italian public schools, which is excellent for language immersion.

**How does Florence compare to Rome for American expats?**
Florence: smaller, more manageable, more art-per-square-meter, stronger American academic community, better food-to-tourist-ratio in non-center areas, better base for Tuscany road trips, slightly worse weather than Rome. Rome: larger American community, better international connectivity, more job opportunities, more Mediterranean climate, more chaotic.

**Is Florence safe?**
Yes, very. Florence has low violent crime. Like Rome, pickpocketing occurs in crowded tourist areas. Residential neighborhoods are very safe. One of Italy's safer cities to live in.
