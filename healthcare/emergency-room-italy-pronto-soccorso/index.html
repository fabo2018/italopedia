Emergency Room in Italy (Pronto Soccorso): What Americans Need to Know

Italy's emergency rooms are called Pronto Soccorso. They operate on a triage color system, treat everyone regardless of insurance or residency status, and can have waiting times from 20 minutes to 6 hours depending on how serious your case is. Here is what to expect and how to navigate it.

The Italian Pronto Soccorso (PS) is the equivalent of the US emergency room — and it is, by most measures, excellent. Italian emergency medicine is well-trained, well-equipped, and universal: anyone who walks through those doors gets treated. No insurance card required, no ability-to-pay check at the door, no billing threat in the waiting room. You present with a problem; they assess it; they treat it.

What you need to know before you arrive: the triage system determines how long you wait, and non-life-threatening cases can wait a very long time. Understanding where your case falls in that system helps you set realistic expectations — and helps you decide whether the Pronto Soccorso is actually the right choice for your situation.

Emergency Numbers in Italy

Italian emergency numbers
  • 118 — Medical emergency / ambulance (equivalent of US 911 for medical)
  • 113 — Police (Polizia di Stato)
  • 112 — European universal emergency number (works for all emergencies in all EU countries)
  • 115 — Fire brigade
  • 1500 — Ministry of Health information line

For medical emergencies: call 118 or 112. Both dispatch ambulances. 112 has multilingual operators and is easier for non-Italian speakers. Save both in your phone before you need them.

The Triage Color System

Italian Pronto Soccorso uses a color-coded triage system to prioritize patients by severity. Unlike the US system (which varies by hospital), Italian triage is nationally standardized:

ColorPriorityCondition typeTarget wait
Rosso (Red)ImmediateLife-threatening: cardiac arrest, major trauma, severe respiratory failureImmediate
Arancione (Orange)UrgentSerious but stable: chest pain, stroke symptoms, severe pain15 minutes
Azzurro (Blue)Semi-urgentModerate: fever, moderate pain, laceration needing stitches60 minutes
Verde (Green)Non-urgentMinor: sprain, minor wound, non-severe pain120–240+ minutes
Bianco (White)Not urgentShould not be in PS: conditions manageable by a GP3–6+ hours (or redirected)

A triage nurse (infermiere di triage) assesses you within minutes of arrival and assigns your color code. This nurse is not making a full diagnosis — they are quickly categorizing urgency. If you disagree with your code, you can request reassessment, but code inflation (trying to get a higher priority) is monitored and penalized.

What Happens When You Arrive

  1. Registration (accettazione): You give your name, date of birth, and (if you have it) your codice fiscale and tessera sanitaria. Do not worry if you do not have these — you will still be seen. Provide your passport for identification.
  2. Triage: A nurse assesses you and assigns a color code. This is quick — typically 5–10 minutes after arrival.
  3. Waiting room: You wait according to your color priority. New arrivals with higher priority will be seen before you even if they arrived after you — this can feel frustrating but is the correct system.
  4. Medical assessment: A doctor sees you, takes history, examines you, orders tests if needed.
  5. Treatment and observation: Treatment administered. You may be admitted or discharged.
  6. Discharge paperwork (dimissione): You receive a written summary (lettera di dimissione) with diagnosis, treatment, and any follow-up instructions. Keep this document — it is important for continuity of care.

Managing Without Italian

Italian ER staff are trained medical professionals. Most have enough English to handle medical emergencies — "where does it hurt," "how long," "any allergies" — with varying degrees of fluency. In genuine emergencies, medical communication is more visual and demonstrative than conversational.

Practical tips:

  • Use Google Translate for written communication if needed — type your symptom description and show the phone screen
  • Point to body parts directly rather than relying on anatomical terms
  • Carry a medical summary card in Italian with your blood type, allergies, chronic conditions, and current medications. You can create one before arrival using Google Translate and have your Italian-speaking acquaintances review it.
  • In a major city, ask if there is a mediatore linguistico (language mediator) available — some hospitals have this service for non-Italian speakers

For non-life-threatening situations: calling your Italian-speaking friend or neighbor to accompany you to the PS is the most practical language solution. Do not be shy about asking for this support.

Costs for Insured and Uninsured

Italian SSN-enrolled residents: Most Pronto Soccorso care is covered by the SSN. If your visit results in admission, there is no cost. For outpatient PS visits (you are treated and discharged), a ticket may apply — typically €25 for non-urgent cases (green or white code). Red and orange code visits are free even for uninsured patients. Your SSN tessera sanitaria is presented at registration.

Tourists and non-SSN visitors (including Americans without Italian residency): You will be treated and then billed. Costs for a typical non-urgent PS visit (examination, basic tests, treatment): €80–€200. For complex cases with extensive diagnostics: can be higher. These costs are dramatically lower than equivalent US ER costs. Pay at discharge; credit cards are accepted at most hospitals.

With international insurance: Your Cigna Global, AXA, or Allianz Care policy covers emergency care. The PS visit may be on a direct-billing arrangement if your insurer has a relationship with the hospital, or you pay and submit for reimbursement. Call your insurer's emergency line (usually 24/7) from the PS waiting room to confirm coverage and get a case number.

Waiting Times: The Honest Reality

Green and white code cases (the majority of visits) often wait 2–5 hours, especially in evenings and on weekends. Peak PS times: evenings, weekends, and the period around local festivals (Ferragosto in August creates unusual PS volume as regular medical services close).

Italian PS are overwhelmed in many regions due to a combination of staffing shortages and misuse (people bringing non-emergency issues to the PS because they cannot get a timely medico di base appointment). This is the system's most significant weakness.

If you have a genuine emergency: go immediately, regardless of expected wait time. The triage system will prioritize you correctly. If you have a non-urgent issue: consider whether the Guardia Medica (after-hours service) or a private urgent care clinic is more appropriate.

When NOT to Use Pronto Soccorso

Common situations that should NOT go to the PS (but often do):

  • Fever without severe symptoms
  • Mild gastroenteritis
  • Dental pain (go to a dentist or, if closed, the Guardia Medica)
  • Minor cuts not requiring stitches
  • Chronic condition medication running out
  • Prescription refills

These situations belong with your medico di base (during hours), the Guardia Medica (after hours), or a private urgent care clinic. Using the PS for non-emergencies not only means a very long wait for you — it clogs the system for people with genuine emergencies.

Guardia Medica: After-Hours Care

The Guardia Medica (also called Continuità Assistenziale) is Italy's after-hours primary care service. It operates outside regular medico di base hours (evenings, nights, weekends, holidays) and provides urgent but non-emergency medical care — fever management, minor injuries, prescription renewals in genuine need situations.

Find your local Guardia Medica number through your ASL or by calling 118 and asking. The service is usually in a fixed location (often near a hospital or ASL office) and may also make home visits for patients who cannot travel.

FAQ

Will I get a bill after the Pronto Soccorso even if I have SSN enrollment?
For non-urgent cases (green/white code), yes — a ticket of approximately €25 is charged to SSN-enrolled patients for non-urgent PS visits. The reasoning is to discourage inappropriate PS use for non-emergency conditions. Red and orange code visits are free regardless of enrollment status.
What if I need an ambulance in a rural area?
Call 118. Italian emergency medical services operate throughout the country including rural areas, though response times are longer in remote locations. The 118 dispatcher will guide you through what to do while waiting for the ambulance and can provide first aid instructions over the phone.
Can I leave the Pronto Soccorso while waiting?
Yes — you can leave at any time. If you leave after triage but before seeing a physician, inform the triage desk so they do not continue holding your place. If you leave against medical advice after beginning treatment, you will be asked to sign a dimissione volontaria (voluntary discharge) form acknowledging you are leaving without completing treatment.
