Top Private Hospitals in Italy: Where Americans Go

Italy's public hospitals are excellent for emergencies. For planned procedures, specialist consultations, and situations where English communication is essential, these are the private facilities American expats consistently use — by city.

FB
By Fabrizio Boggio
Updated May 15, 2025 10 min read VERIFIED
✉ 𝕏 ⌘

Italy has one of the highest-ranked healthcare systems in the world. Its public hospitals (ospedali pubblici) handle emergencies excellently and cost nothing to SSN-enrolled residents. But private hospitals (case di cura private, policlinici privati) offer what public hospitals often cannot: shorter waiting times, English-speaking staff, dedicated international patient services, and direct billing to international insurance providers like Cigna Global and Allianz Care.

For planned procedures, cancer treatment, complex surgery, or any situation where communication precision matters, most experienced American expats in Italy use private facilities.

Public vs Private: When to Use Each

Quick guide
  • Emergency: always go to the nearest Pronto Soccorso (public) — Italian emergency medicine is excellent, and emergencies are treated regardless of insurance status
  • Planned specialist consultation: Private clinic is typically faster (days vs months for public referral) and allows direct specialist access without GP referral
  • Major surgery (elective): Both public and private are medically excellent; private offers shorter wait times and better English communication
  • Oncology and complex specialties: Italy's public university hospitals (policlinici) are often the best option — public oncology centers at institutions like San Raffaele and Gemelli are world-class
  • Routine checks and diagnostics: Private is usually faster and more flexible for scheduling

Milan

Ospedale San Raffaele

Italy's most internationally recognized private research hospital. Part of the IRCCS (Scientific Institute for Research, Hospitalization and Health Care) network. World-class oncology, cardiology, and neurology. Has a dedicated international patient office with English-speaking staff. Accepts major international insurance providers. Used by affluent Italians and expats throughout northern Italy.

Via Olgettina 60, Milan. Ospedale-sanraffaele.it

Istituto Clinico Humanitas

Consistently ranked among Italy's top hospitals in surveys of Italian medical professionals. Strong oncology, orthopedics, and gastroenterology. International patient services available. Research hospital with access to clinical trials. Many specialist physicians speak English.

Via Alessandro Manzoni 56, Rozzano (south Milan). Humanitas.it

Clinica San Pio X

Smaller private hospital in central Milan. Well-regarded for planned surgical procedures and specialist consultations. More accessible location than San Raffaele or Humanitas for central Milan residents.

Rome

Policlinico Gemelli (Università Cattolica)

While technically a university hospital (not fully private), Gemelli operates private patient services and is consistently ranked Italy's top hospital overall. Known for oncology, cardiac surgery, and neurosurgery. Pope John Paul II was treated here. Has an international patients office. Direct billing with major insurers.

Largo Agostino Gemelli 8, Rome. Policlinicogemelli.it

Salvator Mundi International Hospital

Rome's most internationally-oriented private hospital, historically serving the diplomatic and expat community. English is widely spoken. Smaller facility — better for consultations and minor procedures than complex surgery. Strong reputation with the American community in Rome specifically.

Viale delle Mura Gianicolensi 67, Rome

Campus Bio-Medico

Private university hospital on Rome's southern outskirts. Strong orthopedics, oncology, and minimally invasive surgery. Less centrally located but excellent for planned procedures.

Florence

Clinica Villa Donatello

Long-established private clinic in north Florence. Well-regarded by the local expat community. English-speaking physicians in most departments. Accepts major international insurance.

Via Pierluigi da Palestrina 10, Florence

Villa Chiara (Affidea)

Diagnostic and outpatient services with English capability. Better for diagnostic imaging and specialist consultations than acute care. Part of the international Affidea network, which simplifies insurance coordination.

Turin (Piedmont)

Clinica Cellini

Turin's most respected private clinic. Strong surgical capabilities, modern diagnostics, and a well-organized outpatient department. Not specifically oriented toward international patients but has English-speaking physicians in most specialties.

Via Cellini 5, Turin

Clinica Pinna Pintor

Orthopedic specialty clinic in central Turin. Known for hip and knee replacement, sports medicine, and spine procedures. Frequently used by Americans in the Piedmont region for orthopedic consultations and surgery.

For Piedmont residents generally

The regional network of ASO (Aziende Ospedaliere Ordini) public hospitals in Piedmont — including Molinette (CTO/Maria Adelaide campus) in Turin — are among northern Italy's better public facilities. For complex cases, they often rival or exceed private options. The Mauriziano and Città della Salute hospital systems in Turin have specific departments that are nationally recognized.

Bologna

Clinica Villalba (GVM Care & Research)

Part of the GVM group, Italy's largest private hospital network. Strong cardiology and vascular surgery capabilities. English available. Direct billing with major international insurers.

Villa Erbosa

Well-regarded private clinic in northern Bologna. Good for planned procedures and specialist access. Some English-speaking staff.

Smaller Cities and Rural Areas

Outside major cities, purely private hospitals become less common. The standard approach for expats in smaller towns and rural areas:

  • Use the local public Pronto Soccorso for emergencies — quality is generally adequate throughout northern and central Italy
  • For specialist consultations, travel to the nearest major city's private clinic — many can arrange same-week appointments
  • Consider telemedicine for initial consultations with English-speaking physicians; several Italian telemedicine platforms have English-speaking doctors
  • For complex or significant conditions, traveling to Milan, Turin, or Bologna for a second opinion at a specialist center is routine for Italians too

International Insurance and Direct Billing

Most major Italian private hospitals accept direct billing (fatturazione diretta) from the major international insurance providers: Cigna Global, Allianz Care, AXA International, and Bupa. This means you do not pay out of pocket and then claim reimbursement — the hospital bills the insurer directly.

To use direct billing:

  1. Call the hospital's international patient office before your visit
  2. Provide your insurance policy number and confirm your insurer is on their direct billing list
  3. Request pre-authorization from your insurer for significant planned procedures (most policies require this)
  4. Present your insurance card at check-in

Not all private hospitals have international patient offices. Smaller private clinics may require upfront payment and reimbursement. Always confirm billing procedures before treatment.

FAQ

Is Italian private hospital care as good as US care?
For most procedures, yes — and often better. Italy's top private and university hospitals (San Raffaele, Gemelli) are internationally ranked. The technology is modern and the physician training is rigorous. The differences are administrative and logistical: less paperwork, less litigation culture, and generally more direct physician access than in the US.
How much does a private hospital stay cost in Italy?
Costs vary widely by procedure. A one-night stay for a planned surgical procedure at a major Milan private hospital might cost €2,000–€5,000 all-in. A complex cancer treatment course could be significantly more. These are still substantially lower than equivalent US costs. With international insurance covering direct billing, your out-of-pocket is typically the policy deductible only.
What if I need care in a city far from where I live?
Italians routinely travel to different cities for specialist care — this is culturally normal, not unusual. Many hospitals that serve medical tourists (Italians traveling for care) have concierge services to help with accommodation and logistics. For planned major procedures, traveling 2–3 hours to the best facility for your condition is a reasonable decision and commonly made.
