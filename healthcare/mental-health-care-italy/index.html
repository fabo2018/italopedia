Mental Health Care in Italy: Public and Private Options for Americans

Mental healthcare in Italy is available — but navigating it in a second language, finding an English-speaking therapist, and maintaining continuity of care during the transition from the US requires preparation. Here is the honest picture of what exists, what works, and where the gaps are.

By Fabrizio Boggio· Published August 21, 2024· Updated May 15, 2025·11 min read

Italy has a unique mental health history. The Basaglia Law (Law 180/1978) — named for psychiatrist Franco Basaglia — closed Italy's psychiatric hospitals and mandated community-based mental health care, making Italy the first country in the world to formally legislate the closure of its asylum system. This was a landmark moment in psychiatric reform. The legacy is a community mental health network that, in theory, provides care close to where people live rather than in institutions.

The reality for American expats is more nuanced: the public mental health network (DSM — Dipartimento di Salute Mentale) exists and is technically accessible to SSN-enrolled residents, but waiting times are long, the cultural model differs from American therapy culture, and English-speaking professionals within the public system are rare. Most Americans managing ongoing mental health needs in Italy use private practitioners — often in combination with teletherapy from US-based providers.

Italy's Approach to Mental Health

Italian mental health culture differs from the American model in ways worth understanding before arrival:

  • Therapy culture is less normalized: In the US, therapy is widely discussed, openly practiced, and socially accepted across demographic groups. In Italy — particularly outside major cities and among older generations — therapy carries more stigma. This does not mean Italians do not seek help; it means the cultural conversation around it is different, and the infrastructure reflects that history.
  • The dominant therapeutic model is psychoanalytic/psychodynamic: Italian psychology training and practice has been historically more influenced by psychoanalytic traditions than American practice. CBT (cognitive behavioral therapy), DBT, EMDR, and other evidence-based approaches are available in Italy — particularly in major cities — but less uniformly distributed than in the US.
  • Psychiatry and psychology are separate tracks: In Italy, psychiatrists (specialisti in psichiatria, licensed to prescribe) and psychologists (psicologi, licensed for therapy but not prescribing) are clearly separated professions, more so than in the US. This means medication management and therapy may involve two separate professionals.

The Public System (DSM Centers)

Every Italian ASL (Local Health Authority) operates a DSM (Dipartimento di Salute Mentale), which includes:

  • CSM (Centro di Salute Mentale): Community mental health center providing outpatient psychiatric consultations, medication management, and psychological support. This is the entry point into the public mental health system — your medico di base refers you to the CSM.
  • SPDC (Servizio Psichiatrico di Diagnosi e Cura): Inpatient psychiatric ward, typically within a general hospital, for acute psychiatric crises
  • Residential and semi-residential facilities for more intensive community-based care

Access: through your medico di base's impegnativa referral, or by presenting directly to the CSM for urgent need. Free for SSN-enrolled residents (no ticket for mental health services in many regions).

Realistic limitations for American expats:

  • Waiting times for a first CSM appointment: 2–6 months in most cities
  • Ongoing therapy within the public system: often limited in frequency (monthly or bi-monthly sessions rather than weekly)
  • English-speaking providers within the CSM system: rare outside major cities
  • The model is more crisis-intervention oriented than ongoing therapeutic support

Private Therapy in Italy

The private mental health market in Italy has grown significantly, particularly in northern cities and among younger professionals. Private psychologists, psychotherapists, and psychiatrists practice throughout Italy at fees that are substantially lower than US private pay rates.

Typical private fees in Italy (2025):

  • Initial psychiatric consultation: €80–€180
  • Ongoing psychiatric appointment: €60–€120
  • Psychotherapy session (50 min): €50–€120 in smaller cities; €80–€160 in Milan and Rome
  • Initial psychological assessment: €100–€200

Compared to US private pay rates ($150–$300/session for therapy; $250–$500 for psychiatry), Italian private rates are meaningfully more affordable. International insurance policies typically cover private mental health care under the mental health benefit — check your policy for annual limits and co-insurance requirements.

Finding English-Speaking Mental Health Professionals

This is the primary practical challenge for most American expats. English-speaking therapists exist in all major Italian cities — finding them requires targeted searching:

  • Psychology Today's online directory (psychologytoday.com/intl/it) — includes Italian therapists who list English as a working language
  • The American Embassy in Rome maintains a non-endorsed list of English-speaking mental health professionals in Rome. Your nearest consulate may have a similar resource.
  • Internations and expat community Facebook groups — first-person referrals from other Americans who have found English-speaking professionals in your city
  • Search "psicologo anglofono [city]" or "psichiatra anglofono [city]" — many Italian professionals who trained internationally or work with expat populations list their language capabilities on their websites
  • International private hospitals in major cities often have mental health departments with English-speaking staff

Availability by region:

  • Milan: Good availability of English-speaking mental health professionals, particularly in the private sector and at international hospitals
  • Rome: Strong availability, especially in the Parioli/international neighborhood area; diplomatic community has driven demand
  • Florence: Moderate availability, partly due to the academic community
  • Turin: Available but requires more searching; Italian-language practitioners are the norm
  • Smaller cities: Limited; expect to travel to the nearest large city or use teletherapy

Online and Teletherapy Options

For many American expats in Italy, the most practical solution for ongoing mental health support is teletherapy with US-licensed providers. Several considerations:

US-licensed therapist via telehealth: Many US therapists now see clients internationally. The legal position varies — some US state licensing boards restrict licensees from providing therapy to clients physically located outside the US; others do not. Your current US therapist may be willing to continue seeing you remotely; ask directly about their position on international clients before you leave.

Italian teletherapy platforms: Platforms like TrovaPsico, Unobravo, and Mentemia offer teletherapy in Italian from Italian-licensed providers. Some list English-language capability. These are regulated by the Ordine degli Psicologi and operate within the Italian professional framework.

International teletherapy platforms: BetterHelp and similar services operate internationally. Note that BetterHelp practitioners are US-licensed and subject to US professional standards; the Italian professional licensing framework does not apply to them when seeing Italian-resident clients online.

Time zone: Italy is 6–9 hours ahead of US time zones. US East Coast therapists working regular hours can see Italian-resident clients in early morning (7am ET = 1pm CET). West Coast therapists in the evening (7pm PT = 4am CET) — this only works for clients with unusual schedules.

Psychiatric Medications in Italy

Most psychiatric medications prescribed in the US are available in Italy under Italian brand names or as generics. The AIFA (Agenzia Italiana del Farmaco) regulates medication availability. Key notes:

  • SSRIs and SNRIs: All commonly used antidepressants (fluoxetine, sertraline, paroxetine, escitalopram, venlafaxine, duloxetine) are available under Italian names. Generic versions are standard and SSN-covered.
  • Mood stabilizers: Lithium, valproate, lamotrigine, quetiapine all available and covered.
  • Atypical antipsychotics: Risperidone, olanzapine, aripiprazole, clozapine all available.
  • ADHD medications: Methylphenidate (Ritalin) is available in Italy but more tightly regulated than in the US. Amphetamine-based medications (Adderall, Vyvanse) are not approved in the EU. ADHD management in Italy relies on methylphenidate and non-stimulant options. If you are currently managed with amphetamines, discuss your situation with your US psychiatrist before moving — you will need to transition to available medications.
  • Benzodiazepines: Available but strictly regulated in Italy (class A prescription, short supplies, no refills without new prescription). Long-term benzodiazepine prescriptions that are routine in some US practices face more resistance in Italian prescribing culture.

Bring a 3–6 month supply from the US during the transition period, especially for medications with tighter Italian regulation. Your Italian psychiatrist will prescribe Italian equivalents once established as a patient.

The Expat Mental Health Reality

International relocation is one of life's more significant stressors, even when chosen and welcomed. Americans moving to Italy commonly experience:

  • A "honeymoon phase" (months 1–3) of high energy and novelty
  • A "culture shock plateau" (months 4–8) where bureaucratic frustrations, language barriers, and social isolation create genuine difficulty
  • Gradual adaptation and integration (months 9–18), typically resolving into a stable new normal

This is not weakness — it is documented adjustment psychology. Building a mental health support plan before you leave is not pessimism; it is preparation. The expats who navigate the culture shock plateau most successfully tend to be those who have planned social infrastructure (existing relationships, community organizations, regular activities) and who have a mental health support resource they can access if needed — whether that is teletherapy, a local English-speaking therapist, or awareness of when to seek help.

Crisis Resources in Italy

Mental health crisis resources in Italy
  • 118 — Medical emergency; also dispatches psychiatric emergency response for acute mental health crises
  • Telefono Amico (02 2327 2327) — Italian helpline for emotional support (Italian language)
  • Telefono Azzurro (19696) — Children and youth crisis line
  • Crisis Text Line International — Text HOME to various numbers depending on country; operates in Italy in English
  • Your nearest US Embassy/Consulate — American Citizens Services can provide referrals to English-speaking emergency mental health resources in any city

If you are experiencing a mental health crisis in Italy: call 118 for emergency response, go to any Pronto Soccorso where psychiatric emergency care is available 24/7, or contact your nearest US consulate for an English-language resource referral. You will be treated.

FAQ

Can I continue seeing my US therapist via video call after moving to Italy?
Possibly — it depends on your therapist's licensing situation and their comfort with international clients. Ask your therapist directly before you move. Many therapists are willing to continue with established clients internationally; others are restricted by their state licensing board. Having this conversation before departure is much easier than after.
Does Italian international health insurance cover therapy sessions?
Most international health plans (Cigna Global, Allianz Care, AXA International) include a mental health benefit that covers private therapy sessions, subject to annual limits (typically 20–30 sessions/year) and co-insurance. Check your specific policy. Pre-existing mental health conditions may face waiting periods or sub-limits — review your policy carefully before relying on it for mental health coverage.
Are there English-language support groups for expats in Italy?
Yes — primarily in major cities. AA (Alcoholics Anonymous) operates English-language meetings in Rome, Milan, Florence, and other cities. Al-Anon has English meetings in major cities. Expat community groups (Internations, American expat Facebook groups) sometimes organize peer support activities. The International Women's Club of various Italian cities historically has provided social support infrastructure for newcomers. Ask in your local expat community what exists in your specific city.
