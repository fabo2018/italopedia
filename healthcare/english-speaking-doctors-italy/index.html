# Finding English-Speaking Doctors in Italy: The Complete Guide for Americans (2025)

One of the most common anxieties Americans have before moving to Italy: "What if I get sick and can't communicate with my doctor?" It's a legitimate concern. Italy's healthcare system is excellent, but it operates almost entirely in Italian. Navigating it without language skills is genuinely challenging.

The good news: English-speaking doctors are available in every major Italian city — you just have to know where and how to find them. This guide tells you exactly how.

Understanding Italy's Healthcare Structure

Before finding a doctor, understand how the system is organized. Italy's SSN (Servizio Sanitario Nazionale) is structured around a gatekeeping GP model — different from U.S. healthcare where you can see any specialist directly:

  1. Medico di Medicina Generale (MMG) — your assigned family doctor / GP. The central figure. For most healthcare needs, you start here.
  2. GP issues a referral (impegnativa) — for specialist visits within the SSN
  3. Specialist visit — cardiology, dermatology, orthopedics, etc.
  4. Hospital care — emergency or admitted care, via the Pronto Soccorso (ER) or planned admission

The English-speaking challenge applies at every level. Your GP is who you'll interact with most frequently — finding one who speaks English well is the highest-priority step.

Step 1: Find an English-Speaking GP

Method 1: Ask Your ASL Directly

When you go to your local ASL (Azienda Sanitaria Locale) to enroll in the SSN, you can ask:

"Ho bisogno di un medico di base che parli inglese. Ce n'è qualcuno disponibile?"

(I need a GP who speaks English. Is there anyone available?)

The ASL office has a list of available GPs accepting new patients. Not all offices have English-speaking GPs available, but in major cities and areas with significant expat populations, they exist.

Method 2: Consulate Lists

Italian consulates in the U.S. often maintain lists of English-speaking doctors in major Italian cities for their citizens and Americans abroad. Check your Italian consulate's website or call them. The U.S. Embassy in Rome and U.S. Consulates in Milan, Florence, and Naples also maintain lists.

U.S. Embassy Rome: it.usembassy.gov — search "medical assistance" for their doctor lists

U.S. Consulate Milan: it.usembassy.gov/embassy-consulates/milan/

Method 3: Expat Community Networks

Online expat communities are often the most reliable source of practical, current recommendations:

  • Facebook groups: "Americans in Italy," "Expats in Milan/Rome/Florence/Turin/Bologna" — post asking for English-speaking GP recommendations
  • InterNations: internations.org — international expat community with city chapters across Italy
  • Meetup.com: English-speaking groups in Italian cities often share doctor recommendations
  • Reddit: r/ItalyExpats has regular threads on healthcare navigation

Method 4: International Clinics and Practices

In major Italian cities, there are clinics specifically serving international patients — with English-speaking staff throughout:

Rome:

  • Rome American Hospital (Ospedale Americano di Roma): romehospital.com — private hospital with English-language care; significant American expat history
  • International Medical Center (IMC): imcrome.com — English-language GP and specialist practice
  • Studio Medico Internazionale: Various locations; ask your consulate

Milan:

  • EuroAmerican Hospital: English-language care for expats
  • Humanitas Research Hospital: humanitasresearch.it — internationally oriented; English widely spoken among specialists
  • IEO (European Institute of Oncology): International patients unit

Florence:

  • American Hospital Florence (Ospedale Privato) — ospedale-americano.it — specifically English-language private care
  • Dr. Stephen Kerr Medical Service — British doctor, well-regarded in Florence expat community
  • Studio Medico Marchetti: English-speaking GPs and specialists

Turin:

  • Several private practices in the Crocetta neighborhood specialize in international patients; ask at the U.S. Consulate in Milan (has jurisdiction over Turin) for current list
  • International online GP platforms (see below) are popular among Turin expats

Bologna:

  • Private practices serving university international community — ask at AUSL Bologna
  • InterNations Bologna chapter maintains recommendations

Naples:

  • More limited English options than northern cities; consulate list is most reliable
  • Private clinics in Vomero and Chiaia neighborhoods have English-speaking doctors

Step 2: Finding English-Speaking Specialists

Within the SSN (With GP Referral)

Once your GP issues a referral (impegnativa) for a specialist, you (or your GP's office) books the appointment at the local hospital or clinic. When calling to book, ask:

"C'è un medico specialista che parla inglese?"

(Is there a specialist who speaks English?)

Large university hospitals in major cities — Policlinico di Milano, Gemelli (Rome), Careggi (Florence), Sant'Orsola (Bologna), Molinette (Turin) — have specialists accustomed to international patients, particularly in departments that see complex or referred cases.

Private Specialist Access

Private specialist visits bypass the SSN referral system and allow direct booking. The international clinics listed above handle referrals within their networks.

For finding English-speaking private specialists in any Italian city:

  • Doctolib.it: doctolib.it — Italy's major medical appointment booking platform. Filter by specialty and location; some doctors list languages spoken.
  • Miodottore.it: Similar platform, good specialist coverage
  • iDoctors.it: Specialist booking, some profiles list languages

When you find a potential specialist online, check their profile for language information. If not listed, call the practice and ask:

"Il dottore parla inglese?" (Does the doctor speak English?)

Step 3: Emergency Care — What to Do

If you need emergency care and don't speak Italian, here's what to know:

Call 118 (Italian Emergency Number)

Italy's emergency medical number is 118 (equivalent to U.S. 911 for medical). Dispatchers are trained to manage calls from non-Italian speakers. State "Parlo inglese" (I speak English) immediately and they will usually connect you to an English-speaking operator or work through the emergency.

At the Pronto Soccorso (Emergency Room)

Major Italian hospital ERs in cities with significant tourist and expat populations have English-speaking staff — not guaranteed, but common. At check-in:

  • Show your health card (Tessera Sanitaria) or insurance card
  • State symptoms clearly using simple English or a translation app
  • "Ho dolore qui" + pointing works universally

Google Translate works reasonably well for medical terms in Italian — keep it on your phone.

Medical Translation Apps

  • Google Translate with offline Italian download — works without internet
  • iTranslate Medical — medical terminology focus
  • Speak & Translate — real-time voice translation, useful in consultations

Step 4: Dental Care in English

Italian dentists vary considerably in English proficiency. In major cities and tourist areas, many dentists speak English — it's a competitive advantage they advertise.

Finding an English-Speaking Dentist

  • Same consulate/embassy lists that cover doctors often list dentists
  • Doctolib.it and Miodottore.it have dentist listings with language filters
  • Expat community recommendations (most reliable)
  • Yelp Italy and Google Maps reviews often mention language ability

What to Ask at First Contact

"Parla inglese?" (Do you speak English?) — if yes, proceed

"Quanto costa una visita di controllo?" (How much for a check-up?) — to confirm pricing

Step 5: Mental Health and Therapy in English

Finding English-speaking psychologists and therapists in Italy has become significantly easier with the growth of online therapy platforms.

In-Person English-Speaking Therapy

  • Psychology Today Italy: psychologytoday.com — has an Italy directory with language filters
  • Expat community groups regularly share therapist recommendations
  • International clinics (American Hospital networks) have psychology departments

Online Therapy Platforms

For Americans in Italy who prefer English-language therapy, online platforms work well:

  • BetterHelp: U.S. platform; operates internationally; all English
  • Talkspace: Similar to BetterHelp
  • Alma: U.S. therapy network accessible internationally via telehealth
  • Livi: European telehealth platform with English speakers

Cost: Online therapy platforms typically run $65–120/week (U.S. platforms). Italian private therapists: €60–120/session.

Building Your Italian Healthcare File

When you arrive in Italy, create a personal medical summary in Italian — a one-page document your doctors can reference. Include:

  • Condizioni mediche (medical conditions): any chronic diagnoses
  • Farmaci attuali (current medications): with Italian generic names if possible
  • Allergie (allergies): medications and other
  • Interventi chirurgici (past surgeries)
  • Storia familiare (family history): relevant hereditary conditions
  • Gruppo sanguigno (blood type)
  • Vaccinazioni (vaccinations): recent vaccines with dates

Use Google Translate or a professional medical translator to create this document. Hand it to every new doctor at the first visit — it eliminates the need for extensive verbal medical history-taking.
