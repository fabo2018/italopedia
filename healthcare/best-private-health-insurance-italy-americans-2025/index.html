Best Private Health Insurance for Americans in Italy: 2025 Comparison

Italian consulates require health insurance for long-stay visas (minimum €30,000 coverage). Once resident, voluntary SSN enrollment at ~€2,000/year is often cheaper than private insurance. Here is how to choose.

By Fabrizio Boggio
Updated May 2026 12 min read VERIFIED
✉ 𝕏 ⌘
Quick Answer

Americans moving to Italy need health insurance that Italian consulates will accept for visa purposes AND that works practically in Italy. Top options: Cigna Global (best all-around), AXA International (best for retirees), Allianz Care (best network in Italy), Bupa Global (best for high-end coverage), IMG Global (most affordable). Plans start from approximately €150/month for under-40s. Alternatively, enrolled Italian residents can access Italy's national health service (SSN) via voluntary enrollment at approximately €2,000/year — often cheaper than private insurance for older applicants.

Do You Need Private Health Insurance in Italy?

For the Italian visa: Yes, mandatory. All Italian long-stay visas require proof of health insurance covering Italy with: minimum coverage of €30,000 (some consulates require €50,000), coverage for medical repatriation, and coverage for the entire validity period of the visa.

After you arrive and become a resident: Optional, but worth evaluating. As a registered Italian resident, you can access Italy's SSN (national health service) via mandatory enrollment (if you work in Italy) or voluntary enrollment at approximately €2,000/year.

What Italian Consulates Require

Each consulate has slightly different requirements, but generally your policy must show: minimum medical coverage of €30,000–€50,000 (varies by consulate), medical repatriation coverage, policy in English, coverage starting from visa issuance, and policyholder name matching passport.

💡 Tip

Get a "confirmation of coverage" letter from your insurer specifically stating the coverage amount, validity dates, and that Italy is covered. Generic policy documents sometimes do not satisfy consulate checkers.

The Top International Health Insurance Plans

1. Cigna Global — Best All-Around

Best for: Overall value; Americans who want familiar-feeling insurance with good international network.

Key features: Extensive hospital network in Italy, US customer service, range of plans from basic to comprehensive, modular (add dental, vision, maternity).

2025 indicative premiums (non-smoker):

  • Age 35, basic: ~€180/month
  • Age 50, standard: ~€320/month
  • Age 60, comprehensive: ~€550/month

What we like: Cigna has strong recognition among Italian consulates. Their "Global Health Benefits" plan is frequently cited by expat communities as consulate-approved.

What to watch: The cheapest plans have significant deductibles. Read the fine print on pre-existing condition coverage.

2. AXA International / AXA Global Healthcare — Best for Retirees

Best for: Retirees; those prioritizing direct billing with Italian hospitals.

Key features: Strong direct billing network in Italy, good coverage for chronic conditions, recognized by Italian consulates.

2025 indicative premiums:

  • Age 35: ~€160/month (basic)
  • Age 55: ~€400/month (standard)
  • Age 65: ~€650/month (comprehensive)

What we like: AXA's direct billing relationships with major Italian hospitals mean you often pay nothing out-of-pocket at time of treatment.

What to watch: Premiums increase significantly after age 60.

3. Allianz Care — Best Italian Network

Best for: Extensive Italian hospital network; digital nomads and younger expats.

Key features: One of the largest insurer networks in Italy, strong app-based claims management, competitive pricing for under-50s.

2025 indicative premiums:

  • Age 30: ~€140/month (essential)
  • Age 45: ~€280/month (standard)
  • Age 60: ~€500/month (comprehensive)

What to watch: Claims process can be slower than Cigna; customer service less streamlined for Americans.

4. Bupa Global — Best for High-End Coverage

Best for: High earners who want premium, no-limit coverage.

Key features: No annual maximum on many plans, access to top-tier private hospitals globally, concierge services, comprehensive mental health coverage.

2025 indicative premiums:

  • Age 40: ~€350/month (classic)
  • Age 55: ~€650/month (premium)

What to watch: Premium price point. Not necessary for most Americans in Italy who have access to the SSN.

5. IMG Global — Most Affordable

Best for: Budget-conscious Americans; those on shorter stays.

2025 indicative premiums: Age 35: ~€100–€130/month (essential).

What we like: The most affordable option that satisfies Italian consulate requirements.

What to watch: Network and service quality below the top providers. Better for visa compliance than for actual day-to-day use in Italy.

The SSN Voluntary Enrollment Option

Once you are a registered Italian resident, you can enroll voluntarily in Italy's national health service (SSN):

Annual fee (2025): Approximately €2,000/year for a single adult (income-based; may be less for lower incomes).

What you get: Free GP visits with your medico di base, free emergency care, specialist visits at reduced cost (€30–€50 co-pay with referral), prescription medications at reduced cost, access to Italian public hospitals.

The hybrid approach: Many Americans maintain a basic private international policy (for consulate compliance and international travel coverage) plus voluntary SSN enrollment. The private policy costs less because the SSN covers Italy-based care.

Pre-Existing Conditions

Pre-existing conditions are the biggest variable in international health insurance for Americans moving to Italy.

  • Cigna Global: Offers plans with pre-existing condition coverage after a waiting period (typically 2–3 years). Moratorium underwriting available.
  • AXA: Similar to Cigna — waiting periods apply, full medical underwriting available.
  • Allianz: Full medical underwriting. Pre-existing conditions may be excluded or covered with premium loading.
  • Bupa: Comprehensive plans with full medical underwriting — pre-existing conditions covered at higher premiums.

Key advice: If you have significant pre-existing conditions, get insurance BEFORE you leave the USA while you are still on US coverage.

Medicare: Does It Work in Italy?

Medicare does not cover care outside the USA (with very limited exceptions that do not apply to Italy). Medicare Parts A and B, Medicare Advantage plans, and Medigap plans all provide zero coverage in Italy.

Should you suspend Medicare? You can drop Part B (saving approximately $174/month in 2025 premiums). However, if you return to the USA later, you will face a lifetime late enrollment penalty of 10% per year you went without Part B coverage. Weigh this carefully.

How to Choose: Decision Framework

  • Under 45, healthy: Allianz or IMG Global for basic coverage + voluntary SSN enrollment once resident
  • 45–60, some medical history: Cigna Global Standard or AXA + voluntary SSN once resident
  • Over 60 or significant health history: Cigna Comprehensive or Bupa + SSN voluntary enrollment
  • High-net-worth, want zero compromise: Bupa Global Premium
  • Primarily concerned with visa compliance at minimum cost: IMG Global Essential

Frequently Asked Questions

My Italian consulate rejected my insurance — what now?
Get a specific letter from your insurer confirming coverage amount, validity, and Italy. If the insurer's minimum coverage is below the consulate's requirement, upgrade your plan.

Can I use travel insurance for my Italian visa?
Generally no. Italian consulates require health insurance, not travel insurance. Verify the policy explicitly covers medical care and meets the €30,000 minimum.

Is Italian private health insurance (not international) an option?
Once you are an Italian resident with a permesso, you can buy Italian domestic health insurance (assicurazione sanitaria integrativa) from companies like UniSalute, Previmedical, and Generali. These are often cheaper than international insurance for Italy-only coverage but do not cover you internationally.
