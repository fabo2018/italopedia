Specialist Visits in Italy: How the Referral System Works

In Italy, you generally cannot walk into a specialist's office. You need a referral (impegnativa) from your primary care physician first. The public system has excellent specialists — but waiting times can be months. Here is how to navigate both the public and private routes.

The Italian National Health Service (Servizio Sanitario Nazionale) is a comprehensive universal healthcare system that provides high-quality medical care, including specialist consultations, at little or no direct cost to enrolled residents. Americans who enroll in the SSN — typically after obtaining their permesso di soggiorno and registering at the ASL (Local Health Authority) — are entitled to use the system on the same terms as Italian citizens.

The system is organized around a gatekeeping model: your primary care physician (medico di base, also called medico di medicina generale) is the entry point for most specialist care. Without their referral, the public system's specialist appointments are not available to you.

Your Medico di Base: The Gateway

When you enroll in the SSN, one of the first things you do is choose a medico di base from the list provided by your ASL. This is your assigned general practitioner for the Italian system. You will see them for:

  • General health concerns and routine checkups
  • Prescriptions for medications (ricette)
  • Referrals to specialists (impegnative)
  • Requests for blood tests, imaging, and diagnostic exams
  • Sick notes (certificati di malattia) if you are employed in Italy

Appointments with your medico di base are free under the SSN. Most medici di base have office hours (ambulatorio) several mornings per week and take walk-ins or bookings. Many now offer online booking through their practice website or through the regional healthcare portal.

Finding a medico di base who speaks English: this varies dramatically by region and city. In larger cities with international populations (Milan, Rome, Florence, Turin), English-speaking medici are findable. In smaller towns, you may need to work with an Italian-speaker. Bring a trusted Italian-speaking friend or use a translation app for your first few appointments if your Italian is limited.

The Impegnativa: How to Get One

The impegnativa (also called ricetta rossa — "red prescription" for the color of the paper form) is the referral document your medico di base issues for specialist visits, diagnostic tests, and imaging. It authorizes you to access SSN specialist services at the reduced ticket price.

To get an impegnativa:

  1. Make an appointment with your medico di base
  2. Explain your symptom or concern
  3. If they agree a specialist is warranted, they write the impegnativa
  4. The impegnativa specifies the type of specialist or exam needed (e.g., "visita cardiologica" for a cardiology consultation)
  5. You take the impegnativa to book the specialist appointment through the SSN

The medico di base has discretion over what they refer. If you request a specific specialist and your doctor does not believe the referral is medically indicated, they may decline. If you believe their assessment is incorrect, you can seek a second opinion from another medico or access the specialist privately.

Booking the Specialist Appointment

With your impegnativa, you book the specialist appointment through one of:

  • CUP (Centro Unico di Prenotazione): Italy's centralized booking system. Available by phone (number varies by region), at ASL offices, at post offices, and often online through your regional healthcare portal. You present your impegnativa code and tessera sanitaria, and the system finds available appointments.
  • Directly at the hospital's CUP desk
  • Online through your regional portal (available in most regions with SPID authentication)

You choose from available appointments at SSN facilities in your ASL network. You can generally choose from multiple facilities in your region — though the one with the shortest wait may not be nearest to you.

The Ticket (Copayment)

Most SSN specialist visits and exams require a ticket — a fixed copayment that represents a portion of the cost. The amount depends on the type of visit and your income category:

  • Standard specialist consultation: €15–€60 depending on specialty and region
  • Diagnostic tests (blood work, ECG): often free or minimal copayment
  • Imaging (X-ray, ultrasound, MRI): €15–€80 depending on the exam

Exemption categories (esenzione dal ticket): people over 65 with income below certain thresholds, individuals with specific chronic conditions, pregnant women, children under 14, and recipients of certain disability benefits pay reduced or zero ticket. The exemption code goes on the impegnativa. New residents typically pay standard ticket rates unless they qualify for an exemption category.

Waiting Times: The Real Picture

This is where the Italian SSN system creates genuine frustration. Waiting times for specialist visits vary enormously by region, specialty, and urgency classification:

  • Urgent (urgente/U): Required within 72 hours. Designated for conditions requiring prompt attention.
  • Urgent-deferrable (breve/B): Within 10 days
  • Deferrable (differibile/D): Within 30 days
  • Routine (programmabile/P): Within 120 days

The urgency classification is set by your medico di base on the impegnativa. For routine specialist visits, waiting times of 2–6 months are common in many regions and specialties. In the south and for some specialties in all regions, waits can extend to 12+ months.

Practical reality: many Italian residents — especially for non-urgent specialist needs — bypass the SSN queue and go private. The cost of a private specialist visit (€80–€250) is considered a reasonable price for getting seen in days rather than months.

The Private Route: When to Skip the Queue

Accessing a specialist privately means contacting the specialist's private practice directly, without an impegnativa. You pay out of pocket (or through international insurance).

When private makes sense:

  • You need to be seen within days or weeks, not months
  • You need a specific specialist (not just whoever the SSN assigns)
  • The visit is for a straightforward consultation where the private cost is modest (€80–€150)
  • You have international insurance with direct billing to private clinics

Many Italians use both systems strategically: SSN for routine care and chronic condition management, private for planned specialist consultations where they want faster access or a specific physician.

Intramoenia: The Hybrid Option

Most Italians do not know this option exists, and almost no foreigners do: Italian law allows SSN physicians to see patients privately within their SSN facility, outside of their SSN hours, for a fee. This is called attività libero-professionale intramuraria or intramoenia.

Intramoenia visits typically cost 30–50% less than fully private clinic visits, take place at the same hospital or clinic where the physician works in the SSN, and happen much faster than the SSN queue. Ask your medico di base whether the specialist you need offers intramoenia services — many do.

FAQ

Can I see a specialist without a referral in Italy?
Yes, privately. The referral requirement only applies to accessing SSN (public) specialist services at the discounted ticket price. Any Italian specialist can be seen privately without a referral — you book directly and pay the private rate. Emergency specialist care (Pronto Soccorso) also does not require referral.
What if my medico di base won't give me a referral I think I need?
You have options: request a written explanation of why the referral is declined (this sometimes prompts reconsideration); seek a second opinion from another medico di base; or go privately to the specialist without a referral. You are not required to use the SSN referral pathway for any condition.
I don't have SSN enrollment yet. Can I still see a specialist?
Before SSN enrollment, you access all healthcare privately (or through your international health insurance). The SSN referral system is only available once you are enrolled. For Americans in Italy before SSN enrollment — whether waiting for the permesso di soggiorno or on a visa without SSN entitlement — private care is the standard approach.
