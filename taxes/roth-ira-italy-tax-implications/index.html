Roth IRA in Italy: Tax-Free Doesn't Mean Tax-Free

A Roth IRA is tax-free in the United States. The Italian tax authority does not recognize the Roth IRA as a pension vehicle. The consequence: Roth distributions that cost you nothing in US tax may create Italian tax liability — at rates up to 43%. Here is what we know and what remains uncertain.

By Fabrizio Boggio
Updated May 10, 2025 11 min read VERIFIED
✉ 𝕏 ⌘
Tax disclaimer: The Italian tax treatment of Roth IRA distributions is an unsettled area. This article presents the current state of professional opinion and available guidance. Consult a qualified US-Italy tax professional for your situation.

The Roth IRA is one of the most powerful retirement savings vehicles in the American tax code. You contribute after-tax dollars, the money grows tax-free inside the account, and qualified distributions in retirement are completely tax-free in the United States. For most American savers, the Roth is the ideal long-term account.

When you move to Italy, this tax-free benefit becomes uncertain. Italy does not have a Roth IRA equivalent. The Italian tax code does not recognize the Roth's pre-taxed-at-contribution logic. When you take a distribution, Italy sees money coming out of an account — and it may want to tax that money.

Roth IRA Basics: The US Picture

For context:

  • Roth IRA contributions are made with after-tax dollars (no deduction on contribution)
  • All growth inside the Roth is tax-free
  • Qualified distributions (age 59½+, account open 5+ years) are completely tax-free in the US
  • No required minimum distributions during the owner's lifetime
  • Roth IRAs cannot be contributed to from non-earned income (no Italian-source rental income, dividends, etc.)

Why Italy Does Not Recognize Roth Treatment

The 1999 US-Italy Tax Treaty Article 18 covers private pensions — it gives Italy taxing rights over distributions from private pension plans once you become an Italian resident. The Roth IRA was created in 1997, after the treaty was negotiated, and is not mentioned anywhere in the treaty text.

Italy's Agenzia delle Entrate has not issued a specific ruling on Roth IRA distributions for Italian residents. This silence creates uncertainty, but the weight of current professional opinion is that Italy would treat Roth distributions as taxable income — either under Article 18 (as a pension distribution) or as ordinary income — without giving credit for the fact that contributions were already taxed in the US.

The core problem: Italy's tax system does not track what you paid tax on before arriving. When money comes out of a Roth IRA, Italy sees a distribution from a foreign account. It does not see "you already paid US tax on the principal; this is just a return of your own after-tax money."

What the US-Italy Treaty Says (and Doesn't)

Article 18 of the 1999 US-Italy treaty:

Pensions and other similar remuneration beneficially owned by a resident of a Contracting State in consideration of past employment shall be taxable only in that State.

The key questions for a Roth IRA under this article:

  1. Is a Roth IRA a "pension" under Italian law? — Uncertain. Italy may treat it as a pension (Article 18 applies, Italy taxes it) or as a capital account (different treatment).
  2. If it is a pension under Article 18: Italy has taxing rights — but should it tax the full distribution or only the earnings component, given the contribution was already taxed in the US?
  3. The treaty has no explicit provision for the Roth's pre-taxed-at-source character.

No authoritative Italian or US ruling has resolved these questions as of May 2025. Different Italian tax practitioners take different positions.

Current Professional Positions

The spectrum of professional opinion among US-Italy tax specialists:

  • Conservative position: Italy will tax Roth distributions as ordinary income under IRPEF at full bracket rates. This is the most cautious planning assumption — if you plan for the worst and it does not happen, you have a windfall; if you do not plan for it and it does happen, you have a large unexpected liability.
  • Middle position: Italy taxes Roth distributions under Article 18 as pension income, but should give credit for the after-tax contribution basis — meaning only the earnings (not the principal returned) are taxable. This is plausible but requires the Agenzia delle Entrate to accept a basis calculation they have never formally endorsed.
  • Optimistic position: The Roth IRA is a capital account, not a pension; Italy cannot tax qualified distributions under the treaty; the tax-free US treatment should be honored. This is the least supportable position under current Italian law.

Most experienced practitioners advise planning under the conservative assumption until Italy provides explicit guidance or case law develops.

The Practical Impact for Italian Residents

What this means concretely:

  • If you have a $500,000 Roth IRA and plan to withdraw $50,000/year: Under the conservative Italian treatment at a 35% IRPEF rate, you face ~€17,500 per year in Italian tax on distributions that cost you nothing in US tax. Over a 20-year retirement, that is €350,000 in unexpected Italian tax.
  • The Foreign Tax Credit does not help here: Since you pay no US tax on Roth distributions, there is no US tax against which to credit Italian tax paid. The double taxation problem for Roth distributions is more severe than for traditional IRA distributions (where FTC coordination works effectively).

Strategies for Roth Holders Moving to Italy

  1. Take Roth distributions before establishing Italian tax residency. Roth distributions taken while you are still a US resident (before 183 days in Italy / before anagrafe registration) are US-only events. If you have appreciated Roth funds you plan to use in early retirement, consider drawing them down before your Italian residency start date.
  2. Convert traditional IRA money to Roth before moving — carefully. Roth conversions done while you are a US resident are taxed in the US. Once you are an Italian resident, a Roth conversion may be taxable in Italy as well (as a "deemed distribution and recontribution"). Complete major Roth conversions before Italian residency begins.
  3. Hold the Roth for post-Italian-residency years. If you intend to eventually return to the US, a Roth IRA continues growing tax-free. It may make sense to not draw down the Roth while in Italy (using other funds instead) and preserve the Roth for the years when you are back in a US-only tax situation.
  4. Seek a formal ruling. In exceptional cases, working with an Italian tax attorney to seek an interpello (private ruling) from the Agenzia delle Entrate on the specific treatment of your Roth distributions may provide certainty — though this is expensive and the outcome is uncertain.

Should You Stop Doing Roth Conversions?

If you are an Italian resident and considering Roth conversions: the traditional argument for Roth conversions (pay tax now at lower rates, save at higher rates later) breaks down when Italy taxes the conversion as Italian income (IRPEF up to 43%) plus the US taxes the conversion as ordinary income. You could be paying 43% Italian + some US rate on the same dollar with no offsetting benefit.

Once you are an Italian resident, Roth conversions are generally inadvisable without specialist analysis. Pause any automated Roth conversion program until you have reviewed the Italian implications with a qualified advisor.

FAQ

If I take a Roth distribution in Italy and Italy taxes it, can I at least claim a credit on my US return?
No — because the Roth distribution is tax-free in the US, there is no US tax liability against which to apply an Italian Foreign Tax Credit. This is the unique double-taxation problem of the Roth: the US gives you zero tax, Italy potentially gives you a full tax bill, and there is no credit mechanism to offset it. This distinguishes Roth from traditional IRA distributions, where FTC coordination works effectively.
Can I roll my Roth IRA into an Italian pension account to avoid Italian taxation?
No practical mechanism exists for this. US retirement accounts cannot be transferred into Italian pension systems. The assets must remain in the US-custodied Roth IRA.
What if the Italian Agenzia issues guidance that Roth distributions are exempt?
This would be the best-case scenario. Given Italy's ongoing effort to attract foreign residents (via flat tax regimes and other incentives), there is a policy argument for favorable Roth treatment. But as of 2025, no such guidance exists and it would be unwise to plan on it emerging.
