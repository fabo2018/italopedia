IMU Property Tax in Italy: When You Owe It and How to Calculate It

IMU is Italy's annual property tax. Primary residences are generally exempt — but the exemption disappears the moment your property is not your registered primary home. For Americans who own Italian property as a vacation home or investment, IMU is a real, recurring cost that needs to be budgeted and paid proactively.

IMU (Imposta Municipale Propria) replaced the old ICI (Imposta Comunale sugli Immobili) and became the primary Italian property tax starting in 2012. It is a municipal tax — set by individual comuni within national limits — levied annually on ownership of Italian real estate. Unlike the US property tax system, Italy's IMU is self-assessed: you calculate it yourself, fill out a payment form, and pay. No bill arrives automatically.

What IMU Is

IMU is an annual wealth tax on the value of Italian real estate. It is levied on the owner (or the holder of a usufruct or surface right) of Italian property. The tax base is the cadastral value — not the market value — which keeps effective rates moderate compared to what a market-value-based system would produce.

IMU is distinct from TARI (garbage tax) and TASI (services tax, now absorbed into IMU). When people say "Italian property tax," they typically mean IMU.

Who Owes IMU

IMU is owed by:

  • Owners of Italian real property (residential, commercial, agricultural land, building sites)
  • Holders of usufruct rights (diritto di usufrutto)
  • Holders of long-term surface rights (diritto di superficie)
  • Tenants under certain long-term lease arrangements that transfer owner-like rights

Non-residents who own Italian property owe IMU on their Italian properties — residence status does not exempt you from IMU on properties you own. An American who owns an Italian vacation home and lives in the US pays IMU on that Italian property.

The Primary Residence Exemption

The main IMU exemption: primary residential property (abitazione principale) is fully exempt from IMU, as long as it is not classified as a luxury property (categories A/1, A/8, A/9 in the catasto).

To qualify for the primary residence exemption:

  • You must be registered as resident (residenza anagrafica) at the property address in the comune's anagrafe
  • The property must be your habitual abode (dimora abituale)
  • Both conditions must be met — registration without actually living there does not qualify

For Americans who have moved to Italy and registered at their Italian address: their Italian home is exempt from IMU. For Americans who own an Italian property but live elsewhere (US or another Italian address): IMU is owed on the property at the standard rate.

How to Calculate IMU

The IMU calculation formula:

IMU = Rendita Catastale × Revaluation Coefficient × IMU Coefficient × Municipal Rate

Step by step:

  1. Find the rendita catastale: This is the cadastral annual income value registered for your property. It appears on the property's visura catastale (obtainable from the Agenzia delle Entrate or your notary). For a typical Italian apartment it might be €400–€1,200/year.
  2. Apply the revaluation coefficient: Multiply rendita catastale × 1.05 (5% national revaluation applied since 1997).
  3. Apply the IMU coefficient: For residential properties (category A, except A/10): multiply by 160.
  4. This gives the base IMU value. Then multiply by the municipal rate (aliquota IMU) set by your comune.
  5. Prorate for partial year ownership if you acquired the property during the year.
Example calculation: vacation home in Tuscany

Rendita catastale: €800/year
× 1.05 revaluation = €840
× 160 IMU coefficient = €134,400 (taxable base)
× 1.06% municipal rate (typical non-prima-casa rate) = €1,424/year IMU

Paid in two installments: ~€712 on June 16 (first installment) and ~€712 on December 16 (balance).

Municipal Rates and Where to Find Yours

Each comune sets its own IMU rates annually within the national limits:

  • Standard rate (abitazione diversa dalla principale): Up to 1.06% maximum
  • Primary residence luxury: 0.5% (standard) — but note regular prima casa is fully exempt
  • Agricultural land and building sites: Different rates apply

Municipal rates must be published by the comune by October 28 of the prior year. Most comuni publish their delibera IMU on their website. You can also look them up through the Agenzia delle Entrate's Portal IMU database at agenziaentrate.gov.it.

If your comune did not publish its rates by the October 28 deadline, you pay the national standard rate.

Payment: June 16 and December 16

IMU is paid in two installments:

  • First installment (acconto): June 16 — 50% of the prior year's total IMU (or a calculated advance for first-year owners)
  • Second installment (saldo): December 16 — the remaining balance, calculated on the current year's actual IMU based on the current year's municipal rates

Payment method: IMU is paid via F24 form — Italy's standard tax payment form. You fill out the F24 with the property details and amounts, then pay at any Italian bank, online through your Italian bank's portal, or through a tabaccheria/post office. You can also pay online through the Agenzia delle Entrate's website with SPID authentication.

The F24 codes for IMU vary by property category and municipality. Your commercialista or an Italian property management service can prepare the F24 for you; the forms are also available on the Agenzia delle Entrate website with guidance on code selection.

There is no automatic bill — you calculate, you prepare the F24, you pay. Missing the June 16 or December 16 deadlines triggers immediate penalties (30% on the unpaid amount for significant delays) plus interest.

Americans Who Own Italian Property but Don't Live in Italy

If you own Italian property but are not an Italian resident — whether you live in the US or in another Italian property at a different address — IMU is owed at the standard non-prima-casa rate. The property does not qualify for the primary residence exemption.

Practical management: many non-resident property owners use an Italian commercialista or property management service to calculate and pay their IMU. The cost of this service (typically €100–€300/year) is well justified by the complexity of the F24 preparation and the risk of missing deadlines.

The US does not tax Italian property holdings directly (beyond the potential capital gains when sold), but you should report Italian real estate on your Italian dichiarazione dei redditi (if you are an Italian resident) and on Form 8938 and potentially the FBAR if you hold the property through a financial vehicle rather than direct ownership.

FAQ

My property is rented out full-time as an Airbnb. Do I owe IMU?
Yes. Short-term rental income does not change your IMU obligation as owner. You owe IMU on any Italian property you own that is not your registered primary residence, regardless of whether it is rented, vacant, or occupied.
I bought a property in Italy mid-year. How is the first year's IMU calculated?
IMU is prorated by months of ownership. If you purchased July 15, you owned the property for 6 months of the year (July counts if you owned it for at least 15 days of the month). Your annual IMU is multiplied by 6/12. Your first December payment covers the first year's prorated amount.
Is IMU deductible on my US tax return?
IMU on a foreign property may be deductible as a foreign real property tax on Schedule A if you itemize deductions — subject to the $10,000 SALT deduction cap that applies to state and local taxes. This is a situation where your US CPA's guidance is essential, as the deductibility depends on how the property is classified for US purposes (personal use, rental, etc.).
