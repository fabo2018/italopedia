Tax Residency in Italy: When Americans Become Liable

Italy's tax residency rules are deceptively simple on the surface — 183 days in Italy — but the anagrafe registration trigger and the worldwide income principle make the first-year calculation more complex than most Americans expect.

By Fabrizio Boggio
Updated April 10, 2025 11 min read VERIFIED
✉ 𝕏 ⌘
Tax disclaimer: Tax residency determination is fact-specific. Consult a qualified US-Italy tax professional for your situation.

Every American who considers moving to Italy eventually asks: when does Italy start taxing me? The honest answer is: earlier than you might expect, and on more income than you might expect.

Italy taxes residents on worldwide income — the same principle the United States applies to American citizens regardless of location. Once you become an Italian tax resident, Italy has a claim on your US dividends, your 401(k) distributions, your rental income from a US property, and your capital gains from selling US stocks. The US-Italy tax treaty and the Foreign Tax Credit system prevent genuine double taxation in most cases — but the planning required to navigate the two systems correctly starts before you arrive.

Italy's Three Residency Tests

Italy's tax residency rules are codified in Article 2 of the TUIR (Testo Unico delle Imposte sui Redditi). An individual is an Italian tax resident for a given calendar year if, for the majority of that year (183+ days, or 184 in leap years), they meet any one of three conditions:

  1. Registered at the Anagrafe — enrolled in Italy's civil population registry (the anagrafe) at any Italian municipality
  2. Domicile in Italy — the center of your vital interests, business, and personal relationships is Italy (Art. 43 Civil Code definition)
  3. Residence in Italy — your habitual place of abode is in Italy

These are three independent tests. Meeting any one of them for 183+ days in a calendar year makes you an Italian tax resident for that entire year. You do not need to meet all three.

The 183-Day Rule Explained

The "majority of the year" threshold is 183 days (out of 365) in a standard calendar year. Days are counted as calendar days of presence in Italy, not working days. Partial days count as full days. Days of arrival and departure both count.

The calculation is for the full calendar year (January 1–December 31), not a rolling 12-month period. This is different from how some other countries count residency days.

If you were in Italy for 200 days in a given calendar year and met the domicile condition (your home, family, and primary activities are there), you are an Italian tax resident for that entire calendar year — including the 165 days you were elsewhere.

The Anagrafe Registration Trigger — The Trap

Here is the provision that surprises most Americans: being registered at the anagrafe for the majority of a year (183+ days) makes you an Italian tax resident, regardless of how many days you were physically present in Italy.

This means: if you arrive in Italy in January, register at the anagrafe in February, and then travel extensively (spending only 100 days physically in Italy for the rest of the year), you are still potentially an Italian tax resident — because you were registered at the anagrafe for more than 183 days of the calendar year.

This is a material planning point for Americans who plan to spend part of the year in Italy and part in the US during a transition year. Delaying anagrafe registration to late in the year, or deregistering (canceling your registration) before you reach 183 days, can affect your tax residency status — though it also affects your permesso di soggiorno validity. The competing obligations require careful coordination.

First-Year Complexity

The first calendar year in Italy is typically the most complex from a tax perspective. You are a split-year tax resident — part of the year you were a US resident (full US tax obligations, no Italian obligations), and part of the year you became an Italian resident (Italian obligations kick in).

Italy does not formally recognize split-year residency in its domestic law in the way the UK does, for example. The official Italian position is that once you are a tax resident for the majority of the year, you are a resident for the full year. In practice, the Agenzia delle Entrate takes a pragmatic approach for income earned before you arrived, but this is an area where professional guidance is essential.

The US, by contrast, does recognize split-year residency for the first year in certain circumstances — the "first year choice" election (if using the physical presence test) can affect how the two countries' claims on your income are coordinated for the arrival year.

Worldwide Income Taxation

As an Italian tax resident, you must declare worldwide income on your Italian annual tax return (Modello Redditi PF). This includes:

  • US salary or self-employment income
  • US dividends and interest
  • US capital gains
  • US Social Security (even though Italy cannot tax it under the treaty — you still declare it)
  • 401(k)/IRA distributions (Italy taxes these under Article 18 of the treaty)
  • Rental income from any country
  • Any other global income

Declaring does not automatically mean paying Italian tax on everything. The US-Italy treaty, the Foreign Tax Credit, and exemptions under Italian law reduce or eliminate Italian tax on many categories. But declaration is required — failing to declare is a separate violation from failing to pay, and both carry penalties.

US Obligations Continue Regardless

This is the fundamental reality of being an American abroad: the US taxes American citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live or how long. Becoming an Italian tax resident does not reduce your US filing obligations. You continue to file:

  • Form 1040 (US annual tax return) — due April 15, automatic extension to June 15 for Americans abroad
  • FBAR (FinCEN 114) if aggregate foreign financial accounts exceed $10,000 at any point in the year
  • Form 8938 (FATCA) if foreign financial assets exceed threshold ($200,000 for single filers abroad at year-end or $300,000 at any point)

The Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116) and the US-Italy treaty provisions reduce or eliminate double taxation on most income types. But the filing obligation does not go away.

Leaving Italy: Exit from Tax Residency

When you leave Italy permanently, you need to formally exit Italian tax residency. This involves: deregistering from the anagrafe of your Italian municipality, enrolling in AIRE (the registry of Italians living abroad, if you have Italian citizenship), and filing a final Italian tax return for the year of departure covering the period you were a resident.

Italy has anti-abuse rules for tax residency exits: if you move to a country on Italy's "blacklist" of tax havens, the Agenzia delle Entrate may challenge your exit and continue treating you as an Italian tax resident. Countries not on the blacklist (US, most EU countries) do not trigger this challenge.

Arrival Timing Strategy

The date you arrive and register in Italy determines your tax residency status for the arrival year. General guidance:

  • Arrive before July 2: You will likely be an Italian tax resident for the arrival year (183+ days of physical presence or anagrafe registration possible). This simplifies Italian obligations but means Italian tax applies to your full-year income.
  • Arrive July 2 or later: You are in Italy for fewer than 183 days of the calendar year. You may not become an Italian tax resident until the following calendar year, depending on when you register at the anagrafe. This is more tax-advantageous for people with large US-source income that they want to realize in the US before becoming Italian tax residents (e.g., selling appreciated US investments before Italy has a claim on the gains).

The interaction between immigration requirements (which want you to register as soon as possible) and tax planning (which may benefit from delayed registration) requires careful navigation. This is a core planning discussion to have with your US-Italy tax specialist before you book your one-way flight.

FAQ

If I spend 6 months in Italy and 6 months in the US, which country taxes me?
If you spend exactly 182 days in Italy and 183 in the US (and are not anagrafe-registered for 183+ days), you are not an Italian tax resident for that year. The US taxes you as always. In the following year, if the balance tips the other way, Italy becomes your tax residence. The calculation is strictly per calendar year.
Does having a property in Italy make me an Italian tax resident?
Owning property in Italy does not by itself trigger Italian tax residency. Residency is about presence and registration, not property ownership. Non-resident foreigners own Italian property regularly and pay Italian property taxes (IMU) without being Italian tax residents.
What is Italy's presumption of tax residency?
Italy applies a presumption that individuals registered at the anagrafe are Italian tax residents. This presumption can be rebutted — but the burden falls on you to prove that your domicile was actually elsewhere despite the Italian registration. For Americans spending substantial time in Italy, this is rarely worth challenging.
