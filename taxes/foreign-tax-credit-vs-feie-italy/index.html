Foreign Tax Credit vs FEIE for Americans in Italy: Which One to Use

Both mechanisms reduce your US tax bill on foreign income. But they work differently, they exclude each other on the same income, and choosing the wrong one can cost you thousands. Here is the full Italy-specific analysis.

Tax disclaimer: This article is informational only. US-Italy tax law is complex and situation-specific. Consult a CPA or tax attorney licensed in both the US and Italy before making filing decisions.

Every American living abroad eventually confronts the same question: the IRS taxes you on worldwide income regardless of where you live. Italy taxes you on worldwide income once you become a resident. Without careful planning, you pay both.

Congress has provided two tools to prevent double taxation: the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) and the Foreign Tax Credit (FTC). They are not interchangeable. They have different mechanics, different caps, and different interactions with Italian tax law. Choosing incorrectly does not just mean filing the wrong form — it can mean paying significantly more tax than legally necessary.

The Fundamental Difference

The FEIE excludes a portion of your foreign earned income from US taxation entirely. If you qualify and elect it, the excluded income simply does not appear in your US taxable income calculation. The 2025 exclusion ceiling is $126,500 per person.

The FTC does not exclude income — it gives you a dollar-for-dollar credit against your US tax liability for taxes you have already paid to a foreign government. If you paid €10,000 in Italian IRPEF, you can credit approximately $10,800 (at current exchange rates) against your US tax bill.

The critical difference: the FEIE only applies to earned income (wages, self-employment). The FTC applies to all income types including dividends, interest, rental income, and capital gains.

How the FEIE Works

To claim the FEIE (Form 2555), you must meet one of two tests:

  • Bona Fide Residence Test: You are a bona fide resident of a foreign country for an uninterrupted period covering an entire tax year. If you moved to Italy, registered at the anagrafe, and have a permesso di soggiorno, you generally qualify.
  • Physical Presence Test: You were present in foreign countries for at least 330 full days during any 12-month period.

The 2025 exclusion amount is $126,500. If you earn $180,000 in self-employment income in Italy, you exclude the first $126,500 and pay US self-employment tax on the remaining $53,500. Note: the FEIE does not exclude self-employment tax — only income tax.

How the Foreign Tax Credit Works

The FTC (Form 1116 for individuals) credits foreign taxes paid against your US tax liability. The credit is limited to the US tax attributable to that foreign income — you cannot use excess foreign tax credits to offset US-source income.

Italy's IRPEF brackets for 2025:

Taxable income (Italy)IRPEF rate
Up to €28,00023%
€28,001 – €50,00035%
Over €50,00043%

The US top marginal rate is 37%. For any Italian resident earning over approximately €50,000, their Italian tax rate (43%) exceeds their US marginal rate. This means Italian taxes paid will generally exceed US taxes owed on the same income — producing excess FTCs that can be carried forward one year or back ten years.

Why Italy Changes the Math

Most standard expat advice defaults to recommending the FEIE for earned income. That advice was written for countries with lower tax rates than the US — think Thailand, Portugal (historically), or UAE. It does not hold for Italy.

Italy's high IRPEF rates mean that for most Americans earning salary or self-employment income in Italy:

  • Italian taxes paid will exceed US taxes owed on the same income
  • The FTC will eliminate the US tax liability entirely, often with credits left over
  • The FEIE provides no additional benefit and forfeits the ability to carry forward excess credits

The exception: Americans earning US-source income that is exempt from Italian taxation under the treaty. In those cases, the interaction is more complex.

Three Scenarios: Which to Choose

Scenario A: Employed by Italian company, salary €65,000

Italian IRPEF on €65,000 ≈ €19,000+. US federal tax on same income at 22% bracket ≈ $14,300. FTC of $20,570 (€19,000 at 1.08) eliminates all US tax liability with ~$6,270 in excess credits to carry forward. Use FTC.

Scenario B: US-based remote worker, income $90,000

If you are a US employee working remotely for a US company, your income may not be subject to Italian IRPEF under the treaty — or it may be, depending on your residency status. If you are paying Italian tax at 35%+ on $90,000 and your US rate is 22%, FTC wins again. Use FTC.

Scenario C: Freelancer earning $50,000, no Italian income tax paid yet

In your first year before you have established Italian tax residency (less than 183 days in Italy), you may not owe Italian tax yet. Without foreign taxes to credit, the FEIE is the only tool available. Use FEIE for year 1 only.

The FEIE Trap for Retirees

Retirees living in Italy face a specific pitfall: Social Security benefits, pension income, and IRA/401(k) distributions are not "earned income" under the FEIE definition. They cannot be excluded. The FEIE is entirely irrelevant to retirement income.

For retirees, the FTC is the only mechanism. The US-Italy tax treaty also provides specific provisions for Social Security: under Article 20 of the 1999 treaty, US Social Security is taxable only by the United States — Italy cannot tax it. This means no Italian tax is paid on Social Security, and therefore no FTC is available to offset US tax on that income.

Planning implication: retirees with substantial Social Security plus investment income should model carefully, as the US tax on Social Security may be unavoidable, while investment income may be fully offset by FTCs on Italian taxes paid.

Switching Between FTC and FEIE

You can switch between the FEIE and FTC, but there is a cost. If you have been claiming the FEIE and switch to the FTC, you must wait five years before you can re-elect the FEIE. This is why the initial choice matters — switching later is expensive in terms of flexibility.

If you move to Italy and incorrectly claim the FEIE in year one, then realize the FTC is better, you are locked out of the FEIE for five years. In practice, this rarely matters because Italy's high rates make the FTC clearly superior for most earners — but it reinforces why getting the choice right in year one is important.

Interaction with Italy's Flat Tax Regime

Italy's flat tax regime (regime forfettario for small businesses, and the €100,000 flat tax for high-income new residents) creates a wrinkle. Under the €100,000 flat tax regime, all foreign-source income is taxed at a flat €100,000 annually — regardless of amount. There is no itemized Italian tax return for foreign income, which means no Italian tax paid on individual income categories that could be credited line-by-line on Form 1116.

The IRS has not definitively ruled on whether the €100,000 flat substitute tax qualifies for FTC treatment. Practitioners are divided. Some argue it is a creditable foreign tax; others argue its lump-sum nature disqualifies it. This is an area where you must engage a specialist — the wrong call can mean a five-figure error.

If you are considering the €100,000 flat tax regime, budget for specialist tax advice. The potential tax savings are substantial — but the US-Italy interaction requires careful analysis for your specific income profile.

Frequently Asked Questions

If I use the FTC and Italy taxes me more than the US, do I lose that credit?
No. Excess FTCs that exceed your current-year US liability can be carried back one year or forward ten years. They are not lost — they are a future tax asset.
Can a married couple split — one uses FEIE, one uses FTC?
Yes. Each spouse files Form 2555 or Form 1116 independently, and different elections are permissible on the same joint return. This can be advantageous if one spouse earns less than the FEIE ceiling and the other earns more.
Does the FEIE reduce self-employment tax?
No. Self-employment tax (Social Security and Medicare) applies to net self-employment income regardless of the FEIE election. The FEIE only reduces income tax. This is a significant cost for freelancers — 15.3% SE tax on net earnings up to the Social Security wage base ($176,100 in 2025).
What form do I use for the Foreign Tax Credit?
Form 1116 for individuals. It is filed with your Form 1040. There are separate Form 1116 instances for different income categories (general limitation, passive income, etc.). Your tax software or CPA handles this.
