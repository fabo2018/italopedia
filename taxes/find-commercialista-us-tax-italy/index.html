How to Find a Commercialista Who Knows US Tax in Italy

The average Italian commercialista has never seen a Form 1040, has never heard of FBAR, and does not know the Foreign Tax Credit from the FEIE. Here is how to find the rare professional who has — and how to not pay too much for it.

You have moved to Italy. You have a permesso di soggiorno. You have a codice fiscale. You have an Italian bank account. Now you need someone to handle your taxes — both the Italian ones and the US ones you are still legally required to file.

The problem: these are two completely different tax systems, governed by different laws, filed in different languages, with different deadlines and different penalties for non-compliance. Most professionals specialize in one. Very few specialize in both. And even fewer can afford to advertise the niche, so they are genuinely hard to find.

Why This Is Harder Than It Sounds

Italy has roughly 120,000 practicing commercialisti — licensed accounting professionals who handle bookkeeping, tax returns, and business compliance for Italian residents. The profession is well-regulated and highly credentialed. What Italian commercialisti are almost never trained on: the US tax code.

The reverse is equally true. US CPAs and enrolled agents know the 1040, the FBAR, FATCA reporting, and the foreign tax credit mechanics. What they typically do not know: Italian IRPEF brackets, the regime forfettario, Italian VAT (IVA), or the specifics of the US-Italy tax treaty as interpreted by the Agenzia delle Entrate.

You need both perspectives. The question is how to find them efficiently.

The Two Professional Types You Need

In practice, most Americans in Italy end up working with two professionals who communicate with each other:

1. An Italian commercialista with expat experience — handles your Italian dichiarazione dei redditi (annual tax return), your IVA if you run a business, your regime forfettario if applicable, and your Italian compliance obligations. Must speak English and understand that you have US filing obligations that affect Italian planning.

2. A US CPA or enrolled agent specializing in expats — handles your Form 1040, FBAR (FinCEN 114), Form 8938, Form 2555 or 1116, and any PFIC issues with Italian investment accounts. Ideally has experience specifically with Italy, not just "international."

Some firms offer both in one. These are rare but exist — mostly in Milan, Rome, and a few operate fully remote.

Where to Actually Find Them

For Italian commercialisti with expat experience

  • American Chamber of Commerce in Italy (AmCham Italy) — maintains a member directory; search for accounting/tax members. AmCham members serving American clients self-select for English fluency and US awareness.
  • Facebook groups: "Americans in Italy," "Expats in Italy," "Moving to Italy Americans" — ask for recommendations specifying your region and income type. These groups generate real referrals from real people who have used the professionals.
  • Reddit: r/ItalyExpats, r/digitalnomad — search threads for commercialista recommendations with Italy context.
  • Your US consulate's American Citizens Services — maintains a list of local English-speaking professionals. Not an endorsement, but a useful starting list.
  • Direct outreach to expat-heavy cities — commercialisti in Florence, Milan, Bologna, and Rome with foreign client bases often have websites in English. Search "commercialista Milano expat" or "commercialista Firenze americani."

For US CPAs and EAs specializing in Italy

  • IRS directory of enrolled agents — filter by "Form 2555" and "international" experience. Not Italy-specific but narrows the field.
  • Greenback Expat Tax Services — US-based firm specializing exclusively in expat returns; has Italy-experienced preparers.
  • Taxes for Expats — similar to Greenback; Italy-specific experience varies by preparer; ask directly.
  • Bright!Tax — another well-regarded expat specialist; Italy cases are routine for them.
  • FAEA (Federation of American Women's Clubs Overseas) — their Rome and Milan chapters maintain referral lists for US-tax-aware professionals.

The Five Questions to Ask Before Hiring

Ask these in your first consultation — ideally before you pay anything. A professional who cannot answer these competently is not qualified for your situation.

1
"How many American clients do you currently have?"

Any number below 10 is a yellow flag. Below 5 is a red flag. You want someone for whom American clients are a meaningful portion of their practice, not an occasional curiosity.

2
"Are you familiar with FBAR and FATCA reporting requirements?"

A blank stare or uncertain answer means they are not qualified. FBAR (Report of Foreign Bank Accounts) and FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act) are non-negotiable US filing obligations for Americans abroad with foreign accounts. Penalties for non-compliance start at $10,000 per violation.

3
"Do you coordinate with a US CPA, or do you handle the US return yourself?"

Either answer can be acceptable — what you want is clarity. If they claim to handle both the Italian and US returns, ask about their US credentials. If they coordinate with a US partner, ask who and whether there is a combined fee arrangement.

4
"How does the US-Italy tax treaty affect my Social Security income?"

The correct answer: under Article 20 of the 1999 US-Italy tax treaty, US Social Security benefits are taxed exclusively by the United States — Italy cannot tax them. A commercialista who does not know this (or who says Italy does tax Social Security) does not have the treaty knowledge you need.

5
"What is your fee for a first-year filing for an American with [your income type]?"

Get a number. Vague answers ("it depends") are normal at this stage — but push for a range. First-year complexity is higher than ongoing years, so expect a premium on year one.

Red Flags in the First Meeting

  • "I can handle the American taxes too" — said by an Italian commercialista with no stated US credentials. Italian and US tax law are separate professions. Be skeptical of this claim without verification.
  • No English-language communication capability — if the initial consultation is entirely in Italian with no accommodation for your situation, your ongoing relationship will be frustrating at minimum and error-prone at worst.
  • Unfamiliarity with the existence of the US-Italy tax treaty — the 1999 treaty is the foundation of planning for Americans in Italy. Not knowing it exists is disqualifying.
  • Extreme low fees — a US expat tax return in Italy has genuine complexity. Any quoted fee below €300 for an Italian dichiarazione and below $400 for a US 1040 with foreign income should raise questions about depth of service.
  • Reluctance to communicate with your other professional — your Italian commercialista and your US CPA need to coordinate. A professional who will not communicate with their counterpart is creating risk for you.

When You Can Handle It Yourself

Not everyone needs a professional for everything. If your situation is simple, you may be able to DIY portions:

Potentially DIY: US Form 1040 with only W-2 wage income and Foreign Tax Credit using software (TurboTax has an expat version); FBAR filing via the BSA e-filing system (it is free and straightforward for simple accounts).

Always use a professional: Italian dichiarazione dei redditi (the interface with the Agenzia delle Entrate is not designed for non-Italian speakers); any year involving self-employment, rental income, or business ownership; any year involving Italian investments, Italian real estate purchase or sale; FATCA Form 8938; any year you are uncertain about your tax residency status.

First-Year Checklist: What to Give Your Professionals

Come prepared to your first meeting. Your professionals will need:

  • Prior three years of US tax returns (1040s)
  • Prior year FBAR if filed
  • Codice fiscale
  • Italian bank account statements (all accounts)
  • Permesso di soggiorno details and dates
  • Any Italian employer documentation (CU form — equivalent of W-2)
  • Social Security statements if applicable
  • US brokerage statements (dividends, capital gains, 1099-B)
  • IRA/401(k) distribution statements if applicable
  • List of all foreign financial accounts and approximate balances

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use an American CPA for everything and skip the Italian commercialista?
No. Your Italian dichiarazione dei redditi must be filed in Italy, in Italian, with the Agenzia delle Entrate. A US CPA is not licensed to practice Italian tax law. You need Italian-side representation. The question is whether that person also coordinates on US matters or whether you have a separate US specialist.
Is there a directory of English-speaking commercialisti in Italy?
No comprehensive public directory exists. The most reliable sources remain expat community referrals (Facebook groups, Reddit) and the AmCham Italy member directory. Your consulate's American Citizens Services can also provide a non-endorsed list.
Do I need a commercialista if I am only in Italy on the digital nomad visa?
Possibly not for the first year, if you do not establish Italian tax residency. But once you are in Italy for 183+ days in a calendar year, you are an Italian tax resident and a commercialista becomes necessary.
