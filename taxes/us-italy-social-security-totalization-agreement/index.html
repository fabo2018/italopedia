US-Italy Social Security Totalization Agreement: What Americans Need to Know

Prevent double taxation, combine work credits, and understand the 2025 Social Security Fairness Act — the essential guide for Americans in Italy.

If you've worked in both the United States and Italy, you might be paying social security taxes twice — or missing out on benefits you've earned. The US-Italy Social Security Totalization Agreement prevents the first problem and solves the second.

This is one of the least-known but most important agreements for Americans who have lived, worked, or plan to retire in Italy. This guide explains exactly what it does and how it affects you.

What Is the Totalization Agreement?

The United States and Italy signed a bilateral Social Security Totalization Agreement that has been in force since 1978. It does two things:

1. Prevents dual Social Security taxation Without the agreement, Americans working in Italy could be required to pay Social Security contributions to both the US (SSA) and Italy (INPS) simultaneously on the same income. The agreement prevents this duplication.

2. Allows combining of work credits If you haven't worked long enough in either country alone to qualify for retirement benefits, you can combine your work credits from both countries to meet the minimum requirements.

Preventing Double Taxation: Who Pays What

Under the agreement, workers generally pay into the Social Security system of the country where they work, not both.

For Americans Working in Italy

If you work for an Italian employer in Italy (employed or self-employed), you generally pay Italian social security (INPS) and are exempt from US Social Security taxes on that same income.

Exception — Detached Worker Rule: For most totalization agreements, Americans temporarily sent to work in another country can remain covered by US Social Security for up to 5 years. Italy's agreement is different — it uses a "coverage territory" approach rather than the standard detached-worker rule. Check with a cross-border tax advisor for your specific situation.

For Americans Working Remotely for US Employers from Italy

This is a gray area. Remote workers employed by US companies but physically located in Italy may have complex dual obligations. The specific facts matter significantly. A cross-border tax advisor is essential.

Combining Work Credits: The Most Important Benefit for Retirees

This is where the agreement most directly helps Americans who've split their careers between the US and Italy.

How US Social Security Credits Work

You need 40 quarters (10 years) of US Social Security contributions to qualify for US retirement benefits. Each year you can earn up to 4 credits based on your earned income.

How Italian Social Security (INPS) Works

Italy measures coverage in weeks of contributions. To qualify for Italian pension benefits, you need minimum weeks/years depending on your birth year and the pension type.

The Totalization Solution

If you don't qualify for benefits from either country alone (not enough credits in either), the agreement lets you combine your credits from both systems:

Example: You worked 6 years in the US (24 credits — not enough for US benefits, which requires 40) and 8 years in Italy. Under totalization, the US can consider your Italian credits to determine if you qualify. Each country then pays a proportional benefit based on the credits you actually earned there.

Important limits:

  • US can only count Italian credits if you have at least 6 US quarters (1.5 years) of US credits
  • Italy can only count US credits if you have at least 52 weeks of Italian contributions
  • Each country pays only for its own period — you get proportional benefits from each, not double

How to Apply for Totalization Benefits

For US Social Security Benefits Using Italian Credits

Contact the US Social Security Administration at your local SSA office, or:

  • If you're in the US: call SSA at 1-800-772-1213
  • If you're in Italy: contact the US Embassy in Rome which handles SSA inquiries for Americans in Italy

SSA will coordinate with INPS to verify your Italian work record.

For Italian Pension (INPS) Benefits Using US Credits

Contact INPS (Istituto Nazionale della Previdenza Sociale) at your local INPS office in Italy. You'll need your Italian codice fiscale and documentation of your US Social Security work history (available from SSA).

The 2025 Social Security Fairness Act: What Changed

In January 2025, the Social Security Fairness Act was signed into law in the US, repealing two provisions that had previously reduced benefits for many Americans:

  • Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP): Previously reduced US Social Security benefits for people who also received a pension from work not covered by US Social Security (including Italian INPS pension). Now repealed.
  • Government Pension Offset (GPO): Previously reduced spousal and survivor benefits for people receiving government pensions. Now repealed.

What this means for Americans in Italy: If you receive or will receive both US Social Security and Italian INPS pension, you can now receive your full US Social Security benefit without reduction. This is a significant change for many Italian-American retirees who had been receiving reduced benefits.

If you were previously subject to WEP or GPO reductions, you may be eligible for a retroactive payment.

Taxation of Social Security and Italian Pension in Italy

Once you're an Italian tax resident, both your US Social Security and your Italian INPS pension may be subject to Italian income tax. How this works depends on whether you're using the standard tax regime or one of Italy's flat tax options:

Standard Italian taxation:

  • Italian pension (INPS): Subject to Italian IRPEF (23%–43%)
  • US Social Security: Subject to Italian tax, but the US-Italy tax treaty provides some protection

7% Flat Tax for Foreign Retirees (Art. 24-ter TUIR): If you establish residency in an eligible Southern Italian municipality, foreign-sourced income (including US Social Security and any non-Italian pension) is taxed at a flat 7% for up to 10 years. This is completely separate from the Totalization Agreement but relevant to most retirees considering Italy.

The Italian INPS System: What It Is

For Americans who work in Italy and pay Italian social security:

INPS (Istituto Nazionale della Previdenza Sociale) is Italy's public pension authority. Contributions are mandatory for:

  • Employees: employer and employee both contribute
  • Self-employed: contribute through Gestione Separata (a separate INPS fund)
  • Freelancers (liberi professionisti): contribute to professional fund (cassa previdenziale) or INPS

Italian pension eligibility (as of 2025): Generally requires age 67 with at least 20 years of contributions (pensione di vecchiaia), or age 63 with 41+ years of contributions (pensione anticipata).

FAQ

Does the Totalization Agreement mean I don't have to pay US taxes in Italy? No. The Totalization Agreement only covers Social Security contributions and benefits — not income taxes. The separate US-Italy Income Tax Treaty handles income tax. Americans living in Italy still file US federal income tax returns annually.

Can I receive both a US Social Security benefit and an Italian INPS pension? Yes, if you've earned benefits in both systems. Each country pays its proportional share based on your credits there. Since the repeal of WEP in 2025, you receive your full US benefit without reduction.

I worked in Italy in the 1990s — do those years count? Yes, if you paid INPS contributions in Italy, those years may count toward totalization. The agreement covers Italian credits earned after 1936.

I'm self-employed in Italy on a Digital Nomad Visa — what do I pay? Self-employed people in Italy typically contribute to INPS's Gestione Separata. Whether you also owe US self-employment taxes depends on your specific situation and the Totalization Agreement's coverage rules. Consult a cross-border tax advisor.

Where can I get my Italian social security record? At any INPS office in Italy with your codice fiscale, or online via the INPS portal with a SPID digital identity.
