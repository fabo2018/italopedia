Cryptocurrency Tax in Italy 2025: Complete Rules for American Residents

Italy overhauled its crypto tax framework in 2023 and added a flat-tax election for 2025. American residents face a dual challenge: Italian capital gains tax AND US reporting on the same transactions. Here is the complete picture.

FB
By Fabrizio Boggio
Updated May 15, 2025 13 min read VERIFIED
✉ 𝕏 ⌘
Tax disclaimer: Crypto tax law in both Italy and the US is evolving rapidly. Consult a qualified professional before making filing decisions.

Italy was one of the last major European economies to establish a clear cryptocurrency tax framework. The wait ended with the Legge di Bilancio 2023 (Law 197/2022), which for the first time treated crypto assets as a defined class subject to explicit capital gains taxation. For 2025, those rules remain in force with one meaningful addition: a simplified flat-tax election for long-term holders.

American residents of Italy face the situation that defines expat life: two tax systems, both with legitimate claims on the same gains. Understanding how they interact — and how the Foreign Tax Credit prevents genuine double taxation — is the core planning challenge.

Italy's 2025 Crypto Tax Framework

Italy classifies cryptocurrency as crypto-attività under Article 67 of the TUIR. Gains are treated as "miscellaneous capital gains" — the same category as foreign currency gains. Key rules for 2025:

  • Crypto-to-crypto swaps are taxable events
  • Using crypto to buy goods or services is a taxable event
  • NFT transactions follow the same rules
  • Transfers between your own wallets are NOT taxable events
  • Crypto received as salary or freelance payment is taxed as ordinary income

The 26% Rate and the €2,000 Threshold

The capital gains rate on crypto in Italy is 26%. The threshold: if your total crypto holdings never exceeded €2,000 in value at any single point during the year, gains may be exempt. Once the portfolio touches €2,001 at any moment, the exemption disappears and all net realized gains are taxable at 26%.

Threshold trap to avoid

If you held crypto worth €1,900 in January but €2,100 in March, the threshold is breached for the entire year — even if you sold at a loss. Check all-time-high portfolio valuations, not just end-of-year balances.

The Flat Tax Election for 2025

For the 2025 tax year, taxpayers can elect to pay €1,000 per €10,000 of crypto holdings as of January 1, 2025, as a substitute tax (imposta sostitutiva) instead of calculating actual gains. At €50,000 of holdings, the flat tax is €5,000. If your actual gains would have exceeded ~€19,200, the 26% rate would cost more — making the flat election advantageous.

Important: the IRS may not treat this Italian flat substitute tax as a creditable foreign tax under FTC rules. Do not assume paying the flat Italian tax eliminates US capital gains obligations.

Staking, DeFi, and Airdrop Income

Per Agenzia delle Entrate Circular 30/2023:

  • Staking rewards: Ordinary income, taxed at IRPEF bracket rates (up to 43%) — significantly higher than the 26% capital gains rate
  • DeFi yield / liquidity pool rewards: Same as staking
  • Airdrops: Ordinary income at fair market value on receipt date
  • Hard forks: Treated as zero-cost-basis capital gain events

The US takes a similar approach — staking and airdrops are ordinary income. This creates FTC alignment: both systems tax rewards as income, making credit coordination more straightforward than for capital gains.

US Obligations in Parallel

Crypto is US property under IRS Notice 2014-21. Every transaction must be reported on Form 8949 and Schedule D regardless of Italian tax paid. Key 2025 US rates:

  • Short-term gains (held under 12 months): ordinary income rates up to 37%
  • Long-term gains (held 12+ months): 0%, 15%, or 20%. Most Americans in Italy land at 15%.

Avoiding Double Taxation

The Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116) is your primary tool. If you realize a €10,000 Bitcoin gain, Italy taxes it at 26% (€2,600). The same gain at 15% US long-term rate equals roughly $1,620. You credit the Italian tax against US liability, reducing your US bill to zero on that transaction. The excess Italian tax credit carries forward 10 years.

This works cleanly for capital gains. It is more complex for staking income (which requires separate FTC baskets) and may not work at all for the flat substitute tax election.

FBAR and FATCA for Crypto

Current IRS position (2025): cryptocurrency itself is not reportable on FBAR or Form 8938. However, if your exchange account holds a fiat EUR balance, that fiat balance in a non-US account may trigger FBAR reporting if aggregate foreign accounts exceed $10,000. EU-based exchanges that hold EUR balances are foreign financial accounts for FBAR purposes. This area is actively evolving — Congress has proposed crypto FBAR requirements multiple times.

What to Report on Your Italian Return

Crypto gains go on Quadro RT of the Modello Redditi PF. Staking income goes in Quadro RL. You need transaction-level records: acquisition date, cost in EUR, sale date, proceeds in EUR, and calculated gain/loss per transaction. Use dedicated crypto tax software (Koinly, CoinTracking, or TaxBit all generate Italian tax reports) for anything beyond a handful of trades.

FAQ

If I bought Bitcoin in the US before moving to Italy, does Italy tax those gains?
Italy taxes gains on disposals made while you are an Italian tax resident, regardless of when the asset was acquired. If you sell Bitcoin while living in Italy, Italy taxes the gain — even if you bought it in 2019 in New York. Cost basis is your original purchase price.
Is Coinbase reportable to Italian authorities?
Under EU DAC8 (effective 2026), European crypto platforms will report account holder information to EU tax authorities. Non-EU platforms serving EU residents will also have reporting obligations. Voluntary compliance is increasingly important ahead of this regime.
Can I offset crypto losses against other Italian capital gains?
Yes. Crypto losses can offset other capital gains in the same Quadro RT basket (financial capital gains). They cannot offset ordinary income or wages. Losses can be carried forward four years within the same category.
