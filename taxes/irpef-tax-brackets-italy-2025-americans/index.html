IRPEF Tax Brackets in Italy: What Americans Pay in 2025

Italy consolidated to 3 IRPEF brackets in 2024. Here's what Americans pay, how deductions work, and how IRPEF interacts with your U.S. tax obligations.

IRPEF (Imposta sul Reddito delle Persone Fisiche) is Italy's personal income tax — the equivalent of U.S. federal income tax. If you're an Italian tax resident, IRPEF applies to your worldwide income. Understanding the brackets, deductions, and how IRPEF interacts with your U.S. obligations is essential for financial planning as an American in Italy.

2025 IRPEF Tax Brackets

Italy's IRPEF uses a progressive rate structure. The 2025 brackets (established by the Legge di Bilancio 2024 and effective for 2025) are:

Taxable IncomeIRPEF Rate
€0 – €28,00023%
€28,001 – €50,00035%
Over €50,00043%

Important change from prior years: Italy consolidated from 4 brackets to 3 effective 2024. The previous 25% bracket (€15,001–€28,000) was eliminated, with the 23% rate now applying to all income up to €28,000.

Legal reference: TUIR (DPR 917/1986) as amended by L. 213/2023 (Legge di Bilancio 2024)

Additional Taxes on Top of IRPEF

IRPEF is not the only tax on your income. Italian residents also pay:

Addizionale Regionale (Regional Income Tax)

A regional surcharge on top of IRPEF, set by each Italian region:

RegionRate
Piedmont1.62%
Lombardy1.23%
Tuscany1.42%
Lazio (Rome)1.73%
Campania (Naples)2.03%
Sicily1.73%
Emilia-Romagna1.33%

Rates are applied to the same taxable income as IRPEF.

Addizionale Comunale (Municipal Income Tax)

Each municipality (Comune) sets its own surcharge, typically 0.1% – 0.8% of taxable income. Your Comune's rate is applied on your tax return.

Total Effective Tax Rate Example

American living in Milan, €40,000 taxable income:

  • IRPEF: (€28,000 × 23%) + (€12,000 × 35%) = €6,440 + €4,200 = €10,640
  • Regional (Lombardy 1.23%): €40,000 × 1.23% = €492
  • Municipal (Milan ~0.5%): €40,000 × 0.5% = €200
  • Total Italian income tax: ~€11,332 (28.3% effective rate)

What Counts as Taxable Income (Base Imponibile)

If you're an Italian fiscal resident (183+ days/year in Italy, or primary residence/center of vital interests in Italy), taxable income includes:

Redditi da lavoro dipendente (employment income from Italian employer) Redditi da lavoro autonomo (self-employment — Partita IVA, Regime Ordinario) Redditi da capitale (capital income — interest, dividends) Redditi fondiari (property income — Italian rental income) Redditi diversi (miscellaneous — capital gains, etc.) Foreign income — as an Italian resident, worldwide income is taxable

Excluded from IRPEF (taxed separately or exempt):

  • Income taxed under Regime Forfettario (flat-rate self-employment) — taxed at 15% or 5%, separate from IRPEF
  • Some financial income taxed with 26% flat substitute tax (cedolare secca)
  • Foreign income covered by the Flat Tax for New Residents regime (€100,000/year flat)

Key Deductions That Reduce Your IRPEF

Italy's IRPEF system includes several deductions and credits that reduce your actual tax bill:

Detrazioni per Lavoro (Work Income Credits)

If you have Italian employment or self-employment income, you receive automatic credits that reduce IRPEF. The credit scales down as income increases:

  • Income under €15,000: credit of €1,880 (employment) or €1,265 (self-employment)
  • Income €15,001–€28,000: graduated credit
  • Income over €50,000: reduced credit

Detrazioni per Carichi Familiari (Family Credits)

Credits for dependent family members:

  • Dependent spouse: up to €800/year credit
  • Each dependent child under 21: handled through INPS "assegno unico" (universal child allowance) rather than tax credits from 2022

Deduction for Mortgage Interest (Prima Casa)

19% deduction on up to €4,000/year of mortgage interest on your Italian primary residence.

Medical Expense Deduction

19% deduction on medical expenses exceeding €129.11/year. Includes doctor visits, prescriptions, dental, glasses.

Italian Rental Income Deduction Options

Rental income from Italian property can be taxed under:

  • Regime Ordinario: Added to IRPEF income; deduct 5% of rent as flat expense
  • Cedolare Secca: Flat 21% (or 10% for certain contracts) substitute tax — often more convenient

No Mundo Deduction (for Foreign Tax Credit)

When you've already paid taxes on income abroad (U.S. taxes on U.S.-source income), you claim a credito d'imposta per redditi prodotti all'estero (foreign tax credit) on your Italian return. This prevents the same income from being fully taxed twice.

How IRPEF Interacts With U.S. Tax Obligations

As a U.S. citizen living in Italy, you have tax obligations to both countries. The US-Italy Tax Treaty (1999) and domestic law prevent most double taxation:

The Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) Approach

Most Americans in Italy use the Foreign Tax Credit strategy:

  • Pay IRPEF in Italy on income
  • Claim those Italian taxes paid as a credit against U.S. tax liability on the same income
  • Since IRPEF rates (23–43%) are generally equal to or higher than U.S. rates (10–37%), Italian taxes often fully offset U.S. liability

Result: You pay Italian rates (higher), zero additional U.S. tax on the same income.

The FEIE Alternative

The Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (Form 2555) excludes up to $126,500 (2024) of earned income from U.S. taxable income. However:

  • You cannot claim both FEIE and FTC on the same income
  • FEIE doesn't help with passive income (dividends, interest, pensions)
  • For most Americans in Italy where IRPEF rates exceed U.S. rates, the FTC is usually more advantageous

Get a dual-qualified U.S./Italian accountant to model which approach saves you more in your specific situation.

IRPEF Filing: The Italian Tax Return

Who Files

All Italian fiscal residents with income above the minimum thresholds must file an annual return.

Filing not required if:

  • You only have employment income from one Italian employer (your employer withholds via CU)
  • Income is below threshold (~€8,000 for employees)

Filing required if:

  • You have self-employment income (Partita IVA Ordinario)
  • You have foreign income
  • You have multiple income sources
  • You want to claim deductions beyond what was withheld

The Forms

Modello 730: Simpler form for employees and retirees; processed through your employer or a CAF (tax assistance center); refunds/balances handled via payroll Modello Redditi PF: Full personal income tax return; required for self-employed and those with foreign income; filed directly with Agenzia delle Entrate or through a commercialista

Deadlines

  • Modello 730: Submitted by September 30 (for prior year income)
  • Modello Redditi PF: Submitted by November 30 (for prior year income)
  • Tax payments: IRPEF due in two instalments — June (80% of estimated liability) and November (remaining 20%)

Using a Commercialista

For Americans with U.S. and Italian income, a commercialista (Italian CPA) who understands U.S.-Italy tax situations is essential. Cost: €300–800/year for routine compliance. Worth every cent to avoid errors that trigger audits or penalties.

Special Regimes That Replace IRPEF

Two major regimes allow Italian residents to pay a substitute tax instead of standard IRPEF rates:

Regime Forfettario (Flat Rate)

For self-employed with annual revenue under €85,000. Pay 15% (or 5% for first 5 years) on a percentage of revenue — no IRPEF, no IVA, minimal compliance. The most favorable tax regime in Italy for qualifying freelancers and consultants.

Flat Tax for New Residents (€100,000/year)

For qualifying new residents: pay €100,000/year flat tax on all foreign income regardless of amount. Italian-source income taxed normally under IRPEF. Advantageous for those with very high foreign income (generally €500,000+/year).

Practical Tax Planning for Americans in Italy

Open a Partita IVA in Forfettario if you qualify. At 5% tax for 5 years on self-employment income, it's one of the most favorable tax regimes in the developed world.

Time your Italian residency establishment carefully. Becoming an Italian fiscal resident mid-year means you're taxed as Italian resident for the full year only if you spend 183+ days. If you arrive in August, you might not be Italian resident for that year.

Keep records of all U.S. taxes paid. These are your FTC documentation for your Italian return. Your Italian commercialista needs your U.S. 1040 and any tax payment records.

Declare foreign assets (Quadro RW). Italian residents must declare foreign bank accounts, investments, and real estate on their Italian tax return. The IVAFE (0.2% on foreign financial assets) and IVIE (0.76% on foreign real estate) apply. Failure to declare carries significant penalties.
