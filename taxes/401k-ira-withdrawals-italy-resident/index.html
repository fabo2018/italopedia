401(k) and IRA Withdrawals as an Italian Resident: The Tax Reality

The US-Italy tax treaty gives Italy primary taxing rights over most US pension distributions. What that means for your retirement income planning, the withholding problem, and the RMD rules that follow you across the ocean.

By Fabrizio Boggio· Published April 11, 2024· Updated May 20, 2025·12 min read
✓ Sources: US-Italy Tax Treaty 1999 (Art. 18, 20) · IRS Publication 575 · SECURE Act 2.0
Tax disclaimer: Pension and retirement income taxation across two jurisdictions is highly fact-specific. This article provides a framework — engage a qualified US-Italy tax professional for your specific situation.

Retiring to Italy with substantial US retirement accounts is a genuinely complex financial situation — and one that most financial advisors in the US are not equipped to navigate. The US-Italy tax treaty determines which country gets to tax your distributions, but the treaty language is ambiguous in places, the IRS and Agenzia delle Entrate interpret it differently in practice, and the interaction between Italian IRPEF and the US Foreign Tax Credit creates planning opportunities that require careful orchestration.

This guide covers the framework. The details of your specific situation — account types, income levels, other income sources, Italian residency status — will determine the optimal strategy. Use this as orientation, not as a substitute for professional advice.

What the US-Italy Treaty Says About Pensions

The 1999 US-Italy Tax Treaty (last updated by the 2009 protocol) distributes taxing rights over pension income between the two countries. The key articles:

  • Article 18 (Private Pensions): Distributions from private pension plans — including 401(k), traditional IRA, 403(b), and similar employer-sponsored plans — are "taxable only in the Contracting State in which the recipient is a resident." As an Italian resident, Italy has exclusive taxing rights over these distributions. The US should not tax them — though US withholding creates a practical problem we address below.
  • Article 20 (Government Service / Social Security): US Social Security benefits are taxable only by the United States. Italy cannot tax them. This is the reverse of the private pension rule and a significant planning advantage for retirees with substantial Social Security income.

Traditional 401(k) and Traditional IRA

Under Article 18, Italy taxes traditional 401(k) and IRA distributions at ordinary IRPEF rates. For 2025, IRPEF brackets are: 23% up to €28,000, 35% on €28,001–€50,000, and 43% above €50,000.

For most American retirees in Italy, their Italian-sourced income is low (perhaps €0–€20,000 from Social Security and minor Italian sources) and their 401(k)/IRA distributions make up the bulk of their income. The IRPEF on those distributions will typically fall in the 23–35% range, depending on distribution size.

The Foreign Tax Credit works here: the Italian IRPEF paid on your 401(k) distributions can be credited against any residual US tax obligation on the same income. Since Article 18 gives Italy exclusive taxing rights, US tax should theoretically be zero — but withholding at source is a practical issue.

Roth IRA: The Unsettled Question

The Roth IRA is a uniquely American tax construct that Italy does not recognize. In the US, Roth qualified distributions are tax-free — you paid tax going in, and withdrawals are tax-free in retirement. Italy does not have an equivalent concept and does not give favorable treatment to Roth distributions based on the US tax treatment.

The Agenzia delle Entrate's position: Roth distributions are potentially taxable as ordinary income in Italy. The treaty does not explicitly address Roth IRAs (which were created after the treaty was negotiated). Practice varies among Italian tax professionals — some argue the Roth falls under Article 18 (private pensions, taxable in Italy); others argue the tax-free treatment should be honored; others are unsure.

This is one of the most important reasons to engage a US-Italy specialist before retiring here. See our dedicated article: Roth IRA in Italy: Tax-Free Doesn't Mean Tax-Free.

Social Security: Treaty-Exempt from Italy

Article 20 of the treaty is clear: US Social Security is taxable only by the United States. Italy has no taxing rights over it. For the 50–85% of Social Security that is taxable under US law, you owe US tax at your marginal rate — and no Italian tax credit offsets that, because no Italian tax is paid on this income.

Planning implication: retirees who optimize to maximize Social Security income relative to 401(k)/IRA distributions may inadvertently create a higher US tax burden (since Social Security triggers US tax with no Italian offset) while minimizing Italian-taxable income. Model both scenarios carefully.

The US Withholding Problem

Here is the practical problem: your 401(k) custodian in the US withholds federal income tax from distributions at the statutory rate (typically 20% mandatory withholding for eligible rollover distributions) regardless of treaty provisions. The IRS does not automatically reduce withholding because you live in Italy.

To reduce US withholding based on treaty provisions, you must file Form W-8BEN with your custodian claiming treaty benefits, and potentially attach a statement claiming Article 18 relief. Not all custodians accept this for domestic accounts — this is an area where practice differs between financial institutions.

The practical alternative used by many expats: accept the US withholding, file your US 1040 claiming a refund for over-withheld tax, and pay Italian IRPEF on the distribution. The net result is roughly the same, but your cash flow has a timing mismatch — you pay Italian tax in one year and get the US refund 6–12 months later.

RMDs Do Not Stop at the Border

Required Minimum Distributions are an IRS rule tied to your age, not your residence. Under SECURE Act 2.0, the RMD starting age is 73 (rising to 75 in 2033). Whether you live in Peoria or Perugia, you must take your RMD from traditional IRAs and 401(k)s starting at age 73 or face a 25% excise tax on the amount you should have withdrawn.

Italy will tax your RMD distributions as ordinary income under Article 18 — same treatment as any other traditional IRA distribution. The RMD calculation itself is a US concept and is done using IRS Uniform Lifetime Tables applied to your December 31 account balance.

Withdrawal Sequencing Strategy

The standard US-based Roth conversion ladder advice — convert traditional IRA money to Roth in low-income years before RMDs begin — changes significantly when you live in Italy. Because Italy taxes 401(k)/IRA distributions but not Social Security, and because Italy taxes Roth distributions (potentially), the optimal sequencing in Italy may differ from the optimal US sequencing.

A rough framework for Italian residents:

  • Social Security income: take as early or late as US analysis suggests, but remember that Italy does not tax it — it creates US tax liability but not Italian
  • Traditional IRA/401(k): distributions taxed by Italy at IRPEF rates; Foreign Tax Credits reduce US liability; large withdrawals push into the 35–43% Italian bracket
  • Roth IRA: treat with caution until the Italian tax treatment is clarified for your situation; distributions may not be tax-free in Italy
  • General principle: spread distributions across tax years to avoid pushing Italian income into the 35–43% bracket if possible

FBAR and Reporting

Your US retirement accounts — 401(k), IRA, Roth IRA — held at US custodians are US financial accounts. They are NOT reportable on FBAR (FinCEN 114), which covers only foreign financial accounts. You do not report your Fidelity or Vanguard IRA on FBAR.

Italian pension-equivalent accounts, if you ever open one, would be foreign accounts and FBAR-reportable. US-held accounts: never FBAR-reportable regardless of where you live.

FAQ

Can I keep my 401(k) in the US while living in Italy?
Yes. There is no requirement to liquidate or move your US retirement accounts when moving abroad. You keep them at your US custodian, continue taking distributions (or deferring under RMD rules), and handle the tax reporting in both countries as described above.
Does Italy tax my 401(k) on the entire balance or just withdrawals?
Italy taxes distributions as you take them — not the underlying account balance. The account sitting at Fidelity is not an annual Italian tax event. Only actual distributions or in-plan conversions trigger Italian taxation.
If I retire to Italy under the 7% flat tax for southern regions, does that change things?
Yes, significantly. The 7% flat tax on all foreign-source income (available in certain southern Italian municipalities) would apply a 7% rate to your 401(k)/IRA distributions instead of standard IRPEF rates of 23–43%. This is one of the most compelling tax advantages for American retirees with substantial US retirement income — and one of the primary financial arguments for settling in Calabria, Basilicata, or parts of Sicily rather than Tuscany.
What happens to my 401(k) if I die while living in Italy?
Your 401(k) beneficiary designations control distribution — Italian succession law does not override US beneficiary designations on qualified retirement accounts. However, Italy may have inheritance tax implications on the value transferred to Italian-resident beneficiaries. See our guide on Italian inheritance and gift tax.
