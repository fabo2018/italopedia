Capital Gains Tax in Italy 2025: Investments, Property, and Crypto

Italy uses a flat 26% rate for most financial asset gains and special rules for property. American residents face additional US reporting on the same gains. Here is the complete framework.

By Fabrizio Boggio· Published August 28, 2024· Updated May 12, 2025·11 min read
✓ Sources: TUIR Art. 67–68 · D.Lgs. 461/1997 · Agenzia delle Entrate

Italy is not a zero-capital-gains-tax country — but it is also not as punishing as some European neighbors. The flat 26% rate for financial asset gains is lower than Italy's top ordinary income rate of 43%, and property gains have meaningful exemptions for primary residence and long-term holdings. For Americans, the picture is more complex because US law taxes the same gains independently — but the Foreign Tax Credit generally eliminates double taxation.

The Italian Capital Gains Framework

Italian capital gains are governed primarily by Articles 67–68 of the TUIR and D.Lgs. 461/1997. The system distinguishes between:

  • Financial capital gains (plusvalenze finanziarie): gains from securities, funds, derivatives, crypto — taxed at a flat 26% substitute tax (imposta sostitutiva)
  • Real property capital gains (plusvalenze immobiliari): gains from selling real estate — taxed as ordinary income (IRPEF) or, by election, at a flat 26%
  • Qualified participations: stakes of more than 20% in unlisted companies — taxed partly as ordinary income

Financial Assets: Stocks, ETFs, Mutual Funds

The 26% flat substitute tax applies to net gains from:

  • Listed Italian and foreign stocks
  • ETFs (exchange-traded funds)
  • Mutual funds and SICAVs
  • Bonds (above a de minimis threshold for government bonds)
  • Options, warrants, and other derivatives
  • Cryptocurrency (see dedicated section)

Losses offset: Capital losses within the financial assets category can offset gains in the same category, with a 4-year carry-forward for net losses. You cannot offset financial capital losses against ordinary income (salaries, business income).

How Italian banks handle it: If your brokerage account is held at an Italian bank or Italian broker (amministrato regime), the bank withholds the 26% automatically on each gain — you do not have to report it yourself. If you hold foreign accounts (US brokerage), you self-report on Quadro RT of your Italian return.

Property Capital Gains

Property capital gains in Italy depend heavily on holding period and primary residence status:

Property gain exemptions
  • Held more than 5 years: Generally exempt from capital gains tax (the most common scenario for long-term owners)
  • Primary residence (prima casa): Exempt if the property was your registered primary residence for the majority of the ownership period
  • Inherited property: Generally exempt regardless of holding period
  • Held under 5 years, not primary residence: Taxable — either as ordinary income (IRPEF, up to 43%) or at the flat 26% rate by election at the notary

For Americans buying Italian property as an investment: if you sell within 5 years, you will pay Italian capital gains tax (26% by election at notary is the standard approach). The same gain is reportable on your US Schedule D. The FTC on Italian capital gains tax paid reduces your US liability.

Cryptocurrency

Crypto capital gains follow the same 26% flat rate as other financial assets, with a specific €2,000 portfolio-value threshold below which gains may be exempt. The rules are complex and were overhauled in 2023. See our dedicated guide: Cryptocurrency Tax in Italy 2025.

US Capital Gains Reporting in Parallel

Every capital gain you realize on Italian or foreign assets must be reported on your US Form 1040, Schedule D and Form 8949 — regardless of where you live. The US does not exempt foreign-sourced capital gains for Americans abroad. There is no FEIE equivalent for capital gains (the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion covers earned income only).

US capital gains rates for 2025:

  • Short-term (under 12 months): ordinary income rates up to 37%
  • Long-term (12+ months): 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on total income

Using the Foreign Tax Credit on Capital Gains

The Foreign Tax Credit allows you to credit Italian capital gains tax paid against your US capital gains tax on the same assets. The credit is computed on a separate Form 1116 for the "passive income" basket (where most financial capital gains live).

Key point: Italy taxes at 26% flat; the US taxes long-term gains at 15–20% for most Americans. If your Italian tax rate (26%) exceeds your US rate (15%), you will have excess FTCs that can be carried forward 10 years. In this scenario, you pay Italian tax but owe zero US tax on those gains — and bank the excess credit for future use.

The PFIC Problem for Americans in Italy

This is an important issue that many Americans in Italy are unaware of. Under US tax law, foreign mutual funds, ETFs (non-US exchange listed), and investment vehicles held outside the US qualify as Passive Foreign Investment Companies (PFICs). The US taxes PFICs at punitive rates — ordinary income rates plus an interest charge on deferred gains — rather than the preferential capital gains rates that would apply to US-domiciled equivalents.

What this means in practice: if you buy Italian mutual funds, EU-listed ETFs, or similar Italian investment products, you may face dramatically higher US taxation than you expected, even with FTCs. Many American financial advisors who work with Italian expats recommend holding US-domiciled ETFs in US brokerage accounts rather than buying Italian or EU investment products, specifically to avoid the PFIC regime.

Before investing in any Italian or EU financial product, confirm with your US tax advisor whether it qualifies as a PFIC under US rules. The compliance cost and tax cost of a PFIC investment can far exceed any Italian tax savings.

FAQ

If I sell US stocks (held in a US brokerage) while living in Italy, does Italy tax the gain?
Yes. Italy taxes Italian residents on worldwide capital gains, including gains from US stocks held in US accounts. The gain is reportable in Quadro RT of your Italian return. The FTC reduces your US tax on the same gain. Your US broker will also issue a 1099-B as normal.
Can I offset my Italian property gain against Italian investment losses?
No. Property capital gains and financial capital gains are in different Italian tax categories and cannot offset each other. Financial losses offset financial gains (4-year carryforward). Property gains stand alone.
Is dividend income taxed at the same 26% rate?
Yes — Italian-source dividends and most foreign dividends received by Italian residents are subject to the same 26% substitute tax. Italian banks withhold automatically; foreign dividends self-reported on Quadro RL.
