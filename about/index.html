The story behind
Italopedia

Italopedia is a one-person editorial operation in Piedmont, Italy. This is who I am, why I built this, and how the work actually gets done.

Sara · photo placeholder
Sara Bianchi · editor-in-chief · Piedmont, Italy

Ciao, I'm Sara.

I grew up in Brooklyn with an Italian grandmother who put a tablespoon of olive oil in everything — including, somehow, the morning coffee. In 2019, after a decade as a journalist in New York, I moved back to my grandmother's region of Piedmont. I have been here since.

I started Italopedia in 2023 because the existing English-language information about moving to Italy was — to put it gently — bad. Most of it was written by people who had never actually walked into an Italian questura. Some of it was AI-generated nonsense. Almost none of it cited official sources. The errors were sometimes funny and sometimes expensive.

This site is my attempt to do it right. Every guide is researched against the Italian government's own publications, verified against my own walking-in-and-doing-it experience, and reviewed by an Italian commercialista or lawyer when the subject demands it. When the law changes, I update the page. When I get it wrong, I fix it publicly.

What you'll find here

Italopedia covers the eight categories that matter most to Americans moving to Italy: visas, residency, taxes, healthcare, property, citizenship, regions, and cost of living. The guides are long because the topics are genuinely complex — Italian bureaucracy does not reward summarisation. But they are also organised so you can find what you need quickly: hubs at the category level, deep articles where the detail lives, sidebars with the practical next steps.

"My promise to readers: I will not waste your time. Every fact you read here was tracked back to an Italian government source. Every procedure was performed, not paraphrased."

— Sara Bianchi

How a guide gets made

01
Source from Italian agencies
Every fact traces back to esteri.it, agenziaentrate.gov.it, salute.gov.it, or the Gazzetta Ufficiale.
02
Verified by walking the process
If the article describes a queue at the questura, someone (usually me) has stood in that queue.
03
Professional review
Tax, immigration and inheritance pieces are reviewed by an Italian commercialista or lawyer.
04
90-day re-verification
Every article is revisited at least quarterly. "Last verified" dates are honest — we don't backdate.
