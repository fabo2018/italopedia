Italy Investor Visa (Golden Visa): €500k Business / €1M Bond — Complete 2025 Guide

Four investment options (€250k–€2M), a 30-day Nulla Osta, a 2-year permit, and one of the most powerful flat-tax pairings in Europe. Everything Americans need to know about Italy's investor visa program.

Quick Answer

Italy's Investor Visa (Visto per Investitori) grants a 2-year renewable residence permit to Americans who make a qualifying investment: €500,000 in an Italian company, €250,000 in an innovative startup, €2,000,000 in government bonds, or €1,000,000 in a philanthropic initiative. The visa uses a fast-track Nulla Osta (pre-clearance) system — the government committee must respond within 30 days. It pairs powerfully with Italy's €100,000 flat tax regime for high-net-worth Americans.

What Is the Italy Investor Visa?

Introduced in 2017 and governed by Art. 26-bis of the Immigration Law (D.Lgs. 286/1998), the Italy Investor Visa was designed to attract foreign capital into the Italian economy. Unlike many European golden visa programs that require real estate purchases, Italy's program focuses on productive investments — companies, startups, bonds, and philanthropy.

The visa grants:

  • A 2-year residence permit (permesso di soggiorno per investitori), renewable for 3 years
  • The right to live and work in Italy
  • The ability to bring immediate family members
  • A pathway to permanent residency after 5 years and citizenship after 10 years

The Four Investment Options

Option 1: €500,000 in an Italian Company

Invest at least €500,000 in the share capital of an Italian company (SRL or SpA) that is not a real estate company. The company must be incorporated in Italy and operating. This is the most popular option for Americans who want to participate in or control an Italian business.

Option 2: €250,000 in an Italian Innovative Startup

Invest at least €250,000 in the share capital of an Italian innovative startup registered in the Italian Business Register's special section. This is the lowest threshold and attractive for Americans with entrepreneurial interests. Italy has a robust startup ecosystem in Milan, Turin, and Rome.

Option 3: €2,000,000 in Italian Government Bonds

Purchase at least €2,000,000 in Italian government bonds (Titoli di Stato) and hold them for at least 2 years. The most passive option — no business involvement required, purely financial.

Option 4: €1,000,000 in a Philanthropic Initiative

Donate at least €1,000,000 to a qualifying Italian public interest initiative — cultural, educational, immigration-related, scientific research, or restoration of cultural heritage. Unlike the other options, this is a donation, not an investment — you do not get it back.

Who Qualifies?

To apply for the Italy Investor Visa, you must:

  • Be a non-EU citizen (Americans qualify)
  • Have a clean criminal record
  • Hold a valid passport
  • Have the financial resources to make the qualifying investment
  • Not be subject to any entry ban in the Schengen Area

There is no minimum net worth requirement beyond the investment itself. No language requirement. No age restriction.

The Application Process: Step by Step

Step 1: Apply for the Nulla Osta (Pre-Clearance)

The Italy Investor Visa uses a unique pre-clearance system called the Nulla Osta, managed by a dedicated government committee (Comitato per gli investitori esteri). This is what makes the Italian investor visa faster than most Italian visa processes.

Documents required:

  • Valid passport
  • Curriculum vitae
  • Description of the proposed investment
  • Proof of available funds (bank statements, investment portfolio statements)
  • Criminal background check from your country of residence
  • Declaration of intent to make the investment
⏱ Timeline

The committee issues the Nulla Osta within 30 days of receiving a complete application. This is legally guaranteed.

Step 2: Apply for the Visa at the Italian Consulate

Once you have the Nulla Osta, apply for the actual visa at the Italian consulate in your US state of jurisdiction. Additional documents needed: Nulla Osta certificate, completed visa application form, visa fee, health insurance, proof of accommodation in Italy. Processing time: typically 2–4 weeks after Nulla Osta.

Step 3: Enter Italy and Complete the Investment

You have 3 months from visa issuance to enter Italy and make the qualifying investment. The investment must be completed after entering Italy, not before.

Step 4: Apply for the Permesso di Soggiorno

Within 8 business days of arriving, apply for your permesso di soggiorno per investitori at the local questura via the standard kit postale process. Your permesso will be issued for 2 years.

Step 5: Renew for 3 Years

After the initial 2-year permit, renew for 3 years. You must demonstrate that the investment is still in place and maintained.

The €100,000 Flat Tax Connection

The Italy Investor Visa pairs powerfully with Italy's €100,000 flat tax regime (Art. 24-bis TUIR). Americans who establish Italian residency under this regime pay a flat €100,000 annual tax on all foreign-source income, regardless of amount, for up to 15 years.

For high-net-worth Americans: make a €500,000 investment in an Italian company → qualify for the Investor Visa → establish Italian tax residency → elect the €100,000 flat tax → all US income (dividends, capital gains, rental income) is taxed at a fixed €100,000/year in Italy. This combination is one of the most powerful tax planning structures available to wealthy Americans considering European residency.

Family Members

Your spouse, minor children, and dependent adult children can receive residence permits as family members (familiare al seguito). They receive the same duration permit as the primary investor.

Path to Citizenship

  • After 5 years of continuous legal residence: eligible for long-term EU residence permit
  • After 10 years of continuous legal residence: eligible to apply for Italian citizenship by naturalization (A2 Italian required)

Practical Considerations for Americans

Due diligence on startup investments: The €250,000 startup option is appealing but risky. Italy's startup ecosystem is real but less mature than Silicon Valley. Conduct thorough due diligence with a local advisor.

Currency risk: Your investment is in euros. If the dollar strengthens, your invested capital is worth less in dollar terms.

US tax implications: A significant investment in an Italian company creates Italian-source income that interacts with both tax systems. Work with a dual-qualified US-Italian tax advisor before committing.

The philanthropic option: The €1M donation receives no financial return. However, it may be deductible on your Italian and possibly US tax return.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I buy Italian real estate with investor visa funds?
No. Real estate investment does not qualify under any of the four options. Italy deliberately excluded real estate to avoid the speculation issues seen in other European golden visa programs.

Do I need to live in Italy full-time?
No minimum stay requirement exists for the investor visa itself. However, to maintain Italian tax residency (and benefit from the flat tax), you must spend more than 183 days/year in Italy or be registered in the anagrafe.

Can I lose the visa if the company I invested in fails?
If your investment is lost due to company failure, you risk losing eligibility for renewal. Keep documentation of the investment's status and consult your lawyer if the company faces financial difficulties.

Is the €100,000 flat tax guaranteed for 15 years?
The regime is statutory law and has been maintained since 2017 with no retroactive changes to existing beneficiaries. However, Italian tax legislation changes annually.
