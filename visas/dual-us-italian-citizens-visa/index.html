Dual US-Italian Citizens: Do You Need a Visa to Move to Italy?

Short answer: no. As an Italian citizen, Italy is your home and you have the unrestricted right to live there. But "no visa required" comes with a set of specific obligations — AIRE registration, anagrafe enrollment, tax implications, and passport logistics — that dual citizens often navigate incorrectly. Here is the complete picture.

The visa and permesso di soggiorno system that most of this site describes is built for non-EU nationals who need permission to live in Italy. Italian citizens do not need permission — Italy is their country. If you have obtained Italian citizenship (through jure sanguinis, naturalization, or birth), you have the unconditional right to live, work, and reside in Italy without any additional immigration formality.

This does not mean the administrative process is entirely smooth. Italy still requires you to register your presence through the domestic administrative system. The difference is that this is a registration of an existing right, not an application for permission.

Why You Need No Visa

Italian citizenship grants you full rights as both an Italian national and an EU citizen. As an EU citizen:

  • You have the free movement right under EU law (Directive 2004/38/EC)
  • You can live and work in any EU member state without immigration formality
  • You enter Italy — and all Schengen Area countries — using your Italian passport or Italian identity card; you are not subject to immigration control
  • The 90/180 Schengen day limit does not apply to you
  • You do not apply for a permesso di soggiorno (that is only for non-EU nationals)

From Italy's perspective, you are returning home. The administrative response is registration, not approval.

What You DO Need When Moving to Italy

Even without a visa or permesso, there are mandatory steps when you relocate to Italy:

  1. Cancel your AIRE registration (if you were registered as an Italian living abroad)
  2. Register at your comune's anagrafe (population registry) as a resident
  3. Obtain or update your Italian identity card (carta d'identità) — useful for travel within the EU and Schengen Area
  4. Obtain or renew your Italian passport if it has expired
  5. Ensure your codice fiscale is active and update it with your Italian address

None of these requires external approval. They are all administrative registrations that confirm your status and give you access to Italian services (healthcare, voting, official documents). The anagrafe registration also triggers your Italian tax residency — see the tax section below.

AIRE: Canceling Your Registration

AIRE (Anagrafe degli Italiani Residenti all'Estero) is the Italian registry of Italian citizens who reside abroad. If you became an Italian citizen while living in the US, or if you were previously registered in Italy and then moved abroad, you should be on the AIRE register at the Italian consulate in the US.

When you move back to Italy, you need to cancel your AIRE registration and transfer to the domestic anagrafe. The process:

  1. Notify the consulate of your departure from the US (some consulates handle this proactively; others require a formal notification)
  2. Register at your Italian comune's anagrafe (the anagrafe automatically notifies AIRE of your return)

In practice, registering at the anagrafe usually handles the AIRE cancellation automatically — the two systems communicate. But follow up with your consulate if you have concerns about the AIRE status being updated.

Note: if you obtained Italian citizenship via jure sanguinis and registered at the consulate without previously being in Italy's domestic anagrafe, your first registration step is simply the anagrafe enrollment — there may be no existing AIRE record to cancel.

Registering at the Anagrafe

The anagrafe (registro della popolazione) is the civil population registry of your Italian municipality. Registering there is mandatory for Italian citizens residing in Italy and grants you:

  • Access to the Italian National Health Service (SSN) — free enrollment, choosing a medico di base
  • The right to vote in Italian elections
  • An Italian official address for all administrative purposes
  • Access to public services (libraries, social services, discounted municipal cultural programs)

What to bring to the anagrafe:

  • Italian passport or identity card
  • Your codice fiscale
  • Proof of Italian address (lease, property deed, or a declaration from a family member who is already resident at the address)
  • A self-declaration of your change of residence (modulo di dichiarazione di residenza — available at the anagrafe office or downloadable from your comune's website)

The anagrafe officer will typically verify your residence within 45 days of your registration (a municipal officer may knock to confirm you actually live at the address). Provisional registration is granted immediately; official confirmation follows after verification.

Which Passport to Use Where

Dual nationality creates a practical question that every dual US-Italian citizen navigates: which passport do you travel on?

Passport usage rules
DestinationUseWhy
Entering Italy / EU / SchengenItalian passportYou are an EU citizen — use EU rights
Entering the United StatesUS passportUS law requires citizens to enter/exit the US on a US passport
Traveling within the EU/SchengenItalian passport or ID cardIdentity card is sufficient within Schengen
Non-EU countriesWhichever offers better entry rightsItalian (EU) passport often has more visa-free access; check specific country

Critical US rule: The US does not formally recognize dual nationality, but it also does not prohibit it. What US law does require: US citizens must enter and leave the United States using their US passport. If US border officers ask whether you are a US citizen, always confirm it — never deny US citizenship to a US official, even if you have a second passport. Failure to do so is a violation of US law and can create serious legal complications.

In practice: keep both passports. Board your flights to Italy on your Italian passport; board flights to the US on your US passport. You may show both at Italian border control if questions arise about your entry status.

Tax Implications for Dual Citizens in Italy

This is where "dual citizen" becomes "dual taxpayer." Registering at the Italian anagrafe triggers Italian tax residency — as of the date of registration, you are an Italian tax resident subject to worldwide income taxation by Italy.

The United States taxes its citizens on worldwide income regardless of residence. So from the day you register in Italy:

  • Italy taxes your worldwide income (IRPEF)
  • The US continues to tax your worldwide income (1040)
  • The US-Italy tax treaty and Foreign Tax Credit prevent most double taxation — but planning is essential

Key planning points for dual citizens specifically:

  • The arrival-date strategy that applies to non-citizens also applies to dual citizens — registering after July 2 means you are likely not an Italian tax resident for that calendar year
  • The Italian flat tax regime (€100,000 annual substitute tax on all foreign income) is available to new Italian residents — including dual citizens who have not been Italian residents for 9 of the past 10 years
  • FBAR and FATCA reporting obligations continue for dual citizens exactly as for other Americans abroad

Italian Military Service: A Concern?

Italy suspended compulsory military service in 2004 and moved to a professional all-volunteer military. Italian male citizens born after January 1, 1985 have no compulsory military service obligation. Citizens born earlier who never served in the Italian military while residing in Italy are generally not retroactively called up upon returning to Italy. This is not a practical concern for the vast majority of Italian-Americans returning to Italy in adulthood.

Voting Rights as a Dual Citizen in Italy

Italian citizens have the right to vote in Italian national elections, European Parliament elections, and local elections in their registered municipality. Once you register at the anagrafe, you are automatically added to the electoral rolls. You will receive voting notifications for elections at your Italian address.

American dual citizens can vote in both Italian and US elections — there is no legal conflict. You remain eligible to vote in US elections by requesting an absentee ballot from your last US state of residence through the Federal Voting Assistance Program (FVAP). Most states permit Americans living abroad to vote in federal elections indefinitely.

FAQ

I just obtained Italian citizenship through jure sanguinis. I live in the US. What steps do I take to then move to Italy?
First, ensure your Italian citizenship is registered with your consulate and that you have your Italian passport. When you are ready to move, book a flight to Italy — no visa application needed. On arrival, register at the anagrafe of your chosen Italian municipality within 20 days (the legal standard) and present yourself as a returning Italian citizen. You will be registered as a resident and begin accessing Italian services from that date.
Does my spouse (US citizen, not Italian) need a visa if I move to Italy?
Yes. Your spouse, if not an EU/Italian citizen, still needs a visa to reside in Italy long-term. However, as the EU citizen spouse, you have significantly enhanced rights to bring your non-EU family member — the EU free movement rules (Directive 2004/38/EC) provide that non-EU spouses of EU citizens in their host EU country have facilitated residence rights. This is more favorable than the standard non-EU third-country national pathway. Your spouse would apply for a carta di soggiorno di familiare di cittadino UE rather than a standard permesso di soggiorno.
Do I lose my Italian citizenship if I do things that are associated with foreign citizenship (like voting in US elections)?
No. Italy does not revoke citizenship for dual nationals who exercise the rights of their other nationality — including voting in foreign elections, holding foreign public office, or using a foreign passport. Italy formally recognizes dual nationality and does not require Italian citizens to renounce other citizenships.
I was registered in Italy as a child and later moved to the US. Am I still technically an Italian resident?
Probably not — if you moved to the US and registered with AIRE (or should have), you would have been removed from the domestic Italian anagrafe. However, if you simply moved without formally deregistering, you may technically still appear in the Italian anagrafe. This can cause complications — contact your Italian comune directly to clarify your registration status before returning.
