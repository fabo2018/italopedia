Elective Residence Visa Italy: The Complete 2025 Guide

Italy's most popular long-stay visa for American retirees and passive-income earners — the requirements, the documents, the consulate process, and what happens in your first 60 days in Italy. Verified against the Ministero degli Affari Esteri's 2025 guidance.

⚡ Quick answer

The Elective Residence Visa (ERV) is for Americans with at least €31,000/year in stable, passive income — pensions, dividends, rental income, royalties, annuities. You may not work in Italy on this visa. You must have an Italian residence (rented or owned), health insurance, and apply through the Italian consulate with jurisdiction over your US state. Processing takes 60–120 days.

What the ERV actually is

The Elective Residence Visa — visto per residenza elettiva, often abbreviated ERV or "visa for residency by choice" — is Italy's long-stay visa for Americans who can support themselves without working in Italy. It is the path used by the overwhelming majority of US retirees, by trust-fund recipients, by writers and artists with passive income, and by anyone with a stable stream of investment, rental, or royalty income who wants to live in Italy long-term.

It is not a digital nomad visa. It is not a work visa. And — this is the part that catches most Americans by surprise — it explicitly forbids you from working, including for a US employer remotely, while you are an Italian resident on this visa.

⚠ Important

Italian consulates have grown notably stricter on the "no work" requirement since 2023. If your income includes any active component — salary, consulting fees, side income — you should consider the Digital Nomad Visa instead. We have an entire guide on the digital nomad pathway.

Who qualifies in 2025

You qualify for the ERV if you meet all four of the following conditions:

  1. You hold a US passport valid for at least three months beyond your intended stay, with at least two blank pages.
  2. You have stable, predictable passive income of at least €31,000 per year for a single applicant, €38,000 for a couple, plus 20% per dependent child. Most consulates ask to see this income proven over the previous three years.
  3. You have arranged Italian accommodation — either an owned property (rogito notarile) or a long-term lease (registered at the Agenzia delle Entrate) for a minimum of one year.
  4. You have private health insurance valid in Italy, with a coverage minimum of €30,000 and no significant exclusions. Travel insurance does not qualify.

What you may not do on this visa: work, freelance, consult, run a business, or earn active income from anywhere in the world. Italian residents on the ERV are full Italian tax residents — every euro of your worldwide income becomes reportable in Italy.

The €31,000 income requirement — what counts

The income threshold of €31,000 per year is the minimum. Most successful applicants demonstrate substantially more — €40,000 to €60,000 is typical for a single applicant, and well over €80,000 for couples buying property in expensive regions.

Liquid assets as backup

Even if your passive income clears €31,000, almost every consulate wants to see substantial liquid assets — typically the equivalent of €100,000 to €200,000 — as a buffer. Houston and Miami consulates are notably strict; San Francisco and Boston are slightly more lenient. Brokerage statements, bank statements and property valuations all count.

Required documents (full checklist)

Every consulate publishes its own list, and they vary. The following is the consolidated list — bring everything, and your consulate will tell you what they ignore.

📋 Document principles

Every non-Italian document must be: (1) original, (2) apostilled by the issuing state's Secretary of State, (3) translated into Italian by a sworn translator. Bring two copies of everything — one for the consulate, one for the questura in Italy.

  • Long-stay visa application form (Italian government form; download from your consulate's website)
  • US passport + photocopy of the biographical page
  • 2 recent passport photos (35×45mm, white background — same specs as US passport)
  • Proof of accommodation in Italy — either rogito for purchased property or a registered lease (contratto di locazione) of at least 12 months
  • Proof of passive income — three years of US tax returns (1040s), brokerage and pension statements, Social Security letters, all translated
  • Bank statements — last 12 months, all accounts, sometimes translated
  • FBI criminal background check — obtained through an FBI-approved Channeler, apostilled by US Department of State, translated
  • State-level background check for every state lived in over the last 5 years
  • Health insurance certificate — €30,000+ minimum coverage, valid in Italy, no significant exclusions, in Italian or English
  • Birth certificate — long-form, apostilled, translated
  • Marriage certificate (if applying as a couple) — apostilled, translated
  • Detailed motivation letter (in Italian, ideally) explaining why you wish to reside in Italy
  • Codice fiscale — if you don't have one, the consulate will issue one with your visa
  • Visa fee — €116 (varies slightly by year)

Choosing your consulate

You must apply through the Italian consulate with jurisdiction over the US state where you legally reside. You cannot consulate-shop. The ten US consulates are:

  • Boston — MA, ME, NH, RI, VT, CT (partial)
  • Chicago — IL, IN, IA, KS, MI, MN, MO, NE, ND, OH, SD, WI
  • Detroit — MI residents (sub-jurisdiction of Chicago)
  • Houston — TX, OK, AR, MS, AL, LA, KY, TN
  • Los Angeles — Southern CA, AZ, NV (Clark Co), HI
  • Miami — FL, GA, SC, NC, PR
  • New York — NY, NJ, CT (partial), DE, PA (eastern)
  • Philadelphia — PA (western), VA, WV, MD, DC
  • San Francisco — Northern CA, OR, WA, AK, ID, MT, UT, WY, NV (non-Clark)
  • Washington DC — Embassy; jurisdiction overlaps with Philadelphia for some districts
💡 Tip · 2025 booking reality

Boston, New York and Miami have appointment waits of 4–8 months as of May 2026. San Francisco, Detroit and Philadelphia are running 2–3 months. Book your appointment before gathering documents, not after — you can always cancel and rebook.

Step-by-step application timeline

Months -12 to -6: Income and asset establishment

If you don't yet have three years of demonstrable passive income, this is when you set it up. Begin taking regular IRA withdrawals, register rental properties properly, document dividend streams. Consulates look at history, not just current snapshots.

Month -6: Begin document collection

Order your FBI background check via a channeler service (Accurate Biometrics, FieldPrint, or IDEMIA — about 2–4 weeks). Request long-form birth and marriage certificates from your state of birth. Request the apostille from your state Secretary of State — this is usually fast, but document mail times eat 2–3 weeks.

Month -5: Translation

Engage an Italian sworn translator (traduttore giurato) — your consulate maintains a list. Budget $20–35 per page. The translator's certification must accompany every document.

Month -4: Insurance + housing

Lock down your health insurance policy (we recommend comparing Cigna Global, AXA International, and Allianz Care). Secure your Italian housing — either complete the rogito on purchased property, or sign and register a 12-month lease.

Month -3: Book the appointment, package documents

Most consulates use Prenot@Mi for booking. Some use third-party systems (VFS Global). Slots open at midnight local time on a rolling basis — set an alarm.

Month -2 to 0: Attend appointment, await decision

The interview is usually 20–40 minutes. The consular officer will ask why Italy, how you plan to support yourself, what you'll do with your time. Speak honestly and bring duplicate copies of everything. Processing is typically 30–90 days from interview.

After arrival in Italy

The visa is one half of the equation. Once you arrive in Italy, you have a sequence of bureaucratic milestones — get them in the right order:

  1. Within 8 working days: declare your presence at the questura (dichiarazione di presenza)
  2. Within 60 days: obtain the kit postale and apply for your permesso di soggiorno at any Italian post office. Cost: ~€80 + €76 marca da bollo + €30 plastic card fee = around €186 total.
  3. Within ~90 days: register at the anagrafe of your comune (residence registration)
  4. After anagrafe registration: apply for tessera sanitaria (health card) and consider SSN voluntary enrollment
  5. Practical: open an Italian bank account, get an Italian SIM, register with a medico di base (family doctor)

Common pitfalls (and how to avoid them)

  • Income from active sources: If your "rental income" is actually you managing the property like a job, the consulate may flag it as work.
  • One-time large gains: A single big year of capital gains looks unstable. Show recurring, predictable income.
  • Insufficient liquid assets: Meeting €31,000 in income but having only €20,000 in the bank is usually rejected. Liquidity matters.
  • Insurance policy with exclusions: "Pre-existing conditions excluded" is a red flag. The policy must cover essentially everything.
  • Short-term lease or hotel reservation: An Airbnb booking is not housing proof. The lease must be 12+ months and registered.
  • Translating documents yourself: Self-translation, even if you speak fluent Italian, will be rejected. Use a sworn translator.

Is the ERV right for you?

The Elective Residence Visa is the right choice if you are: a retiree with Social Security and/or pension income; a person with substantial dividend or rental income; an early-retired professional whose investments throw off €31k+ annually; a writer or artist living on royalties. The ERV is not the right visa if you plan to keep working — even remotely for US clients. For that, look at the Digital Nomad Visa.

For everyone else, the ERV remains the cleanest path into Italian residency: the requirements are objective, the documents are knowable, and the rewards — quiet residency in one of the most beautiful countries in the world, with the option to convert to citizenship after ten years — are real.
