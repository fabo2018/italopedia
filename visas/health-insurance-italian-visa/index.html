Health Insurance for Italian Visa: What Consulates Actually Accept

One in five Italian visa applications gets delayed because of the wrong insurance document. Here is exactly what to buy, what to avoid, and how to submit it so the consulate has nothing to reject.

The Italian consulate does not care how comprehensive your US health plan is. It does not care that you are covered by Medicare Parts A through D. It does not care that you have a Gold-tier BlueCross plan with a $500 deductible. None of those plans are valid in Italy and none of them will satisfy the visa requirement.

What the consulate requires is a specific type of document: an international health insurance policy, issued in English, explicitly valid in Italy and the entire Schengen Area, covering emergency medical treatment and repatriation, for the full duration of your intended stay.

That is the rule. Everything below explains how to satisfy it efficiently.

The Legal Requirement

The legal basis is the Schengen Borders Code and the implementing regulations for Italian national visas. The requirement applies to all D-category long-stay visas: elective residence, digital nomad, family reunification, student, and investor visas.

The Italian Ministry of Foreign Affairs states on the official visa portal (vistoperitalia.esteri.it) that applicants must present:

"Travel medical insurance covering all Schengen Member States for the whole duration of the journey/stay, with a minimum coverage of €30,000 for emergency medical care, hospitalisation, and repatriation to the country of origin."

This is the floor. Some consulates — notably New York, Chicago, and Los Angeles — apply stricter interpretations in practice. We cover those differences below.

Coverage Minimums Consulates Enforce

The official minimum is €30,000. In practice, most experienced immigration attorneys recommend purchasing at least €50,000 in coverage to give consular officers no room to question adequacy. Several providers' standard international plans start at €100,000 or $500,000, which exceeds requirements comfortably.

The five things your policy must show
  1. Your full legal name — exactly as it appears on your passport
  2. Policy effective dates — covering the entire visa period, with no gap
  3. Geographic coverage — must explicitly name Italy and/or the Schengen Area
  4. Coverage amount — minimum €30,000 / $33,000 for emergency medical
  5. Emergency repatriation coverage — medical evacuation back to the US

Missing any one of these five elements is sufficient grounds for rejection. The most common failure is a policy that covers "international travel" without explicitly naming the Schengen Area. Some US travel policies list specific countries rather than regions — if Italy is not listed by name, the consulate will not accept it.

The following providers are consistently accepted at US Italian consulates for all long-stay visa types. I have personal experience with three of them, and I have collected submission reports from readers across all five.

The most frequently recommended provider in the expat community. Cigna Global's Silver, Gold, and Platinum plans all explicitly cover the Schengen Area and produce an insurance certificate that formats perfectly for consular submission. Annual premiums for a 50-year-old American run approximately $1,800–$3,200 depending on tier and deductible. AXA International (Schengen Insurance)

AXA offers two relevant products: a short-stay Schengen policy for visa purposes only (not suitable for the D-visa applicants who plan to live in Italy) and a full international health plan. If you are applying for an elective residence visa or digital nomad visa, you want the global health plan, not the Schengen travel policy. The travel-only policy will be rejected for long-stay applications because it does not cover ongoing healthcare, only emergencies.

Allianz Care

Allianz Care (formerly Allianz Worldwide Care) is excellent for applicants over 60. Their premiums for older applicants are more competitive than Cigna's, and their network of direct-billing hospitals in northern Italy is strong. For retirees moving to Piedmont, Lombardy, or Emilia-Romagna specifically, Allianz Care is worth a direct quote comparison.

IMG Global (Patriot International)

IMG's Patriot International plan is the most affordable option that consistently passes consular review — premiums start around $800/year for a 45-year-old. The tradeoff is a more limited network for ongoing care. For applicants who plan to transition to the Italian National Health Service (SSN) within 12 months of arrival, IMG Patriot International is a cost-effective bridge policy.

GeoBlue (BlueCross BlueShield International)

GeoBlue is the international arm of BCBS and is accepted at all US consulates. It is particularly well-suited to applicants who want continuity with their existing BCBS relationship. Note that GeoBlue's standard plans exclude pre-existing conditions in the first year — a material limitation for applicants with chronic conditions.

What Is NOT Accepted

This section prevents the most common mistakes.

These will be rejected
  • Medicare and Medicaid — US government programs, no international coverage
  • US employer health plans — geographic coverage ends at the US border
  • Short-term travel insurance (World Nomads, SafetyWing basic plans) — insufficient coverage amounts; not designed for visa submission
  • Credit card travel coverage — no certificate of insurance formatted for consular use
  • COBRA continuation coverage — same limitation as employer plans
  • AXA Schengen Travel Insurance — fine for tourist visas, not for D-category long-stay applications
  • SafetyWing Nomad Insurance — the basic plan fails the €30,000 minimum; the Remote Health plan passes, but check Schengen language carefully

The Insurance Document Checklist

What to actually submit with your visa application:

  1. Certificate of Insurance — the one-page summary document from your provider, not the policy document itself (which can be 40 pages)
  2. Policy schedule or declarations page — showing coverage amounts and geographic scope
  3. Italian translation — some consulates (notably Chicago) request a certified Italian translation; call ahead to confirm your specific consulate's requirement

Do not submit the full policy booklet. Submit the certificate and the declarations page. Consular officers process hundreds of applications per week — a clean two-page insurance submission is far better received than a 40-page PDF.

Long-Stay vs Short-Stay Requirements

There is an important distinction that trips up first-time applicants. There are two different insurance requirements depending on the visa type:

Short-stay (C-visa, up to 90 days): A basic Schengen travel insurance policy with €30,000 emergency coverage is sufficient. This is what tourist visa applicants need.

Long-stay (D-visa, over 90 days): This is where most Americans get confused. The D-visa — which covers elective residence, digital nomad, and other long-stay visas — requires a full international health insurance plan, not a travel policy. The distinction matters because:

  • Travel policies often have a maximum trip duration of 90 or 180 days
  • Travel policies often exclude non-emergency care
  • Some travel policies are not accepted as proof of coverage by the questura when you apply for your permesso di soggiorno

Buy a full international health plan. The few hundred dollars you save buying a travel policy instead will cost you a rejected application and a rescheduled consulate appointment four months later.

A Note from My Own Application

When I moved to Piedmont, I applied for the elective residence visa through the Italian Consulate General in New York. The officer reviewing my application flagged my insurance certificate — not because the coverage was insufficient, but because the document did not explicitly list "Italy" alongside "Schengen Area." My policy covered the Schengen Area, which includes Italy, but the officer wanted to see Italy named.

I called Cigna Global the same day. They issued a supplementary letter confirming Italy was included in the geographic coverage, at no charge, within 48 hours. The application was approved.

The lesson: before submitting, call your insurance provider and ask them to confirm in writing that Italy is explicitly covered. If your certificate only says "Schengen Area," request an addendum that names Italy specifically. It takes ten minutes and costs nothing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need insurance before or after I arrive in Italy?
Before. The insurance certificate must be submitted with your visa application in the US, before you travel. Coverage must begin on or before your planned entry date into Italy.
Can I switch insurance providers after I arrive?
Yes, once you are in Italy with your visa. However, if you are in the process of enrolling in the Italian SSN (which takes 3–12 months), maintain your international policy until your SSN enrollment is confirmed in writing.
Does my Italian visa insurance cover me in other Schengen countries?
Yes — that is the explicit requirement. Schengen Area coverage means you are covered across all 27 Schengen member states, including France, Germany, Spain, and the rest.
How much does good expat health insurance cost per month?
For a 50-year-old American, budget $150–250/month for a solid international plan (Cigna Global Silver, Allianz Care Essential). Younger applicants (30–40) pay $80–130/month for equivalent coverage.
What happens if my insurance expires while I am in Italy?
Your permesso di soggiorno renewal will be at risk. The questura requires proof of health coverage at renewal — either private insurance or SSN enrollment. Never let your coverage lapse without a replacement in place.
