# Italy Student Visa for Americans: How to Study in Italy Long-Term (2025)

The Italian student visa (Visto per Studio) is one of the most underused pathways for Americans who want to spend extended time in Italy. While most articles focus on retirement and work visas, the student visa offers a legitimate, accessible route for anyone who:

  • Wants to study at an Italian university (degree or exchange)
  • Is enrolled in an Italian language school for an extended period
  • Plans to attend a vocational program, art school, or cooking school
  • Wants more than 90 days in Italy without qualifying for a work or retirement visa

It's particularly relevant for Americans in their 20s–40s who can't yet retire but want to spend a year or more in Italy with a legal visa structure.

Types of Italian Student Visas

Italy issues different student visas depending on what you're studying:

1. University Degree Program (Corso di Laurea)

For full enrollment at an Italian university — bachelor's, master's, or PhD programs.

2. Exchange Program

For students enrolled at a U.S. university participating in an exchange with an Italian university (Erasmus+, direct bilateral agreements, etc.).

3. Language School (Corsi di Lingua)

For full-time language courses at authorized Italian language schools. Requires a minimum of 20 hours/week of coursework.

4. Professional/Vocational Training

For recognized professional courses — art schools (Accademia di Belle Arti), music conservatories, culinary schools, design institutes.

5. Research

For academic researchers at Italian universities or research institutes.

Is the Student Visa Right for You?

Who Uses It

Young Americans (22–35): The most common group. Gap year, career change, want to experience Italy deeply. Language school enrollment provides the legal structure.

University students: Semester or full-year exchange, sometimes full degree enrollment.

Retirees who want language immersion: Some Americans in their 50s–60s use a language school visa as their initial Italian visa while exploring longer-term options (Elective Residency, etc.).

Career changers studying Italian professional fields: Culinary arts, design, fashion, art restoration.

Who It's NOT For

  • People who want to work in Italy (student visa prohibits most employment)
  • People who primarily want to travel through Europe (Schengen tourist visa is simpler)
  • People who have passive income qualifying for the Elective Residency Visa (use that instead)

Income Requirements

There is no "passive income" requirement for the student visa like the Elective Residency Visa. Instead, you must demonstrate:

Sufficient funds to support yourself for the duration of your stay. The guideline (periodically updated) is based on the social integration allowance:

  • For stays up to 1 year: approximately €461.49/month of available funds (multiply by months of stay)
  • For a 12-month student visa: approximately €5,537 in accessible funds

This is a minimum — most consulates want to see more. A realistic demonstration: bank statements showing €8,000–15,000 in accessible savings, or proof of parental support, scholarship, or financial sponsorship.

Scholarship students: If you have an Italian government scholarship (Borse di Studio MAECI), European scholarship (Erasmus+), or U.S.-funded scholarship, these substitute for the financial requirement.

Required Documents

Core Documents

  1. Completed visa application form — via the Italian consulate portal
  2. Valid U.S. passport — minimum 6 months validity beyond stay
  3. Two passport photos (35×45mm, white background)
  4. Proof of enrollment — letter of acceptance from Italian university or language school
  5. Proof of financial means — bank statements, scholarship documentation, parental guarantee
  6. Proof of accommodation in Italy — university dormitory assignment, rental contract, or host family letter
  7. Health insurance — valid in Italy for the full duration; minimum €30,000 coverage
  8. Application fee — approximately €50 (lower than other Italian long-stay visas)
  9. Criminal background check — not always required for student visas; verify with your consulate

Additional Documents by Program Type

University degree program:

  • Enrollment certificate (certificato di immatricolazione)
  • University acceptance letter
  • High school diploma (apostilled + Italian translation) for bachelor's applicants
  • Previous university transcripts for master's applicants

Language school:

  • Enrollment confirmation from the school (must be an authorized institution)
  • Course schedule confirming minimum 20 hours/week

Art/music/culinary school:

  • Acceptance letter from the school
  • Any portfolio or audition documentation

Italian Universities: Key Facts for Americans

Cost

Italian public university tuition is dramatically lower than U.S. institutions:

  • State university tuition: €600–4,000/year (income-based fees — lower for students with low parental income)
  • Private universities: €5,000–20,000/year (still far below U.S. equivalents)

Compare: U.S. state university tuition averages $10,000–30,000/year; private: $30,000–60,000+.

Language

Most Italian university programs are in Italian. However, Italy has been expanding English-language degree programs, particularly at master's level:

  • Politecnico di Milano: Extensive English master's in engineering, design, architecture
  • Bocconi University (Milan): Business and economics programs in English
  • University of Bologna: Several English master's programs
  • Politecnico di Torino: Engineering programs in English
  • University of Padova: Growing English-language offerings

For bachelor's degrees in Italian: You need B2 Italian minimum. CILS or CELI B2 certification is typically required for enrollment.

Applying to Italian Universities

Direct application: Most Italian universities have online application portals. U.S. academic credentials need to be translated, evaluated (dichiarazione di valore from the Italian consulate), and sometimes apostilled.

Exchange programs: If your U.S. university has a partnership with an Italian institution, use the exchange pathway — significantly simpler paperwork.

Erasmus+ (for current U.S. university students): If your U.S. university participates in Erasmus+, you may be eligible for funded exchange semesters at EU (including Italian) universities.

Language Schools: The Flexible Route

For Americans who want to live in Italy legally without committing to a full degree program, enrollment in a full-time Italian language school is the most flexible pathway.

What Qualifies

The Italian consulate requires a language school that:

  • Is officially recognized (recognized by the Italian Ministry of Education or registered as a school — not just a private tutor)
  • Offers a minimum of 20 hours per week of instruction
  • Issues an enrollment certificate (certificato di iscrizione)

Major Italian Language Schools

Language schools offering long-term immersive programs that support student visa applications:

Florence:

  • Scuola Leonardo da Vinci: scuolaleonardo.com
  • Instituto Europeo: istitutoeuropeo.it

Rome:

  • Dilit International House: dilit.it
  • Scuola Leonardo da Vinci Rome branch
  • Centro Machiavelli: centromachiavelli.it

Siena:

  • Università per Stranieri di Siena: unistrasi.it — official language university; courses eligible for student visa

Perugia:

  • Università per Stranieri di Perugia: unistrapg.it — one of Italy's most respected Italian-for-foreigners institutions

Bologna, Milan, Turin, Naples: Various accredited schools; ask the school specifically whether their program supports visa applications

Cost

Full-time Italian language school (20+ hours/week):

  • €600–1,500/month depending on school, city, and program level
  • Most schools offer 3-month, 6-month, and 12-month programs
  • Some include accommodation placement services

What You Can (and Can't) Do on a Student Visa

You CAN:

  • ✅ Study full-time
  • ✅ Work part-time — up to 20 hours per week (Italian law permits student visa holders to work up to 1,000 hours/year, which averages ~20 hours/week)
  • ✅ Travel throughout the Schengen Area
  • ✅ Enroll in SSN healthcare (for enrolled university students)
  • ✅ Open an Italian bank account
  • ✅ Establish Italian residency (Residenza)

You CANNOT:

  • ❌ Work full-time (over 20 hours/week during academic term)
  • ❌ Start a business or work as self-employed (Partita IVA) — requires a different visa
  • ❌ Remain in Italy without maintaining active enrollment

20-Hour Work Limit

The 20-hour work limit is a genuine restriction. However, for Americans using the student visa as a lifestyle visa (living in Italy while studying Italian), the combination of part-time work + language school can cover a significant portion of living expenses.

Realistic scenario: 20 hours/week teaching English to Italians (via private tutoring or certified language schools) at €15–25/hour = €1,200–2,000/month — enough to cover much of living expenses in smaller Italian cities.

After Your Studies: Conversion Options

Upon completing a program or after your student visa expires, you have options:

Conversion to work visa (Nulla Osta Lavoro): If you've received a job offer from an Italian employer, convert to a work visa. The annual "Click Day" (Decreto Flussi) allocates work visas — highly competitive.

Conversion to self-employment (Partita IVA): With the right business plan, convert to a self-employment visa.

Elective Residency Visa: If you've reached retirement age or have passive income, convert to the Elective Residency Visa.

Permesso per attesa occupazione: A 1-year "job-seeking" permit available to graduates of Italian universities, allowing you to remain in Italy while looking for work.

Step-by-Step Application Summary

  1. Get enrolled in your Italian program (language school or university)
  2. Book consulate appointment via prenotami.esteri.it
  3. Gather documents (2–4 weeks)
  4. Attend appointment at Italian consulate
  5. Wait for visa (2–4 weeks processing)
  6. Enter Italy before visa validity expires
  7. Report to Questura within 8 days (if in private accommodation)
  8. Apply for Permesso di Soggiorno per Studio within 60 days
  9. Register at your Comune (Anagrafe) once Permesso is in progress
