Italy Self-Employment Visa: For Freelancers and Business Owners (2025)

The self-employment visa is for Americans who want to actually work in Italy — with Italian clients, in an Italian business, practicing a profession under Italian registration. Here is who qualifies, what documents to prepare, and why most freelancers should look at the digital nomad visa first.

✉ 𝕏 ⌘

The Italian self-employment visa (visto per lavoro autonomo) is one of the least-discussed visa categories for Americans — in part because the digital nomad visa, launched in 2024, now handles many of the cases that previously required the self-employment visa. But the self-employment visa remains the correct route for a specific set of applicants: those who want to work with Italian clients, run an Italian business entity, or practice a regulated profession in Italy.

Understanding which visa applies to your situation is the first and most important step.

Who This Visa Is For

The self-employment visa is the right choice if you:

  • Want to open a business with Italian clients (a restaurant, a consulting firm, a design studio)
  • Want to practice a regulated profession in Italy (architect, lawyer, engineer, doctor) under Italian professional registration
  • Want to register as a sole proprietor (ditta individuale) or open a partita IVA to invoice Italian companies
  • Want to engage in commercial or artistic activities that have Italian sources of income
  • Plan to start an Italian company (SRL or SNC) as a director or partner

The critical difference: the digital nomad visa is designed for people whose income comes from abroad. The self-employment visa is for people who will generate income from Italian sources. If you work remotely for US clients from an Italian apartment, you almost certainly want the digital nomad visa. If you want to open an Italian photography studio, consult for Italian companies, or practice as a licensed architect in Italy, you need the self-employment visa.

Requirements and Documents

The self-employment visa application requires a more complex document package than most other Italian visas:

  • Business plan or professional project describing the activity, projected income, target clients, and economic viability. This is not a formality — consulates review business plans carefully. A credible plan with specific client commitments is significantly stronger than a generic statement of intent.
  • Proof of financial resources sufficient to support yourself during the establishment period. Bank statements showing at least 6–12 months of living expenses (at Italian cost of living) are standard.
  • Any existing contracts or letters of intent from Italian or international clients
  • Evidence of professional qualifications (degrees, certifications, professional licenses) relevant to the activity
  • Italian accommodation proof (lease agreement, hotel reservation, or property ownership)
  • Health insurance valid in Italy for the full visa period — same requirements as other long-stay visas (see our health insurance guide)
  • Criminal background check (FBI background check for Americans, apostilled and translated)
  • Completed visa application form (Schengen long-stay form)
  • Valid US passport with at least 3 months validity beyond the intended stay

Professional Recognition: The Hidden Obstacle

If you want to practice a regulated profession in Italy — medicine, law, architecture, engineering, dentistry, nursing, pharmacy — you face an additional requirement that can take years to resolve: professional credential recognition.

Italy requires that professionals practicing regulated activities hold qualifications recognized by the relevant Italian professional order (ordine professionale). A US law degree does not automatically qualify you to practice as an avvocato in Italy. A US architecture license does not automatically allow you to sign Italian construction permits as a registered architetto.

The recognition process involves: applying to the relevant Italian professional order (Ordine degli Avvocati, Ordine degli Architetti, etc.), having your qualifications reviewed against Italian equivalency standards, potentially sitting compensatory examinations, and waiting 6–24 months for a decision.

If your planned business is an unregulated activity — consulting, design services, writing, software development, photography, marketing — this obstacle does not apply. You can operate with a partita IVA without professional registration.

Opening a Partita IVA as an American

The partita IVA is your Italian VAT and tax identification number for self-employed activities. It is the Italian equivalent of a US self-employed taxpayer identification number, but it also registers you in the Italian business registry.

To open a partita IVA as an American, you need: an Italian codice fiscale, Italian residency (the permesso di soggiorno), and a visit to the Agenzia delle Entrate. Most Americans opening a partita IVA choose the regime forfettario (flat-rate tax regime), which applies a simplified 5–15% flat tax on a percentage of revenues. It is the Italian equivalent of a simplified sole proprietor structure.

Important: you cannot open a partita IVA before arriving in Italy with your visa and permesso di soggiorno. The business plan in your visa application describes what you intend to do; the actual registration happens after arrival.

The Income Requirement in Practice

The legal minimum income requirement for the self-employment visa corresponds to the Italian minimum annual income for self-employed workers — approximately €8,500 per year at current rates. In practice, consulates expect to see a credible projection of substantially more, particularly because the visa requires demonstrating that the business is economically viable.

A practical benchmark: a business plan projecting €20,000–€30,000+ in annual revenue from identified clients, combined with €15,000–€25,000 in liquid savings for the start-up period, is a defensible application. Applications projecting €8,500 and presenting no existing client relationships are routinely rejected.

Application Process Step by Step

  1. Prepare business plan, financial documentation, and supporting documents
  2. Book appointment at your jurisdiction's Italian consulate in the US (3–8 months lead time at major consulates)
  3. Submit application at the consulate appointment
  4. Await decision (6–12 weeks processing)
  5. If approved, collect visa and enter Italy within the visa validity period
  6. Within 8 days of arrival, submit kit postale at the post office for permesso di soggiorno application
  7. Register at anagrafe within 20 days
  8. Open partita IVA at Agenzia delle Entrate
  9. Collect permesso di soggiorno at questura (3–8 months after kit postale submission)

Note: unlike the elective residence and digital nomad visas, the self-employment visa is subject to the annual decreto flussi (immigration quota decree), which allocates a limited number of self-employment visas each year. The decree is typically published in the autumn. Applications filed before quota allocation may be held pending.

After Arrival: Permit and Residency

The permesso di soggiorno issued after approval of a self-employment visa is valid for 2 years initially, renewable for 2-year periods. After 5 years of continuous legal residence, you may apply for a long-term EU residence permit (permesso di soggiorno CE per soggiornanti di lungo periodo).

FAQ

Can I hire Italian employees on a self-employment visa?
Yes, if you have established a proper Italian business entity. A sole proprietor with a partita IVA can hire employees in Italy, subject to Italian labor law, INPS contributions, and the standard hiring process. Seek advice from an Italian labor law specialist (consulente del lavoro) before hiring.
Can I switch from a digital nomad visa to a self-employment visa inside Italy?
Changing visa category inside Italy is possible in some circumstances but generally requires returning to the consulate process. See our guide: Changing Visa Type Inside Italy.
What if my business fails? Does Italy deport me?
Italy does not automatically revoke a self-employment visa if a business struggles. At permit renewal, you must demonstrate continued economic activity. If you have no income and no active business for a sustained period, renewal may be denied. Maintain documentation of your economic activities throughout.
