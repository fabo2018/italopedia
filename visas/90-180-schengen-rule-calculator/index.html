The 90/180 Schengen Rule Explained: How to Calculate Your Days

Most Americans misunderstand this rule — they think they get 90 days per entry, or 90 days per calendar year, or that the clock resets when they leave. None of that is correct. Here is exactly how the rolling 180-day window works, with examples.

By Fabrizio Boggio
Updated April 25, 2025 10 min read VERIFIED
✉ 𝕏 ⌘

Americans do not need a visa to visit Italy — or any Schengen country — for tourism. The US and the EU have a visa-free arrangement that lets Americans enter without advance permission. What Americans do need to understand is the limit on that free access: 90 days in any 180-day rolling period. Exceed that limit and you have violated EU immigration rules.

The rule is simple to state and counterintuitive to apply. The 180-day window is not a fixed block of time — it rolls. Every day, the window shifts forward one day and the earliest day of the previous window drops off. This means you cannot think in calendar terms ("I've been here 90 days, so I'll leave and come back in January"). You need to calculate backward from any given entry date.

The Rule, Simply Stated

Article 6 of the Schengen Borders Code (Regulation EU 2016/399):

Third-country nationals may stay in the Schengen Area for a period of no more than 90 days in any 180-day period.

The "any 180-day period" is the key phrase. It is not a fixed period — it is rolling. On any given day you want to enter the Schengen Area, the question is: how many days have I spent in Schengen in the 180 days prior to and including today?

The Rolling Window — The Part Everyone Gets Wrong

The single most common misunderstanding: Americans assume the 90-day limit resets after 90 days out of the Schengen Area. It does not. The 180-day window moves with you.

Imagine you are looking back from today. Count back exactly 180 days. Everything in that window — all your days in any Schengen country — counts toward your 90-day limit.

If you spent 90 days in Italy in June–August and left on September 1, you cannot re-enter on October 1 and have 90 fresh days. On October 1, looking back 180 days gets you to April 4 — and all those summer days still fall within the window. You have zero days available. You will need to wait until the summer days start dropping out of the window one by one.

How to Calculate Your Days

The European Commission's official short-stay calculator is the most reliable tool: ec.europa.eu/assets/home/visa-calculator

To manually calculate:

  1. Identify today's date (or the date you want to enter)
  2. Count back 180 calendar days from that date
  3. List every day you were physically present in any Schengen country during that 180-day period
  4. Sum those days. If the total is under 90, you may enter for (90 minus total) more days.

Days counting rules: The day of entry and the day of departure both count as days present. If you enter July 1 and leave July 3, that is 3 days (July 1, 2, and 3). There is no "partial day" credit — both arrival and departure days are full count days.

Worked Examples

Example A: The summer visitor who thinks they can come back

Maria visits Italy June 1–August 29 (90 days). She leaves August 29. She wants to return November 1.

On November 1, looking back 180 days = May 5. Her 90 Schengen days (June 1–Aug 29) all fall within the May 5–November 1 window. She has 0 days available on November 1. She needs to wait until June 1 days start dropping off — so she can first return on December 1 (when June 1 drops out), and can only stay 1 day; she reaches 90 again when August 29 days are all out, which is February 25.

Lesson: After using 90 full days, you need roughly 90 days out before the window fully clears.

Example B: The part-year visitor planning ahead

James wants to spend approximately 45 days in Italy twice a year. He visits March 1–April 14 (45 days) and plans to return September 1.

On September 1, looking back 180 days = March 5. His March 1–April 14 stay: days from March 5 onward fall in the window (March 5–April 14 = 41 days). He has 90 − 41 = 49 days available starting September 1. He can stay September 1–October 19 (49 days) and be compliant.

Lesson: Partial-year stays create complex calculations. Always check before each entry.

Which Countries Count

All Schengen Area member states count together as one zone for the 90/180 rule. Days in France, Germany, Spain, Netherlands, Greece — all count toward your 90 days alongside Italy. There is no separate 90-day allowance per country.

Schengen Area members as of 2025: Austria, Belgium, Croatia, Czech Republic, Denmark, Estonia, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Hungary, Iceland, Italy, Latvia, Liechtenstein, Lithuania, Luxembourg, Malta, Netherlands, Norway, Poland, Portugal, Slovakia, Slovenia, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland.

Not Schengen: Ireland, Romania, Bulgaria, Cyprus. UK (post-Brexit). These countries have their own entry rules, separate from Schengen. Days spent in the UK do not count toward your Schengen 90 days.

How an Italian D-Visa Changes Everything

An Italian D-visa (long-stay visa, any category) or a valid Italian permesso di soggiorno does not count against your Schengen 90/180 days for Italy. You can live in Italy continuously — that is the entire point of the D-visa.

The complication: when you leave Italy to visit other Schengen countries on your Italian D-visa, those days may or may not count against your Schengen short-stay allowance for those other countries. The rules here were amended by EU Regulation 2019/1155:

  • Travel to other Schengen states with a valid Italian D-visa: generally treated as short-stay visits subject to the 90/180 rule for the other countries
  • In practice, border control between Schengen countries is largely absent, so day-counting for within-Schengen travel is rarely enforced — but the legal rule exists

For most Americans living in Italy on a D-visa who occasionally travel to France or Spain: the practical risk of enforcement is low, but technically those days count. If you are spending significant time in multiple Schengen countries outside Italy, be aware of this.

What Happens If You Overstay

Overstaying the 90/180 limit is a violation of the Schengen Borders Code. Consequences in Italy specifically:

  • Discovery at exit: Border officers check passport stamps and may calculate your days. An overstay discovered at departure can result in a formal notice, a fine (Italy: typically €5,000–€10,000 for overstay), and a formal entry ban (divieto di ingresso) from the Schengen Area for 1–5 years.
  • Discovery within Italy: If stopped by police while overstaying (routine stop, traffic check), a formal expulsion order (decreto di espulsione) can be issued.
  • Future visa applications: A recorded overstay makes future Italian D-visa applications significantly harder — the consulate will scrutinize your compliance history.

Do not overstay. The administrative and practical consequences are serious, and the 90/180 rule is not ambiguous or discretionary — it is EU law uniformly applied.

ETIAS: What Is Coming

ETIAS (European Travel Information and Authorisation System) is the EU's pre-travel authorization system for visa-exempt visitors — similar to the US ESTA for visitors to the US. It was scheduled for 2024 but has been repeatedly delayed. As of mid-2025, the most recent official guidance indicates a phased rollout beginning in late 2025 or 2026.

What ETIAS will mean for Americans:

  • Before each visit to the Schengen Area, you will need to register online, pay a small fee (€7), and receive pre-authorization
  • ETIAS authorization is valid for 3 years or until your passport expires
  • It does not change the 90/180 rule — it is an additional pre-authorization layer, not a new allowance
  • Americans living in Italy on a D-visa do not need ETIAS for their Italian residency — it applies to short-stay tourist visits

FAQ

Does flying from Italy to Spain and back count against my Schengen days?
If you are in Italy on a short-stay tourist basis (no D-visa), then yes — days in Spain also count against your 90-day Schengen limit. If you are in Italy on a valid D-visa, your days in Italy do not count, but your days in Spain technically do count as short-stay Schengen days.
Can I reset my 90 days by briefly going to the UK?
No. The UK is not in the Schengen Area, so days in the UK do not count toward your Schengen 90/180. But leaving to the UK and coming back does not reset your Schengen counter either — the rolling window counts backward from any given entry date regardless of where you went in between.
My US passport has no Italian entry stamp from my last visit. How will border control know how long I stayed?
Italy (and most Schengen countries) use the Entry/Exit System (EES), which is being rolled out alongside ETIAS. This electronic system records entries and exits digitally — no physical stamp required. Do not rely on missing stamps as a de facto protection against overstay detection.
