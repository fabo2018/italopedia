Living in Florence as an American Expat: Complete Guide (2025)

The complete guide to living in Florence as an American — best neighborhoods (Oltrarno, Santo Spirito, Pitti), real costs, the enormous expat community, the tourist problem, and honest pros and cons.

Florence is Italy's most concentrated city — cramming more Renaissance art, architecture, and food culture into 10 square kilometers than anywhere else on earth. For Americans, it's also the most familiar Italian city — partly because so many have studied abroad here, partly because so many Americans have written about, painted, or fallen in love with it.

Living here is different from visiting. Magnificent but also challenging, crowded, expensive, and increasingly complex as the city navigates its relationship with mass tourism.

Why Florence Draws American Expats

The art and architecture are incomparable. Living surrounded by the greatest concentration of Renaissance art in the world — where you eat lunch near Michelangelo's David and walk daily across a bridge that Dante knew.

Strong American expat infrastructure. Florence has one of Italy's most established American communities, built around NYU Florence, Syracuse in Florence, API, CAPA, and dozens of other American university programs.

Scale. Florence is just 360,000 people in the historic center — genuinely walkable and human-scale.

Tuscany as a backyard. Chianti wine country, Siena, San Gimignano, Lucca, and the Cinque Terre are all within 90 minutes.

The Honest Challenges

Tourism is extreme. Florence receives 13–15 million tourists per year in a city of 360,000. In summer, the historic center is genuinely overwhelmed.

Cost of housing is high and rising. Short-term rental platforms have reduced available long-term housing significantly. Central 1BR apartments rent for €1,400–1,800/month. Prices rose ~8% in 2025.

The summer heat. Florence in July and August can hit 38–40°C. Air conditioning in older apartments is often inadequate.

Best Neighborhoods for American Expats

Oltrarno (including Santo Spirito and San Frediano)

The neighborhood locals and expats both love most. Vibe: Artisan workshops, bohemian, authentic, lively piazza life Rent: €1,300–1,800/month for 1BR Best for: Anyone who wants authentic Florentine neighborhood life

Centro Storico (Duomo, Santa Croce)

Rent: €1,500–2,200/month for 1BR Best for: People who want to be at the center of it all and can afford it

San Marco / San Lorenzo

Academic and student-adjacent Florence Rent: €1,200–1,600/month for 1BR Best for: Students, academics, people who want central living at slightly lower cost

Pitti / Boboli Area

Quiet, prestigious, near everything Rent: €1,400–1,900/month for 1BR Best for: Professionals and retirees wanting quality residential life with Oltrarno access

Campo di Marte / Coverciano

Residential Florence at better prices Rent: €900–1,300/month for 1BR Best for: Families, budget-conscious expats

The American Community in Florence

  • American university programs: NYU Florence, Syracuse in Florence, SACI, API, CAPA
  • The Florentine: English-language newspaper for expats
  • British Institute of Florence: Cultural events, language courses, community hub
  • American Church in Florence: Community anchor for many American expats

Transport in Florence

  • Buses: ATAF/Busitalia network. Monthly pass €35.
  • Trams: Line 1 and Line 2. Expanding network.
  • Bikes: Florence is relatively flat and increasingly cycle-friendly.
  • Trains: Santa Maria Novella station is central. High-speed trains to Bologna (35 min), Rome (1.5 hrs), Milan (1.75 hrs).

FAQ

Is Florence affordable on a US retirement income? Comfortably, yes — if you're outside the absolute center. The Elective Residency Visa requires ~€31k/year passive income, which is achievable for many American retirees.

Is Florence good for families? Yes — particularly in Oltrarno, Campo di Marte, and near American university campuses. International schools exist but are expensive (€15,000–25,000/year).

Is Florence safe? Yes, very. Florence has low violent crime. Like Rome, pickpocketing occurs in crowded tourist areas. Residential neighborhoods are very safe.
