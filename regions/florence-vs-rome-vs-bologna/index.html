# Florence vs Rome vs Bologna: Which Italian City Is Best for American Expats? (2025)

Florence, Rome, and Bologna are three completely different versions of Italy. Florence is a Renaissance jewel overwhelmed by its own beauty. Rome is a chaotic, magnificent capital where ancient history shares the sidewalk with scooters. Bologna is Italy's best-kept secret — a city that Italians consider the country's most livable but that most Americans can't find on a map without looking.

If you're choosing between these three for your Italian life, this guide cuts through the romanticism and gives you the practical comparison you actually need.

The One-Line Summary

  • Florence: Most beautiful, most expensive, most tourist-saturated
  • Rome: Biggest city, best infrastructure, best English-language services
  • Bologna: Best value, best food, most genuinely livable

What Your Rent Gets You

Florence:

  • €1,000/month: 1-bedroom in Campo di Marte or Gavinana (outer but decent)
  • €1,400/month: 1-bedroom in Oltrarno (inner, real neighborhood feel)
  • €1,800/month: 1-bedroom in Santo Spirito (close to center)

Rome:

  • €1,000/month: 1-bedroom in Pigneto or Ostiense (inner, trendy)
  • €1,400/month: 1-bedroom in Garbatella or Flaminio
  • €1,800/month: 1-bedroom in Trastevere (central, tourist area)

Bologna:

  • €900/month: 1-bedroom in inner neighborhoods (San Donato, Mazzini)
  • €1,200/month: 1-bedroom close to the center (Università area, San Vitale)
  • €1,500/month: 2-bedroom near the center

Lifestyle and Atmosphere

Florence

Florence is as beautiful as advertised — and that beauty creates its own problems. The historic center (roughly 4km by 2km) is a UNESCO World Heritage Site with 15 million tourists per year. For context: the entire city has 370,000 residents and receives tourists at a ratio of 40:1.

What this means for daily life:

  • Restaurant prices in the center are calibrated for tourists (€18–25 for pasta)
  • Summer (June–September) is loud, crowded, and hot (35°C/95°F regularly)
  • Airbnb competition reduces apartment supply and inflates rents
  • The streets around the Duomo and Uffizi are not where you want to go grocery shopping

But:

  • Walk out your door in Oltrarno and the beauty is genuinely extraordinary
  • The Florentine pace (slower than Rome, more cultured) suits many Americans
  • Cultural density — world-class museums, galleries, concerts — within walking distance
  • Scale: Florence is small enough to know well within 6 months

Best Florence neighborhoods for expats: Oltrarno (authentic, beautiful, south of the Arno), Campo di Marte (real neighborhood, 20-min walk to center), Gavinana (quiet, affordable), Fiesole (hillside village, bus to center, extraordinary views).

Rome

Rome is Italy's most complex city — its scale, chaos, bureaucracy, and magnificence all amplified. It rewards patience and penalizes those who want things to work efficiently.

What Rome gives you:

  • The world's greatest concentration of history within a city
  • Italy's most developed English-language services — because Rome has always been international
  • The largest American expat community in Italy
  • Mediterranean climate: warmer winters than Florence or Bologna
  • Culinary diversity: Roman trattorias (cacio e pepe, carbonara, amatriciana) PLUS every world cuisine

What Rome costs you:

  • The bureaucracy is genuinely worse here. Permesso di Soggiorno wait times at the Rome Questura are among Italy's longest.
  • Traffic is overwhelming — driving in Rome is an experience unto itself
  • Scale: 2.8 million people means you can live in Rome for years without it feeling familiar
  • Neighborhood quality varies block by block in ways that aren't obvious from listings

Best Rome neighborhoods for expats: Pigneto (hipster, vibrant, good value), Garbatella (authentic Roman working class, beautifully maintained), Prati (safe, near Vatican, international-feeling), Ostiense (up-and-coming, creative, good transit), Testaccio (food market, authentic Rome, slightly touristy but real).

Bologna

Bologna doesn't get in its own way. It's a city that functions — beautifully, practically, with food as a civic religion.

What Bologna gives you:

  • 40km of arcaded streets (portici): you walk everywhere in any weather, any season
  • Italy's best food at neighborhood prices (this is not hyperbole — Italians agree)
  • A university city energy — 90,000 students means the city skews young, vibrant, international
  • Geographic centrality: 37 minutes to Florence, 1 hour to Milan, 2.5 hours to Rome
  • Genuine Italian life without tourist saturation

What Bologna lacks:

  • The "wow" factor of Florence or Rome's centers
  • Beach proximity (2.5 hours to Adriatic; not bad but not a quick day trip)
  • International airport connections (Guglielmo Marconi airport has European flights but fewer intercontinental routes than Milan or Rome)

Best Bologna neighborhoods for expats: The area around Via dell'Indipendenza and Piazza Maggiore (central, walkable), San Vitale (student area, vibrant), Mazzini (solid residential), Bolognina (up-and-coming north of center).

Rome wins this category clearly. The combination of tourism infrastructure, diplomatic community, international organizations, and the U.S. Embassy makes Rome Italy's most English-accessible city.

Florence has a large study-abroad and expat community that creates English services, but these are concentrated in tourist areas.

Bologna's English accessibility is improving (partly due to university internationalization) but lags Florence and Rome.

Gemelli (Rome) and Sant'Orsola (Bologna) are among Italy's top 5 hospital complexes by most rankings. Careggi (Florence) is excellent but slightly below these two.

For most healthcare needs, the differences are irrelevant. If you have a complex, specific medical condition requiring a national reference center, Rome and Bologna edge ahead.

Expat Community

Florence

Florence has a large, established American expat community — many driven by study abroad programs (NYU Florence, Syracuse in Florence, over a dozen others) and the long tradition of Anglo-American writers and artists in the city. Facebook groups like "Americans in Florence" and "Expats in Florence" have thousands of active members. English-language bookstores (Paperback Exchange), English-language social clubs, and regular expat events.

Downside: The expat community in Florence is significantly younger than in Rome or Bologna — dominated by 20-somethings on semester programs and young digital nomads. If you're 50+ looking for peers, the community is there but you may need to look harder.

Rome

Italy's largest and most diverse expat community. Americans in Rome include diplomats, academics, journalists, artists, tech workers, and retirees. Long-established institutions: American University of Rome, American Chamber of Commerce in Italy, American Women's Club of Rome, Rome's American expat Facebook groups (40,000+ members).

The result: If you want an English-speaking support network from day one, Rome is the easiest landing.

Bologna

Smaller but growing expat community, significantly boosted by the presence of Johns Hopkins University Bologna Center and the University of Bologna's internationalization push. InterNations Bologna chapter is active. Facebook group "Expats in Bologna" is smaller than Rome or Florence equivalents but highly engaged.

Bologna's expat advantage: Smaller community means more genuine connections. People actually meet, have dinner, form friendships — rather than the diffused, anonymous larger community of Rome.

Bureaucracy and Visa Processing

This is a rarely-discussed but practically important factor.

Questura (immigration office) efficiency:

  • Bologna Questura: Generally positive reputation. Permesso di Soggiorno appointments available within 2–4 months; processing reasonably efficient.
  • Florence Questura: Mixed reputation. Backlogs have been significant; service quality variable.
  • Rome Questura: Notoriously backlogged. Some Americans wait 6–9 months for Permesso appointments. The sheer volume (Rome processes more Permssi than any city except Milan) creates system strain.

Commune (Anagrafe) efficiency:

  • Bologna: Efficient for a major Italian city; appointments available online
  • Florence: Variable; some neighborhoods faster than others
  • Rome: Long waits; large municipality means more bureaucratic friction

If minimizing bureaucratic friction matters to you: Bologna wins clearly.

The Verdict: Who Should Choose Each City

Choose Florence If:

  • You specifically want to be in Tuscany
  • Culture, Renaissance art, and the Florentine atmosphere are central to your vision of Italy
  • Your budget is €2,500–3,500/month as a single person or €3,500–5,000 as a couple
  • You're OK with tourist saturation (it becomes background noise after 6 months)
  • You plan to use Italy as a base for traveling Tuscany and the rest of Italy

Choose Rome If:

  • You value English-language accessibility above all else
  • You have a career reason to be in Italy's capital
  • You want the largest American expat community and social infrastructure
  • Mediterranean climate matters (warmer winters than Florence/Bologna)
  • You've done your research on neighborhood selection and can avoid the tourist-price zones

Choose Bologna If:

  • Budget is a real constraint
  • You want the best quality of life per euro spent
  • Food culture matters to you (Bologna will ruin you for every other city)
  • You value genuine Italian life over glamour and international recognition
  • You plan to travel frequently by train (Bologna's train hub is unmatched)
  • You're moving with a partner and need a 2-bedroom apartment without financial stress

My honest recommendation: If you don't have a specific reason to be in Florence (Tuscany life) or Rome (career, English services, specific community), choose Bologna. You'll live better, spend less, and — after the initial adjustment period — love it more.
