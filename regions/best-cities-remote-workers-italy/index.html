Best Italian Cities for Remote Workers and Digital Nomads in 2025

Italy ranks high on lifestyle for remote workers but low on operational ease. Internet in apartments can be slow, coworking is less developed outside Milan, and the time zone (CET) means early mornings for US West Coast calls. Here is the honest ranking — city by city — with the information that actually matters for working remotely.

The Instagram version of remote working in Italy — open laptop, Aperol Spritz, Tuscan hillside — is real. The operational version — finding an apartment with fiber internet in a city with functioning coworking spaces where you can join calls during US business hours without waking at 3am — requires more research.

Italy is genuinely excellent for remote workers who plan carefully and accept the tradeoffs. The lifestyle return is exceptional. The infrastructure, administrative overhead, and timezone math require honest accounting before you commit.

The Italy Remote Work Reality Check

Before the city rankings, three facts every remote worker needs:

  • Time zone: Italy is CET (UTC+1 winter, UTC+2 summer). For US East Coast workers, your work day starts at 3pm and runs to 11pm. For US West Coast, it is 6pm–midnight. This works if your role is async-heavy. It is a genuine constraint if you have frequent live meetings.
  • Internet: Italy's fiber rollout is ongoing. Fiber is available in most cities and many apartments, but older buildings with older infrastructure can have connection issues. Always confirm internet speed before signing a lease — ask for a Speedtest screenshot, not just "there is WiFi."
  • Visa: Remote workers employed by non-Italian employers should use the Digital Nomad Visa (minimum €28,000/year income). Those working with Italian clients need the Self-Employment Visa. See our visa guide for the full analysis.

Bologna — Best Overall Pick for Remote Workers

Bologna is the city that consistently surprises Americans who expected to love Florence and end up staying in Bologna. It is a university city — 100,000 students in a metro area of 400,000 — which means the infrastructure, the bars, the bike lanes, the food, and the social scene are calibrated for people who are here to live, not to visit.

Internet: Fiber widely available. Median download speed in Bologna: approximately 85 Mbps. Coworking spaces: Kilowatt, Impact Hub Bologna, Ufficio.co. Growing scene, not yet London-dense but functional.

Cost: 2-bedroom apartment in centro storico: €900–€1,400/month. Coffee: €1.10. Lunch at a trattoria: €12–€18. Below Milan and Florence by 20–30%.

Practical factors: High-speed rail to Milan (35 min), Florence (37 min), Rome (2 hrs). International airport with connections to major EU hubs. English widely spoken by the university-educated population. Excellent food — Bologna's culinary reputation is not hyperbole.

Downsides: Hot and humid in July–August. Some areas of the historic center are noisy due to the student population. Limited direct flights compared to Milan or Rome.

Turin — Underrated, Affordable, Genuinely Livable

Turin (Torino) is consistently underrated by Americans who visit Italy. It was Italy's first capital, is now a post-industrial city reinventing itself through design, culture, and tech, and offers the most normal Italian city experience — meaning high quality of life without tourist surcharges or tourist crowds.

Internet: Fiber excellent in the center and established neighborhoods. Tim, Eolo, and Vodafone all service the city well. Median download speed: ~90 Mbps. Coworking: DoppioLavoro, Toolbox, The Net Value — a small but active coworking ecosystem.

Cost: Turin is notably cheaper than Milan (30–40%) with similar infrastructure. 2-bedroom apartment: €750–€1,200/month. The Vanchiglia and Crocetta neighborhoods offer excellent quality-of-life at reasonable rents.

Practical factors: 45 minutes to Milan by high-speed train. Good international airport (Caselle) with direct routes to London, Paris, and other European hubs. Strong cycling infrastructure. Museum scene punches above its weight (Egyptian Museum is the best outside Cairo). Near the Alps for skiing and hiking.

Note from personal experience: I live in Piedmont, and Turin is where I go for everything the region cannot provide. The quality of everyday life — restaurants, transport, cultural infrastructure — is exceptional for its price point. Americans who choose Turin over the more obvious alternatives are rarely disappointed.

Downsides: Fewer non-Italian expats than Milan or Florence (which can be both a positive and a negative depending on your preference). Some areas northwest of the center are less attractive. Foggy in autumn and winter.

Milan — Most Professional, Most Expensive

Milan is the obvious choice if your remote work involves any Italian business relationships, regular trips to European offices, or a desire to be embedded in a professional international community. It is Italy's business capital, and it shows — in the infrastructure, the networking, the airport connections, and the price of everything.

Internet: Fiber coverage excellent throughout the city. Median download speed: ~95 Mbps. Coworking: the most developed scene in Italy by far — Talent Garden, Copernico, WeWork, Impact Hub, and dozens of independent spaces. Finding a desk is not an issue.

Cost: Expensive by Italian standards. 2-bedroom apartment in Navigli or Isola: €1,400–€2,200/month. Good but not cheap food. Coffee and transit comparable to other Italian cities.

Practical factors: Two international airports (Linate for EU/domestic, Malpensa for intercontinental). Direct flights to New York, Chicago, Miami, and other US cities. Excellent public transit. Fashion and design industry events that are genuinely world-class.

Downsides: The most expensive Italian city. Dense urban environment — you are living in a city, not in Italy's picturesque idea of itself. The tourist appeal of Florence or Rome is absent; Milan's appeal is operational excellence and cosmopolitanism, which either resonates or it does not.

Florence — Beautiful, but Plan for the Trade-offs

Florence (Firenze) is the city most Americans imagine when they think of moving to Italy. It is also the city where the gap between the fantasy and the operational reality is widest. The beauty is real. The tourist infrastructure, tourist pricing, and tourist crowds in the historic center are equally real.

Internet: Fiber available but coverage in older buildings is more variable than in Bologna or Turin. Some historic buildings have structural challenges for modern internet installation.

Cost: Higher than Turin or Bologna. 2-bedroom in the historic center: €1,200–€1,800/month. Prices in Oltrarno and San Frediano (across the Arno) are somewhat lower and more residential in character.

Practical factors: Excellent rail connections. Small but functional international airport. A large English-speaking expat community — easier to meet other Americans here than almost anywhere in Italy. Strong arts and cultural infrastructure.

Downsides: Heavily touristic in the historic center — some neighborhoods feel more like a theme park than a city in summer. The Airbnb effect has pushed many locals to the suburbs. Summers are very hot. Coworking scene is less developed than Bologna or Milan.

Palermo — Cheapest Major City, Growing Expat Scene

Palermo is the emerging choice for remote workers who prioritize cost and warmth over northern European operational efficiency. Sicily's capital has improved dramatically in recent years and is becoming a legitimate base for budget-conscious remote workers.

Internet: Improving but still behind northern cities. Fiber available in central areas; some gaps in coverage. Check carefully before committing to an apartment.

Cost: The most affordable major Italian city for renters. 2-bedroom apartment in a good neighborhood: €550–€900/month. Food substantially cheaper than the north.

Practical factors: Palermo airport has direct connections to Rome and several European cities. The city qualifies for the 7% flat tax for pensioners scheme (officially for pensioners, but the tax advantage makes it worth understanding for other income types too).

Downsides: Administrative efficiency is lower than the north — bureaucratic processes can be slower. Internet infrastructure is still catching up. The city requires more patience and more Italian than the northern cities.

Smaller Cities Worth Considering

  • Lecce (Puglia): Exceptional quality of life, very low cost, warm climate, strong university city energy. Internet improving. Growing digital nomad interest.
  • Padova/Padua: University city near Venice with better livability than Venice itself. Faster internet, lower rents than Venice or Verona.
  • Trieste: Underrated port city at the northeastern edge of Italy. Central European character, excellent coffee culture, lower rents, good rail connections.
  • Brescia: Midway between Milan and Verona. Often overlooked but has all the operational infrastructure of a northern Italian industrial city at lower prices than Milan.

Italy Internet: What to Actually Expect

Italy ranked 25th in the EU in average fixed broadband speed as of early 2025 — not at the bottom, but not a benchmark performer. The national fiber rollout (Piano Italia 1 Giga) is improving coverage rapidly, but the rollout is uneven.

What to do before signing a lease:

  1. Ask the landlord for the exact internet provider and ask for a Speedtest result from the apartment
  2. Check fiber availability at the address on the TIM wholesale checker (grossomodo.tim.it)
  3. Have a 4G/5G backup plan — Iliad Italia offers unlimited 5G data for €9.99/month; keep this as your backup for calls during internet outages

Which Visa for Remote Workers

Remote workers employed by or contracted with non-Italian clients should use the Digital Nomad Visa (launched 2024) — minimum €28,000/year gross income, requires health insurance, requires Italian accommodation proof. This is now the correct pathway and was created specifically for this situation.

Remote workers who want to work with Italian clients or register an Italian business entity need the Self-Employment Visa. See our complete guide: Self-Employment Visa Italy.

FAQ

Does Italy have specific coworking incentives for digital nomads?
Not federally. Some municipalities (notably in southern Italy) have created incentive programs that include coworking access as part of residency packages — Civita di Bagnoregio, Riace, and a handful of others. These are typically aimed at attracting permanent residents to depopulating areas rather than short-term nomads, but they are worth investigating if you want a specific base.
What is the best Italian city for a family with school-age children?
Bologna and Turin are consistently rated highest by expat families for the combination of international schools, livable neighborhoods, lower cost than Milan, and quality of infrastructure. Florence has international schools as well but is significantly more expensive. See our separate guide: Best Italian Cities for Families.
Is Italian language ability important for day-to-day remote work life?
Your remote work itself is presumably in English. Your daily life in Italy will benefit substantially from intermediate Italian — not for work calls, but for dealing with landlords, the anagrafe, healthcare appointments, shopping outside tourist areas, and integrating into a neighborhood rather than just visiting it. Invest in Italian before you arrive; apps and the first six months in the country will carry you further than you expect.
