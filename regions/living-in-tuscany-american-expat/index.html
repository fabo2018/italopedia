Living in Tuscany as an American: The Real Expat Guide (2025)

The complete, honest guide to living in Tuscany as an American — best cities and villages, real costs, what daily life looks like, and how to make it work on different budgets.

By Fabrizio Boggio

⚡ Quick answer

Tuscany has two distinct modes: urban (Florence, Siena, Lucca, Arezzo — services, transport, community) and rural (farmhouses, hilltop villages — the postcard life, but a car and strong Italian are essential). A single retiree in Siena or Lucca lives comfortably on €1,850–2,300/month. A couple in Florence suburbs or Siena: €2,950–4,050/month. Practical advice: start urban, go rural later — after you've built local roots and know what you're signing up for.

Tuscany is the Italy that Americans have been dreaming about since Frances Mayes published Under the Tuscan Sun in 1996. Rolling hills, cypress trees, medieval hilltop towns, Chianti, olive oil, and a pace of life that seems designed to undo every American anxiety. The dream is real — and so are the practical realities that the magazine spreads don't show.

This guide covers both: what makes Tuscany extraordinary for American expats, and what you need to understand before buying a farmhouse or signing a lease in Siena.

Tuscany in Numbers

Tuscany is not one place. The region covers 23,000 square kilometers (roughly the size of New Hampshire + Vermont combined) and contains wildly different environments:

  • Florence (Firenze): International city, urban, tourist-heavy, most expensive
  • Siena: Medieval gem, university town, quieter than Florence
  • Arezzo, Lucca, Pistoia, Prato: Mid-size Tuscan cities with authentic character
  • The Chianti countryside: Wine estates and farmhouses between Florence and Siena
  • The Maremma coast: Wild Etruscan coast, largely undiscovered
  • The Garfagnana: Remote mountains in northern Tuscany
  • Val d'Orcia: UNESCO-listed landscape of rolling hills and cypress-lined roads

The right place for you depends entirely on what you want from Tuscany.

Urban vs. Rural Tuscany: The Core Choice

This is the decision that determines everything else:

Urban Tuscany (Florence, Siena, Lucca, Arezzo)

What you get: - All the services of a functioning Italian city - Public transportation - International healthcare facilities - English-speaking communities - Cultural institutions, restaurants, events - Easier bureaucratic navigation (more international-facing offices)

What you give up: - The postcard landscape (cities, even beautiful ones, don't look like the Val d'Orcia) - Quiet and privacy - The "living in the Tuscan countryside" experience

Rural Tuscany (Chianti, Val d'Orcia, Maremma, hilltop villages)

What you get: - The landscape that every Tuscan photograph shows - Genuine agricultural life (harvests, seasons, local producers) - Privacy, space, quiet - Often significantly lower costs (property purchase; but higher running costs)

What you give up: - Convenience — everything requires a car - Immediate access to good healthcare - A social life beyond small expat/local communities - Fast internet in some areas (though fiber is expanding)

Most Americans who've lived in rural Tuscany long-term will tell you: start urban, go rural later. The romance of a farmhouse 20km from the nearest town fades faster than expected when you need a plumber who speaks Italian, your car breaks down, or you have a health scare.

Best Locations for American Expats

Florence

Italy's cultural capital is covered in detail in our dedicated Florence cost of living guide. Summary for this context:

  • Most expensive Tuscan city
  • Best infrastructure, most international
  • Historic center is tourist-saturated; Oltrarno and inner suburbs are the places to live
  • 1-bedroom apartment: €900–2,500/month depending on location

Siena (Population: 53,000)

Siena is where many Americans who've outgrown Florence end up. It's a perfectly preserved medieval city — the Piazza del Campo is considered one of Europe's finest public spaces — with a university (Università di Siena, founded 1240), a genuine local culture, and significantly less tourist pressure than Florence.

Why Siena works for expats: - Small enough to know your neighborhood; big enough to have services - Genuinely beautiful (UNESCO World Heritage City) - Good train connection to Florence (1.5 hours) and Rome (3 hours) - Palio di Siena (the famous horse race) twice yearly — an extraordinary cultural event to be present for

Rent in Siena: | Apartment | Monthly Rent | |-----------|-------------| | Studio | €550–900 | | 1-bedroom | €750–1,200 | | 2-bedroom | €1,000–1,600 |

The Siena tradeoff: Siena is inland and has no direct fast train to anywhere. Connections require changing. For frequent travelers, this is annoying. For those settling in for the long haul, it's irrelevant.

Lucca (Population: 91,000)

A walled Renaissance city in northwestern Tuscany, about 30 minutes by train from Pisa (airport) and 1.5 hours from Florence. Lucca is flat, which makes it famous for its cycling culture — locals ride bikes everywhere within the walls.

Why Lucca works for expats: - Smaller and less touristy than Florence or Siena - Excellent train connections (Pisa airport, Florence) - Vibrant food market (Mercato del Carmine) - Good English-speaking expat community (it's been a quiet favorite for decades) - Proximity to the Tuscan coast (Forte dei Marmi, Viareggio, 30–45 minutes)

Rent in Lucca: | Apartment | Monthly Rent | |-----------|-------------| | Studio | €500–850 | | 1-bedroom | €700–1,100 | | 2-bedroom | €900–1,500 |

Arezzo (Population: 100,000)

Less famous internationally but a genuine Tuscan city with its own distinct identity. Home to one of Italy's best antique markets (Fiera Antiquaria, first Sunday of each month), the stunning frescoes of Piero della Francesca, and excellent restaurants.

The practical advantage: Arezzo sits at the rail junction of the Florence-Rome high-speed line — excellent connections in all directions.

Rent in Arezzo: | Apartment | Monthly Rent | |-----------|-------------| | Studio | €450–750 | | 1-bedroom | €600–1,000 | | 2-bedroom | €800–1,300 |

Montepulciano, Montalcino, Pienza, Cortona (Smaller Hilltop Towns)

These are the towns that appear in every Tuscany photograph. They're beautiful, small (populations 5,000–15,000), and have thin infrastructure for daily life. Services are limited. Healthcare access is in nearby larger towns. Full-time living here requires real commitment to the rural lifestyle and strong Italian.

That said, some Americans live in these towns very happily — particularly retirees who have fully embraced the Italian rural pace and have developed local relationships over years.

Buying Property in Tuscany

Tuscany is one of the most sought-after property markets in the world. This drives prices in desirable areas but also creates significant opportunity in less-obvious locations.

Location Apartment €/sqm House €/sqm
Florence center €3,500–7,000 N/A (mostly apartments)
Siena €2,000–4,000 €2,500–5,000
Lucca €2,000–3,800 €2,000–4,500
Chianti countryside €1,800–4,000 €1,500–4,000
Val d'Orcia / Maremma €1,200–3,000 €1,000–3,500
Rural/restoration projects €300–800 (requires renovation budget)

Cascade effect: As buyers priced out of Florence look to Siena, Siena prices have risen. Those priced out of Siena look to Arezzo or Cortona. The Tuscan market has moved significantly since 2019.

Buying as an American: Key Points

  • Americans can buy property in Italy on a non-discriminatory basis (U.S.-Italy bilateral agreements + EU principle of non-discrimination)
  • You need a Codice Fiscale to complete any property purchase
  • The purchase goes through a Notaio (notary) — mandatory under Italian law; the notary is responsible for title verification and registration
  • Transaction costs: Budget 8–12% of purchase price for taxes, notary fees, and agent fees (for non-primary residence buyers)
  • Primary residence benefit (Agevolazioni Prima Casa): If you establish the property as your Italian primary residence within 18 months of purchase, you pay reduced registration tax (2% vs. 9% for non-primary)

Official reference: DPR 131/1986 (tax on property transfers); Nota II-bis, Article 1

Life in the Tuscan Countryside: The Honest Assessment

The farmhouse dream is real for some people and a rude awakening for others. Here's what 10 years of expat community observation tells me:

Who Thrives in Rural Tuscany

  • People who genuinely wanted rural life, not just Italian life
  • Those with fluent or near-fluent Italian (B2+)
  • Those who have local relationships — neighbors, tradespeople, the alimentari owner
  • People who are happy with their own company for extended periods
  • Those with sufficient financial cushion for maintenance costs (old stone farmhouses are beautiful but expensive to maintain)

Who Struggles

  • People who moved for the photo and discovered the isolation
  • Those who assumed British/American expat communities would provide social infrastructure (they do, to a degree, but they don't replace local integration)
  • Those who expected convenience at Italian-city levels in areas without infrastructure
  • Anyone with health concerns who underestimated the importance of good healthcare proximity
  • Remote workers who discovered unreliable internet in older rural buildings (fiber is expanding but not universal in deep countryside)

Healthcare in Tuscany

Tuscany's regional health service (Azienda Sanitaria / ASL Toscana) is one of Italy's better-regarded regional systems. The main hospital districts:

  • Florence: AOU Careggi (university hospital, regional center for complex care)
  • Siena: Policlinico di Santa Maria alle Scotte (university hospital)
  • Pisa: Azienda Ospedaliero-Universitaria Pisana
  • Arezzo: Ospedale San Donato

In rural areas, you'll have access to small local hospitals (Ospedali di Comunità) and clinics, with transfers to regional hospitals for complex cases.

Enrollment: Once you have Italian residency and a Permesso di Soggiorno, register with the local ASL to get a GP (Medico di Medicina Generale) assigned. Free for residents; voluntary fee for non-employed non-EU residents.

Reference: aslcentro.toscana.it (Florence/Pistoia/Prato)

Practical Tips for Tuscany

Don't buy a rural property without visiting in winter. Tuscany in July is paradise. The same farmhouse in January — cold, foggy, isolated, with heating bills to match — is a different experience. Always visit in the off-season before committing.

Budget for a car. Outside the main cities, a car is non-negotiable. Italian car maintenance costs: insurance €600–1,200/year; inspection (revisione) every 2 years €60–80; fuel at €1.60–1.90/liter.

Learn Italian to at least B1 before arriving. I say this for all of Italy, but it's especially true in Tuscany outside Florence. Lucca, Siena, Arezzo — these are genuinely Italian places where English is limited outside the tourist center.

Find a good geometra before buying rural property. A geometra (licensed surveyor/property technician) will identify structural issues, zoning compliance problems, and outstanding obligations on rural properties that are invisible to a casual inspection. Cost: €500–1,500 for a property survey. Worth it.

The agriturismo path. Several Americans have found a middle path: renting (or buying) an apartment in a city like Siena or Lucca, while having access to a friend's or family's agriturismo (working farm) for weekends. This gives urban-week, rural-weekend balance without committing to remote living.
