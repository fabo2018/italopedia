Living in Rome as an American: The Complete Expat Guide (2025)

From Prati to Garbatella, SSN enrollment to Questura waits — the unfiltered guide to American life in Rome.

Rome is where empires were built and where the Catholic Church still runs one of the world's most powerful institutions from a 0.44 square kilometer state inside the city. It has more UNESCO World Heritage Sites than any other city on earth. It also has Italy's worst traffic, Italy's most backlogged immigration offices, and the kind of bureaucratic chaos that can turn a simple errand into a half-day adventure.

For Americans, Rome is simultaneously the easiest Italian city (most English-language services, largest expat community, most international infrastructure) and one of the most overwhelming. Whether it works for you depends on who you are and what you need from Italy.

This guide gives you the real picture.

Rome at a Glance

  • Population: 2.87 million (city); 4.3 million (metro area)
  • Region: Lazio
  • Italy's rank: Capital city, largest city
  • Climate: Mediterranean — mild winters (avg. January 8°C/46°F), hot dry summers (avg. July 32°C/90°F)
  • Airport: Fiumicino (FCO) — Italy's busiest international airport; direct flights to most U.S. cities

Why Americans Choose Rome

The English Factor

Rome has Italy's most developed English-language infrastructure — a direct result of centuries of international presence (Vatican, diplomatic corps, tourism industry, study abroad programs). In practical terms:

  • English-speaking doctors, dentists, lawyers, and accountants are readily available
  • The U.S. Embassy and multiple consulates operate here
  • American University of Rome, John Cabot University, and dozens of U.S. study abroad programs maintain offices
  • Expat-oriented services (international schools, bilingual real estate agents, English-language bookshops) exist in abundance

If you're arriving in Italy for the first time and want to land softly — with English-language support around you — Rome is the easiest entry point.

The Career Angle

Rome is home to Italy's government ministries, diplomatic community, United Nations agencies (FAO, WFP, IFAD all headquartered here), international NGOs, and the Vatican's global operations. For Americans working in diplomacy, international development, journalism, academia, or the Catholic Church, Rome is where the work is.

The History

Living in Rome means living inside 2,800 years of continuous human history. Your morning commute passes the Pantheon (built 125 AD, still standing, still free to enter). The Forum is a 10-minute walk from Trastevere. The Colosseum is lit up at night from half the city's rooftops.

For Americans coming from cities where "old" means 1920, this is genuinely transformative.

The Food

Roman cuisine is deceptively simple and extraordinarily good. Cacio e pepe (pecorino, black pepper, pasta — that's it). Carbonara (eggs, guanciale, pecorino, black pepper). Amatriciana. Artichokes alla romana. Supplì (fried rice balls with mozzarella). Pizza al taglio by weight. Gelato that ruins you for anything else.

Rome's Best Neighborhoods for American Expats

Prati

The neighborhood immediately north of the Vatican. Wide, tree-lined streets, Haussman-esque architecture, excellent food markets (Mercato Trionfale — one of Rome's largest), relatively quiet by Roman standards.

Why it works: Safe, walkable, excellent transport (metro, buses), many English-speaking professionals live here. Close to the Vatican but not overrun by tourists. Rent: 1-bedroom €1,200–1,800/month; 2-bedroom €1,600–2,500/month

Trastevere

Rome's most beloved neighborhood. Medieval streets, ivy-covered buildings, the most beautiful piazzas in the city (Piazza di Santa Maria in Trastevere). Historically working-class Roman, now a mix of Romans, expats, and students.

The honest note: Trastevere is heavily touristed and increasingly Airbnb-saturated. Summer nights can be extremely loud. It's best experienced as an occasional destination rather than a permanent home — unless you find an apartment away from the main tourist circuits. Rent: 1-bedroom €1,400–2,200/month

Pigneto

East of the historic center, formerly working-class, now one of Rome's most vibrant emerging neighborhoods. Strong arts and music scene, excellent trattorias, more authentic Roman daily life than tourist areas.

Why it works: 25–30 minutes by tram/bus to center; significantly cheaper than central areas; genuinely Roman atmosphere; growing young expat community. Rent: 1-bedroom €900–1,400/month

Garbatella

One of Rome's hidden gems. A 1920s planned neighborhood with a distinct architectural identity (fascist-era rationalist buildings around garden courtyards). Deeply Roman, few tourists, excellent local restaurants, excellent metro access (Line B — 15 minutes to Termini).

Why it works: Authentic Rome at prices well below the center; strong local community; metro access makes it practical. Rent: 1-bedroom €850–1,300/month

Ostiense / Testaccio

Adjacent neighborhoods south of the Aventine hill. Testaccio is Rome's original slaughterhouse district — now a food destination (Mercato Testaccio is Rome's best food market). Ostiense is younger, more creative, home to Roma Tre University.

Why it works: Excellent transport, real neighborhood feel, competitive prices, Rome's best food market on your doorstep. Rent: 1-bedroom €950–1,500/month

Flaminio / Parioli

North of the historic center. Flaminio is upscale residential Rome — wide streets, MAXXI (national museum of contemporary art), Auditorium Parco della Musica. Parioli is Rome's wealthiest residential neighborhood — elegant, quiet, expensive.

Why it works (Flaminio): Good value for a central-north location; tram to Piazza del Popolo; beautiful architecture. Rent (Flaminio): 1-bedroom €1,100–1,700/month

Transportation in Rome

Rome's public transport system covers the city but has limitations compared to northern Italian cities.

Metro

Two main lines (A and B), crossing at Termini. Limited coverage — the historic center has few metro stops due to the impossibility of digging without hitting archaeological remains. Line C (partially open) is being extended.

Buses and Trams

Extensive network but frequently unreliable. Rome's bus system covers everywhere the metro doesn't, but delays and overcrowding are common.

Monthly Pass

€35/month covers all ATAC transport (metro, bus, tram) within Rome.

Cycling

Rome is improving cycling infrastructure but remains challenging — steep hills in many areas, aggressive drivers, cobblestone streets. E-bikes have made cycling more viable. Lime and Bird scooters operate in the city.

Car

Not recommended for central Rome residents. ZTL (Limited Traffic Zone) cameras cover most of the historic center. Parking is a constant challenge. Many Romans who own cars leave them in the suburbs and take transit to the center.

Healthcare in Rome

Rome has Italy's most extensive healthcare infrastructure, both public and private.

SSN (Public)

Major reference hospitals:

  • Policlinico Gemelli — Catholic university hospital; one of Italy's top institutions; Pope's hospital of choice
  • Policlinico Umberto I — Large teaching hospital, Sapienza University
  • Ospedale San Camillo-Forlanini — Major public hospital, west Rome
  • Bambino Gesù — Italy's premier children's hospital

English-speaking SSN care: More available in Rome than any other Italian city. Ask at enrollment for GPs who speak English — they exist.

Private Healthcare

  • Rome American Hospital (Ospedale Americano): romehospital.com — historically the expat hospital of choice; English throughout
  • International Medical Center (IMC): English-language GP and specialist practice
  • Villa Stuart: Private sports medicine and orthopedic clinic

U.S. Embassy Medical List

The U.S. Embassy maintains a list of English-speaking doctors and dentists in Rome: it.usembassy.gov/u-s-citizen-services/doctors/

Bureaucracy: Rome's Biggest Challenge

Let's be direct: Rome has Italy's most backlogged immigration and residency bureaucracy. This affects Americans in practical ways:

Permesso di Soggiorno

The Rome Questura (via Teofilo Patini) processes more Permessi than any office in Italy. Wait times for appointments: 3–6 months. Processing after submission: 6–12 months. The ricevuta (receipt) keeps you legal in the interim, but the delays are real.

Strategy: Submit your Permesso application at the Post Office as soon as possible after arriving. Don't wait for the "right moment."

Anagrafe (Residency Registration)

Registering at your Municipio (Rome has 15 Municipi) requires an appointment. Online booking via comune.roma.it. Wait times: 4–8 weeks for appointments at busy Municipi.

Strategy: Book the Anagrafe appointment the same week you arrive. The wait is long enough that starting immediately matters.

Tip: Smaller Comuni for Citizenship Applications

If you're pursuing Italian citizenship by descent and considering basing yourself in Italy to apply via Comune, do not apply in Rome. Rome's Ufficio di Stato Civile for citizenship is overwhelmed. Choose a smaller city (Bologna, Turin, Udine, a small Comune in any region) for dramatically faster processing.

Practical Tips for Americans Moving to Rome

Choose your neighborhood carefully. Rome is huge — the wrong neighborhood choice means long commutes and isolation. Visit potential neighborhoods at different times of day before signing a lease.

Learn at least survival Italian. While English is available in Rome, daily life (markets, neighbors, small shops, local bureaucracy) is in Italian. Even basic Italian transforms your experience.

Get a scooter (motorino) or e-bike. Romans use them for good reason — they're faster than cars in traffic, easier to park, and navigate the centro storico streets that buses can't. An electric scooter is an excellent investment for Rome living.

Expect administrative delays and plan around them. Everything in Rome takes longer than it should. Build 2–3x the expected time into any bureaucratic task. Frustration is normal; it passes.

Explore beyond the tourist center. The Appia Antica (ancient Roman road with intact original stones), the Castelli Romani (volcanic lake towns 30 minutes south), Ostia Antica (remarkably preserved ancient Roman port city, 30 minutes by train), Orvieto (1.5 hours) — Rome is an extraordinary base for day trips and weekend escapes.
