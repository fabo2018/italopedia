Living in Rome as an American Expat: The Complete Guide (2025)

Everything Americans need to know about living in Rome — best neighborhoods by lifestyle, real costs, healthcare, transport, the expat community, and honest pros and cons.

Rome is the city that rewires your relationship with time. Nowhere else do you casually eat lunch next to a 2,000-year-old aqueduct, or watch the sun set over a skyline that has barely changed in centuries. For many Americans, Rome is the Italian dream made concrete.

It's also the most complex Italian city to live in. The bureaucracy is slower here than anywhere else in Italy. The traffic is genuinely chaotic. And the cost of living is among the highest in the country. But the Americans who make it work in Rome almost universally say the trade-offs are worth it.

Why Americans Choose Rome

History and culture at impossible density. Living here means having the Pantheon, the Colosseum, Vatican Museums, and Campo de' Fiori as your daily backdrop.

The largest American expat community in Italy. Over 500,000 foreign residents in Rome, with a particularly strong American presence — drawn by the American Academy, international schools, the FAO, embassies, and the Vatican.

Mediterranean climate. Long hot summers, mild winters, and 300+ days of sunshine.

International connectivity. Fiumicino (FCO) is one of Europe's better-connected airports with direct routes to major US cities.

The Honest Downsides

Bureaucracy at its worst. Rome's Questura is notoriously backlogged. Permesso di Soggiorno appointments regularly take 4–6+ months.

Traffic and chaos. Rome has 2.7 million people and infrastructure not designed for modern traffic. The metro covers only 2 lines.

Cost. A central 1BR apartment runs €1,200–1,800/month. The overall monthly budget for a comfortable single person is €2,500–3,200.

Tourist pressure. If you live in popular neighborhoods (Trastevere, Centro Storico), you're sharing your street with millions of tourists.

Best Neighborhoods for American Expats

Trastevere

Vibe: Charming, cobblestone, lively, touristy Rent: €1,300–1,800/month for 1BR Best for: Young expats, digital nomads, people who love nightlife and don't mind tourists

Prati

Vibe: Elegant, residential, quiet, very central Rent: €1,400–2,000/month for 1BR Best for: Professionals, retirees, families who want central Rome without tourist chaos

Testaccio

Vibe: Authentic, working-class turned gentrifying, food-obsessed Rent: €1,100–1,600/month for 1BR Best for: Food lovers, young professionals wanting authentic Roman neighborhood life

Monteverde

Vibe: Residential, family-friendly, green, peaceful Rent: €1,100–1,600/month for 1BR Best for: Families, retirees, people who want space and quiet within the city

Pigneto / Ostiense

Vibe: Alternative, artsy, younger, more affordable Rent: €900–1,300/month for 1BR Best for: Digital nomads, young creatives, budget-conscious expats

Transport in Rome

Metro: Only 2 lines (A and B), intersecting at Termini station. Frequent delays. Monthly pass: €35/month — extraordinary value covering all buses, trams, and metro. Scooter: The Roman solution to everything. A used scooter (€1,000–3,000) transforms your relationship with the city.

Healthcare in Rome

Rome has some of Italy's best hospitals — Policlinico Gemelli, Bambino Gesù, and several other major public hospitals.

Private options: Salvator Mundi International Hospital and Clinica Valle Giulia are popular with expats and have English-speaking staff. Private GP: €80–150/visit.

FAQ

Is Rome safe for American expats? Generally yes. Rome has low violent crime rates. The main concerns are pickpocketing in tourist areas and around Termini station.

How long does Permesso di Soggiorno take in Rome? Longer than most Italian cities. Expect 6–12 months from application to physical card.

How does Rome compare to Milan for expats? Rome: better weather, more history, larger American community, more relaxed pace, higher tourist pressure. Milan: more efficient bureaucracy, stronger job market, more modern infrastructure, worse weather.
