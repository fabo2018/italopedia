Best Italian Cities for Families with Children in 2025

Moving to Italy with children changes the calculus. International schools, pediatric healthcare, outdoor space, traffic safety, and the realistic cost of raising children in Italy all matter as much as the romanticized lifestyle reasons for the move. Here is an honest city-by-city guide for families.

By Fabrizio Boggio· Published January 25, 2025· Updated May 18, 2025·13 min read

Italy is genuinely excellent for families. Italian culture is child-positive in ways that Americans find striking — children are welcome in restaurants, public spaces are safe, public parks are well-maintained, and the pace of life outside major cities is calmer than most American metros. The challenges are real too: international school fees are high, pediatric specialists can have long waits in the public system, and car-dependent suburbs are less comfortable to navigate without a car.

The right city for your family depends heavily on your children's ages, your language flexibility, your school preferences, and your budget.

What Matters Most for Families

Family decision framework
  • School-age children: International school availability and cost is the dominant factor — it will be your largest single expense after housing
  • Young children (under 6): Italian public nursery schools (asilo nido and scuola dell'infanzia) are high quality and inexpensive; international school need is lower until primary age
  • Teenagers: IB diploma availability is critical; the International Baccalaureate is accepted by US universities and offered at select international schools
  • Healthcare access: Pediatric specialist availability matters; Northern Italy is better served than the South
  • Outdoor space: Families benefit from neighborhoods with parks and safe pedestrian areas; city centers vary significantly

International vs Italian Schools: The Choice

The school decision is the most consequential family decision in Italy. Two main options:

International schools: Instruction in English, generally following UK or American curricula, with IB diploma at the secondary level. Accepted seamlessly by US universities. Expensive: annual fees typically €12,000–€25,000 per child in major cities. The social community is international, English-dominant, and well-suited to expat children arriving without Italian.

Italian public schools: Free, high quality, instruction in Italian. A powerful Italian immersion experience that produces fluently bilingual children within 1–2 years if they start young enough (under 10). The challenge is the first year's language adjustment. Italian universities are the standard destination; US university applications require careful SAT/ACT preparation in addition to Italian graduation requirements.

Many families choose Italian public school for younger children (who adapt quickly) and international school for teenagers (who face the most academic disruption from a language switch). This hybrid approach requires less financial commitment than all-international but requires good Italian by the time children are in their mid-teens.

Milan — Best International School Options, Most Expensive

Milan has Italy's densest concentration of international schools and is the default choice for corporate expat families with employer-subsidized school fees. For self-funded families, Milan's cost of living plus international school fees is the highest combination in Italy.

International schools in Milan:

  • American School of Milan (ASM) — US curriculum, PreK–12, Noverasco di Opera (south Milan). Fees: ~€20,000–€25,000/year
  • International School of Milan (ISM) — IB programme, Trieste area of Milan. Fees: ~€18,000–€23,000/year
  • Sir James Henderson British School of Milan — UK curriculum, GCSE/A-levels. Fees: ~€16,000–€20,000/year

Family neighborhoods: Brera, Navigli, Porta Romana (central), and the southern suburbs near ASM (Noverasco, Opera, Lacchiarella). Families often prioritize proximity to school over city center location.

Practical family factors: Excellent pediatric healthcare at Ospedale Buzzi and Mangiagalli. Strong parks in the outer neighborhoods. Good cycling infrastructure. Relatively high cost: 2-bedroom family apartment in a family-friendly neighborhood: €1,400–€2,200/month.

Bologna — Best Balance of Quality and Cost for Families

Bologna is the family city that experienced expats consistently recommend. The university culture means the city is intellectually lively; the size (smaller than Milan, Rome, or Florence) means it is manageable; the food culture means even picky children eat well; and the cost is meaningfully lower than the larger Italian cities.

International schools near Bologna:

  • International School of Bologna (ISB) — IB curriculum, PYP through Diploma. Fees: ~€12,000–€16,000/year. Smaller school with a tight-knit community.
  • Collegio Aldini Valeriani — Technical school with English-track options (less relevant for younger children)

Family neighborhoods: San Donato, Colli Bolognesi, Borgo Panigale. Many Bologna expat families choose the hillside neighborhoods (Colli) for larger apartments with outdoor space at relatively modest cost.

Practical family factors: Bologna has Italy's best early childhood education system (the Reggio Emilia approach originated in the region). Public parks are extensive and well-maintained. Family-friendly city culture. High-speed rail to Milan (35 min) makes day trips and school-related travel efficient.

Turin — Underrated Family City with Strong Infrastructure

Turin is the city I know best, and it consistently surprises families who expected to prefer somewhere more famous. The infrastructure is excellent, the cost is moderate, the public spaces are genuinely child-friendly, and the Alps are 45 minutes away for skiing and hiking.

International schools in Turin:

  • International School of Turin (IST) — IB curriculum, PreK–12, Chieri (south Turin). Fees: ~€13,000–€17,000/year
  • Liceo Internazionale Europeo — Bilingual Italian-English, more affordable but less internationally recognized curriculum

Family neighborhoods: Crocetta, Borgo Po, San Salvario (more urban), Collina Torinese (more suburban with gardens). Crocetta is widely regarded as Turin's premier family neighborhood — quiet, well-served by schools, good housing stock.

Practical family factors: Excellent pediatric hospital (Ospedale Regina Margherita, one of Italy's best children's hospitals). Strong park infrastructure along the Po riverfront. Excellent public transit for a city its size. Proximity to Alpine skiing is a genuine lifestyle advantage for active families.

Florence — Beautiful but Expensive for Families

Florence's combination of tourist-market pricing, dense historic center, and limited outdoor space in the city center makes it a less-than-obvious family choice despite its cultural appeal. Families in Florence tend to settle in neighborhoods outside the centro storico — Oltrarno, Fiesole, or the hillside villages surrounding the city.

International schools:

  • International School of Florence (ISF) — IB, Osmannoro (northwest Florence). Fees: ~€15,000–€19,000/year
  • Florence International School — Smaller alternative with English-instruction options

Family neighborhoods: Oltrarno, Campo di Marte, Fiesole (hillside, excellent for families with outdoor space, slightly removed from city center).

Practical factors: Beautiful environment for children growing up. Strong art education. Moderate-sized international school community. More expensive than Bologna or Turin for comparable family apartments.

Rome — Largest Expat Family Community, Most Complex

Rome has the largest international school community in Italy and the most established expat family infrastructure — partly due to the diplomatic, EU, and NGO community that has always been based there. It is also the most complex and exhausting city to live in as a family: traffic, noise, heat, and bureaucratic inefficiency in the local administration make daily life higher-friction than northern cities.

International schools:

  • American Overseas School of Rome (AOSR) — US curriculum, K–12. Fees: ~€17,000–€22,000/year
  • International School of Rome (ISR) — IB curriculum. Fees: ~€16,000–€20,000/year
  • New School Rome — UK-oriented, smaller school. Fees: ~€14,000–€18,000/year
  • St. George's British International School — UK curriculum, large school. Fees: ~€14,000–€19,000/year

Family neighborhoods: Parioli, Vigna Clara, EUR (more residential southern district), Trastevere (charming but crowded). Many international school families cluster around the school location — verify before choosing your neighborhood.

Smaller Cities for Families

Several smaller Italian cities offer excellent family quality of life, though with limited or no international school options:

  • Parma: Consistently ranked among Italy's most livable cities. Excellent food culture, very safe, good healthcare, lower cost than Bologna. If your children are young enough to integrate into Italian school, Parma is exceptional.
  • Trento: Alpine city, bilingual Italian-German environment, very high quality of life, excellent schools. Smaller expat community but outstanding infrastructure.
  • Lucca (Tuscany): Small, walkable, very safe, beloved by expat families who are comfortable with Italian school immersion. Limited international school options nearby.
  • Verona: Well-balanced city, good infrastructure, lower cost than Milan or Florence, some international school options nearby.

Using the Italian Public School System

For families committed to Italian school integration, Italy's public education system is high quality and free — and children who start under age 10 typically achieve full fluency within 18–24 months.

What to expect in the first year:

  • Schools are required to provide Italian language support (L2 Italian) for children with non-Italian mother tongue
  • The social integration is often faster than the academic — children make friends through play before they speak Italian well
  • Expect homework from the first year — Italian schools assign more regular homework than many US elementary schools
  • The school year runs September–June, with a long summer break; school hours vary but typically involve early morning through early afternoon, with afternoons off

FAQ

Will Italian international school credits transfer to US universities?
IB (International Baccalaureate) diploma graduates are well-received by US universities — many award college credit for IB higher-level courses. US curriculum international schools (like ASM and AOSR) are directly equivalent to US high schools. Italian liceo diploma requires supplementary SAT scores and sometimes additional requirements for US university applications.
At what age does it become harder to integrate into Italian school?
Generally, children under 10 adapt with relative ease. Ages 10–14 is a transition period — harder but manageable with good support. Teenagers entering Italian high school (liceo) at 15+ face the most significant challenge: the academic content is demanding and requires Italian fluency to access. Most families with teenagers in that range choose international school.
Do families need a car in Italian cities?
In major cities with good public transit (Milan, Bologna, Turin, Rome), families can manage without a car for daily life. For school pickup in suburbs, weekend trips, and medical appointments, a car becomes increasingly useful. In smaller cities and towns, a car is generally necessary. Many Italian families with children in major cities own one car for convenience even when transit is available.
