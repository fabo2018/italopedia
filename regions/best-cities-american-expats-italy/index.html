Best Cities for American Expats in Italy: The Honest 2025 Guide

The real comparison of Italy's best cities for Americans — ranked by cost, English accessibility, expat community, healthcare, culture, and quality of life. No fluff, just the truth.

FB
By Fabrizio Boggio
Updated May 2026 9 min read VERIFIED
✉ 𝕏 ⌘

Every "best cities in Italy" article tells you Florence is romantic, Milan is fashionable, and Rome is historic. You already know that. What you need to know is: which city actually works for an American expat living on $2,500–5,000/month, needing reliable healthcare, building a real life — not just a vacation?

I live in Italy and have spent time in all the cities on this list. This is the honest comparison.

What Makes a City Work for American Expats

The variables that actually determine quality of life for American expats:

  1. Cost — Can you live comfortably on your budget?
  2. English accessibility — How difficult is daily life without fluent Italian?
  3. Healthcare — Is the public hospital system reliable? Are English-speaking doctors available?
  4. Expat community — Are there other Americans/English speakers to connect with?
  5. Infrastructure — Internet, transport, airport connectivity
  6. Lifestyle fit — Urban energy, cultural depth, pace of life
  7. Visa processing — Which consulates and Questure are most efficient?

Here's how Italy's major cities stack up.

The Rankings

🥇 1. Bologna — Best Overall for Expats

Population: 390,000
Region: Emilia-Romagna
Monthly budget (single, comfortable): €1,800–2,500

Bologna doesn't make the travel magazine covers. That's exactly why it's the best city for long-term expat life.

Why Bologna wins:

Cost: 25–30% cheaper than Florence, 35–40% cheaper than Milan. A 1-bedroom in a good neighborhood: €900–1,300/month. You can live genuinely well here without financial stress.

Livability: The arcaded streets (portici — 40km of covered walkways) mean you walk everywhere in any weather. The city is compact and flat. Bikes work everywhere.

Food: Italy's undisputed food capital. Tagliatelle al ragù (the original Bolognese). Mortadella. Prosciutto di Parma. Parmigiano Reggiano. You eat extraordinarily well without trying.

Transit hub: Bologna is Italy's rail crossroads. Florence 37 minutes, Milan 1 hour, Rome 2.5 hours, Venice 1.5 hours. The rest of Italy is a quick train ride away.

Healthcare: Policlinico di Sant'Orsola and Istituto Ortopedico Rizzoli are internationally respected institutions. Emilia-Romagna's regional health service is one of Italy's best-funded.

Expat community: University town — 90,000+ students — means English is more widely spoken here than in cities its size elsewhere in Italy. Good expat support networks.

Downsides: Less famous = fewer tourists = fewer expat tourist-industry services. No beach. Winters are cold and foggy (the Po Valley "nebbia").

Best for: Retirees who want genuine Italian life at a reasonable cost; remote workers; anyone who's been tempted by Florence but balked at the price.

🥈 2. Turin (Torino) — Best Value in the North

Population: 870,000
Region: Piedmont
Monthly budget (single, comfortable): €1,700–2,300

Turin is my home base and Italy's most undervalued expat city. It has everything Milan has — except the fashion industry and the price.

Why Turin works:

Cost: 30–35% cheaper than Milan. 1-bedroom in Crocetta or San Salvario (excellent neighborhoods): €800–1,100/month. The city's history as Italy's first capital and industrial powerhouse means serious infrastructure at lower prices than you'd expect.

Architecture: Turin's Baroque city center is genuinely magnificent — more coherent and impressive than most tourists realize. The arcaded streets, the Po river views, the hill panorama. It's beautiful in a way that doesn't get photographed enough.

Cuisine: Piedmontese cuisine is Italy's most sophisticated — white truffles, tajarin pasta, bagna cauda, the Barolo and Barbaresco wines. This is where fine dining lives.

Location: 45 minutes to ski resorts (Sestriere, Bardonecchia), 90 minutes to the French Riviera (Nice, Cannes), 45 minutes to the Milan airport hub, 2 hours to Florence.

Healthcare: Strong. Ospedale Molinette and CTO are major referral centers. Turin has multiple English-speaking GPs and private clinics with international experience.

Expat community: Smaller than Rome or Milan but active — Torino Expats group on Facebook has thousands of members. U.S. expats are well-represented.

Downsides: Cold, foggy winters. Less internationally famous (some Americans feel they're "missing the real Italy"). Fewer English-language services in neighborhoods away from the center.

Best for: Budget-conscious expats who want northern Italian quality of life; those who prioritize food, wine, and mountains over beach proximity.

🥉 3. Florence — Best for Beauty, Hardest Budget

Population: 370,000
Region: Tuscany
Monthly budget (single, comfortable): €2,200–3,200

Florence is the Italy most Americans imagine. The Uffizi, the Duomo, Chianti around the corner, Renaissance at every turn. It's as beautiful as advertised — and harder to afford than advertised.

Why Florence works (for the right person):

Density of culture: Nowhere in Italy packs more world-class art, architecture, and food into such a small area. Walking to the Uffizi on a Tuesday morning, having lunch at a corner trattoria in Oltrarno, cycling along the Arno — the daily beauty is real.

Scale: Florence is small enough to know your neighborhood deeply. Your barista knows your order by week 2. This intimacy is rare in larger cities.

Expat community: Large, established, English-speaking. University study abroad programs mean significant English-language service infrastructure.

Healthcare: AOU Careggi is one of Italy's best university hospital complexes. Good private clinic options. English-speaking specialists available.

Downside — Tourist saturation: May through September, the historic center is overwhelmed with tourists. If you live there, it becomes background noise, but the prices (restaurants, services) reflect tourist demand year-round.

Downside — Cost: A comfortable life in a real neighborhood (Oltrarno, Campo di Marte, Gavinana) costs €2,200–2,800/month. The historic center costs more. On $2,500/month, you're comfortable but not free.

Best for: Retirees or remote workers with budgets above $3,000/month who specifically want Tuscan life and cultural immersion; people who've visited multiple times and know what they're getting.

4. Rome — Biggest City, Most Complex

Population: 2.8 million
Region: Lazio
Monthly budget (single, comfortable): €2,000–3,000

Rome is where the bureaucracy is headquartered — Ministries, consulates, Vatican. It's also Italy's most diverse and internationally connected city.

Why Rome works:

English everywhere: Rome's tourism industry is massive. English is widely spoken in neighborhoods like Prati, Testaccio, Trastevere, and Pigneto.

Expat infrastructure: The largest American expat community in Italy. American University of Rome, multiple international schools, American Chamber of Commerce. The most "American expat support structure" of any Italian city.

Climate: Mediterranean. Warm winters (8–12°C / 46–54°F in January), long warm summers. More sunshine than northern Italy.

Healthcare: Policlinico Gemelli and Policlinico Umberto I are national reference centers. Rome has more English-speaking doctors than any other Italian city.

Downside — Bureaucracy: Rome's Questura and immigration offices are among Italy's most backlogged. Permesso di Soggiorno waits are notoriously long.

Downside — Cost spikes: Neighborhoods close to tourist attractions (Trastevere, Prati, Monti) have surged in price. Budget expats live in Pigneto, Ostiense, or Garbatella.

Downside — Scale: Rome is enormous. Unlike Bologna or Turin, you can't feel like you "know" Rome. It's a perpetual discovery — some love this, others find it overwhelming.

Best for: Americans who want maximum English-language accessibility; people with U.S. government connections or working for international organizations; those who need large-city resources (international schools, corporate employers).

5. Milan — Best for Career, Hardest Budget

Population: 1.4 million
Region: Lombardy
Monthly budget (single, comfortable): €2,800–3,800

Milan is the right choice if your primary reason for being in Italy is career advancement or business — and it's the wrong choice if budget is a concern.

Why Milan works:

Economic capital: Italy's highest salaries, most international companies, financial industry, fashion, design, tech. If you're working in Italy (not just living there), Milan is where opportunities concentrate.

International connectivity: Malpensa airport is Northern Italy's international hub. Direct flights to dozens of U.S. cities.

English in business: The most English-speaking Italian city in professional contexts.

Healthcare: Italy's best private hospital network (Humanitas, San Raffaele, Policlinico, Istituto Ortopedico Galeazzi). If money is no object, Milan has the best private care in the country.

Downside — Cost: The most expensive Italian city. 1-bedroom in a decent neighborhood: €1,100–1,600. Comfortable life: €2,800–3,500/month minimum.

Downside — Lifestyle: Milan is more cosmopolitan than Italian. If you moved to Italy for the Italian atmosphere, Milan can feel like a European business city that happens to be in Italy.

Best for: Americans working for Italian or international companies; those in fashion, finance, design, or tech; high earners for whom the cost premium is irrelevant.

6. Naples — Best Value, Steepest Learning Curve

Population: 940,000
Region: Campania
Monthly budget (single, comfortable): €1,400–2,000

Naples is the most polarizing city on this list. People who love it are evangelical about it. People who struggle with it struggle hard.

Why Naples works:

Cost: Italy's cheapest major city for quality of life. 1-bedroom in Vomero or Chiaia: €650–900.

Food: The pizza is not a cliché — Neapolitan pizza is genuinely different from anywhere else. The street food, the seafood, the coffee culture.

Energy: Naples has a chaotic, alive energy that more orderly Italian cities lack. The Spaccanapoli, the Castel dell'Ovo, Vesuvius in the background — the drama is real.

The 7% Flat Tax: Campania is an eligible region for the flat tax for new residents — potentially a major financial advantage.

Downside — Language barrier: English is less commonly spoken in Naples than in tourist-heavy northern cities. Italian is more essential here than anywhere on this list.

Downside — Bureaucratic inconsistency: Neapolitan bureaucracy requires more patience than its northern counterparts. Things happen on a different timeline.

Downside — Safety perception: Naples has real crime, concentrated in specific neighborhoods. Expats living in Vomero, Posillipo, or Chiaia report minimal issues. The historic center Quartieri Spagnoli requires more awareness.

Best for: Adventurous retirees with time and language skills; budget-focused Americans; those drawn specifically to southern Italian culture; anyone eligible for the 7% flat tax.

The Right City for Your Situation

On $2,500/month or under: Turin, Bologna, or Naples. Don't try Florence or Milan.

Retired with $3,000–4,000/month: Florence or Rome work comfortably. Bologna and Turin give you a cushion.

Working remotely, any budget: Bologna wins on quality-of-life-to-cost ratio. Milan if you need the international business environment.

Healthcare priority: Rome (largest network), Milan (best private), Bologna (best public system quality).

Slow Italian life, cultural immersion: Bologna, Siena, Lucca — though Siena and Lucca are covered in the Tuscany guide.

Don't know yet: Bologna. It's the safest bet for first-time expats. If you discover you want more glamour, Florence is 37 minutes away. If you want cheaper, Naples is 3 hours. If you want business, Milan is 1 hour.
