Italy's 8-Day Rule: How to Declare Your Presence as an American (2025)

Arrive in a private rental in Italy on a long-stay visa? You have 8 working days to declare your presence at the Questura. Here's exactly how.

One of the first legal requirements every American faces when arriving in Italy with a long-stay visa is the 8-day declaration of presence (dichiarazione di presenza). Miss this step and you technically violated Italian immigration law — even if everything else about your visa and residency application is perfect.

This guide explains exactly who must comply, how to do it, what it means for your Permesso di Soggiorno application, and what happens if you miss the window.

What Is the 8-Day Rule?

Under D.Lgs. 286/1998 (Testo Unico Immigrazione), Article 5, non-EU foreigners entering Italy for stays longer than 3 months must declare their presence to Italian authorities within 8 working days of arrival.

This is separate from — and earlier than — the Permesso di Soggiorno application (which you have 60 days to file). The declaration of presence is just that: a notification that you're in Italy and where you're staying.

Who Must File the Declaration?

Must File (within 8 working days):

  • Americans arriving with a long-stay visa (Type D) — Elective Residency, Digital Nomad, Family Reunification, Student, Work visas
  • Americans staying in private accommodation (rented apartment, house, friend's home, etc.)
  • Anyone planning to stay more than 90 days

Does NOT Need to File:

  • Hotel, B&B, hostel, agriturismo guests — accommodation providers are legally required to register guests with authorities automatically. If you're staying at a hotel, the hotel handles this. You don't need to do anything.
  • Short Schengen tourists (90 days or less) — the standard tourist entry doesn't require a declaration
  • EU citizens — different rules apply under freedom of movement

The Key Distinction: Private vs. Commercial Accommodation

If your first accommodation in Italy is a hotel or other registered accommodation, you're covered automatically. The moment you move into a private rental — even temporarily — you must file your own declaration.

How to File: Step-by-Step

Where to Go: The Questura

The declaration is filed at your local Questura (police headquarters) — specifically the Ufficio Immigrazione (immigration office).

Find your Questura:

  • Google "Questura [your city]"
  • poliziadistato.it → Uffici → Questure

Hours: Italian Questure typically have specific hours for immigration matters — often morning-only (8:00–12:00). Check before you go.

What to Bring

  • Valid passport with your entry stamp and visa
  • Proof of accommodation — rental contract, host's identity document (if staying with someone), or your own property deed
  • Completed dichiarazione di presenza form — available at the Questura or downloadable from poliziadistato.it

If you're staying with an Italian citizen or resident:

  • Your host (the "dichiarante") can file on your behalf, OR
  • You file yourself and list their name and address as your accommodation

The Form: Dichiarazione di Presenza

The declaration (sometimes called "comunicazione di dimora") requires:

  • Your personal details (name, date/place of birth, passport number)
  • Your Italian address
  • The expected duration of stay
  • Your signature

The Questura stamps or acknowledges the form. Keep a copy — you may need it when filing your Permesso di Soggiorno application.

Alternative: Post Office Filing

Some Questure allow the declaration to be filed at the Post Office (Poste Italiane) using the same Kit used for Permesso applications. In practice, the Questura is more reliable — call your local Questura to confirm which method they accept.

The 8-Day Clock: What "Working Days" Means

The 8-day window is 8 working days (giorni lavorativi) — not 8 calendar days. This means:

  • Saturdays, Sundays, and Italian public holidays don't count
  • If you arrive on a Friday, your 8-day clock doesn't include Saturday or Sunday

Italian national holidays (giorni festivi): January 1, January 6, Easter Monday, April 25, May 1, June 2, August 15, November 1, December 8, December 25, December 26

Plus local patron saint days (varies by city — ask locally)

Practical counting example:

  • Arrive Monday → working days: Tue, Wed, Thu, Fri, Mon, Tue, Wed, Thu = deadline is Thursday of following week

When in doubt, count conservatively and file as early as possible. Day 1 is typically the day after arrival.

Connection to the Permesso di Soggiorno

The declaration of presence and the Permesso di Soggiorno are different things:

Dichiarazione di PresenzaPermesso di Soggiorno
Deadline8 working days from arrival60 days from arrival
WhereQuestura directlyPost Office (Kit permesso)
PurposeNotify you're in ItalyApply for residency permit
ResultAcknowledgment stampPermesso card (months later)

The declaration doesn't replace the Permesso — it's a prerequisite step. Your Permesso application will reference your arrival date and your accommodation.

Some Questure offices process both on the same visit for certain visa types. Ask when you call or arrive.

What If You Miss the 8-Day Deadline?

Missing the 8-day window is a technical immigration violation under D.Lgs. 286/1998. In practice:

For most first-time arrivals with valid visas: The consequences are rarely severe if you file late but promptly. Italian immigration authorities generally focus on willful non-compliance rather than bureaucratic delays.

Potential consequences:

  • A formal warning (diffida)
  • Theoretically, an administrative fine
  • In extreme cases (significant delay, other violations), complications with your Permesso application

What to do if you've missed it: File immediately — don't compound the delay. Go to the Questura, explain you arrived on [date], and complete the declaration now. For most Americans on valid long-stay visas who are genuinely trying to comply, this is treated administratively rather than punitively.

Important: Never ignore it. Even if late, filing demonstrates good faith and intent to comply with Italian immigration law.

Practical Scenarios

Scenario 1: Arriving at Fiumicino, Going to a Rented Apartment in Rome

  • Day 0: Arrive at Fiumicino, take taxi/train to your apartment
  • Day 1 onwards: 8-day clock starts
  • Action: Go to Questura Roma (Via Teofilo Patini) during morning immigration hours within 8 working days
  • Bring: Passport, rental contract, completed form

Scenario 2: First Night at a Hotel, Then Moving to Rental

  • Hotel night: covered by hotel registration
  • Move-in day at rental: 8-day clock starts on move-in day (not your arrival in Italy)
  • Action: File declaration within 8 working days of move-in date

Scenario 3: Staying with an Italian Friend

  • Your friend (the host) can file on your behalf at the Questura — this is the "dichiarazione di ospitalità" (hospitality declaration)
  • OR you file yourself listing your friend's address as accommodation
  • Your friend needs: Their identity document; your passport details

Scenario 4: Moving to a New City Within Italy

If you move from one city to another after your initial declaration:

  • File a new declaration at the Questura of the new city within 48 hours of moving
  • This is separate from updating your Residenza at the new Comune — do both

After the Declaration: Next Steps

The declaration of presence is step one. Here's what follows:

Within 60 days: Apply for your Permesso di Soggiorno at the Post Office (using the official "Kit" for foreigners)

After receiving Permesso appointment confirmation: Attend your Questura biometric appointment

After Permesso receipt/card: Register at your Comune's Anagrafe (registry office) to establish Italian Residenza

The declaration of presence → Permesso application → Residenza registration is the standard sequence for every American establishing long-term legal residency in Italy.

Common Questions

Do I need to do this if I'm staying less than 90 days? No — short-stay Schengen tourist visits don't require a formal declaration of presence. This requirement is for long-stay visa holders.

What if my Italian host doesn't want to file on my behalf? You can file yourself — you don't need your host's participation. Just bring their address and their name to write on the form.

Can I file online? Currently, the dichiarazione di presenza must be filed in person at the Questura. Some Questure have online appointment booking, but the actual filing requires your presence.

Does a notarized letter from my landlord work instead? The rental contract itself is the standard proof of accommodation. A notarized letter isn't required — a standard signed rental contract (even preliminary/short-term) is sufficient.
