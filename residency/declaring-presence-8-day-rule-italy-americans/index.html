Italy's 8-Day Declaration of Presence: What Every American Must Do After Arrival

If you are a non-EU citizen entering Italy for stays longer than 3 months, Italian law requires you to declare your presence to the local questura within 8 business days of arrival. Hotels do this automatically — private rentals don't. Here's how to handle it.

Quick Answer

The dichiarazione di presenza (declaration of presence) is a formal notification to Italian immigration authorities that you have entered the country and are residing at a specific address. It is required under D.Lgs. 286/1998 for all non-EU nationals staying beyond the 90-day Schengen tourist allowance. If you are staying in a hotel, the hotel does this automatically within 24 hours. If you are in a private rental, Airbnb, or with a host, you must do it yourself — within 8 business days of arrival.

What Is the Declaration of Presence?

It is separate from — and comes before — the permesso di soggiorno (residence permit) application. Think of it as the first step: you arrive, you declare your presence, and then within 8 days you also go to the post office to begin your permesso application.

For most Americans arriving on a long-stay visa, the kit postale submission at the post office simultaneously functions as your declaration of presence, covering both requirements in a single visit.

Who Must File?

You must file if:

  • You are a US passport holder
  • You arrived with a long-stay visa (type D: elective residence, digital nomad, student, etc.)
  • You are staying at a private address (rental apartment, friend's home, own property)

You do NOT need to file if:

  • You are staying in a hotel, B&B, hostel, or other registered accommodation — these establishments report your presence automatically within 24 hours
  • You are a citizen of an EU/EEA country

The 8-Day Rule: What It Means

"8 business days" means 8 working days (giorni lavorativi) — Saturdays, Sundays, and Italian public holidays do not count. In practice, this gives you approximately 10–12 calendar days from arrival.

💡 Tip

Do not cut it close. Aim to file within the first 5 business days. Italian government offices have unpredictable hours and occasional closures.

How to File: Two Methods

Method 1: Kit Postale at the Post Office (Recommended)

For Americans who have just arrived with a long-stay visa and intend to apply for a permesso di soggiorno, the kit postale submission at the post office simultaneously functions as your declaration of presence. This is the recommended method — it kills two birds with one stone.

Not all post offices handle kit postale — only designated ones (Sportelli Amici). Find yours at poliziadistato.it.

Method 2: At the Questura (Police Headquarters)

Go to the Ufficio Immigrazione of your local questura. Every Italian province has one.

What to bring:

  • Valid US passport (original + photocopy of all pages with stamps)
  • Valid Italian visa (original + photocopy)
  • Proof of your Italian address (lease agreement, declaration from your host, or hotel receipt)
  • Completed dichiarazione di presenza form (available at the questura or at poliziadistato.it)

An officer will accept your declaration, stamp it, and give you a copy. Keep this stamped copy — it proves compliance and will be referenced during your permesso di soggiorno application.

Host Obligations

If an Italian citizen or resident is hosting you, they have their own obligation: they must file a dichiarazione di ospitalità (declaration of hospitality) with the local questura within 48 hours of your arrival. This applies to landlords, friends, and family members.

In practice, many private hosts do not do this, leaving the burden on you. If your host is not filing, go to the questura yourself.

Airbnb and Short-Term Rentals

Major platforms (Airbnb, Booking.com) are increasingly compliant with reporting requirements. Individual Airbnb hosts may or may not be. If you arrive on a long-stay visa and your first accommodation is an Airbnb, do not assume the platform has filed for you. Check with the host or file at the questura yourself.

What If You Miss the 8-Day Deadline?

Missing the deadline is a violation of Italian immigration law and can technically result in an administrative fine. However:

  • For first-time arrivals with a valid visa, enforcement is inconsistent
  • It will not result in immediate expulsion if you have a valid visa
  • It will complicate your permesso di soggiorno application

What to do: Go to the questura immediately, explain the situation honestly, and file the declaration. In most cases, officers are pragmatic with first-time visa holders who are clearly trying to comply.

Practical Checklist

On or before your 8th business day in Italy:

  • Italian address confirmed (lease signed or host ready with dichiarazione di ospitalità)
  • Passport photocopied (all pages with stamps)
  • Visa photocopied
  • Either: go to a designated post office with your kit postale documents (recommended)
  • Or: go to your local questura's Ufficio Immigrazione and file the declaration directly
  • Keep your stamped receipt/declaration copy safe

FAQ

I arrived as a tourist — do I need to file? If you entered on the 90-day Schengen tourist allowance and are staying in a hotel or registered accommodation, no. If staying privately and intending to stay beyond 90 days, yes.

My host filed the dichiarazione di ospitalità — am I covered? In principle, yes. For your permesso di soggiorno, you will still need to file the kit postale at the post office.

Does this affect my US tourist visa waiver for future US entries? No. The Italian declaration of presence has no interaction with US Customs and Border Protection records.
