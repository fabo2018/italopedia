How to Open a Bank Account in Italy as an American (2025)

Complete guide for Americans on opening an Italian bank account — resident vs non-resident accounts, best banks for expats, digital alternatives, documents needed, and the smart banking strategy most expats use.

An Italian bank account isn't optional — it's essential. You need it to pay rent, receive your Permesso di Soggiorno notifications, pay taxes via F24 form, set up utilities, and function in daily Italian life.

The process is more bureaucratic than Americans expect, but there's a smart strategy that makes it much easier. This guide explains your options, the documents you need, the best banks for expats, and the combination most experienced expats use.

Resident vs. Non-Resident Account: The Critical Distinction

Resident Account (Conto Corrente Residente)

Available once you're registered at the Anagrafe and have Italian tax residency.

  • Lower fees
  • Full banking services (IBAN, debit card, credit card, direct debits, F24 payments)
  • Access to mortgages and loans

Non-Resident Account (Conto Corrente Non-Residente)

Available if you don't yet have Italian residency.

  • Higher fees
  • Limited services at some banks
💡 Note

Practical advice: If you arrive in Italy with your Permesso di Soggiorno ricevuta and Anagrafe registration, you can often open a resident account from the start.

What You Need to Open an Italian Bank Account

For a Resident Account:

  • Valid US passport (original + photocopy)
  • Codice Fiscale — absolutely required
  • Proof of Italian address — Anagrafe registration certificate or registered lease
  • Permesso di Soggiorno or ricevuta
  • Proof of income (some banks require this, others don't)

Your Banking Options: A Realistic Assessment

Traditional Italian Banks

Intesa Sanpaolo — Italy's largest banking group. Most branches, deepest integration with Italian bureaucracy. Best for long-term residents, property buyers, anyone who needs an Italian mortgage. Monthly fees: €2–8/month.

UniCredit — Italy's second-largest bank. Similar to Intesa in scale and services. Monthly fees: €2–10/month.

FinecoBank — The best of both worlds — technically a traditional Italian bank but with a completely digital-first approach. Low fees, excellent app. Monthly fees: €0–3.95/month.

Digital Banks (Neobanks)

N26 (German) — Excellent English-language app, instant notifications. Critical limitation: Provides a German IBAN (DE...), not Italian. Monthly fees: €0–16.90/month.

Revolut (Lithuanian) — Multi-currency accounts, excellent exchange rates. Critical limitation: Provides a Lithuanian IBAN (LT...), not Italian.

Wise — Best for sending money US → Italy at lowest exchange rate fees. Not a bank — provides a Belgian EUR IBAN.

The Smart Strategy: Two Accounts

Most experienced expats in Italy use a two-account system:

Account 1: Italian Bank (Intesa Sanpaolo, UniCredit, or Fineco) Purpose: Italian IBAN for rent, utilities, F24 tax payments, anything requiring an Italian bank

Account 2: Revolut or Wise Purpose: Receiving money from the US, converting USD to EUR at the best rates

💡 Note

Why this works: Italian landlords and government systems often insist on Italian IBAN (IT...) for payments. But traditional Italian banks have terrible exchange rates. Keep your Italian account for Italy-specific payments, and use Wise/Revolut for all USD→EUR conversions.

A Note on American Banking in Italy: FATCA Complications

Under FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act), Italian banks must report information about accounts held by US persons to the IRS. Some smaller Italian banks avoid opening accounts for American citizens.

Practical implication: Stick to large banks (Intesa Sanpaolo, UniCredit, Fineco) — they have established FATCA compliance procedures. Avoid smaller local banks.

At the account opening appointment, you'll be asked to complete IRS Form W-8BEN or W-9 to certify your US tax status.

Transferring Money from US to Italy: Best Options

Wise — consistently the best rates and lowest fees. Typically 0.4–0.7% above mid-market rate.

Revolut — good for regular smaller transfers.

Your US bank wire transfer — avoid for anything large. Typically 2–4% above mid-market rate + $25–45 wire fee.

💡 Note

Example savings: Transferring $10,000 USD to Italy via Wise costs approximately $45–70. Via a typical US bank wire transfer: $200–400.

FAQ

Do I need an Italian bank account to rent an apartment? In practice, yes — most landlords require rent payment via Italian bank transfer (SEPA/IT IBAN).

Can I keep my US bank accounts when I move to Italy? Yes — and you should. Keep at least one US bank account. Remember to report all foreign bank accounts on your annual FBAR if aggregate balances exceed $10,000.

What is SPID and do I need it? SPID (Sistema Pubblico di Identità Digitale) is Italy's national digital identity system. You can create your SPID through major banks including Intesa Sanpaolo. Get it as soon as you have an Italian bank account — it unlocks online government functionality.
