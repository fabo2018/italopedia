Registering a Foreign Car in Italy: What Americans Need to Know (2025)

Complete guide to importing and registering a U.S. or foreign car in Italy — the process, costs, homologation requirements, and whether it's actually worth it.

⚡ Quick answer

Importing a US car is rarely worth it: customs duty (6.5%) + IVA (22%) + homologation (€2,000–8,000) typically costs more than the car itself. After 1 year of Italian residency, your US license expires — no bilateral exchange agreement exists, so you must pass the Italian driving exam. EU cars bought abroad must be re-registered in Italy within 60 days of establishing residency. One of the practical questions every American moving to Italy eventually faces: what do I do about a car? You may have a U.S. vehicle you want to bring over, or you may have arrived and need to drive on your U.S. license, or you may be wondering whether the Italian system requires re-registration of a car you purchased in another EU country.

This guide covers all the scenarios — including the most common situation: whether importing a car from the U.S. is actually worth it (spoiler: it usually isn't), and what your real options are.

The Short Answer: Should You Import Your U.S. Car?

Almost certainly no. Here's why:

The cost is prohibitive. Importing a U.S.-specification vehicle to Italy involves: - Shipping: $1,500–4,000 (ocean freight) - Italian customs duty: 6.5% of vehicle value - VAT (IVA): 22% of vehicle value + shipping + customs - Homologation (immatricolazione): €2,000–8,000+ in technical modifications, inspections, and paperwork to convert a U.S.-spec car to EU standards - Registration fees, IPT (provincial car tax), bollo (road tax)

For a $15,000 car, you're looking at total import costs of $8,000–15,000 before you even drive it.

Technical incompatibility. U.S. cars are built to FMVSS (Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards), not EU/ECE standards. Differences include: - Speedometer (mph vs. km/h) - Headlights (DOT vs. ECE standard) - Bumper impact standards - OBD emissions systems (OBD-II vs. EOBD) - Lighting positions and intensity

Converting a car to full EU homologation is a technically complex and expensive process. Some older vehicles (pre-1980) have simpler paths; modern cars are rarely worth the trouble.

Italian used car market. Perfectly good Italian-registered cars are available for €5,000–20,000. Buying locally is faster, cheaper, and comes with Italian registration already in place.

Verdict: Unless you have a very specific, sentimental, or highly valuable vehicle, buy a car in Italy.

Scenario 1: Driving in Italy on Your U.S. Driver's License

When You First Arrive (First Year)

Upon establishing Italian residency (Residenza), you have one year to either: a) Use your U.S. driver's license (alongside an International Driving Permit as translation), or b) Convert your U.S. license to an Italian license

During that first year: - Your U.S. license is valid for driving in Italy - An International Driving Permit (IDP) is strongly recommended — it's a standardized translation of your U.S. license and prevents friction with Italian authorities who may not read English - Get the IDP in the U.S. before moving: AAA issues them for $20 with two passport photos

After One Year of Italian Residency

After one year, your U.S. license is no longer valid for driving in Italy if you are an Italian resident. You must either: - Convert your U.S. license to an Italian one (where possible), or - Take the Italian driving exam

Legal basis: D.Lgs. 285/1992 (Codice della Strada), Article 136; Ministero delle Infrastrutture e dei Trasporti circulars on foreign driving licenses.

Scenario 2: Converting Your U.S. Driver's License to an Italian License

Here's the frustrating reality: Italy does not have a direct reciprocal license exchange agreement with the United States. Unlike countries such as Switzerland or Japan, which have bilateral agreements allowing direct conversion, U.S. licenses cannot be simply "swapped" for an Italian license.

What this means for you:

  1. You can use your U.S. license for the first year of Italian residency (with IDP)
  2. After one year, you must take the Italian driving exam to get an Italian patente
  3. There is no shortcut, regardless of your driving experience

Some good news: - Some U.S. states have informal arrangements where Italian authorities accept the license for the practical (driving) exam only, waiving the theory exam. This is not official policy and varies by province (Motorizzazione Civile office). Ask at your local Motorizzazione. - The written exam can be taken in English at authorized exam centers.

The Italian Driving Exam Process

  1. Enroll at an authorized driving school (autoscuola) — required for the theoretical exam and optional for practical
  2. Obtain a certificato medico — medical certificate from an authorized doctor confirming fitness to drive (~€50)
  3. Take the theory exam (esame teorico) — 40 multiple-choice questions, 30 minutes; maximum 4 errors allowed. Can be taken in English.
  4. Take the practical exam (esame pratico) — with a Motorizzazione examiner
  5. Receive your patente — Italian driving license

Cost of Italian driving school + exam: - Full course at autoscuola: €600–1,200 (includes lessons, exam preparation, fees) - If you already drive confidently: focus on theory study and pay for the exam without taking all lessons

Reference: Ministero delle Infrastrutture e dei Trasporti mit.gov.it; D.Lgs. 285/1992

Scenario 3: Registering a Car Purchased in Another EU Country

If you bought a car in Germany, France, or another EU country and drive it to Italy after becoming an Italian resident, you must re-register it in Italy within 60 days of establishing Italian residency.

The Re-Registration Process (Reimmatricolazione)

  1. Obtain a COC (Certificate of Conformity) — from the vehicle manufacturer or importer. Shows the car meets EU standards. Cost: €50–200 from manufacturer.

  2. Visit the Agenzia delle Entrate or a Centro Revisioni (inspection center)** for the Italian technical inspection (Collaudo per reimmatricolazione).

  3. Submit to Motorizzazione Civile (or use an authorized Auto agency/STA):

  4. Original foreign registration documents
  5. COC certificate
  6. Proof of identity + Codice Fiscale
  7. Proof of Italian residency

  8. Insurance (polizza RCA — Italian liability insurance)

  9. Pay fees:

  10. IPT (Imposta Provinciale di Trascrizione) — provincial tax, varies: €100–500 depending on province and vehicle type
  11. Bollo ACI (registration fee): ~€32

  12. Various administrative fees: €50–150

  13. Receive Italian plates (targhe italiane) and updated documents

Processing time: 2–6 weeks through an agency (pratiche auto agency handles paperwork for €100–250 fee; strongly recommended to avoid bureaucratic back-and-forth).

Legal basis: D.Lgs. 285/1992 (Codice della Strada), Articles 93–96; Provvedimento MIT on vehicle registration

Scenario 4: Importing a U.S. Car (If You Really Must)

If you have a specific, valuable, or sentimental reason to import a U.S. car, here's the process:

Step 1: Shipping

Use a car shipping broker specializing in U.S.-to-Italy transport. Common routes: East Coast ports (Baltimore, New York) to Genoa or Livorno.

  • RORO (Roll-on/Roll-off): $1,200–2,500; car driven onto ship; exposed on deck
  • Container shipping: $2,500–4,500; more protected

Typical transit time: 2–4 weeks.

Step 2: Italian Customs (Dogana)

Upon arrival in Italy, the car clears Italian Customs at the port:

  • Import duty: 6.5% of vehicle value (customs-declared)
  • IVA (VAT): 22% applied to: vehicle value + shipping + customs duty
  • Total tax burden: Approximately 30% of vehicle value

Exception — Duty-Free Import for Transferring Residents (Cambio Residenza): If you are moving your primary residence to Italy and the car has been registered in your name for at least 6 months before you moved, you may import it duty-free and IVA-free under the Personal Property Relief (esenzione per trasloco residenziale).

  • The car must have been owned and used by you in the U.S.
  • You must not have been an Italian resident in the previous 12 months
  • You must establish Italian residency within 12 months of import

Official reference: EU Regulation 1186/2009 (relief from customs duty on personal property); DPR 633/1972 (VAT exemption provisions)

This exemption can save €3,000–10,000+ on a typical car import. If you're moving to Italy with a car you want to bring, time the import to coincide with your residency establishment.

Step 3: Homologation (Omologazione/Immatricolazione)

The most complex step. A U.S.-spec car must be certified as complying with EU standards:

  1. Find a certified omologazione technician — specialized shops exist in major Italian cities and ports
  2. Technical modifications that may be required:
  3. Headlight replacement/adjustment (DOT → ECE)
  4. Speedometer modification (mph markings)
  5. Rear fog light installation
  6. EU-compliant bumpers (if different)
  7. Emissions compliance (EOBD)
  8. Tires (DOT vs. EU markings)
  9. Inspection by Motorizzazione Civile — the car is physically inspected
  10. Homologation certificate issued

Cost: €2,000–8,000 depending on vehicle age, make, and how different the U.S. spec is from EU. Luxury cars, SUVs, and trucks are typically more expensive to homologate.

Alternative: Individual Approval (Approvazione Individuale) If your car cannot be homologated to EU type approval standards, you can seek individual vehicle approval. More expensive and complex, but an option for rare or custom vehicles.

Step 4: Italian Registration

After homologation: - Register at Motorizzazione Civile - Pay IPT, ACI fees - Get Italian insurance (required before registration) - Receive Italian license plates (targhe)

Italian Car Ownership: The Ongoing Costs

Once you have an Italian-registered car (whether imported or purchased in Italy), these are your annual costs:

Cost Amount
Bollo auto (annual road tax) €100–400 depending on horsepower/engine
RCA insurance (mandatory liability) €500–1,500/year (varies by location and record)
Revisione (biennial inspection, equivalent to UK MOT) €60–80 every 2 years
Fuel €1.60–1.90/liter (gasoline); €1.55–1.75 (diesel)
ZTL fines (if you drive in restricted zones) €80–160 per violation

Insurance note: Italian RCA (Responsabilità Civile Auto) is mandatory and prices vary significantly by region. Northern Italy (Piedmont, Lombardy) is relatively affordable; Naples and Campania are the most expensive due to higher fraud rates.

Practical Recommendations

For most Americans moving to Italy: Buy a used car in Italy after arrival. You'll save thousands versus importing, and you'll avoid months of bureaucratic complexity. Budget €5,000–15,000 for a reliable used Italian car (Fiat, Alfa Romeo, Volkswagen — all commonly available).

Buy through a licensed dealer (concessionario) or use a trusted private seller. Italian used car buying has its own scams — avoid "too good to be true" deals, check the vehicle's visura PRA (registration history at aci.it) before buying.

Consider starting without a car in cities. If you're settling in Florence, Milan, or Bologna, you may find you don't need a car at all for the first year. Get settled, understand the public transport system, then decide if a car adds value.

Use a pratiche auto agency for any registration paperwork. These are specialized offices (often combined with insurance agencies) that handle all Italian vehicle administrative procedures. Their €80–200 fee buys you days of saved bureaucratic effort.
