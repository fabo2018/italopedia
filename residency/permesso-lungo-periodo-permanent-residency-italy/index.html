Permesso di Lungo Periodo: Italy's Permanent Residency After 5 Years (2025)

After 5 years of continuous legal residence, Americans can apply for Italy's EU long-term residence permit — essentially permanent residency. Valid 5 years, renewable indefinitely, granting near-citizen rights including unrestricted work authorization.

Quick Answer

The Permesso di Soggiorno UE per Soggiornanti di Lungo Periodo is Italy's version of permanent residency for non-EU citizens. After 5 years of continuous legal residence, you can apply. It is valid for 5 years and renewable indefinitely. Main requirements beyond 5 years of residence: sufficient income (approximately €6,500/year), basic Italian language at A2 level, and a clean criminal record. Processing time: typically 4–12 months depending on your questura.

What Is the Permesso di Lungo Periodo?

The Permesso di Soggiorno UE per Soggiornanti di Lungo Periodo (commonly called "permesso di lungo periodo" or "carta di lungo periodo") is Italy's long-term residence status for non-EU citizens, implemented pursuant to EU Directive 2003/109/EC.

Unlike your regular permesso di soggiorno (which expires every 1–2 years and must be renewed based on your original visa type), the lungo periodo:

  • Is valid for 5 years
  • Is renewable indefinitely — as long as you continue to reside in Italy
  • Grants work authorization in any sector without restrictions
  • Allows you to move to another EU country and apply for long-term residence there more easily
  • Cannot be revoked except for serious criminal offenses or 12+ consecutive months abroad

Eligibility Requirements

1. Five Years of Continuous Legal Residence

You must have been legally and continuously resident in Italy for at least 5 years immediately before your application. "Continuously" means: no gaps in your permesso di soggiorno, you have not been absent from Italy for more than 6 consecutive months or 10 months total during the 5-year period.

The clock starts from the date of your first permesso di soggiorno, not from your visa or arrival date.

2. Sufficient Income

You must demonstrate income at least equal to Italy's assegno sociale:

  • Single applicant: approximately €6,500/year (updated annually)
  • With dependent spouse: approximately €9,750/year

This is a lower income threshold than the Elective Residence Visa minimum — most Americans on elective residence visas already far exceed this.

3. Italian Language at A2 Level

Since 2012, applicants must demonstrate basic Italian language competency at A2 level.

How to prove A2: Pass the official CILS or CELI Italian language exam; hold a diploma or degree taught in Italian; have completed a recognized Italian language course with an A2 certificate.

💡 Tip

A2 is genuinely basic — simple sentences about familiar topics. Most Americans who have lived in Italy for 5 years will have acquired this naturally. Do not wait until your 5th year to start thinking about the language requirement. Enroll early and get the certificate ready.

4. Clean Criminal Record

No serious criminal convictions. The questura will check Italian criminal records. You must also declare foreign criminal records. Certain crimes (terrorism, drug trafficking, organized crime, crimes against minors) are automatic disqualifiers.

5. Valid Accommodation

Proof that you have suitable housing in Italy — a lease agreement, property deed, or declaration from a host.

How to Apply: Step by Step

Step 1: Gather Your Documents

  • Valid passport (original + photocopies of all pages)
  • Current permesso di soggiorno (original + photocopy)
  • Completed application form (Modello 1, available at questura or poliziadistato.it)
  • Marca da bollo (€16) + Bollettino postale (€30.46)
  • Proof of 5 years of continuous residence (permesso renewals showing unbroken chain)
  • Income documentation: last tax return, bank statements, pension letters
  • Italian language certificate (CILS/CELI A2 or equivalent)
  • Italian criminal record certificate (from Tribunale)
  • Proof of accommodation (lease or property deed)
  • 4 passport photos + codice fiscale

Step 2: Submit at the Post Office (Kit Postale)

The lungo periodo is submitted through the kit postale system at a designated Sportello Amico post office — the same system used for regular permesso renewals. The post office verifies documents, issues a ricevuta with a questura appointment date, and forwards your application electronically to the questura.

Step 3: Attend Questura Appointments

You will typically have a biometrics appointment (fingerprints and photo) and a final collection appointment. Processing time: typically 4–12 months depending on your questura's workload.

Maintaining Your Lungo Periodo

Once issued, the lungo periodo remains valid as long as you do not remain outside Italy for 12 consecutive months (or outside the EU for 6 consecutive years), do not acquire another EU country's long-term residence status, and are not convicted of serious crimes.

The lungo periodo is a valuable milestone, but many Americans who reach it continue toward citizenship — it requires only 5 more years (10 total) and opens dramatically more doors in Europe.

Frequently Asked Questions

I had a gap in my permesso — does that reset my 5-year clock?
A short administrative gap with a ricevuta is not a break in legal residence. An actual lapse — entering Italy without valid status — may reset the clock. Consult a lawyer for your specific situation.

Can my family members get the lungo periodo too?
Yes. Family members who have been resident in Italy for 5 years on family-based permits can apply independently.

Can the lungo periodo be transferred to another EU country?
Yes — if you later want to move to Germany, France, or Spain, your Italian lungo periodo makes it easier to obtain long-term residence there. Each EU country has its own rules for this EU-wide status.
