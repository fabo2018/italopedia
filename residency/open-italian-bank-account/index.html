# Opening an Italian Bank Account: The Complete Guide for Americans (2025)

An Italian bank account is not optional for Americans living in Italy. You need it to pay rent (landlords require bank transfers), set up utilities, receive Italian income, pay taxes (via F24), and function in the Italian financial system. Without one, you're paying international wire fees on every transaction and relying on cash in a country that increasingly requires electronic payments.

The complication: Italian banks are more cautious with U.S. citizens than with other nationalities, due to FATCA compliance requirements. Several banks have quietly stopped opening accounts for Americans. This guide tells you which banks still work, what you need to bring, and how to navigate the process.

The FATCA Problem: Why Some Banks Refuse Americans

FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act) requires foreign financial institutions to report accounts held by U.S. persons to the U.S. Treasury. Italian banks that accept U.S. citizen accounts must:

  • Identify U.S. persons (through documentation)
  • Report account information annually to the IRS (via Italian tax authority)
  • Comply with IRS W-9 and related forms

The compliance cost and liability exposure have led some Italian banks to informally discourage or refuse accounts for U.S. citizens. This is legal — banks have discretion in who they serve.

The good news: Many Italian banks still actively accept American customers. You just need to go to the right ones.

Which Italian Banks Accept American Citizens?

Banks That Generally Accept Americans

Intesa Sanpaolo

Italy's largest bank. Has a formal FATCA compliance program and actively accepts U.S. citizens. Widely available nationwide.

  • Branches everywhere
  • Full service: checking, savings, mortgage, investment
  • English support: limited in branches; better via app and online

Unicredit

Italy's second-largest bank. FATCA-compliant, accepts Americans.

  • Good digital banking
  • English customer service available at major branches
  • Mobile app reasonably well-designed

Banco BPM

Major Italian bank, particularly strong in northern Italy. FATCA-compliant.

  • Good in Lombardy, Piedmont, Veneto
  • Decent digital services

BNL (BNP Paribas Group)

French-Italian bank. French parent makes international compliance more straightforward.

  • Good English support
  • International banking familiarity
  • Strong in Rome and major cities

Credem (Credito Emiliano)

Regional bank, strong in Emilia-Romagna. Accepts Americans; personalized service.

Online/Digital Banks (Easiest for Americans)

N26

German digital bank with Italian operations. Opens entirely online. However, N26 does not accept U.S. persons as account holders (U.S. FATCA compliance is their exception). Not an option for Americans.

Hype

Italian fintech; IBAN provided; can work as a secondary account. FATCA compliance unclear — verify before relying on it.

Revolut

UK/EU fintech. Generally accepts U.S. persons for basic accounts. Not a full Italian bank account but useful as a supplement. Cannot receive Italian government payments (F24, SSN contributions).

Wise (formerly TransferWise)

Not a bank but provides an Italian IBAN (via Banking Circle). Useful for receiving transfers; not accepted for all Italian government payments.

For primary Italian banking: stick with traditional Italian banks. Digital alternatives are supplements, not replacements.

What You Need to Open an Account

Required Documents (Standard)

  1. Valid passport (U.S. passport)
  2. Italian Codice Fiscale — your Italian tax number; mandatory
  3. Italian address — proof of where you live in Italy
  4. IRS Form W-9 — your U.S. taxpayer identification (SSN)
  5. FATCA declaration — confirming you're a U.S. person; the bank prepares this

For Non-Residents (No Italian Residenza Yet)

If you don't yet have Italian residency (Residenza), some banks open accounts for non-residents:

  • Additional proof of Italian address (hotel confirmation, Airbnb booking, rental agreement)
  • Evidence of why you need the account (visa application, property purchase, etc.)
  • The account may be restricted until residency is established

Non-resident accounts typically have higher fees and limitations. Establish Italian residency as quickly as possible to upgrade to a standard account.

For Residents (With Italian Residenza)

  • Certificato di Residenza or Permesso di Soggiorno
  • Standard documents above

Step-by-Step: Opening the Account

Step 1: Choose Your Bank

For Americans, Intesa Sanpaolo or BNL are the most reliable choices nationwide. Visit a branch — don't try to open online for your first Italian account.

Step 2: Book an Appointment

In Italy, bank branches handle account openings by appointment. At the branch door, ask: "Vorrei aprire un conto corrente. Posso fissare un appuntamento?" (I'd like to open a current account. Can I make an appointment?)

Step 3: Attend the Appointment

Bring all documents above. The bank representative will:

  • Review your documents
  • Ask about the purpose of the account
  • Have you complete FATCA documentation (W-9 equivalent)
  • Process the application

Important: When asked about your tax residency, be straightforward that you're a U.S. citizen. Don't attempt to obscure this — it creates legal problems and the bank may close your account later.

Step 4: Account Activation

Processing: 1–5 business days. You'll receive:

  • IBAN (your Italian bank account number)
  • Debit card (bancomat) — typically arrives by mail within 5–10 days
  • Online banking credentials
  • Credit card if applied for

Italian Banking Products: What You Need

Conto Corrente (Current/Checking Account)

The standard Italian account for daily transactions. Features:

  • IBAN for wire transfers (bonifici)
  • Debit card (bancomat/Maestro)
  • Online banking
  • Ability to set up recurring payments (domiciliazione bollette)

Typical annual fees: €50–100/year for maintenance (can often be waived with minimum balance or regular income deposits)

Carte di Credito (Credit Cards)

Italian banks issue Visa and Mastercard credit cards to account holders. Easier to get once you've had the account 3–6 months and have income flowing through it.

Note: Credit cards in Italy often have high annual fees (€30–100/year) and lower credit limits than Americans are used to. For purchases, a debit card (bancomat) is more commonly used in Italy than in the U.S.

Conto Deposito (Savings Account)

Italian high-yield savings accounts — linked to your conto corrente but paying higher interest. Currently (2025) offering 3–5% annually on fixed-term deposits following ECB rate increases.

IBAN: How Italian Transfers Work

Italian transfers use IBAN (International Bank Account Number) — typically 27 characters for Italian accounts: IT + 2 check digits + 1 letter (CIN) + 5 ABI code + 5 CAB code + 12 account number.

Example: IT60 X054 2811 1010 0000 0123 456

Receiving money from the U.S.:

When receiving a U.S. wire transfer to Italy, you typically need:

  • Your IBAN
  • BIC/SWIFT code of your Italian bank
  • Bank name and address

International wire fees from the U.S.: $15–45 from most U.S. banks. Use Wise or Revolut to reduce transfer costs (0.3–1% vs. bank fees of $25–50).

Making payments in Italy:

  • Bonifico: Wire transfer; standard for rent payments, utilities
  • Addebito diretto: Direct debit; set up for recurring bills (electricity, gas, internet)
  • PagoPa: Italy's government payment platform; used for taxes, municipality fees, SSN contributions

The FATCA Reporting: What Happens to Your Account Information

Once you have an Italian bank account as a U.S. person, the bank will report to the Italian tax authority (Agenzia delle Entrate) annually, which shares data with the IRS under the Italy-U.S. FATCA Intergovernmental Agreement (IGA) signed November 10, 2015.

What gets reported to the IRS:

  • Account balance at year-end
  • Interest, dividends, and other income
  • Gross proceeds from securities sales (if applicable)

What you must do:

  • FBAR (FinCEN 114): If your Italian account(s) exceed $10,000 combined at any point during the year, file an FBAR by April 15 (October 15 with extension). This is separate from your tax return.
  • Form 8938 (FATCA): If foreign financial assets exceed $200,000 on the last day of the tax year (or $300,000 at any point), file Form 8938 with your 1040.
  • Report interest income on Schedule B of your U.S. 1040.

Reference: Bank Secrecy Act (31 USC 5314); FATCA (IRC Section 6038D); Italy-US FATCA IGA 2015

Managing Two Banking Systems

Most American expats maintain:

  1. U.S. account: For Social Security deposits, U.S. financial transactions, when visiting the U.S.
  2. Italian account: For Italian rent, utilities, taxes, and daily life in Italy

Practical setup:

  • Keep U.S. checking account active (Charles Schwab's international checking account refunds all foreign ATM fees — excellent for expats)
  • Receive Social Security and pension directly to U.S. account
  • Transfer monthly living expenses to Italian account via Wise (cheapest method for regular transfers)
  • Pay Italian taxes and bills from Italian account

Charles Schwab International Checking: No foreign transaction fees, refunds all ATM fees worldwide. Essential for Americans living abroad. Open one before you move.

Troubleshooting: Common Problems

"We can't open an account for U.S. citizens"

Try a different branch or a different bank. FATCA compliance varies by branch familiarity — sometimes a local branch says no while another branch of the same bank says yes. Ask specifically for the "referente FATCA" or the branch manager.

Account frozen after opening

Some banks request additional documentation after opening, particularly for U.S. persons. Respond promptly to any requests. Ignoring them can lead to account freezing.

Can't transfer money from U.S. to Italy

If your U.S. bank is blocking international transfers: use Wise (recommended), Revolut, or OFX as intermediary transfer services. These have much lower fees and fewer restrictions than standard bank wires.
