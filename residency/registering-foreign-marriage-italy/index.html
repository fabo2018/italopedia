Registering a Foreign Marriage in Italy: The Complete Guide for Americans

If you married in the United States and then moved to Italy, your US marriage does not automatically appear in Italian civil records. Registration at the Italian comune links your marriage to the Italian administrative system — affecting everything from joint healthcare enrollment to property ownership to inheritance rights. Here is exactly how to do it.

Italy has a civil registration system (stato civile) that records births, marriages, deaths, and civil unions for people with Italian connections. When you are an Italian citizen — or when you become an Italian resident — your life events enter this system. A marriage that happened in the United States exists perfectly well under US law; it does not automatically exist in Italian records.

Registration is not required to make your marriage legally valid in Italy — Italy recognizes foreign marriages under international private law. But without Italian registration, your marriage does not appear in the administrative systems that Italian bureaucracy relies on for healthcare, residency, property ownership, inheritance, and fiscal matters.

Why Registration Matters

Practical consequences of not registering your US marriage in Italy:

  • Healthcare: Without registered marriage, your non-Italian spouse may have difficulty enrolling as a family dependent on your SSN enrollment
  • Property: Joint property purchases, succession rights, and community property protections under Italian law depend on Italy recognizing your marital status
  • Inheritance: Italian forced heirship (legittima) and spousal inheritance rights are based on marital status as recorded in Italian civil records. An unregistered marriage may create practical complications even if legally valid
  • Visa for your spouse: If your spouse is a non-EU citizen applying for residency in Italy as your family member, Italian immigration authorities require a marriage certificate recognized in the Italian system
  • Tax filing: Italian joint tax declarations (dichiarazione congiunta) depend on marital status being documented in Italian records
  • General bureaucracy: Any Italian form asking for your marital status links to the stato civile records — without registration, you may face complications in processes that assume a registered marriage

Who Must Register

Italian law requires registration of foreign marriages by:

  • Italian citizens who married abroad (they must register the marriage in their comune of residence or their AIRE consulate)
  • Italian residents (of any nationality) who married abroad — if the marriage occurred before Italian residency was established, registration is typically done upon establishing residency

Non-Italian citizens who are not Italian residents and have no Italian administrative connection are not required to register their marriage in Italy. The obligation arises when you establish an Italian administrative connection (through residency, citizenship, or property ownership).

Documents Required

For registering a US marriage in Italy, you need:

□
Original US marriage certificate — long-form (certified copy), issued by the county or state where the marriage was registered. Most Italian civil offices require the document to be recent (issued within the last 6 months); confirm with your comune.
□
Apostille on the marriage certificate — obtained from the Secretary of State of the state where the marriage was registered. For marriages in states where the county clerk issued the certificate, you need the state-level apostille.
□
Certified Italian translation — sworn translation of the marriage certificate by a court-recognized translator. The translation itself needs to be authenticated or the translator must appear before an Italian tribunal to swear the translation's accuracy.
□
Valid passports or identity documents for both spouses
□
Codice fiscale for the Italian citizen or Italian resident applying
□
Marche da bollo (€16 revenue stamps) — typically 1–2 required for the application

Apostille and Translation: The Critical Steps

The apostille authenticates the signature and seal on your US marriage certificate for use in Italy. Without it, the Italian comune will not accept the document. Steps:

  1. Obtain a certified copy of your marriage certificate from the county or state vital records office where the marriage was registered
  2. Send it to the Secretary of State of that state for apostille certification (most states have a mail-in apostille service; fees: $10–$25 per document; time: 2–8 weeks)
  3. Have the apostilled certificate translated into Italian by a sworn translator who will then authenticate the translation before an Italian court (tribunale) or at the Italian consulate

The entire document preparation process — certificate, apostille, translation — typically takes 4–8 weeks if done methodically. Expedited apostille services through private notary services can reduce the apostille wait to days; budget accordingly.

Step-by-Step Process

There are two routes: registering before you arrive (through the Italian consulate in the US) or after you arrive in Italy (through your comune). Both are valid; the practical choice depends on your timeline.

Route A: Registering Via the Italian Consulate in the US

The consulate route is useful if you want your marriage registered before or shortly after you arrive in Italy. Italian citizens living in the US who are registered on AIRE can and should register foreign marriages through their AIRE consulate.

  1. Book an appointment at your jurisdiction's Italian consulate
  2. Bring all required documents (see checklist above)
  3. The consulate transmits the registration to your Italian comune of reference (for AIRE registrants, this is the comune where you were last registered in Italy)
  4. The comune receives the registration and enters it into the stato civile records
  5. Processing time: typically 3–6 months after consulate submission

Confirmation: you can verify the registration was completed by requesting a certificato di matrimonio from your Italian comune (in person, by mail, or increasingly online through Italian administrative portals).

Route B: Registering After Arriving in Italy

Once you are a resident in Italy, you register the marriage directly at the Ufficio di Stato Civile (Civil Registry Office) of your comune. This is the office within the municipal government that handles civil records — births, marriages, deaths, civil unions. It is different from the anagrafe (population registry), though both are usually in the municipio (town hall) building.

  1. Go to the Ufficio di Stato Civile of your comune and ask to transcribe (trascrivere) a foreign marriage certificate
  2. Submit all documents from the checklist above
  3. The officer reviews the documents and begins the transcription process
  4. There may be a publication period (pubblicazioni) — a public notice posted at the comune for a set period to allow objections before the marriage is formally recorded
  5. Once the transcription is complete, you receive a certificato di matrimonio that serves as official Italian documentation of your marriage

Timeline and Fees

  • Document preparation (apostille + translation): 4–8 weeks
  • Consulate processing: 3–6 months after submission
  • Comune processing after Italian arrival: 2–4 months for the transcription
  • Fees: Marche da bollo (~€32), apostille ($15–$25), translation (€80–€150), plus consulate appointment fee if applicable. Total: typically €150–€300 excluding professional help

Common Complications

Name differences: If either spouse's name appears differently on the US certificate than on Italian documents (due to transliteration, anglicization, or nickname use), the comune may request clarifying documentation. Prepare a brief written explanation and any supporting documents that establish the identity connection between the different name versions.

Divorced parties: If either spouse was previously married and divorced, the US divorce decree must also be apostilled and translated and submitted alongside the marriage certificate. Italy must satisfy itself that both parties were legally free to marry.

Religious marriages: Civil marriage in the US is straightforward. Religious-only ceremonies without civil registration in the US are not valid under Italian civil law — you would need to have the civil component documented before Italian registration is possible.

Same-sex marriages: Italian law does not recognize same-sex marriage but does recognize civil unions (unione civile). A US same-sex marriage cannot be transcribed as a marriage in Italy — it is typically transcribed as a civil union instead. See our guide: Italian Civil Union (Unione Civile): Rights and Process.

FAQ

Is my US marriage legally valid in Italy without registration?
Yes. Italy recognizes foreign marriages as legally valid under international private law (DIPr — Law 218/1995) without registration. Registration in the stato civile is an administrative procedure that makes the marriage appear in Italy's records — it does not constitute a new validation of the marriage. However, without registration, the practical administrative consequences described above apply.
How do I request an Italian marriage certificate once registered?
Online through your comune's portal or the national ANPR (Anagrafe Nazionale della Popolazione Residente) portal, by mail to the Ufficio di Stato Civile, or in person at the municipio. The certificate is typically issued same-day or within a few days.
My Italian spouse and I married in the US. Who is responsible for registering in Italy?
The Italian citizen spouse has the legal obligation to ensure the marriage is registered in Italy. In practice, either spouse can initiate the process — it is a joint administrative matter, but the requirement falls primarily on the party with Italian citizenship or Italian residency.
