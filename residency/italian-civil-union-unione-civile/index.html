Italian Civil Union (Unione Civile): Rights, Process, and What It Means for Americans

Italy introduced civil unions for same-sex couples in 2016 — a decade after many comparable European countries. The unione civile grants substantial rights but is not identical to marriage under Italian law. For American same-sex couples moving to Italy, here is exactly what your US marriage becomes in Italy and what practical rights you have.

Italy was among the later Western European countries to recognize same-sex partnerships, doing so with the Cirinnà Law (Law 76/2016) on May 20, 2016 — the same law that established the unione civile as Italy's legal framework for same-sex couples. The law was significant and hard-won; it was also a deliberate compromise, conferring most but not all rights of marriage.

For American same-sex couples planning to move to Italy, the relevant questions are: what does your US marriage become in Italy, what rights does the civil union framework provide, and how do you navigate the bureaucratic process of establishing recognized status?

What the Unione Civile Is

The unione civile is a legally recognized partnership between two adults of the same sex, established before an Italian civil officiant (ufficiale di stato civile) at the comune. It grants the parties mutual rights and obligations that closely, though not entirely, mirror those of marriage under Italian civil law.

The unione civile is distinct from the contratto di convivenza (cohabitation agreement), which is a separate instrument available to both same-sex and opposite-sex unmarried couples offering lighter legal protections without the full civil union framework.

Rights Granted by the Unione Civile

The Cirinnà Law grants civil union partners the following rights:

  • Mutual maintenance obligation: Partners have a legal duty to support each other financially, equivalent to the obligation between spouses
  • Inheritance rights: Civil union partners have the same inheritance rights as spouses — including forced heirship (legittima) claims that protect against being disinherited. This is a significant legal protection.
  • Healthcare: Partners are recognized as next-of-kin for medical decisions; hospital visitation rights; ability to enroll as SSN family dependents
  • Property rights: Joint ownership rules and community property provisions under Italian law apply (by default, the communion of property regime — comunione dei beni — unless you elect separation)
  • Tax filing: Partners can file joint Italian tax declarations (dichiarazione congiunta) in the same way as married couples
  • Name: Partners may choose to assume a common surname (with limitations)
  • Residency rights: A non-EU partner in a civil union with an EU/Italian citizen has facilitated residency rights in Italy similar to a foreign spouse
  • Pension survivor benefits: INPS survivor pension (pensione di reversibilità) extends to civil union partners, as with surviving spouses

Key Differences from Marriage Under Italian Law

The Cirinnà Law explicitly excludes civil union partners from certain provisions of marriage law:

  • Adoption: Civil union partners cannot jointly adopt as a couple under Italian law. This remains a significant legal gap; some judicial rulings have allowed stepchild adoption (adozione del figlio del partner) on a case-by-case basis, but no consistent legal framework exists at the national level.
  • Presumption of parenthood: In a marriage, children born during the union are presumed children of both spouses. This legal presumption does not apply in civil unions.
  • Word "marriage": The Italian legal framework deliberately avoids the term "marriage" (matrimonio) for same-sex unions. Partners are "parti dell'unione civile" (civil union parties) rather than spouses (coniugi) in Italian legal language, though the practical rights are largely equivalent.
  • Divorce procedure: Dissolution of a civil union follows a slightly simplified process compared to marriage dissolution

What Happens to Your US Same-Sex Marriage

This is the question most American same-sex couples need answered first. Italy does not recognize foreign same-sex marriages as marriages in its legal system. When your US same-sex marriage certificate is submitted to Italian civil authorities, it is typically transcribed as a civil union (unione civile) rather than a marriage (matrimonio).

The legal basis: Italy's international private law framework (L. 218/1995) requires that foreign family law acts be recognized in Italy according to the lex loci (law of the place where the act occurred). A US same-sex marriage is valid under US law where it was performed — but Italy's own law does not permit same-sex marriage, so it transcribes the foreign act in the closest equivalent available under Italian law, which is the civil union.

Practical consequence: your US marriage becomes an Italian civil union upon transcription. This gives you all the rights described above, including inheritance, healthcare, and residency rights — but not the rights that differ between marriage and civil union, primarily adoption.

Some Italian courts and legal scholars have argued that foreign same-sex marriages should be fully recognized as marriages — the Court of Cassation has issued nuanced rulings on this. The situation is evolving legally, but as of 2025, the administrative practice is transcription as civil union.

How to Form a Civil Union in Italy

If you do not already have a US marriage to transcribe and wish to form a new civil union in Italy:

  1. Both partners must appear in person at the Ufficio di Stato Civile of your comune
  2. Submit your identity documents (passports, codice fiscali) and, if previously married or in a civil union, documentation of the dissolution
  3. A publication (pubblicazioni) period of approximately 8 days follows, during which the civil union intention is posted publicly to allow objections
  4. The civil union ceremony takes place before the ufficiale di stato civile — it is a brief civil ceremony, not religious, typically in the municipio
  5. Two witnesses are required
  6. The union is registered in the stato civile records immediately

Foreign citizens forming a civil union in Italy must provide a nulla osta (certificate of no impediment to civil union) from their country's authorities, apostilled and translated into Italian. For US citizens, this is obtained through the US Embassy in Rome, which issues a sworn affidavit of eligibility in lieu of a formal nulla osta.

Residency Rights for the Non-Italian Partner

If one partner is an Italian or EU citizen and the other is a non-EU national (including Americans), the civil union provides the non-EU partner with facilitated residency rights. Rather than applying for a standard permesso di soggiorno (which requires meeting income, accommodation, and other requirements), the non-EU partner applies for a carta di soggiorno di familiare di cittadino UE.

This requires:

  • Proof of the civil union (certificato di unione civile)
  • Proof of the Italian/EU partner's EU citizenship and Italian residence
  • Identity documents

    • The application is made to the questura. Processing time: 3–6 months. This is a more favorable path than the standard permesso — lower income requirements and fewer documentation barriers.

    Tax and Inheritance Implications

    Taxes: Civil union partners are treated identically to married couples for Italian income tax purposes — including joint filing, dependent allowances, and the marital deduction system. For Americans, this means the same tax treaty and foreign tax credit analysis applies as for married couples.

    Inheritance: Civil union partners have the same forced heirship and intestate succession rights as spouses. If your partner dies without a will, you inherit as a surviving spouse under Italian law. If your partner makes a will, you have the same legittima rights as a surviving spouse — meaning you cannot be fully disinherited.

    Gift and inheritance tax: Transfers between civil union partners qualify for the same favorable treatment as between spouses — the €1 million per-partner exemption and the 4% rate on the excess apply.

    Dissolving a Civil Union

    A civil union can be dissolved through:

    • Mutual agreement: Both parties declare before the civil officiant that they wish to dissolve the union. Unlike divorce, there is no mandatory separation period — the dissolution can be immediate with mutual consent.
    • Unilateral declaration: Either partner can request dissolution without the other's consent. A 3-month waiting period follows the declaration before the dissolution is finalized.
    • Court proceedings: For dissolution involving asset division, maintenance disputes, or parental matters, court involvement may be necessary

    The dissolution process is notably simpler than Italian divorce (divorzio), which for opposite-sex married couples requires a prior legal separation period of 12 months (6 months for consensual separation).

    FAQ

    Italy may legalize same-sex marriage in the future — will my civil union convert automatically?
    If Italy were to legislate same-sex marriage (which has been a recurring but not yet successful legislative proposal), conversion provisions would likely be included. Based on how other European countries have handled this transition, civil unions would typically either automatically convert or partners would have the option to convert with a simple administrative step. No specific legislation has passed as of May 2025.
    My US same-sex marriage was registered as a civil union by the Italian authorities. Do I need to do anything else?
    Once the transcription is complete and you have a certificato di unione civile, you have the recognized status for Italian administrative purposes. Present this certificate wherever Italian institutions ask for proof of partnership status — healthcare, banks, property transactions, and residency applications.
    Can a same-sex couple form a civil union in Italy if they are not Italian citizens?
    Yes. Italian law allows same-sex civil unions between people of any nationality, including two Americans, in Italy. The administrative requirements (nulla osta or sworn affidavit of eligibility from each person's country) apply, but nationality is not a barrier to forming a civil union in Italy.
