Getting a SIM Card in Italy: The Complete Guide for Americans (2025)

TIM, Vodafone, WindTre, or Iliad — 30–50GB for €10/month. Here's which carrier to pick, what to bring, and how to keep your U.S. number active.

Getting an Italian SIM card is one of the first practical tasks after arriving in Italy — and one of the simplest. Within 30 minutes of landing at any major Italian airport, you can have an Italian number with 30–50GB of data for €10–15/month. Italian mobile plans are dramatically cheaper than U.S. equivalents and the process is straightforward once you know what to bring.

What You Need to Buy an Italian SIM

Italian law requires identity verification for all SIM card purchases. You cannot buy an Italian SIM anonymously.

Required documents:

  • Valid passport (U.S. passport is accepted)
  • Italian Codice Fiscale — required by most carriers for contract plans; sometimes optional for prepaid
  • Italian address — for registration purposes; even a temporary address works initially

Note on Codice Fiscale: Most Italian carriers (TIM, Vodafone, WindTre, Iliad) require your Codice Fiscale for the SIM registration. If you don't have one yet, get it from the Agenzia delle Entrate first — it's free and takes 30 minutes. Some prepaid options (particularly Iliad) will accept your passport alone initially.

Italian Mobile Carriers: Comparison

Italy has four major national carriers and several virtual operators (MVNOs). Competition is fierce — Italian mobile prices are among Europe's lowest.

TIM (Telecom Italia)

Italy's original national carrier. Widest coverage including rural areas and mountains.

Best plans (2025):

  • TIM Wonder — 100GB data, unlimited calls/SMS: ~€9.99/month
  • TIM Power — 200GB data, unlimited: ~€14.99/month

Pros: Best rural/mountain coverage; longest-established network Cons: Slightly more expensive than Iliad/WindTre; customer service can be slow Best for: People living outside major cities or traveling frequently to rural areas

Where to buy: TIM stores (negozi TIM) in every city; airports; some tabacchi

Vodafone Italy

Strong urban coverage; good international roaming.

Best plans (2025):

  • Vodafone Silver — 100GB, unlimited calls: ~€9.99/month
  • Vodafone Special — 150GB, unlimited: ~€12.99/month

Pros: Good roaming in EU; reliable urban network; international brand support Cons: More expensive than discount carriers for equivalent data Best for: Frequent EU travelers; people who want international brand reliability

WindTre

Merged network of former Wind and 3 Italia. Strong urban coverage, competitive pricing.

Best plans (2025):

  • WindTre GO — 100GB, unlimited: ~€7.99/month
  • WindTre Super — 200GB, unlimited: ~€9.99/month

Pros: Very competitive pricing; good data allowances; wide distribution Cons: Rural coverage slightly below TIM; customer service quality variable Best for: Urban residents wanting maximum data at low cost

Iliad Italy

French carrier that disrupted Italian prices when it entered in 2018. No physical stores — sells online and via kiosks.

Best plans (2025):

  • Iliad Giga 150 — 150GB, unlimited calls/SMS in Italy + EU: ~€9.99/month
  • Iliad Giga 250 — 250GB data: ~€13.99/month

Pros: Truly unlimited calls; transparent pricing (no hidden fees or surprise charges); prices locked — never increase Cons: Customer service is limited (mostly online); no physical store to walk into; coverage slightly below TIM in some areas Best for: Budget-conscious users comfortable with online management; people who hate surprise charges

How to buy Iliad: Online at iliad.it or at Iliad kiosks (Simbox) in major supermarkets (Carrefour, Mediaworld)

MVNO (Virtual Operators) — Budget Options

Several virtual operators use the major networks' infrastructure at lower prices:

  • ho.mobile (uses Vodafone network): ~€5.99/month for 100GB
  • Kena Mobile (uses TIM network): ~€5.99/month for 100GB
  • Very Mobile (uses WindTre): ~€4.99/month for 100GB
  • Spusu (uses WindTre): very competitive; no activation fee

Best for: Maximum savings if you live in a city with strong all-carrier coverage

eSIM Options for Americans

Many Americans arrive with unlocked iPhones or Android phones that support eSIM — allowing you to add an Italian number without a physical SIM card.

Italian Carriers with eSIM

TIM: eSIM available — activate online or in store Vodafone: eSIM available Iliad: eSIM available — fully online activation

International eSIM Before Arrival

If you want data connectivity the moment you land (before getting to a carrier store):

  • Airalo — airalo.com: eSIM marketplace; Italy data plans from $5/week; buy before departure
  • Holafly — unlimited data eSIM; good for first 1–2 weeks
  • Google Fi — if you're already a Google Fi subscriber, it works in Italy at international rates

Strategy: Use an international eSIM for your first 1–2 days, then get a proper Italian SIM for long-term use.

Keeping Your U.S. Number

Most Americans living in Italy want to keep their U.S. phone number active for:

  • Banking and 2FA SMS from U.S. institutions
  • Friends and family in the U.S.
  • Medicare/Social Security correspondence

Option 1: U.S. Carrier International Plan

AT&T, T-Mobile, and Verizon offer international plans that work in Italy. Expensive ($10–15/day or $70–100/month add-on) but simple. Better for short stays than long-term living.

Option 2: Port to Google Voice

The cleanest long-term solution:

  1. Port your U.S. number to Google Voice ($20 one-time porting fee)
  2. Cancel your U.S. carrier contract (saves $50–100/month)
  3. Your U.S. number lives on Google Voice — receives calls and SMS via WiFi/data
  4. Works from anywhere with internet — including Italy

Cost: $0/month after porting. Calls to U.S. numbers from Italy via Google Voice: $0.01–0.02/minute.

Option 3: Keep a Cheap U.S. Prepaid SIM

Some Americans keep a cheap U.S. prepaid number (Mint Mobile, Tello, or similar at $10–15/month) active for 2FA and U.S. contacts, while using an Italian SIM for daily use.

Dual SIM Solution

Most modern iPhones (14 and later) and Android phones support dual SIM (either two physical SIMs or one physical + one eSIM). This allows you to carry both a U.S. number and an Italian number simultaneously — the ideal setup for expats.

At the Airport: Buying a SIM on Arrival

All major Italian airports have carrier stores or kiosks where you can buy a SIM immediately upon arrival:

Fiumicino (Rome FCO): TIM, Vodafone, WindTre stores in arrivals Malpensa (Milan MXP): TIM, Vodafone, WindTre in arrivals Linate (Milan LIN): Carrier kiosks available Marco Polo (Venice VCE): Carrier stores in arrivals Galilei (Pisa PSA): Carrier kiosks

Tip: Airport prices are typically the same as city stores — Italian carriers have regulated pricing. Don't pay premium at airport stores; the same plan costs the same everywhere.

Switching Carriers (Portabilità)

If you start with one carrier and want to switch, Italian law guarantees number portability (portabilità) — you keep your Italian number when switching carriers. The new carrier handles the switch; the old carrier must complete it within 3 working days.

This means you can start with any carrier and switch to a better deal later without losing your number.

Italian Mobile Data: What to Expect

4G coverage: Excellent in cities and towns; good on major highways; variable in rural and mountain areas 5G: Expanding rapidly in major cities (Milan, Rome, Naples, Turin, Bologna); limited in smaller cities Data speeds: 4G in Italian cities typically 30–80 Mbps download — perfectly adequate for video calls, streaming, remote work

Hotspot/tethering: Included on most Italian plans — you can use your phone as a WiFi hotspot for your laptop at no extra charge (within your data allowance).

Practical Tips

Unlock your U.S. phone before leaving. Most U.S. carriers lock phones to their network. An unlocked phone accepts any SIM. Request unlocking from your U.S. carrier before departure — it's free and takes 1–3 days.

Buy at a carrier store, not a tabacchi. While many tobacconists (tabacchi) sell SIMs, the staff at carrier stores can better assist with setup, porting, and troubleshooting. Worth going to an official store for your first Italian SIM.

Register your SIM properly. Italian law requires real identity verification. A properly registered SIM cannot be deactivated for registration issues — important for long-term use.

Iliad's no-price-increase guarantee is genuinely valuable for long-term residents. Your monthly plan price is locked — Iliad contractually cannot raise it. TIM and Vodafone have historically raised prices with 30-day notice.
