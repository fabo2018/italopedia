How to Register at the Anagrafe in Italy: Complete Guide for Americans

Step-by-step guide for Americans on how to register at the Italian Anagrafe (municipal registry office) — what to bring, what to expect, and how to handle the home visit. Updated 2025.

If you've just arrived in Italy on a long-stay visa, registering at the Anagrafe is one of the most important bureaucratic steps you'll take — and one of the most misunderstood.

This is not optional. It's a legal requirement. And getting it done properly is what unlocks your Italian healthcare card, your Italian ID card, your bank account, and — years down the line — your path to Italian citizenship.

What Is the Anagrafe?

The Ufficio Anagrafe (registry office) is the municipal office that records the official population of every Italian comune (municipality). Every person who lives in Italy — Italian citizens and foreign residents alike — must be registered there.

When you register, you're completing a process called iscrizione anagrafica (residency registration). Your address becomes your official residenza — the legal address the Italian state uses for everything from tax notices to healthcare enrollment to your citizenship clock.

💡 Tip

Fabrizio's note from Piedmont: Think of the Anagrafe as the Italian equivalent of changing your address at the DMV, applying for a library card, and registering to vote — all at once, and all mandatory.

Why It Matters So Much

Registering at the Anagrafe is required to:

  • Get your Italian ID card (carta d'identità)
  • Enroll in the National Health Service (SSN) and get your Tessera Sanitaria
  • Open an Italian bank account at most local banks
  • Register a vehicle or get an Italian driving license
  • Start your citizenship clock — the 10-year residency countdown for naturalization begins on your date of registration

That last point is critical. Many Americans move to Italy and delay the Anagrafe registration — then discover years later that their citizenship timeline is much further away than expected. Register as soon as possible.

When to Register

  • Legal requirement: within 20 days of establishing your residence in Italy
  • Practical advice: do it within the first week if possible

What You Need to Bring: Full Document Checklist

Mandatory Documents

  • Valid US passport (original + photocopy)
  • Permesso di Soggiorno (residence permit) — OR the postal receipt (ricevuta) proving you've applied
  • Codice Fiscale (Italian tax identification number)
  • Proof of accommodation — one of the following:

- Signed lease agreement registered with the Agenzia delle Entrate - Property deed (if you own the home) - Written declaration from the property owner (dichiarazione di ospitalità)

  • Completed residency declaration form (dichiarazione di residenza)
⚠ Important

Important: Your lease must be registered with the Agenzia delle Entrate. An unregistered lease is not accepted as proof of accommodation.

Step-by-Step: The Registration Process

Step 1: Find Your Ufficio Anagrafe

Go to the Anagrafe of the comune where you actually live. Search "[your town name] ufficio anagrafe orari" to find opening hours. Most offices require an appointment.

Step 2: Submit Your Application

At the Anagrafe desk, hand over your documents and fill out the dichiarazione di residenza form.

Timeline:

  • If you submit in person: residency is provisionally recorded immediately
  • If you submit by email: the anagrafe processes it within 2 working days

Step 3: Wait for the Home Visit

This is the step that surprises most Americans — and the one that causes the most anxiety.

Within 45 days of your application, a municipal official (often a vigile urbano) will visit your home to verify you actually live there.

How to prepare:

  • Make sure your name is on the doorbell and mailbox
  • Be home as much as possible during the 45-day window, especially in the mornings
  • Tell your neighbors or doorman that an official may come
  • If you need to travel, email the Anagrafe to explain
💡 Tip

Fabrizio's tip: In smaller comuni (like many in Piedmont), the verification process is more personal and faster. In larger cities like Milan or Rome, the 45-day window is more strictly observed.

Step 4: Receive Your Official Documents

Once the home visit is completed:

  1. Your iscrizione anagrafica is finalized
  2. You can request your certificato di residenza
  3. You can apply for your carta d'identità at the same office
  4. You can then register with your local ASL to get your Tessera Sanitaria

Common Problems and How to Solve Them

"The Anagrafe won't accept my lease because it's not registered." Landlords sometimes resist registering leases. Legally they must register it. If they refuse, consider finding another apartment.

"I missed the home visit." Contact the Anagrafe immediately. Don't wait — if you don't respond, your application can be cancelled.

"My Permesso di Soggiorno hasn't arrived yet." Yes. The postal receipt (ricevuta) you got when you submitted your Permesso di Soggiorno application is sufficient. You don't need the physical permit card.

The Citizenship Clock Starts Here

The 10-year residency requirement for citizenship by naturalization is counted from your official residenza date — the date your iscrizione anagrafica was accepted. Not the date you landed. Not the date your visa was issued. The Anagrafe date.

Official Sources

  • Anagrafe Nazionale della Popolazione Residente (ANPR)
  • Ministero dell'Interno — Residenza e iscrizione anagrafica

FAQ

Do I need to register at the Anagrafe if I'm only staying for a few months? If you're staying less than 90 days, no. If you're staying more than 90 days on a long-stay visa, yes, registration is legally required within 20 days.

What if I move to a different city in Italy? You must de-register from your current comune and re-register in the new one within 20 days of moving. This does NOT reset your citizenship clock, which is cumulative across all Italian comuni.

Is there a fee to register? Registration itself is free. Requesting a certificato di residenza typically costs €0.50–€2.
