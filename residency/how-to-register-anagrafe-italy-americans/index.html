How to Register at the Anagrafe in Italy: Complete Guide for Americans

The anagrafe registration (iscrizione anagrafica) is the act that makes you an official Italian resident. Here is exactly what to bring, how the process works, and what happens after. Updated 2025.

Quick Answer

The anagrafe is Italy's municipal population register. Registering (iscrizione anagrafica) makes you an official Italian resident. You must do it within 20 days of establishing habitual residence. It unlocks your tessera sanitaria, confirms your Italian address for all official purposes, and is a required step toward a long-term permesso di soggiorno and eventual citizenship. The process is free, handled at your local Comune.

What Is the Anagrafe?

The anagrafe (from the Greek for "registration") is the official population register maintained by every Italian municipality. Every person who habitually lives in Italy — Italian citizen or foreign national — must be registered in the anagrafe of their municipality of residence.

Registration gives you an official Italian address recognized by all government entities, banks, courts, and employers. Without it, you exist in a legal grey area: present in Italy but not officially resident, which limits your access to public services and complicates your tax and residency status.

For Americans, anagrafe registration is typically the second bureaucratic step after obtaining a codice fiscale, and it precedes applying for the permesso di soggiorno (for visa holders) or the EU residence certificate (for those with Italian citizenship).

Who Needs to Register?

You must register at the anagrafe if you:

  • Have moved to Italy and intend to live there habitually (not just visiting)
  • Have obtained a visa that allows residency (elective residence, digital nomad, student, etc.)
  • Have obtained Italian citizenship and are moving to Italy

You do not register at the anagrafe if you are on a tourist visit of up to 90 days with no intent to establish residency, or staying in a hotel or short-term rental with no lease in your name.

The Legal Deadline: 20 Days

Italian law (DPR 223/1989, Art. 13) requires you to declare your residence at the anagrafe within 20 days of establishing habitual residence. For Americans arriving on a visa, this typically means within 20 days of signing a lease or establishing a fixed address. The 20-day rule is rarely enforced against foreigners in the initial period, but it is a legal requirement and you should comply promptly.

What You Need to Register

Required for All Applicants

  • Valid US passport (original + 2 photocopies)
  • Valid Italian visa or residence permit (or proof of pending application — the ricevuta from the post office is accepted)
  • Completed dichiarazione di residenza form (available at the Comune or on the municipality's website)
  • Proof of address in Italy (see options below)

Proof of Address — One of the Following

  • Signed lease agreement (contratto di locazione) registered with the Agenzia delle Entrate
  • Property deed (atto di proprietà) if you own the home
  • Declaration of hospitality (dichiarazione di ospitalità) if you are staying with an Italian resident or property owner who is hosting you
⚠ Important

Your lease must be registered with the Agenzia delle Entrate. An unregistered lease is not accepted. Landlords are legally required to register leases — if yours refuses, you have legal recourse.

If Registering With Family Members

  • Marriage certificate (if registering as a couple) — apostilled if issued in the USA
  • Birth certificates for children — apostilled if issued in the USA
💡 Tip

Apostilles from US states can take 2–8 weeks. If you need to register urgently, start the apostille process before you leave the USA. See the US State Department's process at travel.state.gov.

Step-by-Step: The Registration Process

Step 1: Go to Your Comune's Anagrafe Office

Every Italian municipality has an Ufficio Anagrafe (registry office). In larger cities (Rome, Milan, Florence), there may be multiple branch offices — find yours on the city's official website. In smaller towns, the anagrafe is typically a single office in the main town hall (municipio or palazzo comunale). Most offices are open Monday–Friday mornings. Many larger municipalities now offer online appointments.

Step 2: Submit Your Dichiarazione di Residenza

Submit your completed dichiarazione di residenza form along with all supporting documents. The officer will check your documents and accept the declaration. The submission itself is immediate — your application is recorded the same day. The municipality then has 45 days to verify that you actually live at the declared address.

Step 3: The Verification Period

Within the 45-day window, a municipal official (often a vigile urbano) will visit your home to verify you actually live there. This surprises many Americans but is entirely routine.

How to prepare:

  • Make sure your name is on the doorbell and mailbox
  • Be home as much as possible during the 45-day window, especially in the mornings
  • Tell your neighbors or doorman that an official may come
  • If you need to travel, email the Anagrafe to explain in advance
💡 Tip

In smaller comuni (like many in Piedmont), the verification is more personal and faster. In larger cities like Milan or Rome, the full 45-day window is more common.

Step 4: Receive Your Official Documents

Once the verification is completed, your iscrizione anagrafica is finalized. You can then request:

  • Certificato di residenza — proof of your Italian address (used for dozens of official purposes)
  • Certificato di stato di famiglia — lists everyone registered at your address
  • Carta d'identità — Italian ID card (available to registered residents, including foreigners)

What Happens After Registration

Tessera Sanitaria

Within 2–6 weeks of anagrafe registration, the Agenzia delle Entrate will mail your tessera sanitaria (health card/codice fiscale card) to your registered address. If it does not arrive within 6 weeks, request it directly at any Agenzia delle Entrate office.

Enrollment With a Medico di Base

Once registered, you can enroll with a local general practitioner (medico di base) through your local ASL (Azienda Sanitaria Locale). This gives you access to the national health service (SSN — Servizio Sanitario Nazionale).

Permesso di Soggiorno

For Americans on visas, anagrafe registration is typically required as part of the permesso di soggiorno application or renewal process. Your registered address is the address that appears on your permesso.

Registration in Large Cities vs. Small Towns

The anagrafe process is the same legally across Italy, but the experience varies significantly:

SettingWhat to Expect
Large cities (Rome, Milan, Naples, Florence)Appointments required, often weeks in advance; multiple branch offices — go to the right one for your address; more multilingual staff; processing can be slower
Smaller townsWalk-in service often available; staff may speak limited English — bring a translation app; faster processing; vigili urbani visit is more prompt
💡 Tip

In smaller towns, showing up in person and explaining your situation directly to the officer often produces faster results than following the formal online process.

AIRE: If You Are an Italian Citizen

If you are an Italian citizen who lived in the USA and was registered with AIRE (Anagrafe degli Italiani Residenti all'Estero) at the Italian consulate, when you move to Italy you must de-register from AIRE and register with the anagrafe of your new Italian municipality. The consulate handles the AIRE de-registration; your Comune handles the Italian registration.

The Citizenship Clock Starts Here

The 10-year residency requirement for citizenship by naturalization is counted from your official residenza date — the date your iscrizione anagrafica was accepted. Not the date you landed. Not the date your visa was issued. The Anagrafe date. Many Americans delay registration, then discover their citizenship timeline is further away than expected. Register as soon as possible.

Common Problems and Solutions

"My landlord won't let me register at the rental address."
This is illegal in Italy. Landlords cannot prohibit tenants from registering their residency. If your landlord refuses, you have legal recourse under Italian tenancy law. In practice, informal landlords sometimes resist this because it creates a paper trail for their rental income — their tax problem, not yours.

"I don't have a registered lease."
Options: ask the landlord to register the lease; use a dichiarazione di ospitalità if someone else is the official tenant; or consult a local patronato (free legal aid office) for guidance.

"I was rejected during the verification period."
The most common reason is that the vigili urbani came and no one answered the door. Contact the Comune immediately, explain the situation, and re-submit. It is recoverable.

"My Permesso di Soggiorno hasn't arrived yet."
The postal receipt (ricevuta) from your permesso application is legally sufficient for anagrafe registration. You do not need the physical permit card.

FAQ

Can I register at the anagrafe without a visa?
For stays under 90 days, no. For longer stays, you need a valid visa or residence permit. EU/EEA citizens register under a different EU-law framework.

Does anagrafe registration affect my US taxes?
Registering as an Italian resident can establish Italian tax residency, which interacts with your US tax obligations. The US-Italy tax treaty governs double taxation. Consult a US-qualified tax advisor familiar with expat taxation before registering if this is a concern.

How long does the whole process take?
Submission: same day. Official completion: up to 45 days (verification period). Certificate availability: immediately after approval.

What if I move to a different city in Italy?
You must de-register from your current comune and re-register in the new one within 20 days of moving. This does NOT reset your citizenship clock — residency is cumulative across all Italian comuni.

Is there a fee to register?
Registration itself is free. Requesting a certificato di residenza typically costs €0.50–€2.
