2024 Italian Citizenship Reform: What Actually Changed

The 2024 reform is the most significant change to Italian citizenship law in decades. It adds a generational limit, tightens the application requirements, and affects millions of italoamericans with pending or planned claims. Here is what actually changed, who is affected, and what to do if your claim spans multiple generations.

For over a century, Italian citizenship law operated without a generational limit on descent claims. A great-great-grandchild of an Italian immigrant could, in theory, claim Italian citizenship — as long as the chain of Italian citizenship transmission remained unbroken by naturalization. This made Italy one of the most accessible citizenship-by-descent countries in the world and created a cottage industry of document preparers, immigration lawyers, and genealogy researchers serving the tens of millions of Americans with Italian ancestry.

The 2024 reform ended that era. Law 206/2023, with implementing regulations that came into force in March 2024, introduced a generational cap that limits who can claim through the standard consulate pathway. The change is substantial. The reaction from the italoamerican community was immediate and strong — but the law is in effect, and understanding it accurately is now essential for anyone with a pending or planned claim.

Background: Why the Reform Happened

Italy's citizenship offices, particularly at consulates in South America (Brazil, Argentina) and the United States, were overwhelmed by jure sanguinis applications. Processing times at the Italian Consulate in New York had reached 10–15 years for new applications. The New York consulate, serving the largest Italian-American population outside Italy, had a backlog of hundreds of thousands of cases.

The Italian government's position: the existing law's broad interpretation had created a situation where citizenship was being claimed by individuals with no meaningful connection to Italy — often multiple generations removed from any ancestor who was actually Italian. The reform was framed as restoring the concept of citizenship as a meaningful civic bond rather than a genealogical inheritance accessible indefinitely through the family tree.

Critics — primarily Italian-American advocacy organizations — argued that the reform was discriminatory, that the Italian Constitution guarantees citizenship transmission, and that the backlog problem should have been solved with administrative resources rather than legal restriction. Several legal challenges were filed.

What Actually Changed

The core change in Law 206/2023 and its implementing regulations:

  1. Generational limit introduced: Italian citizenship by descent through the consulate pathway is now available only to the second generation of descendants of an Italian-born citizen — meaning children and grandchildren.
  2. Third generation and beyond: Great-grandchildren (and further) of Italian-born ancestors must now follow a different pathway — typically requiring that a parent be a registered Italian citizen, either by having claimed citizenship themselves or through birth in Italy.
  3. Registration requirement for intermediate generations: For claims involving more than two generations of US-born descendants, the intermediate generations (parent, grandparent) must now have formally registered as Italian citizens before the further-removed descendant can claim.
  4. Application format changes: New applications must include additional documentation and attestations regarding the generational chain.

The Generational Limit in Detail

Let us make this concrete with examples:

Claim still valid under the reform

Your grandmother was born in Italy (Italian citizen). Your father is a US-born son of your grandmother (second generation from the Italian-born ancestor). You are a grandchild of the Italian-born ancestor. Under the reform, you can still claim through the consulate pathway as a second-generation descendant.

Claim affected by the reform

Your great-grandmother was born in Italy. Your grandfather was her US-born son. Your mother was the US-born daughter of your grandfather. You are the US-born great-grandchild of the Italian-born ancestor. Under the reform, this three-generation descent claim is no longer available through the standard consulate pathway for new applications. Your mother would need to have separately claimed Italian citizenship first — and then you could claim through her.

Grandfathering: Are Pending Applications Protected?

This is the question that most affected the hundreds of thousands of Americans with applications already in the Italian consulate pipeline.

The official position: applications formally received and docketed by an Italian consulate or Italian court before the implementing regulations' effective date are generally processed under the rules in effect at the time of submission. This means: if your application was formally acknowledged by the consulate before March 2024, you are generally grandfathered.

The complications:

  • Many Americans had appointments scheduled but had not yet submitted — these are not typically grandfathered
  • Applications in early stages of review without formal docketing vary in treatment by consulate
  • The Italian court (judicial) pathway has different timing considerations
  • Several consulates are still working through the practical implementation of the grandfathering rules

If you have a pending application, contact your consulate directly to confirm your specific application's status under the reform. Do not assume grandfathering without confirmation.

Who Is Most Affected

  • Heavily affected: Americans whose Italian ancestor was their great-grandparent or further removed, and who had not yet submitted a formal application
  • Moderately affected: Americans with applications pending but not formally docketed before the cutoff — uncertain status requiring individual confirmation
  • Less affected: Americans whose Italian ancestor was a grandparent or parent, regardless of the reform — the standard two-generation descent route remains available
  • Not affected: Applications formally submitted before the cutoff, regardless of generation

Alternative Pathways After the Reform

For Americans whose standard jure sanguinis claim is no longer available through the consulate under the reform, alternatives include:

  • Chain registration: If your parent or grandparent (an intermediate generation) can still claim Italian citizenship under the reform, they register as Italian citizens first. Once they are registered Italian citizens, their children can then claim citizenship through them. This is the "chain" approach — building citizenship registration through the generations before your claim.
  • Naturalization: After 4 years of continuous legal residence in Italy (for EU citizens) or 10 years for non-EU citizens. For Americans, 10 years of Italian residency leads to eligibility for naturalization — a much longer but certain pathway.
  • Marriage: After 2 years of residency in Italy while married to an Italian citizen (or 3 years without residency).
  • Legal challenges: Several Italian-American organizations have filed constitutional challenges to the reform. Some early lower court decisions have questioned aspects of the law's application. The legal landscape is still evolving as of 2025 — follow developments if your claim was affected.

What Did NOT Change

To be clear about what the reform did not touch:

  • The 1948 case rules (citizenship through a female line) — these are governed by court cases, not the standard consulate route, and are handled separately
  • The naturalization process — no change to the 10-year residency pathway
  • Citizenship through marriage — no change
  • The citizenship of current Italian citizens — the reform does not retroactively affect anyone who already holds Italian citizenship
  • The document requirements for valid claims — the same documents are required; the generational cap is a new additional requirement

Consulate Practice in 2025

As of 2025, implementation is still somewhat inconsistent across consulates. The New York and Los Angeles consulates have issued the clearest guidance. Some smaller consulates are still working through their procedures for applying the reform to pending and new cases.

Practical advice: if your application involves three or more generations of US-born descendants from an Italian ancestor, speak with an Italian immigration attorney who specializes in citizenship before proceeding or concluding that your claim is dead. The legal challenges are ongoing, some consulate-level discretion exists in ambiguous cases, and the specific facts of your chain matter.

FAQ

My application was submitted in 2022 and is still pending. Am I affected?
If your application was formally received and docketed by the consulate before the implementing regulations' effective date in March 2024, you should be grandfathered. Contact your specific consulate to confirm your application's status. Do not assume — ask for written confirmation of your position.
Is the reform constitutional under Italian law?
That is being tested. Multiple legal challenges have been filed arguing that the reform violates the Italian Constitution's citizenship provisions. Some early court decisions have expressed doubts about specific applications of the reform. The Italian Constitutional Court has not issued a definitive ruling as of mid-2025. The legal situation is evolving.
My great-grandmother was Italian. Is my claim gone?
Not necessarily. The chain registration approach allows you to first establish your parent's (or grandparent's, if living) Italian citizenship — if they qualify as second-generation descendants — and then claim through them. This is a longer process but may still be available depending on your specific family chain and the ages and circumstances of intermediate generations.
Is the reform affecting applicants in other countries the same way?
Yes — the reform applies globally, not just to applications from the United States. Italian-Brazilians, Italian-Argentinians, and other communities with significant historical Italian emigration are equally affected. The political pressure against the reform is strongest in South America, where the Italian-descended population is largest.
