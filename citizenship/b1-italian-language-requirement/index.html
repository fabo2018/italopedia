B1 Italian Language Requirement for Citizenship: Tests, Preparation, and Exemptions

Italy introduced a mandatory B1 Italian language certificate for naturalization citizenship applicants in 2020. Which tests are accepted, how difficult B1 actually is, how to prepare efficiently, and whether living in Italy can exempt you from the test requirement — all answered here.

Italy's B1 Italian language requirement for citizenship applicants was introduced by Ministerial Decree of April 21, 2020 (DM 21/4/2020). Before this change, naturalization applicants had no formal language requirement — the cultural integration was assumed or evaluated informally. The 2020 decree standardized the requirement: applicants for citizenship by naturalization (and, in some interpretations, by marriage) must demonstrate B1-level proficiency in Italian.

B1 is not advanced Italian. It is the lower end of "intermediate" — the level at which you can handle everyday situations, understand common topics, and make yourself understood in standard conversations. For Americans who have been living in Italy for years, this is a threshold they typically reach naturally. For those applying from the US through the jure sanguinis route, the requirement warrants specific preparation.

When the B1 Is Required

The B1 language certificate is required for:

  • Citizenship by naturalization (cittadinanza per naturalizzazione): After 10 years of legal Italian residency, applicants must demonstrate B1 proficiency
  • Citizenship by marriage to an Italian citizen: Applications filed after December 4, 2018 require language proof at B1 or above

The B1 requirement does NOT apply to:

  • Citizenship by descent (jure sanguinis): the language requirement was introduced for naturalization, not descent-based claims — jure sanguinis applicants are not required to pass a language test. (Note: the 2024 citizenship reform changed other aspects of jure sanguinis; it did not add a language requirement.)
  • Children under 18 born or raised in Italy
  • Italian citizens by birth who lived abroad — returning citizens do not need to demonstrate Italian language proficiency

What B1 Italian Actually Means

The Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR) defines B1 as "Threshold" level. At B1, a learner can:

  • Understand the main points of clear standard input on familiar matters regularly encountered at work, school, and leisure
  • Deal with most situations likely to arise while travelling in an area where the language is spoken
  • Produce simple connected text on familiar topics or topics of personal interest
  • Describe experiences, events, dreams, and ambitions, and briefly give reasons and explanations for opinions and plans

In practical Italian terms, B1 means: you can buy things in a shop, navigate a bureaucratic interaction at the comune, understand most of a television news broadcast, read a standard newspaper article with occasional dictionary use, and have a conversation about your daily life, family, and work.

It does not mean you are fluent. It does not require comprehension of specialized vocabulary, complex grammar, or formal register Italian. It is genuinely the intermediate threshold — capable of daily life, not yet comfortable in all settings.

Accepted Tests: CILS, CELI, PLIDA, and Others

Italy accepts certification from four state-recognized Italian language certification bodies. All are calibrated to the CEFR framework:

CILS — Certificazione di Italiano come Lingua Straniera

Issued by: Università per Stranieri di Siena (the University for Foreigners in Siena)
Test format: Reading, writing, listening, speaking (4 sections). Paper-based and increasingly digital.
Session frequency: June and December, at authorized centers worldwide including several US cities
US test centers: Washington DC, New York, Chicago, Los Angeles (check current authorized list at cils.unistrasi.it)
Cost: Approximately €100–€150 for B1 level
Notes: The most widely taken Italian language certification for citizenship purposes; well-recognized by Italian authorities; clear B1 preparation materials available

CELI — Certificati di Conoscenza della Lingua Italiana

Issued by: Università per Stranieri di Perugia (University for Foreigners in Perugia)
Test format: 5 sections including reading, writing, listening, speaking, and use of language
Session frequency: February, April/May, and October/November
US test centers: Limited; primarily in Italy and Europe
Cost: Approximately €80–€130
Notes: Slightly more detailed test format than CILS; highly respected; fewer US test centers than CILS

PLIDA — Progetto Lingua Italiana Dante Alighieri

Issued by: Società Dante Alighieri (international Italian cultural organization)
Test format: 4 sections (reading, writing, listening, speaking)
Session frequency: Monthly or bi-monthly at Dante Alighieri chapters worldwide
US test centers: Available at Dante Alighieri Society chapters in major US cities (New York, Boston, Chicago, San Francisco, Los Angeles)
Cost: Approximately $100–$150
Notes: Most conveniently available in the US due to the Dante Alighieri chapter network; same recognition as CILS and CELI for citizenship purposes

DITALS — Didattica dell'Italiano Lingua Straniera

This certification, issued by Università per Stranieri di Siena, is primarily for Italian language teaching qualifications rather than general language proficiency. It is accepted for citizenship purposes but is less common among citizenship applicants who are not language teachers.

Which test to choose?

For Americans applying from the US: PLIDA is often the most accessible option due to Dante Alighieri Society chapters in most major US cities, with frequent test sessions.

For Americans already living in Italy: CILS is the most common choice; test centers are available in all major Italian cities and the certification is unambiguously recognized.

All three certifications are equally valid for Italian citizenship applications — choose based on proximity of test centers and scheduling convenience.

How Hard Is B1 for an English Speaker?

The US Foreign Service Institute classifies Italian as a Category I language — relatively easy for English speakers, requiring approximately 600–700 hours of classroom study to reach professional working proficiency (B2/C1). B1 is substantially less demanding — achievable in 200–350 hours of structured study for most English-speaking adults.

Italian and English share a large vocabulary of Latinate origin — words like "azione," "governo," "economico," "problematico" are recognizable to any English speaker. Italian pronunciation is highly regular (unlike English or French). The grammar is more complex than English (gendered nouns, verb conjugation, subjunctive mood) but the building blocks are recognizable.

Realistic timelines for a motivated English-speaking adult starting from zero:

  • Self-study only (Duolingo, Pimsleur, Italian media): 18–24 months to comfortable B1
  • Classes (2x/week, plus self-study): 12–18 months to B1
  • Intensive immersion in Italy: 6–12 months to B1 if engaging actively with Italian speakers
  • Full-time dedicated study: 4–6 months to B1 is achievable for high-motivated learners

Exemptions from the Language Test

Applicants are exempt from the B1 language test if they hold:

  • A qualification demonstrating Italian as the language of instruction — if you attended Italian schools and hold an Italian secondary or university diploma, the language requirement is considered met
  • A higher-level Italian language certificate (B2, C1, C2) already on file — B1 or higher is always accepted
  • Certain disabilities that make standardized language testing impractical — evaluated case by case

Living in Italy for 10 years does not automatically exempt you from the test requirement, but the competent prefettura (the government office that processes naturalization applications) can exercise discretion in borderline cases where the applicant's Italian is clearly sufficient through their documented daily life (employment records, medical records, integration evidence). In practice, most prefetture still require the certificate — do not rely on discretion; take the test.

How to Prepare Efficiently

The most effective preparation strategy for the B1 citizenship test:

  1. Start with structured basics: Apps (Duolingo for daily habit, Babbel for structured grammar) build vocabulary efficiently. Use these for the first 3–6 months to establish foundations.
  2. Add conversation practice early: Italki, Preply, or local Italian conversation groups provide the speaking practice that apps cannot. Even 30 minutes/week of Italian conversation accelerates comprehension dramatically.
  3. Use exam-specific practice materials: Each certification body publishes past papers and sample tests on their websites. Take at least 3 full practice tests under timed conditions before your exam. The format is predictable — knowing what to expect eliminates exam-day anxiety.
  4. Immersive media: Italian Netflix content (start with subtitles, move to Italian-only subtitles, then no subtitles), Italian podcasts for learners (Italiano in 3 minuti, News in Slow Italian), and Italian radio (RAI Radio 1) all train listening comprehension — the section that surprises candidates most.
  5. Take a preparation course: Most Italian cultural institutes (Istituto Italiano di Cultura) in US cities offer Italian language courses specifically structured for the B1 certification. These courses know the test format and focus preparation accordingly.

Where to Take the Test

From the US:

  • PLIDA: Dante Alighieri Society chapters — search the Dante Alighieri website for your nearest chapter and their test schedule. Major chapters in New York, Chicago, Boston, San Francisco, Los Angeles, Miami.
  • CILS: Authorized centers listed at cils.unistrasi.it — fewer US locations, primarily in major cities
  • Italian Cultural Institutes (Istituto Italiano di Cultura) in various US cities administer one or more of the above certifications

From Italy (if you are already living there):

  • Test centers in all major cities — CILS sessions in June and December at universities and authorized private language schools
  • Local Italian language schools (scuole di lingua) often serve as test centers and offer preparation courses

What Happens After You Pass

Your certification is valid for two years from the issue date for citizenship application purposes (confirm current rules with your prefettura — validity periods can change). Submit the certification with your citizenship application. The prefettura logs it as your language documentation. You do not need to re-take the test if your certification is within the validity period when your application is reviewed.

FAQ

Can I take the language test in Italy even if I live in the US?
Yes — you do not have to take the test in your country of residence. Some applicants travel to Italy for the test, either combining it with a visit or taking advantage of more frequent test dates in Italy. All major Italian cities have test centers for CILS and other certifications.
I took university Italian courses in the US. Can those substitute for the B1 test?
Generally no. University Italian courses from US institutions are not on the accepted certifications list. The Italian government requires certification from the four recognized certification bodies. However, if your university courses gave you the Italian skills, channel them into passing the B1 test — the test itself is not difficult if you have studied Italian seriously.
Is there a speaking component to the B1 test?
Yes — all three main certifications (CILS, CELI, PLIDA) include a speaking section, typically involving a structured conversation or task with an examiner. This is the section applicants find most anxiety-provoking but is also the most straightforward at B1 level — you are not expected to discuss complex or formal topics, just handle everyday situations in Italian. Practice speaking aloud consistently in the months before your test.
