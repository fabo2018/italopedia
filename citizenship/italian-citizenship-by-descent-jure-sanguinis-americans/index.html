Italian Citizenship by Descent (Jure Sanguinis): Complete Guide for Americans (2025)

Everything Americans need to know about claiming Italian citizenship by descent (jure sanguinis) — including the critical 2025 law changes under Law 74/2025, who still qualifies, documents needed, and alternative paths.

Millions of Americans have Italian ancestry. For decades, Italy offered one of the most generous citizenship-by-descent programs in the world — tracing back generations with no generational limit.

That changed in 2025. On May 24, 2025, Italy enacted Law No. 74/2025 (the "Tajani Decree"), the most significant restriction to Italian citizenship by descent in over a century.

The 2025 Law Change: What Happened and Why

Before May 24, 2025 (Old Rules)

Under Italy's 1912 Citizenship Law, there was no generational limit for jure sanguinis claims. If you could prove an unbroken chain of Italian citizenship back to an Italian-born ancestor alive after Italy's unification in 1861, you could apply — regardless of how many generations back.

What Changed: Law No. 74/2025 (Effective May 24, 2025)

The Tajani Decree introduced a strict generational limit: For applications submitted after March 27, 2025, citizenship by descent is only available through a parent or grandparent born in Italy.

Great-grandparents, great-great-grandparents, and earlier ancestors no longer qualify for new applications.

Why the change? Italian consulates were overwhelmed — applications surged 40% between 2014 and 2024, with massive backlogs in US consulates (often 3+ years for appointments).

The Critical Cutoff: March 27, 2025

  • Applications submitted OR consulate appointments confirmed before March 27, 2025: Processed under the old rules — no generational limit
  • Applications filed or appointments booked on or after March 28, 2025: Subject to the new rules — parent or grandparent only

Who Can Still Claim Italian Citizenship by Descent (New Rules)

Under Law 74/2025, you qualify for jure sanguinis if:

Condition 1: You have an Italian-born parent or grandparent (not great-grandparent or earlier)

Condition 2: That parent or grandparent held only Italian citizenship at the time of your birth

Additional requirement: Your Italian ancestor must not have lost Italian citizenship through naturalization before your parent was born

The Transmission Chain: How Citizenship Passes

Italian citizenship passes from parent to child automatically at birth — but the chain can be broken by naturalization.

Example of an unbroken chain:

  • Your grandfather was born in Italy → Italian citizen
  • Your grandfather naturalized as a US citizen after your parent was born → citizenship already transmitted
  • Your parent was an Italian citizen when you were born → you're an Italian citizen

Example of a broken chain:

  • Your grandfather naturalized as a US citizen before your parent was born → citizenship NOT transmitted
  • You have no claim

The 1948 Rule: Women's Citizenship (Still Applies)

Before 1948, Italian law did not allow mothers to transmit Italian citizenship to their children. If your Italian ancestor in the transmission line is female, and she was Italian before 1948, the judicial route in Italian courts is often necessary. This typically takes 12–24 months.

Full Document Checklist

For Each Person in Your Chain (Italian Ancestor → You)

  • Italian birth certificate (atto di nascita) for the Italian-born ancestor
  • Marriage certificates for every marriage in the chain
  • Naturalization record — proving when your ancestor naturalized (critical for the minor rule analysis)
  • Death certificates for deceased ancestors

For the Applicant (You)

  • Valid US passport
  • Your birth certificate
  • Proof of no criminal record

All Documents Must Be:

  • Apostilled (for US documents submitted to Italian authorities)
  • Translated into Italian by a certified translator
💡 Tip

Fabrizio's note: Allow 6–18 months for document gathering alone, especially for older records.

Two Application Routes

Route 1: Italian Consulate in the US (Administrative Route)

Pros: Lower cost, can be done while living in the US Cons: Extremely long waits — US consulates are backlogged 3–5 years

Route 2: Italian Municipality (If You Move to Italy)

Pros: Potentially faster than US consulate Cons: Requires living in Italy

Route 3: Italian Court (Judicial Route)

For complex cases — including 1948 cases or cases where an applicant wants to bypass the administrative queue. Pros: 12–24 month resolution Cons: Requires an Italian lawyer; higher costs (€3,000–8,000+)

Italian Citizenship by Naturalization: The Alternative Path

As a US citizen residing in Italy:

  • After 10 years of legal continuous residency → eligible to apply
  • Requirements: legal residency throughout, clean criminal record, Italian language knowledge (B1 level)

What Italian Citizenship Gives You

Italian citizenship is also EU citizenship:

  • EU passport — live, work, and travel freely in all 27 EU member states
  • Italian passport — visa-free access to 190+ countries
  • Your children inherit — Italian citizenship passes to your children automatically

FAQ

I have Italian great-grandparents — can I still claim citizenship? Under the new Law 74/2025, new applications based on great-grandparents no longer qualify administratively. If your application was submitted or your consulate appointment was confirmed before March 27, 2025, the old rules apply.

Does Italian citizenship require me to give up US citizenship? No. The US generally allows dual citizenship, and Italy allows it. You would hold both passports.

How long does the jure sanguinis process take? Consulate route in the US: 3–5+ years. Judicial route in Italy: 12–24 months with a lawyer.

Is there a language requirement for jure sanguinis? No. Italian language proficiency is NOT required for citizenship by descent (jure sanguinis). It IS required for citizenship by naturalization (B1 level).
