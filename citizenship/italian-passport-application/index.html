Italian Passport Application: Everything You Need After Getting Citizenship

Once your Italian citizenship is officially recognized, applying for your passport is the logical next step. The process is straightforward — but the consulate appointment wait is often the biggest obstacle. Here's exactly what to expect.

Before You Apply: Prerequisites

You cannot apply for an Italian passport until your citizenship is officially on record in the Italian civil status system. What this means in practice depends on how you obtained citizenship:

  • Jure sanguinis via Italian consulate: Once your consulate has formally recognized your citizenship and your birth/descent records are transcribed in the Italian civil registry (stato civile), you are ready to apply. Many consulates handle the passport application at a follow-up appointment after the citizenship recognition is complete.
  • Jure sanguinis via Italian tribunal: After the court issues its decree (decreto), the judgment must be transcribed in the civil registry of the relevant comune. Once the transcription is confirmed, you can apply for your passport at the consulate or directly in Italy.
  • Citizenship by marriage (matrimonio): After the Ministry of Interior issues the citizenship decree and it is recorded in the civil registry, you can apply.
  • Citizenship by naturalization (naturalizzazione): Same — Ministry decree must be recorded and you must have taken the oath of loyalty (giuramento) before applying for the passport.
  • Citizenship obtained in Italy (10-year residency): Apply directly at your local questura (police headquarters) or at the sportello del cittadino of your comune.
AIRE Registration First

If you are applying at an Italian consulate abroad, you should also be registered (or simultaneously register) with AIRE — the registry of Italians residing abroad. Many consulates will prompt you about this at the passport appointment. If your AIRE status is not in order, some consulates will resolve it at the same appointment; others may require you to sort it out first. It's best to come prepared on both fronts.

Where to Apply: Consulate vs. In Italy

Applying at an Italian Consulate (if you live abroad)

Italian citizens residing abroad apply for their passport at the Italian consulate that has territorial jurisdiction over their place of residence. In the United States, that means one of the ten consular offices: Boston, Chicago, Detroit, Houston, Los Angeles, Miami, New York, Philadelphia, San Francisco, or Washington D.C. (plus the Embassy in Washington, which handles some passport functions).

The consulate appointment is the main bottleneck. High-volume consulates — New York, Los Angeles, Miami — can have appointment wait times of several months. Mid-size consulates may be more accessible. Check your consulate's booking portal regularly; cancellations open slots unexpectedly.

Applying in Italy

If you are in Italy at the time you want to apply — whether you live there or you're visiting — you have two options:

  • Questura (police headquarters): The Italian passport is technically a Polizia di Stato document, and questure are the primary issuing authority. Every provincial capital has a questura with a passport office (ufficio passaporti). Book your appointment online at passaportonline.poliziadistato.it.
  • Comune (city hall): Some comuni also accept passport applications through their sportello anagrafico. Not all comuni offer this service — check locally.
  • Post offices (Poste Italiane): For certain renewals (not first issuances), some post offices participate in the passport application service. This is a convenience option for simple renewals.

Appointment availability in major Italian cities (Milan, Rome, Naples) can also be tight. Smaller questure in less-populated provinces often have much better availability — if you have relatives in a smaller Italian town and you are visiting Italy, applying there may be faster.

Documents Required

The standard document list for a first Italian passport application:

  • 2 identical, recent passport-format photographs — white background, no glasses, eyes open and clearly visible, face must occupy 70–80% of the frame. Exact specifications are posted on the Polizia di Stato website and consulate websites. Photos must be recent (taken within the last 6 months).
  • Completed passport application form — available at questure, comuni, and consulate websites. Bring multiple copies.
  • Proof of Italian citizenship — for first-time applicants after a citizenship recognition process, bring the certificate of citizenship (certificato di cittadinanza) issued by your comune, or a copy of the citizenship recognition decree. If applying at a consulate, your citizenship records should already be in the consulate's system.
  • Valid government-issued ID — for applicants in Italy, this means an Italian carta d'identità or a previous Italian passport if you have one. For first-time applicants abroad, you may use your US passport as ID documentation, accompanied by your Italian citizenship documents.
  • Payment receipt (bollettino) for the €116 passport fee (see Fees section below)
  • For minors: birth certificate, both parents' identity documents (or court order if one parent is absent), and a signed consent form from both parents

Some consulates have additional or slightly different requirements. Always check your specific consulate's official requirements list before the appointment — requirements can vary and change.

Fees and Payment

The standard Italian passport fee as of 2025 is €116.00 for the ordinary electronic passport (passaporto ordinario elettronico). This fee covers:

  • 48-page passport booklet (standard)
  • Electronic chip and biometric data storage
  • 10-year validity for adults

Payment methods vary by location:

  • In Italy at questura or comune: Payment is made via a bollettino postale (postal payment slip) at Poste Italiane, or increasingly via PagoPA online payment system. Do not bring cash to the questura — pay in advance and bring the receipt.
  • At Italian consulates in the US: Each consulate specifies accepted payment methods. Most accept money orders, bank checks, or specific online payment methods. Very few accept cash. Check your consulate's fee page for the exact amount in USD (the consulate converts the euro fee at the current exchange rate, so the dollar amount changes periodically).
Emergency / Urgent Passport

If you need a passport urgently due to documented travel requirements (e.g., imminent documented travel, medical emergency abroad), questure and consulates can process urgent applications. You will need to demonstrate the urgency with documentation (flight booking, medical documents, etc.). There is no additional fee for urgency processing at questure in Italy, though processing priority is given. At consulates, urgent processing procedures vary.

Biometric Requirements

The Italian electronic passport stores biometric data on a chip embedded in the cover. When you apply in person, the following biometric data is collected:

  • Digital photograph: Captured digitally at the questura or consulate at the time of application, or from the photograph you submit (some offices scan the submitted photo; others take a digital photo directly)
  • Fingerprints: Both index fingers are scanned electronically at the time of application. Children under 12 are exempt from fingerprint collection. Children aged 12 and over must have fingerprints taken.
  • Signature: Your signature is captured digitally at the time of application

This is why the Italian passport application requires an in-person appearance — the biometrics cannot be submitted remotely. You must physically appear at the questura or consulate to complete the application.

Processing time varies significantly depending on where you apply:

Processing time is measured from when your complete application is submitted with all documents, biometrics, and payment — not from when you book your appointment. The appointment wait and the processing time are two separate periods.

Once your passport is ready, you pick it up in person at the questura or consulate. Some consulates mail the completed passport — check your consulate's policy.

The Electronic Passport (Passaporto Elettronico)

Italy has issued biometric electronic passports since 2006. The current model is a 48-page burgundy-red booklet with the Italian Republic's emblem on the cover and the biometric chip symbol on the back cover. Key features:

  • RFID chip: Stores your biometric data (photo, fingerprints) and personal information
  • Machine-readable zone (MRZ): Two lines of text at the bottom of the photo page for automated border control reading
  • Polycarbonate data page: The photo page is a single piece of polycarbonate, not a paper page — much harder to tamper with
  • Security features: UV-reactive printing, laser engraving, holographic elements
  • E-gate compatible: At Italian and EU airports with e-gate lanes, Italian passport holders can use automated gates rather than the manned queue

The Italian passport ranks among the most powerful in the world by visa-free access. As of 2025, it provides visa-free or visa-on-arrival access to approximately 190 countries, broadly similar to the US passport in global reach.

Note that a child cannot be added to a parent's passport — each person, including infants, requires their own individual passport. This has been the rule in Italy since 2006.

The passport expiry date is printed on the data page. For travel to some countries, the passport must have at least 3 or 6 months of remaining validity beyond the intended date of travel. For EU travel, even an expired Italian passport or Italian carta d'identità can often be accepted (other EU countries are required to accept valid Italian ID documents for entry), though this varies by country and it's better practice to travel with a valid document.

Using Your Italian Passport to Enter Italy and the EU

This is where the Italian passport becomes practically transformative for dual US-Italian citizens.

Entering Italy

Italian citizens have the right to enter Italy without any limitations or maximum stay periods. When you travel to Italy on your Italian passport, you use the EU/EEA/Swiss nationals lane at border control (the "Passaporti UE" lane), not the non-EU line. You do not fill in a customs declaration for routine stays. You do not need to prove onward travel, accommodation, or sufficient funds. You have the right to remain indefinitely.

At Italian airports with e-gates, Italian passport holders can use the automated gates for fast processing. This is a notable quality-of-life improvement over joining the non-EU queue.

Freedom of Movement in the EU

As an Italian (and therefore EU) citizen, you have the right of free movement throughout the European Union under the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU) and EU Directive 2004/38/EC. This means:

  • You can travel to any EU member state and remain for up to 3 months without registration requirements
  • After 3 months, you can remain in any EU country by registering as a resident there (the requirements vary by country)
  • You can work in any EU country without needing a work visa or permit
  • You can study in any EU country with access to EU student status and corresponding rights
  • You can access healthcare, social security, and other benefits on equal terms with nationals of the EU country where you reside (subject to contribution requirements)

Additionally, as an Italian citizen you can use your passport to travel to Schengen Area countries (which includes most of the EU plus Norway, Switzerland, Iceland, and Liechtenstein) through the EU/EEA lane at border control, with no customs checks between Schengen states.

Practical Travel Tip: Which Passport to Use

When traveling between Italy/EU and the US as a dual citizen, the general practice is: use your Italian passport to enter and exit EU/Schengen territory, and use your US passport to enter and exit the United States. US law requires US citizens to use their US passport to enter and exit the US. Italian law requires Italian citizens to use their Italian passport to enter and exit Italy.

Airlines book you under one passport number — typically the one for the destination country. You carry both passports and present the appropriate one at each border.

Lost or Stolen Passport Abroad

If your Italian passport is lost or stolen while you are outside Italy:

  1. Report to local police immediately. File a police report (denuncia) with the local police force in the country where you are. Get a copy of the report — you will need it.
  2. Contact the nearest Italian consulate or embassy. Italian consulates can issue an emergency travel document (titolo di viaggio di emergenza) to allow you to return to Italy or continue essential travel. This is a single-use, limited-validity document.
  3. Report to your Italian consulate of registration. When you return home to the US, notify your Italian consulate in the US that the passport has been lost/stolen and that you need a replacement. Bring the police report and your previous passport details.

Italian passports are valid identity documents in Italy and EU countries. A lost passport must be reported to prevent misuse — Italian passports are occasionally targets for identity fraud.

Can I apply for an Italian passport without being registered on AIRE?
Technically, some consulates will process the passport application and AIRE registration simultaneously. Others require AIRE registration to be complete before processing the passport. It varies by consulate. The safest approach is to address AIRE registration and passport application together — they are handled by the same office.
My Italian citizenship was just recognized. How long before I can actually apply for the passport?
Once your citizenship records have been fully transcribed in the Italian civil registry (stato civile), you can apply immediately. The delay between citizenship recognition and being able to apply is typically the administrative transcription time — this can range from a few weeks to a few months depending on the comune or consulate involved. Ask your consulate specifically when your records will be ready for a passport application.
Can my children get Italian passports if I have Italian citizenship?
Yes. Children born to an Italian parent inherit Italian citizenship at birth (regardless of birthplace), provided the parent was already an Italian citizen at the time of the birth. If your citizenship was recognized after your children were born, the situation is more complex — you should consult with your consulate, as the applicable rules depend on the specific jure sanguinis laws in effect at the time of each birth.
What is the difference between the Italian passport and the carta d'identità for travel?
The Italian carta d'identità (national identity card) is valid for travel within the EU/Schengen Area but is not valid for entry to most non-EU countries. The passport is valid globally. For travel within Europe, the carta d'identità is a lighter, wallet-sized alternative. For travel to the US, the UK, and most non-EU countries, the passport is required.
I have an expired Italian passport. Can I renew it at the consulate even if I've never applied there before?
Yes. Renewal follows the same process as a new application at the consulate — you submit the same documents, pay the same fee, and attend an in-person appointment. Bring your expired passport to the appointment; the consulate will retain it and issue a new one.
