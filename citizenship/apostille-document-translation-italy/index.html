# Apostille & Document Translation for Italy: The Complete American Guide (2025)

Every American who applies for an Italian visa, residence permit, citizenship, or property purchase will eventually face the same bureaucratic requirement: apostilled documents with certified Italian translations. If you don't know what an apostille is, you're not alone — most Americans encounter this word for the first time when dealing with Italian authorities.

This guide explains exactly what apostilles are, which documents need them, how to get them efficiently, and how to handle Italian translations. Get this right and your applications move smoothly. Get it wrong and you lose weeks to rejected documents.

What Is an Apostille?

An apostille is a standardized international certification that authenticates the origin of a public document. It was established by the Hague Convention of October 5, 1961 (Convention Abolishing the Requirement of Legalisation for Foreign Public Documents) — ratified by both the United States and Italy.

Before the Hague Convention, documents had to go through a lengthy "chain of authentication" to be recognized internationally. The apostille replaced this chain with a single certification.

What an apostille does:

  • Certifies that the signature/seal on a document is genuine
  • Certifies that the person who signed had the authority to do so
  • Makes the document legally recognized in all Hague Convention member countries — including Italy

What an apostille does NOT do:

  • It does not certify the content of the document is accurate
  • It does not replace a certified translation

The apostille is a separate page (or stamp) attached to or placed on the original document, issued by the competent authority in the document's country of origin.

Official reference: Hague Convention of 5 October 1961 (HCCH) — hcch.net/en/instruments/conventions/full-text/?cid=41

Which U.S. Authority Issues Apostilles?

In the United States, apostilles are issued by state Secretaries of State (or equivalent state authority). The federal government issues apostilles only for federal documents.

Document TypeApostille Issued By
Birth certificateSecretary of State of the state where born
Marriage certificateSecretary of State of the state where married
Divorce decreeSecretary of State of the state where divorced
Death certificateSecretary of State of the state where died
State police certificateSecretary of State of that state
Notarized documentsSecretary of State of the state where notarized
FBI background checkU.S. Department of State (federal)
Federal court documentsU.S. Department of State (federal)

U.S. Department of State apostilles (for federal documents like FBI checks):

  • Website: travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/records-and-vital-information/authenticate-your-document
  • Processing time: 6–8 weeks by mail; can be expedited
  • Fee: $20 per document

Documents Commonly Required With Apostille for Italy

For Visa Applications (Elective Residency, Digital Nomad, etc.)

  • FBI background check (federal apostille — U.S. Dept. of State)
  • State criminal background check (state apostille)

For Citizenship by Descent (Jure Sanguinis)

  • U.S. birth certificates (all generations)
  • U.S. marriage certificates (all generations)
  • U.S. death certificates (where relevant)
  • U.S. naturalization records (or USCIS "no record" letter)

For Citizenship by Marriage

  • U.S. marriage certificate
  • FBI background check

For Property Purchase

  • Passport (no apostille needed — passports are recognized internationally without one)
  • Power of attorney (if signing is done remotely — notarized in U.S. + apostille)

For Opening a Bank Account

  • Generally passport only — no apostille required

How to Get a State Apostille: Step by Step

Method 1: Direct to Secretary of State (DIY)

Time: 2–8 weeks (varies by state)

Cost: $5–20 per document (state fees vary)

  1. Get a certified copy of the document (not a photocopy — a certified copy with an official seal/signature). For a birth certificate, this means ordering from the county vital records office, not printing from a database.
  1. Send to Secretary of State with:

- The certified document

- A cover letter specifying "apostille for use in Italy"

- The fee (check, money order, or credit card depending on state)

- A self-addressed return envelope (prepaid, with tracking)

  1. Wait for return — the Secretary of State attaches the apostille page

State-specific resources:

  • California: sos.ca.gov/notary/apostilles — current fee $20/doc; 3–5 business days in person
  • New York: dos.ny.gov/corps/apostilles.html — $10/doc; 1–2 weeks by mail
  • Texas: sos.texas.gov/authentications — $15/doc; 1–3 weeks
  • Florida: dos.myflorida.com/apostilles — $10/doc; 2–3 weeks

Method 2: Authorized Apostille Agencies (Recommended for Speed)

Time: 1–5 business days

Cost: $50–150 per document (agency fee + state fee)

Third-party agencies that specialize in apostille processing can dramatically speed up the process. They have established relationships with state offices and can often get same-day or next-day apostilles.

Reputable agencies:

  • ItsEasy (itseasy.com) — major cities; in-person and mail
  • One Source Process (onesourceprocess.com)
  • Apostille.us — nationwide service
  • Rush My Apostille — expedited service

Worth the cost if: You're on a tight timeline for a visa appointment, or dealing with multiple documents across multiple states.

Method 3: In Person at Secretary of State Office

Many states offer walk-in or same-day apostille service if you visit the Secretary of State's office in person with your certified documents. For California (Sacramento), New York (Albany), or other state capitals — this is the fastest DIY option.

The FBI Background Check Apostille: Special Process

The FBI background check for Italian visa and citizenship applications requires federal apostille from the U.S. Department of State — not a state apostille.

Step 1: Get the FBI Check

Direct FBI Route:

  • Submit fingerprint card + Form FD-258 + $18 fee
  • Timeline: 6–8 weeks
  • Website: fbi.gov/services/cjis/identity-history-summary-checks

FBI Channeler (Recommended):

FBI-approved third-party channelers process your fingerprints faster:

  • Timeline: 3–10 business days
  • Cost: $18 (FBI fee) + $10–25 (channeler fee)
  • Reputable channelers: IdentoGO, Accurate Biometrics, L-1 Identity Solutions

Step 2: Apostille the FBI Check

Send the original FBI results letter to:

U.S. Department of State, Office of Authentications

44132 Mercure Cir, PO Box 1206, Sterling, VA 20166-1206

  • Fee: $20 per document
  • Timeline: 6–8 weeks standard; expedited service available through registered couriers
  • Website: travel.state.gov

Total timeline FBI check + apostille (standard): 8–14 weeks

Total timeline FBI check + apostille (expedited via channeler): 2–4 weeks

Italian Translation: What's Required

Italian law and consulate requirements mandate that foreign-language documents be accompanied by a certified Italian translation (traduzione giurata or traduzione asseverata).

What "Certified" Means in Italy

A certified translation for Italian legal purposes means one of:

Option A: Traduzione Giurata (Sworn Translation)

The translator appears before an Italian court (Tribunale) and swears under oath that the translation is faithful. The court records this oath. This is the gold standard and accepted everywhere.

Option B: Traduzione Asseverata (Asseverated Translation)

Similar to sworn translation but done via a notarized declaration rather than court appearance. Accepted by most Italian consulates and administrative bodies.

Option C: Translation by Consular Officer

Some Italian consulates accept translations performed or verified by consular staff. Ask your specific consulate.

Finding a Certified Translator

In Italy:

  • Search the Tribunale (court) register of sworn translators (interpreti e traduttori iscritti all'albo del Tribunale)
  • Many commercialisti (accountants) and avvocati (lawyers) dealing with foreign clients maintain relationships with sworn translators

In the United States:

  • Italian consulates maintain lists of approved translators for their district
  • American Translators Association (ATA): atanet.org — search Italian translators
  • Local Italian-American organizations often have referrals

Cost per page (English to Italian): €20–50 per page for certified translation; €10–25 per page for non-certified. Plan your document budget accordingly.

What constitutes a "page": Typically 250–300 words of source text.

Documents That ALWAYS Need Translation

  • U.S. birth certificates
  • U.S. marriage certificates
  • FBI background checks
  • U.S. tax returns (when required by Italian consulates)
  • Employment letters
  • Property documents

Documents That May NOT Need Translation

  • Passport (internationally recognized without translation in most contexts)
  • Italian-issued documents (already in Italian)
  • Some standardized EU documents

├── 📁 Apostilled Copies

│   └── ...
├── 📁 Certified Translations
│   ├── [Document] Italian translation
│   └── ...
└── 📁 Scanned Copies (backup)

Always keep originals. Italian authorities return apostilled originals after review, but mistakes happen. Never send your only copy of an original document. Keep certified copies where possible.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Mistake 1: Getting a photocopy apostilled instead of a certified copy

An apostille on a photocopy is worthless. You need an apostille on a certified copy — one issued by the official authority (county vital records, court, etc.) with an original signature or seal.

Mistake 2: Wrong state for apostille

The apostille must come from the Secretary of State of the state that issued the document. A California birth certificate needs a California apostille — you can't get it apostilled in New York.

Mistake 3: Outdated documents

Some consulates and Comuni require documents issued within the last 6 months (for documents that change — like residence certificates). Others accept documents of any age. Confirm requirements for your specific application.

Mistake 4: Translation by an unqualified translator

A bilingual friend's translation, no matter how accurate, is not a certified Italian translation. It will be rejected. Use only qualified translators.

Mistake 5: Apostille before the certified copy is obtained

The sequence matters: get the certified document FIRST, then apostille it. You cannot apostille a document that isn't already a proper certified copy.
