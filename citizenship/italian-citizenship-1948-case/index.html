# Italian Citizenship 1948 Case: The Maternal Line Route for Americans (2025)

If your Italian ancestry passes through a woman who was born before January 1, 1948, you've likely hit a wall with standard jure sanguinis citizenship claims. Italian law, as traditionally applied, blocked citizenship transmission through women before 1948. But a series of Italian court decisions — now well-established in legal practice — has opened a pathway for these cases.

It's called the "1948 Case" (Caso 1948), and it has allowed thousands of Americans to claim Italian citizenship they would otherwise have been denied.

The Problem: Italy's 1948 Citizenship Law

When Italy became a republic in 1948, the new Constitution established equal rights for men and women. But the citizenship law that governed transmission of Italian citizenship through generations (Law 555/1912) predated this — and it treated men and women differently.

Under Law 555/1912 as traditionally applied:

  • An Italian father could transmit citizenship to children born at any time
  • An Italian mother could only transmit citizenship to children born on or after January 1, 1948 (when the Republican Constitution came into force)

This meant: if your Italian great-grandmother emigrated to the U.S. in 1905, married an American, and had children before 1948 — those children were considered to have received no Italian citizenship from her. The chain was broken.

The 1948 Case is the legal remedy for exactly this situation.

The Legal Basis: Court Decisions, Not Legislation

The 1948 Case is not based on a law passed by the Italian Parliament. It is based on rulings by Italian courts — primarily the Corte di Cassazione (Italy's Supreme Court) — that found the pre-1948 gender discrimination unconstitutional under the 1948 Republican Constitution.

Key rulings:

  • Corte di Cassazione, Sezione I Civile, Sentenza n. 4466/2009 — established that Italian courts have jurisdiction over citizenship cases involving the pre-1948 maternal line
  • Corte di Cassazione, Sentenza n. 25310/2014 — further clarified the pathway
  • Multiple subsequent Tribunale di Roma and Tribunale di Napoli decisions confirming the approach

The legal argument: Denying citizenship transmission through women before 1948 violates Articles 3 and 29 of the Italian Constitution (equality principles). Therefore, courts can recognize citizenship claims that administrative channels (consulates, prefetture) refuse.

Important: Because this is court-based, not legislation-based, the standard administrative jure sanguinis process at Italian consulates does not process 1948 Cases. You must go through the Italian judicial system.

Who Has a 1948 Case?

You have a potential 1948 Case if all of the following are true:

  1. Your line of Italian ancestry passes through a woman at some point
  2. That woman was born before January 1, 1948 (or more precisely: the critical birth — her child's birth — occurred before 1948)
  3. The woman was Italian at the time she transmitted (or should have transmitted) citizenship
  4. No one in the line renounced Italian citizenship before the next person in the chain was born
  5. You can document the chain with vital records

The precise rule: The woman must have been Italian at the time her child was born, and that birth must have occurred before January 1, 1948. The child's gender and subsequent transmission follow normal jure sanguinis rules.

Example Scenario

  • Great-great-grandmother: Born in Italy 1870, emigrated to U.S. 1895
  • Great-grandmother: Born in U.S. 1897 (before 1948) — mother was Italian citizen at birth ✅
  • Grandmother: Born in U.S. 1922 (before 1948)
  • Mother: Born in U.S. 1950
  • You: Born in U.S. 1975

Under standard jure sanguinis: citizenship claim fails at the 1897 birth (through maternal line, before 1948).

Under 1948 Case: courts recognize the claim despite the maternal transmission before 1948. Your great-great-grandmother's citizenship should have passed to her daughter in 1897 — the gender discrimination was unconstitutional.

The Court Process: Step by Step

Step 1: Genealogical Research and Document Collection

Before filing anything, you need to build your case. This means collecting:

From Italy:

  • Birth certificate of your Italian ancestor (atto di nascita) from the Comune where they were born
  • Marriage certificates (atti di matrimonio) for each generation
  • Death certificates where relevant
  • Any naturalization records showing when (if ever) Italian ancestors naturalized

From the U.S.:

  • Birth certificates for each generation born in the U.S.
  • Marriage certificates
  • Naturalization records — critical to prove Italian ancestors did NOT naturalize before the relevant birth in the chain
  • Death certificates

For naturalization research:

  • USCIS (NARA) naturalization records
  • Federal court naturalization records
  • State court records pre-1906
  • If no record found: USCIS issues a "no record found" letter — this serves as evidence of non-naturalization

All U.S. documents need apostille + certified Italian translation.

All Italian documents may need certified copies from the Comune.

Timeline for document collection: 3–12 months. Italian Comuni can be slow responding to records requests. Budget time.

Step 2: Find an Italian Lawyer (Avvocato)

The 1948 Case requires an Italian lawyer admitted to the Italian bar. This is not optional — court filings in Italy require legal representation.

You need a lawyer specializing in diritto della cittadinanza (citizenship law). Many Italian firms handle exclusively citizenship cases and have English-speaking staff.

Cost: Legal fees range from €3,000–8,000+ depending on case complexity. Firms that handle many 1948 Cases have streamlined processes that keep costs closer to the lower end for straightforward cases.

Where to find specialists:

  • Italian bar association directory (Consiglio Nazionale Forense — consiglionazionaleforense.it)
  • Referrals from Italian citizenship community groups (Facebook: "Italian Citizenship Assistance," Reddit: r/juresanguinis)
  • Some Italian-American organizations maintain lawyer referral lists

Step 3: File the Petition (Ricorso)

Your lawyer files a ricorso (petition) with the competent Italian civil court. Jurisdiction depends on:

  • If you live in Italy: the Tribunale of your Italian province of residence
  • If you live abroad: Tribunale di Roma has jurisdiction for non-residents

The ricorso establishes:

  • Your full genealogical chain
  • The Italian citizenship of your ancestor
  • The unconstitutional gender discrimination that blocked the standard claim
  • Your request for court recognition of your citizenship

The Italian Ministry of Interior (Ministero dell'Interno) is named as the respondent.

Step 4: Court Proceedings

Italian civil court proceedings for 1948 Cases are typically handled through written briefs (non-adversarial hearings in most cases). The judge reviews:

  • Your genealogical documentation
  • The legal arguments
  • The Ministry's response (which often does not oppose straightforward cases)

Timeline from filing to decision: 1–3 years depending on court backlog. The Tribunale di Roma, which handles the majority of these cases, has varying wait times.

Step 5: Court Decision (Sentenza)

If the court rules in your favor, you receive a sentenza (judgment) declaring you an Italian citizen.

This judgment must be:

  1. Registered with the Italian vital records system (transcribed at the relevant Comune)
  2. Transmitted to the Italian consulate in your country for passport issuance

Step 6: Passport Application

With the court judgment registered, apply for your Italian passport at the Italian consulate in your U.S. consulate district.

Timeline Expectations

  • Document collection: 3–12 months
  • Lawyer engagement and case preparation: 1–3 months
  • Court proceedings: 1–3 years (Tribunale di Roma) or faster at regional courts
  • Registration and passport: 3–6 months after judgment

Total from start to Italian passport: 3–6 years for most cases.

Practical Tips

Start with genealogical research before hiring a lawyer. Many people spend thousands on a lawyer only to discover their chain has a break (ancestor naturalized before the relevant birth). Do the genealogical work first — NARA records, FamilySearch, Ancestry.com — before engaging legal counsel.

Get the USCIS "no record" letter early. This is often the longest-lead document. File the USCIS genealogy search (Form G-1041) as early as possible. Processing can take 3–6 months.

Consider filing in Italy vs. Rome. If you establish Italian residency (which you can do on an elective residency or other visa), you may be able to file at a regional Tribunale rather than Rome, potentially with faster processing.

The 1948 Case and standard jure sanguinis are not mutually exclusive. If your line passes through both maternal and paternal ancestry, you might have a standard jure sanguinis claim on one line and a 1948 Case on another. Explore both with your lawyer.
